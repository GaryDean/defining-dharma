# 6.1 Secular Translation

**Question:** How can traditional dharmic insights be translated into secular frameworks without losing their transformative power? What are successful examples?

## Synthesis
"Translation" is not one operation but a spectrum, and where a project lands on it
decides whether transformative power survives. At one pole is **extraction** — lifting
a technique out of its ethical, cosmological, and communal matrix and reselling it as a
self-help tool; this is what produced clinical mindfulness and its critique as
"McMindfulness," a practice that demonstrably *works* (modestly) yet loses the power to
transform a life or a society [S6][S7]. At the other pole is **reconstruction** —
stripping the metaphysics but keeping the practice *and* the ethics *and* a functional
replacement for whatever the metaphysics used to do (bind, motivate, endure); this is
Stephen Batchelor's secular Buddhism, the Stoicism-to-CBT lineage, and ProSocial's
science-grounded group practice [S1][S3][S4]. The recurring lesson across every success
and failure is the same: **transformative power lives in the practice-plus-ethics-plus-
community bundle, not in the doctrine alone** — so translation succeeds when it preserves
the bundle and re-grounds it this-worldly, and fails when it keeps only the technique. The
honest caveat is that "successful" here means *attested*, not *measured against the
failures that went unwritten*, and traditionalists deny any stripping is loss-free
[S10] — a dispute that turns on the unresolved [[3.3-grounding-problem]].

## Findings

### What "translation" means: from extraction to reconstruction — and recovery
The corpus describes secularization simply as "stripping away supernatural or
metaphysical elements" [A], but stripping is only the dangerous half of the operation;
the decisive question is what is *kept* and what is *put back* in the metaphysics' place.
Pierre Hadot supplies the frame that reorganises the whole question: ancient Western
philosophy *was itself* a "way of life" — a body of **spiritual exercises** (*askēsis*),
not a set of doctrines — and this practical dimension *declined* in modern academic
philosophy [S8]. On Hadot's reading, secular translation of a tradition is therefore
partly an act of **recovery**: restoring the practice-dimension that the source tradition
may itself have buried under doctrine and institution. Batchelor makes exactly this claim
about Buddhism — it "evolved into a religious philosophy, adopting many beliefs of ancient
India, establishing hierarchies of priests, temples, and rituals," and in that process
"the core philosophy of Buddhism became obscured" [S2]. Translation, on this view, is not
betrayal of the original but reclamation of its lost working core.

### Case 1 — Secular Buddhism (Batchelor): deliberate reconstruction
Batchelor is the clearest worked example of reconstruction. He reports that as his
practice "became increasingly secularized," the metaphysical load — "rebirth, karma, and
the Four Noble Truths" — "fallen away," leaving "a much clearer, more focused, and most
importantly, workable and useful basis for a practical philosophy": the **four tasks**
(embracing life, letting reactivity be, seeing it stop, cultivating a path) which "tap
into something that does not require any kind of metaphysical belief… pragmatic… at its
core the question of how to live well… essentially an ethical project" [S1]. Critically,
he does **not** keep only the technique: he retains contemplative practice *and* ethics
*and* community, and at Bodhi College reframes "secular Buddhism" as "secular **Dharma**"
— a path that is "ethical, philosophical, and contemplative," explicitly "more than just
ethics" [S2]. His Mindfulness-Based Ethical Living (MBEL) "mirrors the work of Jon
Kabat-Zinn… in MBSR," but pointedly "removes them from an explicitly therapeutic context"
and *re-inserts the social-ethical scope* MBSR had dropped — treating "homophobia, racism,
and so forth" as central as "greed, fear, and hatred" [S1]. This deliberate widening is
the hinge of the whole note: Batchelor's project is, in part, a *correction* to the next
case. The traditionalist objection stands against all of it: B. Alan Wallace charges that
Batchelor "rewrites history" — that rebirth and karma were load-bearing structure, not
cultural packaging, so no stripping is loss-free [S10].

### Case 2 — MBSR / clinical mindfulness (Kabat-Zinn): extraction, and its price
Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction is the cautionary pole. Jon Kabat-Zinn, a
microbiologist, founded MBSR in 1979 as a modest eight-week clinical program at the
University of Massachusetts Medical School, extracting meditation from its Buddhist
ethical and cosmological setting and delivering it as a stress-reduction protocol [S12].
On its own terms it *works*: the Goyal et al. (2014) meta-analysis in *JAMA Internal
Medicine* found "small to moderate" reductions in anxiety, depression, and pain across
clinical populations [S7]. But Ronald Purser's *McMindfulness* (2019) argues that the very
stripping that made it scalable destroyed its transformative reach: "right mindfulness" is
only one factor of the Buddhist eightfold path, and "the claim that major ethical changes
intrinsically follow from 'paying attention to the present moment, non-judgmentally' is
patently flawed" [S6]. Detached from ethics and community, mindfulness becomes "a banal
form of capitalist spirituality that… avoids social and political transformation,
reinforcing the neoliberal status quo" — co-opted by "corporations, schools, governments
and the military" as a tool of "self-pacification" [S6]. MBSR is thus the project's
sharpest demonstration that **efficacy is not transformation**: a translation can be
clinically validated *and* have lost the point.

### Case 3 — Modern Stoicism: a translation that was always half-secular
Stoicism is the cleanest success because its translation literally produced a branch of
modern psychotherapy. The cognitive theory of emotion at the heart of CBT — that "our
emotions are mainly determined by our beliefs" — descends directly from Epictetus'
*Enchiridion*: "Men are disturbed not by things, but by the views which they take of them"
[S9]. Both founders of cognitive therapy credited the Stoics: Albert Ellis (REBT) said he
had "rescued Epictetus from oblivion," and Aaron Beck wrote that "the philosophical
origins of cognitive therapy can be traced back to the Stoic philosophers" [S3][S9].
Beyond the clinic, Stoicism is reviving as a *practiced* way of life: Donald Robertson
recounts discovering it through Hadot's *Philosophy as a Way of Life* and turning Stoic
"spiritual exercises" — the "view from above," *premeditatio malorum* (negative
visualization), *memento mori* — into guided meditations and handbooks for contemporary
readers [S3]. Here practice and ethics are retained while the metaphysics (the Stoic
providential cosmos, the *logos*) is made optional — though that very move re-opens the
is/ought question of [[3.3-grounding-problem]]: classical Stoic virtue was grounded *in*
the rational cosmos that modern Stoicism quietly drops.

### Case 4 — ProSocial / P-SAGE: functional translation via science
ProSocial World represents a fourth strategy: rather than secularise one tradition, build
the *generic machinery* of group contemplative practice and re-ground it in evolutionary
science. Its P-SAGE program (ProSocial Spirituality Active Group Engagement,
Templeton-funded) integrates "contemplative practices and interspiritual elements into
ProSocial World's scientifically-based framework," organised around "Core Design
Principles" adapted from Elinor Ostrom's principles for governing commons, under David
Sloan Wilson's multilevel-selection theory [S4]. This is Wayne Teasdale's
"interspirituality" operationalised — keep the contemplative practice, swap the
supernatural ground for evolutionary self-understanding [S4]. It is the most explicit
model of *functional* translation (replace what religion did, not what it said); its
weakness, addressed below, is that the available evidence is the program's own promotional
testimony.

### The cross-tradition pattern: not only a Buddhist phenomenon
The same structural move recurs outside Buddhism, which suggests a general grammar of
secular translation rather than a quirk of one tradition. Don Cupitt — the Anglican
theologian whose "Sea of Faith" movement and *Taking Leave of God* (1980) Batchelor names
as the mirror of his own work [S1] — advanced a Christian **non-realism** in which
religious language "does not refer to independently existing supernatural entities but
articulates human ethical and spiritual aspirations," favouring "a this-worldly, embodied
spirituality which bears fruit in ethical and practical living" [S11]. Strip the
metaphysics, keep the practice and ethics, re-ground this-worldly: the recipe is identical
to Batchelor's, executed inside Christianity. That two thinkers working in unrelated
traditions converged on the same operation — and recognised each other as kin — is itself
evidence that secular translation has a transferable structure.

### What "without losing transformative power" requires
Across the four cases, three conditions separate translations that keep their grip from
those that don't:

1. **Preserve the bundle, not just the technique.** Practice, ethics, and community
   travel together; extract the technique alone and you get McMindfulness [S6], whereas
   keeping ethics and community within the practice is precisely Batchelor's corrective
   [S1]. This is why [[5.4-ritual-habituation]]'s installation mechanism — repeated
   embodied practice in a community — is the real carrier of transformation, not assent
   to propositions [S8].
2. **Supply a functional replacement for the metaphysics.** Whatever the supernatural
   anchor did — bind normatively ([[3.3-grounding-problem]]), confer durability
   ([[5.2-evolutionary-psychology]]), motivate sacrifice — must be re-supplied this-worldly:
   existential urgency and ethics (Batchelor), a practiced telos of virtue (Stoicism), or
   evolutionary self-understanding (Wilson) [S1][S3][S4]. Translation that merely *deletes*
   the anchor without replacing its *function* is the predictable failure mode.
3. **Treat the result as a way of life, not a doctrine.** Hadot's point cuts both ways:
   the West already *lost* its practical philosophy once, by letting it harden into
   theory; a secular dharma that becomes mere worldview-without-exercise has translated
   the words and lost the work [S8].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias
This is the most acute corpus-bias case in the project to date, because **the shared
corpus is curated by the secular-translation movement it is being asked to assess.**
Stephen Batchelor is one of its named mentors; a large share of the retrieved material is
secular-Buddhist talk transcripts and AI-generated "future of secular dharma" essays that
treat the success of translation as foregone [S1][S2][A]. On this exact question the
corpus is therefore an *advocate, not a neutral witness*. Every success claim sourced from
inside it had to be demoted to a hypothesis and tested against external critique: the
entire critical weight of this note — Purser's *McMindfulness* [S6], Wallace's "Distorted
Visions" [S10], the bounded Goyal efficacy figure [S7], and even the existence of an
authenticity debate — came from outside the corpus, which only gestures vaguely at
"debates over the authenticity of secular interpretations" and "questions about the
effectiveness of secularized practices" without engaging them [A].

The ProSocial/P-SAGE material is a second, milder instance of the same problem: the
corpus's evidence for its "remarkable success" and "transformative power" is the program's
own testimonials and promotional copy, not independent evaluation [S4]. It is a good model
of *how* to translate functionally, but not yet evidence that the result *retains*
transformative power.

Three things remain genuinely unresolved. **First**, the "something essential is lost"
objection is not decidable from evidence: Wallace holds that rebirth and karma were
load-bearing structure that Batchelor discards by "rewriting history," while Batchelor
treats them as historically debatable scaffolding [S10] — the same essential-versus-
cultural question as [[4.3-indigenous-oral-dharmas]], and it cannot be settled by efficacy
data (MBSR "works" modestly yet may still have lost the point [S6][S7]). **Second**, "transformative
power" is itself undefined and the cases may be measuring different things — Purser means
social/political transformation, Kabat-Zinn clinical change, Batchelor ethical
reorientation. **Third**, there is a hidden denominator: we have the success stories that
got written up, but failed secular translations (dissolved secular sanghas, abandoned
Stoicism apps, defunct secular congregations) are invisible, so "successful examples"
cannot yet be weighed against the rate of attempts — a measurement gap that
[[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]] must set the bar for.
