# 6.2 Digital-Age Dharma

**Question:** What new dharmic frameworks are emerging to address digital-age challenges like information overload, social media, and AI? How do they differ from traditional forms?

## Synthesis
A cluster of new frameworks is emerging — "humane technology" (Tristan Harris / Center for
Humane Technology), the attention-resistance philosophies (James Williams, Cal Newport,
Jenny Odell), and science-grounded collective programs (David Sloan Wilson's ProSocial) —
and what unites them is that they treat **attention** as the contested ground of the good
life [S5][S6][S7][S8]. They differ from traditional dharmas chiefly in the *locus of the
adversary*: classical contemplative paths trained the individual mind against its own
craving and delusion in a world of information *scarcity*, whereas digital-age frameworks
confront an *external, industrialized* adversary — an "extractive attention economy" of
firms using AI to hijack evolved reward systems (supernormal stimuli) in a world of
*abundance* [S6][S1][S10]. This forces a move classical contemplative dharma never needed:
redesign the *environment*, not only the self — a structural and political register
[S5][S2]. Yet the new frameworks also *recover* the old insight that attention is never
ethically neutral (Buddhist *sati* was always morally embedded) [S9]. The cautionary
thread from [[6.1-secular-translation]] returns sharpened: secular mindfulness, the
celebrated translation, can itself become the attention economy's pacifier, so a real
digital-age dharma must be more than a coping technique [S11].

## Findings

### Reading humane tech and attention-resistance as emergent dharmas
None of the figures here call their work a "dharma." Reading humane technology, digital
minimalism, and the "attention ecology" *as* emergent secular dharmas is the project's
interpretive lens, licensed by the working definition that dharmas are plural, adaptive
ethical paths ([[1.3-boundary-definition]]): each offers a way of orienting attention and
a life toward what one values, carried by practices and an ethic. That framing is named
here as interpretation, not as the authors' self-description (see Tensions).

### The new adversary: the extractive attention economy
The defining shift is from an inner to an industrial adversary. James Williams — a Google
advertising strategist turned Oxford philosopher — argues in *Stand Out of Our Light*
(2018) that as information becomes abundant the truly scarce resource is **attention**,
and that the internet and social media are "not knowledge systems or communicative
networks, but ways of attracting attention and redirecting people away from their wills";
human freedom is therefore "threatened by systems of intelligent persuasion" [S6]. Tristan
Harris, an ex-Google design ethicist, founded the Center for Humane Technology (2018) out
of the "Time Well Spent" movement to fight what he calls the "human downgrading" worked by
an "extractive" attention economy in which firms profit by "playing tricks on people's
minds" [S5]. At the limit, attention itself is enclosed and commodified — "the new
frontier of the capitalist process of enclosure" — which is why some theorists reframe the
goal as moving "from an attention economy to an ecology of attention" [S10].

The corpus's distinctive contribution is the *evolutionary mechanism* beneath this. Mark
van Vugt (interviewed for ProSocial) describes social media as "an exaggerated cue, a
supernormal stimulus": humans crave social interaction, but where our ancestors had to
"expend energy and time" to maintain relationships, "social media interactions come very
cheap," and "just as drugs hijack our reward system so do tweets, likes, and shares." The
danger is addiction, and "we have no natural defense mechanisms against the social media
invasion of our lives," so we are thrown back on uneven self-control [S1]. This is the
[[5.2-evolutionary-psychology]] mismatch applied directly to the feed.

The collateral damage is informational and social. With the gatekeeping function of
traditional media eroded, "anyone can distribute any kind of news without a guarantee of
quality," harmful and polarizing content spreads fastest, and — because "the more people
act on their convictions, the stronger and less changeable that conviction becomes" —
sharing entrenches belief; interventions such as "accuracy prompts" can cut willingness to
share misinformation by as much as 20% [S3]. There is even a neural worry: screen-mediated
interaction may operate "less efficiently" for the mirror-neuron system underlying theory
of mind and role-taking, with possible costs to social cohesion (the source cites Sherry
Turkle's *Alone Together* and the literature on democratic polarization) [S4].

### The responses — a spectrum from individual practice to structural redesign
The emerging frameworks array along a spectrum that mirrors [[6.1-secular-translation]]'s
extraction-to-reconstruction axis:

- **Individual practice.** Cal Newport's *Digital Minimalism* (2019) offers "a philosophy
  of technology use" built on intentional selection of a few high-value activities, a
  30-day "digital declutter," and reclaimed solitude, set against software "engineered to
  create behavioral addictions" [S7]. Structurally this is an installation practice in the
  sense of [[5.4-ritual-habituation]] — a ritualized decluttering that re-trains a
  disposition.
- **Re-grounding attention.** Jenny Odell's *How to Do Nothing: Resisting the Attention
  Economy* (2019) argues not for withdrawal but for redirecting attention to "active modes
  of listening and contemplation," resisting "a colonization of the self by capitalist
  ideas of productivity," and staying "in the midst of life"; attention is re-anchored in
  place, bioregion, and community [S8] — a thread that runs forward to
  [[8.1-planetary-dharma]].
- **Structural / collective.** Harris's *humane technology* moves the locus from self to
  *system*: align technology "with humanity's best interests" by redesigning the
  environment and its incentives [S5]. Wilson's ProSocial casts itself as "Morality in the
  Machine Age," insisting that collective intelligence stay distributed among all members
  rather than "restricted to machines or human elites," on the premise that "moral systems
  are the glue" holding groups together and that the Internet Age "can either enhance or
  disrupt moral systems… depending upon how the benefits are distributed" [S2]. Mental-
  immunity training and accuracy-prompt design are public-scale versions of the same
  structural impulse [S3].

### How these differ from traditional forms
- **Locus of the adversary:** inner craving and delusion → external industrial extraction.
  Traditional contemplative dharmas disciplined attention against the *untrained mind*;
  digital-age dharmas defend attention against *engineered* capture [S6][S1].
- **Information regime:** scarcity → abundance. Wisdom was once rare and teacher-gated; now
  the task is filtering an engineered deluge rather than acquiring scarce insight [S6].
- **From self-cultivation to environment-design:** traditional dharma worked only on the
  practitioner; digital-age dharma must *also* act on the attention environment — design,
  regulation, platform governance — a political dimension classical contemplative paths
  lacked [S5][S2].
- **Continuity beneath the rupture:** the new frameworks *recover* the traditional truth
  that attention is ethically loaded. Buddhist *sati* was "never a neutral awareness" but a
  "morally informed and conceptually structured attentiveness" — *yoniso manasikara*,
  "appropriate attention," not "bare attention" — embedded in the eightfold path [S9].
  Williams's account of attention as the capacity to "do what you want to do" (higher-order
  volition) is a secular restatement of right intention, linking to
  [[3.2-metacognitive-function]] [S6].

### The reflexive trap: when the cure becomes the disease
The sharpest finding bridges back to [[6.1-secular-translation]]. The most celebrated
secular-translation success — mindfulness — has itself been absorbed *by* the attention
economy. Corporate and app mindfulness is deployed to make "overworked, underpaid,
stressed-out employees" more productive and resilient rather than to question the system,
shifting focus "away from starting a union or going on strike"; as critics note, "one
becomes mindful to become more productive and produce technologies of mass distraction,
which is quite an irony" [S11]. The mechanism is exactly the one [S9] warns of: when
mindfulness is reduced to decontextualized "bare attention" severed from ethics, it
becomes "an ethically neutral tool easily co-opted." This confirms Purser's
[[6.1-secular-translation]] thesis in a new key — a digital-age dharma that is only a
coping technique can be turned against the practitioner, becoming the system's pacifier
rather than its remedy [S11][S9].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias
This note has the project's largest **coverage gap**: the shared appliedanthropology
corpus is effectively silent on the digital-age question — the survey surfaced an
AI-generated joke-essay ("the probability someone is watching is proportional to the
stupidity of your action"), not analysis. The topic sits outside the corpus's
evolutionary-anthropology and secular-Buddhism core, so 6.2 is **predominantly
externally sourced** (Harris, Williams, Newport, Odell, the *sati* scholarship); the
corpus contributed framing only.

Where the corpus did help — prosocial.world — it is advocacy for its own product. Its
genuine evidentiary value is the *mismatch* read (van Vugt, [S1]) and the *moral-systems /
multilevel-selection* frame (Wilson, [S2]); but its self-description as "Morality in the
Machine Age" and the implication that ProSocial *solves* the problem are promotional and
unevaluated, and are treated here as hypotheses rather than demonstrated efficacy.

Three further limits. **First**, "new dharmic frameworks" is an interpretive stretch: none
of these authors frames their work as a dharma, so reading humane tech / digital minimalism
/ attention ecology *as* emergent secular dharmas is the project's lens, defensible under
[[1.3-boundary-definition]] but to be named as interpretation, not finding. **Second**, the
evidence base is thin and preliminary: the claim that screens degrade theory of mind [S4],
or that minimalism durably improves well-being, is suggestive rather than established, and
the efficacy of these "dharmas" is essentially unmeasured ([[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]]).
**Third**, the individual-practice responses risk the very McMindfulness co-optation they
diagnose [S11], which pushes the real remedy toward the structural register — contested
terrain where attention-as-enclosure is a question of power, not technique
([[7.3-dharma-and-power]]). The deeper AI questions (recommender power, agency, labour
displacement) appear here only as the *engine* of the attention economy and are deferred to
[[8.2-ai-and-dharma]].
