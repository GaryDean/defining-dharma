# 6.4 Gender & Dharma

**Question:** How do dharmic systems encode, enforce, or contest gender roles? Where have dharmas been used to subordinate, and where to liberate, along gender lines?

## Synthesis
Gender is among the very first things a dharma encodes, and the historical record is
lopsided toward subordination: most codified dharmas have sacralized patriarchal
arrangements under cosmic or scriptural warrant — Hindu *strīdharma*, the Buddhist
*garudhammas*, the Confucian "three obediences," and the household codes of the Abrahamic
traditions all converge on female subordination [S4][S7][S9]. Yet nearly every tradition
also carries a liberating counter-current: egalitarian counter-texts (the Buddha's own
envisaged fourfold equality; Galatians 3:28), women mystics and poets who used devotion to
walk out of patriarchy (Mīrābāī, Akka Mahādēvi, the Therīgāthā nuns), and modern feminist
reconstructions (Gross, Wadud) [S1][S5][S7][S8][S10][S11]. So **dharma per se neither
subordinates nor liberates: it is the contested terrain on which gender is fought, and the
same scripture routinely supplies both the chains and the keys.** Underneath, the biology
shows real but non-dichotomous, heavily overlapping sex differences entangled with culture
[S3] — so the steep gender hierarchies dharmas enforce are cultural constructions claiming
natural or cosmic necessity, and feminist reconstruction is a [[6.1-secular-translation]]
move (reclaim the egalitarian core, discard the patriarchal accretion) whose success turns
on whether the patriarchy is accretion or structure.

## Findings

### Gender as a primary object of dharmic encoding (biology vs. culture)
A gendered division of labour is ancient and near-universal in foraging societies — Ken
Wilber, synthesising the feminist anthropology of Lenski, Chafetz, and Nielsen, reports
that "an astonishing 97 percent of foraging societies" assigned hunting to men and
gathering/child-rearing to women, with strong taboos against crossing roles — yet "this
division did not translate into significant differences in status" [S2]. Steep gender
hierarchy is therefore not a simple read-out of the biological substrate; it *intensified*
with later techno-economic forms (notably plough agrarianism, cf. [[2.2-urban-complexity]])
[S2]. The biology itself is qualified: Robert Sapolsky argues against "the myth of sex" —
males and females are "members of the same species with patterned, structured differences
in particular biological and physiological processes, but not in others"; meta-analysis
shows the brain is a continuum of facets, not a male/female dichotomy (the kidney we never
sex, the brain we always do); "both extremes" — no sex differences or only sex differences
— "are incorrect," and personalities "overlap quite a lot" [S3] (cf. [[5.2-evolutionary-psychology]]).
Dharmas convert this modest, overlapping dimorphism into a steep moral hierarchy with
sacred warrant — which is the core operation this note traces.

### The subordinating mainstream (a cross-tradition convergence)
- **Hindu *strīdharma*.** The *Manusmṛti* is explicit: "By a girl, by a young woman, or
  even by an aged one, nothing must be done independently, even in her own house" — woman
  passes from father to husband to son, never autonomous. Womanhood is constructed as
  wifehood (Sanskrit *strī* = both woman and wife), and women's supposedly "uncontrollable"
  sexuality is to be tamed by self-imposed restriction [S4].
- **Buddhist *garudhammas*.** Eight "heavy rules" make *every* nun subordinate to *every*
  monk — a *bhikkhunī* "ordained even for a hundred years" must bow to "a monk ordained but
  that day" [S7]. The orthodox narrative has the Buddha reluctant to ordain women, yielding
  only under Ānanda's pressure, comparing their presence to a "blight" and "disease" [S1].
- **Confucian "three obediences and four virtues."** A woman is to "obey your father before
  marriage," "obey your husband when married," and "obey your sons in widowhood," fulfilling
  the role of "a submissive daughter, wife, and mother" (Ban Zhao, *Lessons for Women*, 1st
  c. CE) [S9].
- **Abrahamic household codes.** Pauline injunctions to wifely submission and gendered
  ritual roles fence women into subordinate positions (the egalitarian counter-text is
  examined below).

Across all four, dharma supplies divine or cosmic sanction for controlling women's
autonomy, sexuality, and reproduction (cf. [[7.3-dharma-and-power]]).

### The liberating counter-current (within each tradition)
The same traditions carry their own opposition:

- **Buddhist.** Batchelor reconstructs a Buddha who "clearly envisaged a community in which
  all members — irrespective of their status as men or women, monastics or laity — are
  entirely equal," vowing not to leave the world until he had "men and women mendicants and
  men and women adherents" accomplished in the dharma [S1]. The **Therīgāthā** — 73 poems
  by or about enlightened elder nuns, the oldest collection of women's poetry in India,
  composed by "queens, courtesans, mothers, widows" — attests women's full capacity for
  awakening [S7]. Rita Gross's *Buddhism After Patriarchy* (1993) argues that "the core
  teachings of Buddhism promote gender equity rather than male dominance, despite the often
  sexist practices found in Buddhist institutions," and the lost Theravāda *bhikkhunī*
  lineage has been revived via Taiwan [S8].
- **Hindu.** Bhakti women-saints used devotion to transgress: Akka Mahādēvi "walked out of
  her marriage and wandered naked, with her body covered only by her hair"; Mīrābāī "left
  her husband and family" for pilgrimage to Krishna. "Intimacy with God offered an
  unprecedented pretext for transgressing gender norms" — often the only avenue of
  self-expression open to women [S5].
- **Christian.** Galatians 3:28 — "there is neither male nor female, for you are all one in
  Christ Jesus" — is read by egalitarians as "the fundamental Pauline theological basis for
  the inclusion of women," though complementarians contest that reading as "divorced from
  its context" [S10]: the liberatory text and the subordinating one sit in the same canon.
- **Islamic.** Amina Wadud's *Qur'an and Woman* (1999) and *Inside the Gender Jihad* (2006)
  reread scripture "from a woman's perspective," arguing "the Qur'an carries a principle of
  equality between man and woman" and that "inequality… is contrary to Islam"; her "gender
  jihad" reframes struggle as the pursuit of gender justice [S11].

### Three nuances against romanticising either pole
1. **Feminine-divine ≠ women's status.** The worship of *Śakti* venerates the goddess's
   power, but women in the same societies remain "generally disadvantaged": they are
   accepted when they embody goddess-virtues (forgiveness, compassion, tolerance) and
   "considered destructive" when assertive [S6]. Goddess-veneration does not track women's
   social position.
2. **The counter-currents were exceptional and costly.** Women mystics typically had to
   *renounce womanhood* — leave marriage, body, household — to claim spiritual authority
   [S5]; theirs was personal escape, not structural reform, and they remained a minority
   against the patriarchal norm.
3. **Reconstruction is a translation wager.** Gross and Wadud reclaim an egalitarian "core"
   and discard patriarchal "accretions" — the same essential-vs-cultural move as
   [[4.3-indigenous-oral-dharmas]] and the same operation as [[6.1-secular-translation]].
   Its validity depends on the contested claim that the patriarchy is accretion, not
   structure — which is exactly what the *garudhammas*-authenticity debate (the rules are
   "absent from Sanskrit and Chinese" versions and may be a later interpolation [S7][S1])
   and the Galatians-context debate [S10] are really about.

### Secular dharmas and gender (reflexive)
Do secular dharmas escape the encoding? The secular-Buddhist reconstruction explicitly
foregrounds gender justice — Batchelor argues for "a complete restoration of equality
between mendicants and adherents of both sexes" [S1], and his MBEL names sexism alongside
greed and hatred as targets of practice ([[6.1-secular-translation]]). But secular
translation can smuggle patriarchy back in: the wellness/mindfulness market is heavily
feminised, the modern Stoicism revival skews masculine, and reactionary quasi-dharmic
movements repackage gender-traditionalism as a secular "order." Most tellingly, the corpus
itself shows how a *scientific* secular dharma can re-naturalise hierarchy — its
gender-biology layer is dominated by essentialist material that reads modest, overlapping
differences as fixed destiny (see Tensions). Shedding the supernatural anchor does not
automatically shed the gender hierarchy — confirming the [[6.1-secular-translation]]
crypto-religion risk and pre-loading [[7.1-failure-modes]].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias
The corpus is **two-faced** on this question. On the reconstruction side it is genuinely
strong: Batchelor's *After-Buddhism* is an excellent gender-egalitarian source that even
flags the *garudhammas* as a patriarchal interpolation "inserted into the canon by
patriarchal hard-liners" [S1]. But on the biology side it leans hard toward **essentialism**:
the retrieval surfaced a cluster of AI-generated essays built around dismissive one-liners
(Amy Alkon, E. W. Howe, Heinlein) that naturalise sex differences and sneer at equality
claims, plus a hereditarian voice asserting personality is "at least 60 percent biologically
determined" and citing innate toy-preferences. The genuinely careful science — Sapolsky's
"both extremes are incorrect," the brain-as-continuum [S3] — is present but was *not* the
loudest signal; the corpus tilts toward biological essentialism, which I balanced with
Sapolsky's qualified view and by importing the comparative-religion gender scholarship
(Hindu, Confucian, Islamic, Christian) wholesale [S4]–[S11]. The corpus contributes the
Buddhist case well and the biology framing badly; almost everything else is external.

Two further cautions. **First**, the evaluative frame "subordinate vs. liberate" is itself a
modern, broadly liberal standard (cf. the WEIRD critique in [[5.3-moral-psychology-development]]
and [[3.4-relativism-universalism]]); many practitioners — including many women — describe
complementarian roles as dignified and chosen, not oppression, and a non-privileging
treatment must register that self-understanding while still naming the documented,
systematic asymmetries of autonomy and authority. **Second**, the archive is itself
patriarchal: dharmic texts were overwhelmingly authored by male elites, so the subordinating
mainstream is over-documented and women's agency under-recorded — which is precisely why the
Therīgāthā and the Bhakti poems matter so much [S5][S7]. What remains unresolved is how much
of the gendered division of labour is evolved versus constructed ([[5.2-evolutionary-psychology]]),
and whether the egalitarian counter-texts are the "true core" or a minority strand
retrospectively privileged by modern values — the reconstruction's central, undecided wager
([[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]]).
