# 6.3 Economics, Work & Inequality

**Question:** How do dharmic frameworks engage material life — work, wealth, debt, and inequality? How have dharmas justified, constrained, or critiqued economic arrangements?

## Synthesis
Across traditions, dharmas treat economic life as **morally embedded** — work, wealth, and
debt are never a neutral sphere governed by its own laws but central matters of the path
itself [S4][S7]. Within that embedding, dharmas have done three different things with
material life. They have **justified** economic orders — Hindu *varna* assigns each class
its occupational duty, and Calvinist election turned disciplined work into a sign of grace
that, secularized, survives as modern meritocracy [S3][S10]. They have **constrained**
transactions — Right Livelihood, the cross-religious prohibition of usury, the Islamic
*zakat*, and the biblical Jubilee all fence the market with moral limits [S5][S9]. And they
have **critiqued** accumulation — most powerfully in David Graeber's demonstration that the
very language of morality (sin, guilt, redemption) is borrowed from debt, and that debt is
the supreme instrument for making the subordinated "feel it's their fault" [S1][S8]. The
distinctively modern condition is the **disembedding** of the economy (Polanyi): the claim
that the market is an autonomous, morally neutral sphere, against which contemporary
economic dharmas — secular and religious alike — argue for re-embedding material life in
human ends [S4][S5]. The deepest tension is that dharmas both bless and bind wealth, and
their binding constraints are routinely circumvented [S6].

## Findings

### The economy as morally embedded (the pre-modern default)
Karl Polanyi's *The Great Transformation* (1944) supplies the organizing frame: in
pre-modern societies the economy was **embedded** in social relations and institutions, not
a free-standing system; land, labour, and money were not treated as commodities [S4]. The
Hindu *puruṣārthas* make this embedding explicit by listing wealth *inside* the ethical
scheme: the four legitimate aims of life are *dharma* (righteousness), *artha* (prosperity),
*kāma* (pleasure), and *mokṣa* (liberation) — so the pursuit of material security is a
sanctioned goal, but "only when pursued within ethical boundaries," and "in cases of
conflict, dharma is considered more important than artha" [S7]. Material life is thus
located *within* the path, never outside it. This is the baseline that the modern
disembedding of the economy (below) overturns.

### Dharma as justifier of economic order
The most consequential — and most contested — function is legitimation.

- **Hindu *varṇa* / *varṇāśrama-dharma*.** Each *varṇa* carries its own occupational
  dharma, and "how healthy a society is depends on how well the [varnas] perform their
  functions." Defenders hold the original system was "based on cooperation, mutual service,
  and commensurate rights and responsibilities," but "what began as a flexible
  classification based on qualities and actions became a rigid, hereditary hierarchy," with
  texts like the *Manusmṛti* binding *varṇa* to birth [S10]. Here dharma supplies divine
  sanction for an economic division of labour — the legitimating function in its starkest
  form (cf. [[7.3-dharma-and-power]]).
- **Calvinism → the Protestant ethic → meritocracy.** Michael Sandel's reading of Weber
  traces the same structure in the West: Calvinism "combined strenuous work with
  asceticism," and disciplined work-but-low-consumption "yields the accumulation of wealth
  that fuels capitalism" — so that "even when the original religious motivations fall away,
  the Protestant ethic of work and asceticism provides the cultural basis for capitalist
  accumulation" [S3]. Predestination, demanding that the elect "prove their election
  through work in a calling," "confers divine sanction on the division of labour" [S3].
  Crucially, worldly success was meant as a *sign* of election, not its source — but, Sandel
  argues, it "proved difficult to resist the slide" from sign to source, producing "an
  early version of meritocratic" thinking [S3]. Modern **meritocracy** is the secularized
  residue: success read as desert and virtue, poverty as failure — a secular dharma that
  re-sacralizes inequality after God has dropped out. Reading it as a [[6.1-secular-translation]]
  in reverse — a translation that *kept* the legitimating function while shedding the grace
  and humility that once tempered it — is this project's framing [A].

### Dharma as constraint on transactions
A second function fences the market with moral limits.

- **Right Livelihood and Buddhist economics.** *Sammā-ājīva* (right livelihood), a factor
  of the eightfold path, requires earning "in a way that is ethical and avoids harm to
  others or to nature" [S5]. E. F. Schumacher's "Buddhist Economics" (1966; in *Small Is
  Beautiful*, 1973) builds this into a counter-economics: where "Western economics is
  materialist," treating work as a negative cost to be minimised and happiness as maximised
  consumption, Buddhist economics "aims… to attain human well-being with the minimum
  necessary means," and sees work "not as a burden, but as a noble and necessary part of
  life — a way to develop one's character" [S5]. The slogan is "economics as if people
  mattered" [S5].
- **The prohibition of usury, and redistribution.** The condemnation of interest is
  strikingly cross-cultural: "major religions including Hinduism, Buddhism, Judaism, Islam,
  and Christianity prohibit interest," with Islam offering "the most explicit, severe, and
  comprehensive prohibitions" against *ribā* [S6]. Constraint is paired with redistribution
  — Islamic *zakat* functions as a wealth tax "to assist disadvantaged individuals" — and
  with periodic reset: the biblical **Jubilee** (Leviticus 25) mandated debt release and
  land readjustment, though "there is no evidence that such wholesale redistribution… was
  ever actually carried out" [S9]. And the constraints leak: every tradition developed
  "legal fictions" — the Jewish *Heter Iska*, the Islamic *Murābaḥa* — to narrow the
  definition of usury and route around the ban [S6]. Dharma's grip on the market is real but
  evadable (cf. [[7.1-failure-modes]]).

### Dharma as critique of accumulation and debt
The corpus's deepest material is David Graeber's anthropology of debt. Graeber's central
claim is that morality and debt share a vocabulary: "in a lot of world religions the word
for sin is the same as that for debt" — the Lord's Prayer originally read "forgive us our
debts," Plato's *Republic* defines justice as "paying one's debts," and in the Vedic texts
"life is a debt you pay to the Gods, sacrifice is the interest" [S1]. Tellingly, "in every
case, they first frame morality as debt, and then say, well, actually, no, not really" —
every tradition feels "shackled to that logic" yet strains to escape it [S1]. Debt, for
Graeber, is "the perversion of a promise… through mathematics and violence," so that "we've
come to see such acts of violence as the very essence of morality" [S1]; and it is "the most
effective way to take a relation of violent subordination and make the victims feel that
it's their fault" — colonial regimes "would charge people for the cost of their own
conquest, via taxes" [S1]. The book's constructive point inverts the moral intuition: "in
the ancient world, it was not repaying debts that was sacred, but one's ability to forgive
or… cancel debts" — Mesopotamian "clean slate" amnesties and the biblical Jubilee model a
freedom understood as "the collective power to readjust the commitments we have to one
another" [S2]. The published thesis (*Debt: The First 5,000 Years*, 2011) frames the moral
puzzle sharply: why insist debts be repaid even when repayment "results in the suffering and
death of innocents" [S8]?

### The modern rupture: disembedding and its discontents
What unites contemporary economic dharmas is a response to Polanyi's diagnosis. The 19th-
century project of a self-regulating market sought to **disembed** the economy from society,
treating land, labour, and money as "fictitious commodities" — things "not created for the
market"; a market society that fully commodifies them, Polanyi warned, "would annihilate
society," provoking a "double movement" of protective counter-responses (labour law,
welfare) [S4]. Today's economic dharmas — Schumacher's Buddhist economics, the usury and
Jubilee traditions, Graeber's debt-abolitionism — converge on a single demand: **re-embed**
material life in human and moral ends [S2][S5]. Graeber sharpens the stakes with a
provocation: wage labour, where "we no longer sell ourselves to employers, we rent
ourselves," would look to anyone from the ancient world like a softened form of the
debt-slavery they most feared [S1]. The disembedded economy, on this reading, reproduces an
ancient bondage in a respectable new form — which is exactly what the meritocratic dharma,
by reading the resulting inequality as just deserts, is built to obscure [S3].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias
The corpus is **rich but lopsided to the left** on this question. Its economic material is
curated around David Graeber — an anarchist anthropologist and Occupy activist — and Michael
Sandel, a communitarian critic of meritocracy, supplemented by Buddhist-economics sources.
The result is a strong tilt toward the *critique* of capitalism and inequality and a
thinness on dharmas that *bless* wealth on their own terms. Tellingly, the Calvinist
material reaches the corpus only through Sandel's critical lens (*The Tyranny of Merit*),
not through Weber directly or through any defender; the prosperity gospel, market-friendly
religious economics, and the Hindu affirmation of *artha* as legitimate wealth are
under-represented, and the comparative usury/*zakat*/Jubilee balance and the *puruṣārtha*/
*varṇa* framing had to be imported externally [S6][S9][S7][S10]. A non-privileging treatment must
therefore foreground these systems' *internal* self-understanding — *varṇa* as a cooperative
ideal of mutual service, Calvinist work as the glorification of God — before applying the
corpus's ready critique [S3][S10].

Two further cautions. **First**, Graeber's interview material is polemical and activist; his
strongest claims — that wage labour is a species of debt-slavery, that debt is "the essence"
of violence-morality — are provocations grounded in real scholarship but contested by
economists, and are reported here as a powerful thesis, not settled consensus [S1]. The
corpus also contains AI-generated essays restating Graeber & Wengrow's "indigenous critique"
(`graebers-indigenous-critique_*.md`); these were used for orientation only, with the
genuine Graeber interviews and the published *Debt* carrying the evidentiary weight [S8].
**Second**, the gap between dharmic economic *ideal* and *practice* is wide and largely
unmeasured: usury bans were routinely circumvented [S6], the Jubilee may never have been enacted
[S9], and whether any constraint-tradition actually governed an economy — versus merely
voicing aspiration — is an open empirical question handed to
[[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]] and [[7.1-failure-modes]].
