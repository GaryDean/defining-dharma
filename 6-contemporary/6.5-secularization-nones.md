# 6.5 Secularization & the "Nones"

**Question:** How large and how fast-growing is the "secular age" the essays invoke — the religiously unaffiliated ("nones") and the "spiritual but not religious" (SBNR) — and is it accurate to call it the fastest-growing religious category? This note grounds the opening demographic claim of the Part 7 essay [[6.2-digital-age-dharma]] draws alongside.

## Synthesis
The "secular age" the essays lean on is real and large, but the popular shorthand that
the "nones" are "the fastest-growing spiritual category on Earth" needs two corrections.
**First, in the West the growth was real but is no longer current.** In the United States the
religiously unaffiliated rose from 16% of adults in 2007 to 23% in 2014 to 29% in 2023–24,
faster than any other religious category over those two decades — but Pew's own headline on
its 2023–24 Religious Landscape Study is that this rise has *levelled off*, plateauing since
about 2021 as the parallel Christian decline stabilised [S1][S4]. So a present-tense growth
claim ("more every year") is no longer safe; a past-tense Western claim ("grew faster than
any other category across the two decades to ~2020") is [S1][S4]. **Second, "nones" and
"spiritual but not religious" are different measured things.** The unaffiliated are defined by
their answer to a religious-identity question (atheist, agnostic, or "nothing in particular");
SBNR is a separate self-description, and Pew measures it as 22% of US adults [S1][S2].
Globally the unaffiliated numbered about 1.9 billion in 2020 (24.2% of humanity), up from
1.63 billion (23.3%) in 2010 — a rise of 270 million driven mainly by religious *switching*,
not birth rates, since the unaffiliated are on average old with low fertility [S3]. That
demographic headwind is exactly why "fastest-growing on Earth" cannot be stated flatly as a
durable trend [S3]. The category is large enough to carry the essays' point — that hundreds
of millions now build a life outside inherited religion — without the overstatement.

## Findings

### The US "nones": 29%, and the plateau
Pew's 2023–24 Religious Landscape Study — fielded in English and Spanish from 17 July 2023 to
4 March 2024 among 36,908 respondents, released 26 February 2025 — finds 29% of US adults
religiously unaffiliated: 5% atheist, 6% agnostic, and 19% "nothing in particular" [S1]. The
trend line is 16% (2007) → 23% (2014) → 29% (2023–24) [S1]. The study's central framing is
stabilisation: the Christian share, after years of decline, "has been relatively stable since
2019," and the unaffiliated share, "after rising rapidly for decades, has leveled off — at
least temporarily" [S1][S4]. Pew put the question directly in a companion piece title: "Has
the rise of religious 'nones' come to an end in the U.S.?" [S4]. This is the single most
important correction for the essay: the growth is a completed two-decade surge, not a
live year-on-year climb.

### "Spiritual but not religious": a distinct 22%
The SBNR label comes from a separate Pew study, "Spirituality Among Americans" (survey of
11,201 US adults, 31 July–6 August 2023; published 7 December 2023) [S2]. It sorts US adults
four ways: 48% both spiritual and religious, 22% spiritual but not religious, 10% religious
but not spiritual, and 21% neither — with 70% describing themselves as spiritual in some way
[S2]. SBNR is thus a US self-description of 22% (~58 million adults), not a global headcount,
and it is not the same measure as the 29% unaffiliated: some SBNR adults are religiously
affiliated, and some "nones" are not spiritual at all [S1][S2]. The essay's standfirst figure
of "hundreds of millions" is defensible as written — conservatively so, since the adjacent
global-unaffiliated category is 1.9 billion — provided the body does not read "hundreds of
millions" as a precise SBNR count [S2][S3].

### The global picture: 1.9 billion, but growing by switching
Pew's "How the Global Religious Landscape Changed From 2010 to 2020" (released 9 June 2025,
drawing on 2,700+ censuses and surveys across 201 countries) reports the religiously
unaffiliated at 24.2% of world population (1.9 billion) in 2020, up from 23.3% (1.63 billion)
in 2010 — a gain of 0.9 percentage points and 270 million people, a 17% increase [S3]. The
report is explicit that this growth ran *against* a demographic headwind: the unaffiliated
"population is relatively old, on average, with relatively low fertility rates," so their
expansion is explained mainly by religious switching, "especially people shedding their
religious identity after having been raised as Christians," rather than by natural increase
[S3]. That mechanism is decisive for the "fastest-growing" claim: a category sustained by
switching from an ageing, low-fertility base is not guaranteed to keep growing as a share of
humanity once the pool of switchers thins.

### Verdict on "fastest-growing spiritual category on Earth"
The claim is true only with a time-and-place qualifier. Across roughly 2007–2020, in the US
and much of the Western world, the unaffiliated were the fastest-growing religious category by
switching [S1][S3]. As a present-tense, global, durable claim it is unsafe: US growth has
plateaued [S1][S4], and global growth rests on switching against low fertility [S3]. The
Part 7 essay's revised wording — "grew faster than any other in the Western world across the
two decades to about 2020 … before its rise levelled off" — is the accurate form [S1][S3][S4].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias
This note is **entirely external-sourced**, and that is itself the corpus-bias finding: the
shared YaTTi corpus (curated around Boehm, Sapolsky, Graeber, Batchelor, Bregman) carries the
*interpretation* of secularisation as a condition — Charles Taylor's "secular age," the
disenchantment thesis — but not the current demographic measurements, which post-date and sit
outside it. Any quantitative claim about the "secular age" the essays invoke therefore had to
be sourced from Pew directly; the corpus cannot supply or check these figures.

Three caveats remain. **First**, the future trajectory is genuinely uncertain and the sources
pull in opposite directions: the 2025 US study projects continued unaffiliated growth *if*
current switching persists [S1], while the global report stresses the low-fertility headwind
that could cap the unaffiliated share once switching slows [S3] — so the essays should avoid
any confident forward projection. **Second**, these are overwhelmingly US and Western figures;
the global 24.2% is heavily weighted by China's large unaffiliated population, and "unaffiliated"
means very different things across cultures (a Chinese "none" and an American "nothing in
particular" are not the same social fact) [S3]. **Third**, all figures are self-report on
survey instruments whose category boundaries (spiritual / religious / affiliated) Pew itself
revises between waves, so cross-year comparisons carry method noise [S1][S2]. None of this
threatens the essays' qualitative point — that a very large, historically novel population now
seeks meaning outside inherited religion — but it forecloses the tidy "fastest-growing on Earth"
slogan.
