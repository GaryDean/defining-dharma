# 4.3 Indigenous Oral Dharmas

**Question:** Examine indigenous dharmic systems (e.g. Native American, Aboriginal Australian) that
predate written traditions. What can oral dharmas teach us about essential vs. cultural elements?

## Synthesis

Indigenous oral traditions are fully-functioning dharmas carried with **no scripture, no named
founder, and no codified doctrine** — proof that an ethical order needs none of those. In the
Aboriginal Australian **Dreaming** (Stanner's "everywhen"), the *Tjukurrpa* is *simultaneously*
ancestral law, moral code, and the structure of reality, fusing past–present–future and inscribed on
the "sentient land" via songlines [S2][S4]. In the Haudenosaunee **Great Law of Peace** an entire
constitution lived orally (on wampum) for centuries, carrying the **Seven Generations** duty to weigh
every decision seven generations downstream [S6]. Read comparatively, these unrelated traditions let
us *triangulate essential from cultural*: what recurs across genetically and geographically separate
oral dharmas — **relationality/kinship with the more-than-human world, totalizing reciprocity,
intergenerational responsibility, ethics fused to a cosmological story, and the moral order treated as
continuous with the natural order** — are candidate *essentials* (the same thin floor as
[[3.4-relativism-universalism]], the same universal function as [[1.2-universal-patterns]]); while the
specific cosmology, mnemonic technology (songline vs. wampum vs. chant), and totemic content are the
*cultural* surface [S1][S3]. Two deeper lessons: oral ≠ unfixed — Aboriginal stories have transmitted
real events with high fidelity for **7,000–13,000 years** [S5] — and indigenous dharmas are
characteristically *place-bound* ("religions of space," not portable time-lines) [S8]. The decisive
caveat is that reading "essentials" off indigenous peoples is exactly how the romantic **"Ecological
Indian"** myth was manufactured, so every candidate-essential is a hypothesis to test, not a proof
[S7].

## Findings

### These are dharmas without scripture, founder, or doctrine
The Aboriginal **Dreaming** is the paradigm. W.E.H. Stanner coined "everywhen" — "one cannot 'fix' the
Dreaming in time; it was, and is, everywhen" — to capture an order in which the ancestral past is also
the living present and the moral law [S4]. The Western Desert *Tjukurrpa* (glossed "Dreamtime") is, in
Sylvie Poirier's ethnography, a "multifarious concept that simultaneously refers to ancestral law or
moral code *and* supernatural reality that fuses the past, present, and future into an eternal
dimension," along whose "culturally significant routes on the sentient land" people navigate [S2].
This is dharma as *total order* — cosmology, ethics, geography, and identity in one — and it is carried
entirely without writing. The Haudenosaunee **Great Law of Peace** (Gayanashagowa / Kaianere'kó:wa),
traditionally dated between the 12th and 15th centuries CE, was an *oral constitution* uniting the Five
(later Six) Nations, "recorded" mnemonically on **wampum** belts; it is celebrated as one of the
world's oldest participatory democracies (its influence on the U.S. Constitution is debated) [S6].

### Transmission: story, song, ritual, place, eldership — and astonishing fidelity
Oral dharmas are transmitted multimodally: **songlines** "encapsulate vast amounts of geographical,
cultural, and spiritual knowledge" through "spoken word, dance, painting, and petroglyphs… combined
to form multi-modal systems," stewarded across generations by custodians and Elders [S5]. Crucially,
*oral does not mean fluid*: Nunn & Reid analysed Aboriginal stories from 21 points around the
Australian coast that describe the sea standing lower than today, and dated them to **7,250–13,070
calibrated years before present** — coastlines drowned at the end of the last glaciation [S5]. This
"extraordinary longevity of oral traditions" depended on specific cultural machinery for
transgenerational accuracy (cross-generational kin-based checking, multi-modal redundancy) [S5]. This
nuances the writing-brings-fixity thesis of [[2.3-pre-axial-ethics]] and parallels the verbatim Vedic
oral transmission of [[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]]: fidelity is an *institutional
achievement*, not a gift of literacy.

### The recurring core (candidate *essentials*)
Across non-interacting oral traditions the same structural features recur:
- **Kinship with the more-than-human ("kincentricity").** Humans and nature are one extended family:
  the Lakota Black Elk — "all of nature is in us, all of us is in nature"; Kimmerer (Potawatomi) —
  humans as "the younger brothers of Creation"; Māori *whenua* (land = placenta); Quechua *Pachamama*;
  Hawaiian *kuleana* binding the first human to care for his elder brother, the taro [S1]. The video
  survey adds responsibilities "to non-human entities" — Winnemem Wintu and salmon, Lakota and buffalo
  [S3].
- **Totalizing reciprocity.** The golden rule — present in "every human society" — is here *extended to
  nature*: "the concept of reciprocity is totalizing… not only among members of the community, but…
  with Nature" (Yunkaporta: "one law… reciprocity") [S1].
- **Intergenerational responsibility.** The Haudenosaunee **Seven Generations** principle obliges
  lawmakers "to consider the effects of their decisions seven generations downstream" [S6] — the
  temporal counterpart of kincentric ethics, seeding [[8.1-planetary-dharma]].
- **Ethics fused to cosmology via story.** Creation narratives "tell us who we are, how we got here,
  and… our relations to the natural world" [S1] — the dharma is *in* the myth, not in a separate code.
- **Moral order = cosmic order.** That law, reality, and morality coincide (Tjukurrpa) directly echoes
  the *ṛta / ma'at / Tao* family of [[1.4-cross-cultural-cognates]]: the same intuition that right
  conduct aligns with the order of things, reached independently [A].

### The variable surface (the *cultural*)
What differs is the *content and vehicle*: the specific cosmology (Dreaming vs. Great Spirit vs.
Pachamama vs. Wākea); the mnemonic technology (songline vs. wampum vs. the Hawaiian *Kumulipo* chant);
the kinship architecture and which non-human kin are central (salmon, buffalo, taro); and — a deeper
axis — **whether the dharma is place-bound or portable**. Vine Deloria Jr. draws the sharpest line:
Native religions are "religions of *space*" — "a particular relationship between a community and a
place," local and particular — whereas Christianity (and, one might add, the Axial and secular
dharmas) are "religions of *time*," universal historical arcs [S8]. Place-boundedness may itself be an
*essential* of the indigenous *type* even though it is not universal to *all* dharmas — a useful
reminder that "essential" is relative to the question being asked.

### So: what oral dharmas teach about essential vs. cultural
Three lessons, drawn by this project rather than by the sources cited above [A]. (1) **The minimal dharma is smaller than the Axial package**: strip away scripture,
creed, and founder and a complete ethical order remains — relational, reciprocal, narrative,
place-embedded — which means those Axial features (doctrine, text, universal claims) are *cultural
additions*, not the essence ([[2.3-pre-axial-ethics]]). (2) **Convergent features across unrelated
traditions are the best available evidence for universals** — the thin floor of
[[3.4-relativism-universalism]] given ethnographic flesh. (3) But (the sting) **the method is
treacherous**: because the "ecological/noble Indian" was built precisely by projecting a wished-for
universal onto native peoples, convergences must be cross-checked against independent evidence
([[1.2-universal-patterns]]), not taken as self-evident.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The romanticization trap — and the corpus walks straight into it.** The richest corpus source
  (Distin's kincentricity essay) asserts that "our Indigenous ancestors lived in relative harmony and
  balance with nature" and frames TEK as humanity's salvation [S1]. Shepard Krech's *The Ecological
  Indian* is the necessary corrective: the "ecological Indian" stereotype "is a foil for critiques of
  European or American society" that also became an internalised "self-image," and in fact Native
  Americans "like all peoples, altered their environments" (Pleistocene overkill, large-scale
  burning, local resource depletion) [S7]. The truth is that indigenous traditions *encode* powerful
  sustainability heuristics **without** their holders being ecological saints — a distinction the
  corpus blurs. [bias flag]
- **"Indigenous" is not one thing.** The corpus survey itself warns that "'Indigenous' encompasses
  thousands of distinct cultures, languages, and governments" — 370 million people, 570 U.S. nations,
  600+ in Canada, 500+ in Australia [S3]. Speaking of "the" indigenous oral dharma homogenises the
  very diversity that is the actual datum; the recurrences are real but so are deep differences.
- **The outsider-access problem (compounding [[4.1-samin-of-java]]).** Much knowledge is restricted or
  sacred; our "essentials" are assembled from what outsiders were shown. The corpus preserves the
  ethical caution from inside: "you should not go around in borrowed feathers" — learn *from* First
  Peoples without pretending to *become* them or to *possess* their knowledge [S2] (→
  [[7.2-appropriation]]).
- **Is "essential" discovered or projected?** Deciding what is essential by surveying traditions risks
  circularity (you find the universal you brought). This note treats convergences as *hypotheses* answerable
  to [[1.2-universal-patterns]], not as proof — and flags that the whole secular-dharma corpus has an
  interest in indigenous traditions *validating* its relational, ecological commitments.
- **Cited largely via secondary sources and one popular essay.** Stanner, Nunn & Reid, the Great Law,
  Krech, and Deloria are cited via journal abstracts, encyclopedic and review sources; the corpus
  material is a transcript, an ethnography-of-dreaming, and a blog essay — not first-hand fieldwork.
