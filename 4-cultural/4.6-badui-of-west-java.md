# 4.6 Badui of West Java

**Question:** Who are the Badui (Urang Kanekes) of West Java — their culture, beliefs (*Sunda Wiwitan*) and common practices (the *pikukuh* customary code)? What dharma analogues do they exhibit (non-alteration of the world, asceticism, forest guardianship), and how do these relate to the project's dharma concepts? How do they compare with the Samin (4.1) and indigenous oral dharmas (4.3)?

## Synthesis

The **Badui** (also Baduy; self-name **Urang Kanekes**, "people of Kanekes") are an indigenous Sundanese community of roughly 9,500–11,700 people inhabiting a state-demarcated ~5,100-hectare forest reserve in Kanekes village, Leuwidamar district, Lebak Regency, **Banten Province** — culturally Sundanese and historically West Javanese, but administratively in Banten, a province separated from West Java only in 2000 [S24][S30]. Their religion is **Sunda Wiwitan** ("primordial Sunda"), an animist–ancestral cosmology centred on the supreme power Sang Hyang Kersa and the divine ancestor Batara Cikal, and their entire existence is governed by **pikukuh** — an oral, immutable customary code whose master maxim, *"lojor heunteu beunang dipotong, pèndèk heunteu beunang disambung"* ("what is long must not be cut short; what is short must not be joined longer"), enjoins total non-alteration of the world as given [S25][S26][S23]. Pikukuh is a textbook **dharma analogue** by this project's working criteria ([[1.3-boundary-definition]]): named, identity-constituting, orally practice-transmitted, telos-oriented (the Badui hold a cosmic mandate to guard Sasaka Pusaka Buana, the "navel of the world"), and marked by reflective, oppositional self-awareness [S23][S27][S32]. But its grounding is religious and supernatural, not naturalistic — placing the Badui, like the Samin ([[4.1-samin-of-java]]) and Subud/Sumarah ([[4.5-subud-and-sumarah]]), firmly in the project's **non-secular boundary zone**, and distinguishing it from a secular ethic precisely in that the prohibitions carry cosmological, not merely social, sanction [S23][S25].

## Findings

### Who the Badui are

The Badui prefer **Urang Kanekes**; "Badui/Baduy" is an exonym of uncertain origin (variously traced to an endemic vine *baduyut*, the Cibaduy river, or Dutch colonial analogy with "Bedouin") [S1][S24][S28]. The community is structured in three concentric tiers of decreasing strictness: the **Inner Badui** (Badui Dalam / Tangtu), comprising the three sacred villages Cibeo, Cikertawana (Cikartawana), and Cikeusik within forbidden territory (*tanah larangan*) and numbering only about 400 people; the **Outer Badui** (Badui Luar / Panamping), the large majority living in roughly 63 surrounding villages (2023) who observe pikukuh less rigidly and may use vehicles and limited modern goods; and the small outermost **Dangka** of two buffer hamlets outside Kanekes proper [S24][S2]. Padjadjaran-University anthropologist **Judistira K. Garna** (10+ years of fieldwork) documented the tripartite structure and the banishment system whereby pikukuh violators are demoted inward-to-outward and subjected to a *nyapu* purification rite [S2].

**Population figures are genuinely contested** and must be cited with their year and provenance. Official Statistics Indonesia (BPS) figures give 11,699 in 2017 (3,413 families) declining to 9,558 in 2023; the Inner Badui hold steady at ~400; the 2026 Seba ceremony drew a subset of ~1,515–1,552 participants [S24][S26][S6][S7]. Older counts run lower (Garna's 1998 seminar gave 5,649; Cultural Survival cited ~5,000 as approximate, noting the actual number "is kept secret" as a matter of custom) [S2][S27]. The sharp 2017→2023 official decline is unexplained in available sources and may reflect out-migration, religious conversion (converts leave the adat register), or differing enumeration methods rather than real population loss [S24][S27].

**Origins are deeply disputed.** The most-cited "refugee" hypothesis — that the Badui descend from Sunda/Pajajaran aristocrats fleeing the Banten Sultanate's 1579 Islamic conquest — is **rejected by the Badui themselves**, for whom the name "Pajajaran" denotes malevolent spirits warded off with chants; they hold instead that they have lived in Kanekes as original guardians since the creation of humanity [S24]. Wessing and Barendregt (2005) reframe the Kanekes not as refugees but as hereditary ritual custodians (*hulun hyang*, "servants of spirits") of the pre-Islamic Sundanese shrine-of-state, the two theories being "not mutually exclusive" [S23]; Aditia Gunawan (2025), reading 14th–16th century Old Sundanese and Old Javanese manuscripts, finds Badui religious practice reflects patterns widespread in early Java and Bali rather than a uniquely Pajajaran inheritance [S29].

### Sunda Wiwitan belief

Sunda Wiwitan resists simple classification: its base is animism–dynamism (veneration of *karuhun* ancestral spirits and *hyang* nature spirits), yet it possesses a supreme power, **Sang Hyang Kersa** ("He Who Has the Will," also Batara Tunggal, "The One"), giving it henotheistic or proto-monotheistic traits — one Indonesian study notes the religion "cannot be separated from the concept of monotheism because there is a supreme power, namely Sang Hyang Kersa" [S25]. The cosmology is layered: a highest realm (Buana Nyungcung) where Sang Hyang Kersa dwells, the human middle world (Buana Panca Tengah), and a forbidden lower realm (Buana Larang), with Hindu deities subordinated beneath the supreme being and the rice goddess Nyi Pohaci Sanghyang Asri in the higher heavens [S25]. The Badui regard themselves as descendants of **Batara Cikal**, eldest of seven *bataras* created at Sasaka Pusaka Buana; in the jawawa news archive Sunda Wiwitan is described as "a blend of ancient Hinduism and shamanism," holding that the Sunda were the first people on earth and that living outside god's original intent invites disaster [S3][S25]. The supreme sacred site is **Arca Domas** (Sasaka Pusaka Buana), a terraced megalithic hill on the upper Ciujung visited annually in the Kalima month by the *pu'un* alone, where moss and rock-pool water serve as harvest divination; Wessing and Barendregt distinguish it from a second ancestor-shrine, Sasaka Domas, while noting the two "often merge" in practice [S23][S24].

### Pikukuh and daily practice

Pikukuh (from *kukuh*, "firmly holding one's stance") is the oral, non-codified body of customary law that orders every domain of life, transmitted by the *pu'un* through proverbial verse and ritual rather than text [S26][S23]. Its concrete prohibitions, consistently documented, forbid: motorised transport, electricity, electronics, soap and chemicals (especially near rivers), footwear, cutting hair, money/gold/silver, glass, and metal nails; in agriculture, ploughing, terracing, irrigation, fertiliser, cash crops, wet-rice (sawah) cultivation, and four-legged livestock such as goats and buffalo — planting is done only with a sharpened bamboo dibble (*tugal*) following the land's natural contour [S2][S3][S28]. The ecological maxim *"gunung ulah dilebur, lebak ulah dirusak"* ("mountains must not be destroyed, valleys must not be damaged") encodes a land-ethic of non-interference; agricultural plots lie fallow 4–7 years [S1][S2]. The ritual year culminates in three linked ceremonies: **Kawalu** (a three-month fasting cycle, ~January–April, during which the Inner villages close to all outsiders), **Ngalaksa** (a rice-noodle feast that doubles as the community's own population census, reported to ancestors), and **Seba** — an annual tribute pilgrimage in which Inner Badui walk barefoot ~60 km one way (~160 km round-trip) to deliver harvest produce and customary messages to the Lebak Regent and Banten Governor [S6][S7][S2]. At Seba 2026 the Badui delivered explicit anti-corruption and river-pollution messages, framing environmental conservation as their customary mandate [S8].

### Guardianship of the forest and modern pressures

Within their ~5,100-hectare reserve (Ichwandi and Shinohara's 2007 survey gives 5,101.85 ha: ~48.85% forest, 50.67% farmland, 0.48% settlement, all sitting within state forest, *kawasan hutan negara*, under customary use), the Badui zone land into sacred entrusted forest (*leuweung titipan*, strictly off-limits), restricted old forest, and working swidden [S30]. Their *huma* dry-rice system maintains an exceptional in-situ agro-biodiversity — Iskandar and Ellen (1999) documented **89 rice landraces**, a diversity greater than neighbouring wet-rice farmers, stored in raised *leuit* granaries [S31]. The conservation logic is theologically grounded: Kanekes is *taneuh titipan* (entrusted land) and *pancer bumi* (the centre of the earth), and damage to it is believed to portend cosmic catastrophe [S32][S27].

This custodianship has long been politically charged. In the early 1980s the Soeharto government's attempt to build schools, roads and electricity inside Kanekes was repelled when Badui elders walked to Jakarta and the president — by their account "deeply superstitious" — told them to plant boundary poles, comparing the Badui to "the roots of the tree" of Indonesia; the state has largely left them alone since [S3]. The Badui opted out of national elections from 1999 on ancestral grounds ("forbidden by our ancestors to be involved in politics, to lie or to take sides"), having previously voted 100% Golkar under the New Order [S9]. **Tourism** has brought friction since the late 1990s — plastic waste, unequal social dynamics, money-based norms — and in July 2020 customary leaders petitioned President Jokowi (by thumbprint) to remove Kanekes from Indonesia's tourism map and delete Badui imagery from Google; the state responded partially, rebranding visits *"saba budaya baduy"* (cultural visit) [S10][S33][S34]. By 2023 some ~9,000 cell-phone numbers circulated (concentrated in the Outer zone), prompting an Inner-Badui request for internet-blackout zones and a documented pattern of "negotiated compliance" with the digital world [S33]. Lebak Regency has formally recognised the Badui ulayat by regional regulation, and in 2023 Kanekes became the first *desa adat* (customary village) in Banten — though it is documented as the only village in Indonesia never to have received central village-development funds (Dana Desa) [S12][S37]. In 2026 the state pressed nutrition and family-planning programmes into Badui territory via a special "Sapa Budaya" cultural approach [S11].

### The dharma analogues

Against the project's working definition ([[1.3-boundary-definition]]), pikukuh registers on every count. It is **named** and internally differentiated (pikukuh tilu — spiritual, social, environmental — plus the ten social codes and the *rukun Baduy* ritual pillars) [S36][S26]. It is **identity-constituting**: adherence maps directly onto the Inner/Outer/Dangka gradient, conversion out of Sunda Wiwitan means exit from the community, and scholars describe Badui identity as an "ethno-nationalism" anchored in pikukuh and *buyut* (taboo) [S24][S26]. It is **practice-transmitted** orally through proverb and ritual cycle, not doctrine [S26]. It is **telos-oriented** toward cosmic guardianship: the Badui were "entrusted to maintain the navel of the universe" and treat the cosmos itself as a moral subject [S32]. And it is **reflectively/oppositionally self-aware** — they self-designate "Sunda Wiwitan" to mark themselves against Islamised neighbours, and assert their code consciously under external pressure [S26][S33].

The shared philosophy corpus offers secular glosses on *why* such a code works without capturing the Badui's own register. Prosocial sources frame taboos — food, dress, sacred objects — as in-group membership markers that enforce cooperation [S18]; Ostrom's design principles (clear boundaries, monitoring, graduated sanctions) describe pikukuh's enforcement mechanics (the *baresan* inspection teams, the *nyapu* and 40-day-labour penalties) [S19][S2]; the sacred-values literature (Atran; Sapolsky's *Behave*) explains the non-negotiable, "just do it" character of the prohibitions [S20]; the supernatural-punishment hypothesis explains how fear of cosmic disruption sustains cooperation [S22]; and indigenous kincentricity research frames the conservation outcome (80% of biodiversity protected on indigenous lands) [S21]. Graeber and Wengrow's comparative material on sacred custodial property — the Aranda *churinga* as legal title to clan land, "the true owners of land... gods or spirits; mortal humans... merely caretakers" — supplies an exact structural parallel to *taneuh titipan*, though the corpus never names the Badui [S16].

### Comparison (Samin and indigenous oral dharmas)

The Badui are structurally analogous to the **Samin** ([[4.1-samin-of-java]]): both are indigenous Javanese/Sundanese counter-societies defining themselves against a dominant Islamic/colonial/modern-state order, both foreground land-ethics and non-alteration, both transmit their code orally as community identity, and both practise non-violent withdrawal [S24][S2]. But they diverge sharply in architecture: Samin's *Agama Adam* is a this-worldly, ethics-first folk dharma without an elaborated cosmology, resisting through *engaged verbal refusal* while living dispersed among non-Samin Javanese; Badui pikukuh is embedded in a full supernatural cosmology (Sang Hyang Kersa, three realms, sacred ancestry, cosmic consequence) and resists through *territorial withdrawal* and spatial boundaries [S23][S25]. Against the **indigenous oral dharmas** category ([[4.3-indigenous-oral-dharmas]]), the Badui share oral, non-scriptural, community-constituting transmission, but their code is unusually named, systematised, and maxim-form — closer to an articulated path than a diffuse folkway. The corpus's nearest live analogue is the Mentawai *Arat Sabulungan* (Siberut), where no tree may be felled without the forest-lord Taikaleleu's permission — the same custodial logic, in a different archipelago tradition [S17].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

The shared **seculardharma / appliedanthropology** philosophy corpus is **thin on the Badui specifically**: it yields only a single direct RAG hit using the word "Baduy" (a Peluso footnote on Badui adat forest management) and a cognate reference to West Java's *Agama Djawa Sunda* in the Hefner/Bagir volume; Sunda Wiwitan, pikukuh, Kanekes, Pu'un and the Inner/Outer distinction return **zero** retrievable results [S14][S15]. (A rich synthetic document on the Badui exists in the underlying SQLite database but is absent from the FAISS index and so unreachable via the API — a technical gap that makes the corpus look thinner than it is.) The wayang.net and prosocial KBs likewise contain no direct Badui material; prosocial's Ostrom/sacred-values frameworks are drawn entirely from Middle Eastern, North American and internet contexts [S17][S18]. This reflects the corpus's known **secular / evolutionary / Western-academic and Javanese-resistance lean** (Boehm, Sapolsky, Graeber, Peluso, Geertz): it is well-tooled for the Samin but has no native purchase on a West Java Sundanese *religious* tradition. Nearly the entire ethnographic substance of this note therefore rests on the **jawawa Indonesian news archive** [S1]–[S13] and external web/scholarly sources [S23]–[S37].

Several cautions follow the exemplars. First, **the Badui are a non-secular indigenous boundary case** like the Samin: Sunda Wiwitan is genuinely religious and cosmological, and honouring the project's non-privileging stance means describing pikukuh in its own supernatural terms (divine mandate, cosmic consequence, *pu'un* genealogically descended from Batara Tunggal) rather than flattening it into covert secular ethics [S23][S27]. Second, **population and origin figures are contested**: the 2017→2023 BPS decline is unexplained, older counts vary widely, the Badui keep numbers secret by custom, and the 1579-refugee origin is a colonial-scholarly hypothesis the Badui reject [S24][S27][S29] — all are reported here with ranges and provenance, never as settled fact. Third, the **romanticisation / "noble savage" / tourism-appropriation risk is acute and structural**: outsider discourse fixes the Badui as a timeless "tribe that rejects technology," uses the exonym rather than *Urang Kanekes*, commodifies their image (influencers gifting smartphones "demi konten"), and deploys the same preservation logic that has historically served dispossession and forced conversion [S33][S34]. The "pure guardian" picture is complicated by documented Outer-Badui cash-crop (robusta coffee) adoption, phone use, and seasonal labour migration [S33]. Finally, the **insider-versus-outsider sourcing problem** is unresolved: there is no accessible first-person Badui theological account; pancer-bumi and cosmic-guardian framings reach us mediated through scholars, journalists and the state, and the most authoritative ethnographies (Garna, Wessing & Barendregt, Iskandar, van Zanten) are themselves outsider readings.
