# 4.1 Samin of Java

**Question:** Explain the origins, beliefs, and dharma of the Samin of Java. How does it
differ from traditional Javanese culture?

## Synthesis

The Samin movement (Saminism; today **Sedulur Sikep**), founded by **Samin Surosentiko**
(born Raden Kohar, 1859–1914) in the teak-forest villages of Blora around 1890, is a near-ideal
case of a **folk dharma**: a culturally-evolved, non-scriptural ethical path — its adherents call
it **Agama Adam**, "the religion of Adam" — that coordinated a peasant community's whole life
around honesty, simplicity, self-sufficiency, non-violence, and the conviction that *land, water
and forest are common property* [S1][S2][S8]. It crystallised as **non-violent resistance** to
Dutch colonial taxation and the enclosure of communal teak forests, but it was never merely a tax
revolt: it was a comprehensive ethical reorientation transmitted *orally* by an illiterate founder
and followers [S4]. It satisfies this project's working definition of a dharma
([[1.3-boundary-definition]]) — a named, identity-constituting, practice-transmitted account of
right living, raised by *reflective, oppositional self-awareness* above the level of inherited
custom. Crucially, it differs from "traditional Javanese culture" **not as an alien import but as a
radical reform from within the peasant (abangan) world**: against the hierarchy Geertz documented,
the Samin deliberately spoke blunt low Javanese (*ngoko*) to everyone — including officials —
refusing the *krama* deference and the *halus* refinement ideal; they rejected both orthodox Islam
and the *priyayi* court order in favour of a "pure Java"; and they replaced ceremonial elaboration
with austerity [S2][S3][S6]. The same dharma operates today in the Sedulur Sikep's environmental
resistance to limestone mining in the Kendeng mountains [S8].

## Findings

### Origins: a peasant movement against colonial enclosure
Samin Surosentiko (Raden Kohar) was a Blora farmer, son of **Raden Surowijoyo (Samin Sepuh)** — by
tradition a nobleman from Ponorogo who renounced aristocratic status to live among commoners in
opposition to Dutch rule [S1][S2]. Samin Surosentiko himself was born in 1859 [S1][S2]. From around 1890 Samin propagated his teachings in Klopoduwur and
neighbouring villages; the movement spread across the Blora–Rembang–Pati–Kudus–Grobogan–Bojonegoro
arc straddling Central and East Java, reaching perhaps 3,000–5,000 followers by 1907 [S1][S2]. Its
triggers were concrete and material: the Dutch forestry service (Boswezen) declared the Randublatung
teak forests restricted in 1897, cutting off customary access, and colonial taxes (land-rent,
poll-tax, produce-tax) pressed on subsistence farmers [S2]. Samin was arrested in 1907 and exiled to
Sawahlunto (West Sumatra), where he died in 1914 — though followers held that he had not died but
attained *moksha* and entered the spiritual realm [S2][S3]. This is the **moral economy of the
peasant** in James C. Scott's sense: a value-system "irrevocably linked to subsistence requirements,"
defending customary rights of access against market-colonial enclosure [S7].

### Beliefs: the Sikep ethical code and "Agama Adam"
Saminist ethics are a compact, this-worldly code rather than a theology. The recurring principles:
**honesty and plain-speaking** (*jujur*); **non-violence**; **self-sufficiency** through one's own
farm labour; **simplicity** and freedom from material excess; not disturbing or harming others;
avoiding envy, greed and quarrels; and the rejection of dishonest gain, especially in trade and
commerce [S1][S2]. Underpinning these is a cosmology in which **land, water and forest are common
property to be used for the common good** — the doctrinal root of both the colonial-era tax/forest
resistance and the present-day ecological struggle [S2][S8]. Their faith, *Agama Adam*, is treated
as humanity's "original religion," with simple prayer at dawn and dusk and no elaborate ritual
apparatus [S2]. Transmission is **oral**: Samin and his followers were illiterate, and although a few
texts (the *Serat Jamus Kalimosodo*) are attributed to the tradition, the ethic was carried by
recitation, example, and embodied practices such as *laku jejek* — walking on foot for days to
community gatherings as a discipline of continuing Samin's teaching [S3][S4]. Adherents now prefer
**Sedulur Sikep / Wong Sikep** ("honest, responsible kin") to "Samin," which had acquired
connotations of backwardness in mainstream society [S3].

### Why it counts as a *dharma* (not merely custom)
Against the criteria of [[1.3-boundary-definition]], Saminism is dharmic rather than mere folkway on
several counts: it offers a *comprehensive* account of how to live (work, speech, property, authority,
death), not a grab-bag of customs; it is *identity-constituting* ("we are the honest people"); it is
*telos-oriented* (an autonomous, truthful, harmonious life on the land); and — decisively — it is
**reflectively and oppositionally self-aware**. Ordinary Javanese village *adat* is inherited custom;
the Samin *self-consciously elevated* a chosen subset of values into a named, principled, defended
path *against* the dominant order. That deliberate elevation, transmitted as identity, is exactly
what converts folkways into a dharma. It also functions as **identity scaffolding and an interface
with power** in the sense developed in [[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]] and
[[7.3-dharma-and-power]] — a dharma defined largely *by* its refusal of the state.

### How it differs from traditional Javanese culture
The contrast is sharpest against the standard anthropological map of Javanese culture — Clifford
Geertz's trichotomy of **abangan** (syncretic peasant), **santri** (observant Muslim), and
**priyayi** (aristocratic-court) streams, and the hierarchical etiquette encoded in the three speech
levels *ngoko* / *madya* / *krama* [S6]. The Samin invert or refuse much of it:
- **Speech-level egalitarianism.** Where status is performed by addressing superiors in deferential
  *krama*, the Samin deliberately used blunt *ngoko* to everyone, officials included — a linguistic
  refusal of the entire hierarchy of deference [S2][S3][S6].
- **Against *halus*, for honesty.** Mainstream Javanese culture prizes the *halus* ideal — refined,
  indirect, emotionally controlled conduct — and ranks blunt directness (*kasar*) as low. The Samin
  made plain, literal honesty an explicit *virtue*, inverting the prestige order [S2][S6].
- **Refusing Islam and the syncretic mainstream.** The nominally Muslim abangan peasantry blends
  Islam with kejawèn mysticism; the Samin rejected orthodox/santri Islam *and* the formal
  religious-administrative order, identifying with a pre-/a-Islamic "pure Java" and leaving the
  religion field blank on their identity cards [S2][S3]. (The nuance: their mystical substrate is
  continuous with heterodox Javanese currents — teachings linked to Sheikh Siti Jenar — so the break
  is with *orthodoxy and institution*, not with all Javanese spirituality [S2].)
- **Simplicity vs. ceremony.** They minimised the elaborate *slametan*/ceremonial life typical of
  Javanese religion in favour of austerity and daily ethics [S2][S6].
- **Refusing the priyayi/state order.** Samin Sepuh's renunciation of nobility, and the movement's
  refusal of tax, corvée, marriage registration and census, reject the aristocratic-bureaucratic
  hierarchy at the heart of traditional Javanese statecraft [S2][S3].

Yet the difference is a **gradient, not a binary**: the Samin are agrarian, mystical, and rooted in
the same abangan peasant world they reform. Saminism is best read as an *internal heresy* of Javanese
culture — using its own materials against its hierarchy — rather than an outside system.

### The resistance as a dharma in action (the comparative lens)
The Samin's signature tactics — refusing taxes, declining to register marriages, and above all
answering officials with deadpan literalism ("Where are you from?" "From the forest." "Where are you
going?" "To the forest.") — are a paradigm of what Scott calls the **weapons of the weak**:
foot-dragging, feigned ignorance, false compliance, and dissimulation, the "infra-politics" of those
who cannot win open confrontation [S2][S7]. Read this way, the Samin dharma *is* a codified,
moralised, identity-bearing version of everyday peasant resistance — ethics and tactics fused.

### Contemporary register: the Kendeng ecological turn
The dharma persists. Since the 2000s the Sedulur Sikep of the **Kendeng** karst have led non-violent
opposition to limestone quarrying and a **PT Semen Indonesia** cement plant across Rembang, Pati and
Grobogan, on the doctrine that "land, water and forests are common property" and that farming and
protecting the water-bearing karst are sacred duty [S8]. The **"Kartini Kendeng"** — nine women
farmers led by figures such as Sukinah — travelled to Jakarta in **April 2016** and again in **March
2017** and sat outside the Presidential Palace with their **feet encased in cement**, a visceral
emblem of *wong cilik* ("little people") immobilised by the industry; one protester, **Patmi (48)**,
died soon after the 2017 action [S8]. Indonesia's Supreme Court ruled for the farmers in October 2016,
but the governor issued a replacement permit within days and the plant proceeded [S8]. The episode
shows the colonial-era dharma — common-property cosmology, non-violence, refusal of extractive
authority — recoded for an environmental age (→ [[8.1-planetary-dharma]]).

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **Doubly-mediated sources (the central caution).** Benda & Castles's foundational finding is that
  Samin and his followers were *illiterate*, so "historians and social scientists must work with
  accounts written about the movement by others" — colonial officials and later ethnographers [S4].
  The "doctrine" we summarise is therefore reconstructed from outsiders, often hostile ones. On top
  of that, the **corpus's own Samin documents (`The-Samin_sonnet.md`, `The-Samin_gpt45.md`) are
  AI-generated essays written inside *this very project's* dharma frame** (their frontmatter literally
  reads "…Dharma, Human Behavioural Evolution") — so they already assert that Samin "is a dharma."
  Citing them as independent confirmation of that thesis would be circular. They are used here only
  for ethnographic particulars, cross-checked against a genuine translated Indonesian source [S3] and
  external scholarship [S4][S6][S8]; the dharma-fit argument is made on this project's own criteria
  ([[1.3-boundary-definition]]), not borrowed from the corpus essays.
- **Contested classification.** Scholars do not agree what the Samin "were": Benda & Castles framed an
  ethical-religious passive-resistance movement [S4]; Korver read it through **millenarism** (salvation
  expectation, the moksha/return-of-Samin motif) [S5]; a Scott-style reading makes it rational
  subsistence-defending peasant resistance [S7]. These lenses pull the "dharma" toward religion,
  prophecy, or class struggle respectively. (Scott never studied the Samin; his frame is applied here
  as analysis, not as his claim.)
- **Romanticisation risk.** It is tempting to enlist the Samin retroactively as proto-environmentalist,
  proto-nationalist, or anarchist heroes. Mainstream Indonesian society long stigmatised "Samin" as
  foolish/backward — the very reason for the Sedulur Sikep renaming [S3] — and the movement's own aims
  were local and customary, not programmatic. Reading modern ecological or rights values *into* it
  should be done as interpretation, flagged as such.
- **"Secular?" — a deliberate boundary case.** Saminism is *not* secular: it has *Agama Adam*, an
  eternal soul, and *moksha* [S2][S3]. Its value to a secular-dharma project is precisely as a
  **boundary case** — a *non-scriptural, non-institutional, this-worldly, ethics-first folk dharma*
  showing that "dharma" need be neither a world religion nor a secular philosophy. This honesty
  matters for the project's non-privileging stance.
- **Geertz himself is contested.** The abangan/santri/priyayi trichotomy used as the "traditional
  Javanese culture" baseline has been widely criticised (priyayi is arguably a class not a religious
  type; most Javanese sit in a grey zone) [S9]. The contrast drawn above is therefore against an
  ideal-type, not a uniform reality.
