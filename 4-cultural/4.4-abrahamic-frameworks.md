# 4.4 Abrahamic Ethics as Dharmic Frameworks

**Question:** Analyze Abrahamic ethical systems — covenant, halakha, sharia, Christian caritas — as
dharmic frameworks. What does the non-privileging stance require us to see in them, and how do they
function like and unlike Indic / secular dharmas?

## Synthesis

On this project's own non-privileging stance ([[3.4-relativism-universalism]]), the Abrahamic
traditions are not "religion" set against dharma or secular ethics — they are *themselves* dharmas:
culturally-evolved, comprehensive paths of right living. The clinching evidence is in their own
vocabulary. **Halakha** is literally "the way to walk" (Hebrew root *halakh*, "to go"); **sharia**
literally "the path to water"; and the earliest Christians called themselves followers of **"the
Way"** [S2][S4][S8] — the same *way/path* metaphor that underlies *dharma* and *mārga*
([[1.1-etymology]], [[1.4-cross-cultural-cognates]]). Like Indic dharmas — and *unlike* the modern,
Protestant-inflected notion of religion-as-private-belief — Judaism and Islam are **orthopraxic**
total orders covering diet, law, ritual, family and daily conduct [S1][S2], and Islam even carries a
built-in teleological floor (the *maqasid al-shariah*: protection of religion, life, intellect,
lineage, property) strikingly like the capabilities-floor of [[3.4-relativism-universalism]] [S5].
What is genuinely *unlike*: their grounding in **covenant** — a historical, personal relationship
with a commanding God — rather than an impersonal cosmic order (*ṛta/Tao*) or a secular posit
[S8]; their linear, eschatological time (Deloria's arch-"religions of time," cf.
[[4.3-indigenous-oral-dharmas]]); and, within Christianity, a partial *inversion* of the path-law
model, since Pauline **caritas/agape** presents itself as love that "fulfils" and transcends codified
law [S7]. What the non-privileging stance *requires* is the reflexive discipline to analyse these
traditions with the same lens used for Buddhism or Ubuntu — neither privileging them as uniquely true
nor (the corpus's actual temptation) dismissing them as the ossified foil against which secular dharma
defines itself.

## Findings

### Do they meet the definition of a dharma? (the way/path test)
Against [[1.3-boundary-definition]], the Abrahamic systems qualify on every criterion — comprehensive
account of right living, identity-constituting, character-forming, transmitted across generations —
and, decisively, they self-describe in the *idiom of the path*. **Halakha**, from the root *halakh*
("to walk"), "translates as 'the way to walk', rather than 'law'… the way a Jew is directed to behave
in every aspect of life," turning even "the most trivial, mundane acts… into acts of religious
significance" [S4]. **Sharia** "literally means 'a path to water'" and "encompasses more than just
law; it involves ethics, morals, customs, and the ways in which one should live life" [S2]. Early
**Christians** were called the people of *the Way* (Acts 9:2; 24:14) before they were called
Christians [S8]. This shared metaphor is not decorative: it shows these traditions conceive ethics
exactly as dharma does — a path one *walks*, not merely a creed one *affirms*.

### Orthopraxy over belief — why Judaism and Islam are especially dharma-like
The single biggest obstacle to seeing this is the modern assumption that religion = belief. The
corpus's own religious-literacy material dismantles it: "many of our assumptions… are based on our
privileging of belief over practice," whereas in antiquity "religious identity was derived just as
much from religious practice as from belief" [S1]. Halakha and sharia are *orthopraxies* — systems of
right *practice* — covering law, diet, ritual, marriage, charity and daily conduct [S2][S4], exactly
the comprehensive practical scope of *varṇāśrama-dharma*. This makes them, structurally, *more*
dharma-like than the belief-centred paradigm assumes.

### Judaism — covenant + halakha
Jewish ethics is grounded in **covenant** (*brit*): a binding, historical relationship between God
and a people (the Sinai covenant; the conditional blessings and curses of Deuteronomy), into whose
"Abrahamic covenant promise" Paul later sought to invite gentiles [S1][S8]. Its lived form is
**halakha** — biblical *mitzvot* elaborated through Talmudic and rabbinic interpretation and codified
in works like the *Mishneh Torah* and *Shulchan Aruch* [S4]. Note two dharma-resonances. First, the
*Mussar* tradition of ethical self-cultivation parallels contemplative character-work: founded by
Rabbi Israel Salanter (1810–1883) among Lithuanian Jewry on the principle that theoretical ethical
knowledge does not suffice and "special actions" are needed to master the passions, it built a
practice repertoire of regular self-analysis, sombre repetition of ethical texts, meditation, and the
*musar-shtibl* or house of moral instruction [S9] — its Kelm school characterised as "patient, pious
self-cultivation" [S10]. Second, Judaism is characteristically **particularist**: election
constitutes Israel as a people in covenant [S11], and rabbinic and later Judaism is non-missionary,
which places it closer to an ethnic/folk dharma than to a proselytising one. ▲ That second claim
holds for rabbinic and post-rabbinic Judaism but is **actively disputed for the Second Temple and
early Roman period**, where Goodman argues active proselytism was absent before the 2nd–3rd centuries
CE [S12] and Feldman reads the same evidence as substantial missionary outreach winning proselytes
and sympathisers [S13]; biblical particularism is also read as carrying a universal horizon rather
than excluding one [S14]. The dharma-comparison is drawn on the rabbinic pattern, not the contested
ancient one [A].

### Islam — sharia, fiqh + the maqasid
**Sharia** is the path; **fiqh** (jurisprudence) is the fallible human work of interpreting it from
Qur'an and Hadith — "interpretation requires interpreters, who are fallible and may disagree" [S2] —
the same living-interpretation-vs-ossification dynamic as [[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]].
Crucially, classical jurisprudence articulates the **maqasid al-shariah**, the higher *objectives* of
the law: al-Ghazali's five — preservation of **religion (dīn), life (nafs), intellect (ʿaql), lineage
(nasl), and property (māl)** — ranked on a scale of necessity (*ḍarūriyyāt / ḥājiyyāt / taḥsīniyyāt*)
[S5]. This is a *teleological floor inside the tradition* — a list of protected human goods
remarkably parallel to Nussbaum's central capabilities ([[3.4-relativism-universalism]]) — and the
basis on which reform movements (e.g. Sisters in Islam) contest patriarchal or extremist readings
[S2].

### Christianity — caritas/agape *and* natural law (two strands)
Christianity is the most *un*-dharma-like of the three at the level of structure, because it contains
a strand that defines itself *against* codified path-law. Pauline ethics makes **agape** (self-giving
love) the whole of ethics: "love is the fulfilling of the law" (Rom 13:10), and Jesus' twofold
command to love God and neighbour grounds the life of faith — so that "in autonomous Christian ethics,
the only real command is to love," contrasting with the detailed commands of the Torah [S7]. *Caritas*
is thus closer to an inner *disposition* (and to *bhakti*-style devotion) than to a halakhic practical
code. **But** a second, strongly dharma-compatible strand runs alongside it: Aquinas's **natural
law**, which synthesises Aristotle with Christianity — *synderesis*, "the habit containing the
precepts of the natural law," grasps the first principle that "good is to be done and pursued, and
evil avoided" [S6]. This is a *rational, naturalistic* ethics grounded in human nature, the direct
ancestor of Foot's neo-Aristotelian naturalism in [[3.3-grounding-problem]] — and it shows
Christianity is not reducible to divine command.

### Like Indic / secular dharmas
- **Comprehensive paths of practice**, identity-bearing and character-forming (esp. halakha, sharia).
- **Cooperation-coordinating**: from the naturalist side, the Ten Commandments and the golden rule are
  "imperfect summaries" of evolved cooperation (de Waal) [S3] — the same function as any dharma
  ([[1.2-universal-patterns]]).
- **A teleological floor**: the *maqasid* protect a list of human goods much as the capabilities
  approach does [S5].
- **A natural-law option**: Aquinas grounds ethics in human flourishing without sole reliance on
  revelation [S6].

### Unlike Indic / secular dharmas
- **Covenantal/divine-command grounding**: a *personal, commanding God* and revealed law, vs the
  impersonal order (*ṛta/Tao*) or the secular posit — the personal/impersonal axis of
  [[1.4-cross-cultural-cognates]] and the Euthyphro problem of [[3.3-grounding-problem]].
- **Linear, eschatological time**: creation→fall→redemption→end, vs cyclical time or the Aboriginal
  "everywhen" ([[4.3-indigenous-oral-dharmas]]).
- **Exclusivist universalism** (in Christianity and Islam): one God, one revealed way for all — in
  tension with dharmic pluralism — though **Judaism is particularist**, so "Abrahamic" is itself
  internally plural.
- **The love-over-law inversion** (Christianity): agape as the supersession of codified path-law [S7].

### What the non-privileging stance requires us to see
Three demands, stated as this project's methodological commitments rather than as findings from the
sources above [A]. (1) **Symmetry of analysis** — apply the same toolkit (cooperation, identity
scaffolding, transmission, failure modes) used on Buddhism or Ubuntu, without a thumb on the scale.
(2) **Catch the foil-reflex** — secular dharma partly *defines itself against* Abrahamic authority
(Batchelor's wariness of "charismatic priests and inflexible dogmas," [[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]]);
the stance requires not mistaking that polemical self-definition for description, and seeing instead
the internal diversity, reform capacity, mystical interiority (Sufism, Kabbalah), and natural-law
rationality of these traditions. (3) **Accept the reflexive bite** — if the Abrahamic paths are
dharmas, then "secular dharma" is *one more path among them*, not a neutral meta-vantage that ranks
the rest (the reflexive consequence of [[3.4-relativism-universalism]]).

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The corpus's secular/post-religious lean is the live bias here.** Curated around Boehm, Sapolsky,
  Graeber and Batchelor, the corpus tends to (a) read Abrahamic ethics *reductively* as evolved
  cooperation-summaries — de Waal's "imperfect summaries" [S3], and a game-theoretic ranking that
  literally scores "Jewish tenets 'beat' Christian ethics" and calls "turning-the-other-cheek…
  exploitable" — and (b) cast institutional religion as the **ossified foil** for secular dharma
  ([[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]]). Both are *privileging-by-dismissal*; this note imports
  serious theology (covenant, *uṣūl al-fiqh*, Thomistic ethics) to counter them. [bias flag]
- **Source-quality hazard in the corpus.** Alongside genuine religious-literacy material, the corpus
  surfaces a *channeled new-religious text* (the *Urantia Book*) on the history of monotheism; it is
  excluded here as non-scholarly — a reminder that the corpus does not weight its sources.
- **"Reclassifying as dharma" can itself be intellectual imperialism.** Filing Judaism, Christianity
  and Islam under an originally Indic category risks the appropriative move of [[7.2-appropriation]] —
  these traditions did not choose the label and might reject it. The claim made here is *analytic*
  (family resemblance per [[1.3-boundary-definition]]), not that they *are* Indian or that "dharma" is
  their truest self-description.
- **Calling them dharmas does not dissolve the metaethical disagreement.** Grounding ethics in a
  commanding God is precisely the grounding the secular project rejects (Euthyphro,
  [[3.3-grounding-problem]]); the reclassification *relocates* that disagreement rather than settling
  it.
- **Internal diversity under-served.** "Abrahamic" spans Orthodox/Reform Judaism, Sunni/Shia/Sufi
  Islam, and Catholic/Protestant/Orthodox Christianity, each with distinct ethics; this note works at
  the ideal-type level and uses the better corpus material (religious-literacy transcripts) that are
  secondary/popular rather than primary scholarship.
