# 4.5 Subud and Sumarah

**Question:** What is the Javanese Subud movement — its origins, beliefs and practice (the *latihan
kejiwaan*)? Who practises it and how widely? How does it relate to the project's dharma concepts (its
very name embeds *dharma* — **Su**sila **Bu**dhi **D**harma)? And how does it compare with the
smaller, quietist Sumarah movement it resembles?

## Synthesis

**Subud** is an international spiritual association founded by the Javanese Muslim **Muhammad Subuh
Sumohadiwidjojo** (1901–1987, known to members as "Bapak"), who traced it to a spontaneous 1925
mystical experience and gave the movement its name — **Su**sila **Bu**dhi **D**harma — from three
Sanskrit-derived Javanese words [S7][S14][S15]. Its sole practice is the **latihan kejiwaan**
("spiritual exercise"), a wordless, spontaneous surrender to "the Power of God" received through an
"opening" and transmitted person-to-person rather than taught [S14][S18]. Subud disavows being either
a religion or a teaching, yet its whole practice turns on submission to a divine power — a definitional
tension that places it, like the Samin ([[4.1-samin-of-java]]), in the **non-secular boundary zone**
this project tracks at [[1.3-boundary-definition]] [S14]. It spread worldwide from 1957 and today has
roughly **10,000 members in about 83 countries** [S14], a small footprint relative to its reach.
**Sumarah** (Paguyuban Sumarah) is its quietist near-relative: a Javanese *kebatinan* movement founded
in 1935 whose name *is* the Javanese word for total surrender, practising silent guided meditation
(*sujud*) rather than dynamic latihan, and which has stayed small (~6,000–10,000) and largely Javanese
[S1][S2][S21][S23]. Both grew from the same early-1930s Yogyakarta mystical circle, both reframe
surrender as the ethical core of a named way of life, and both are regulated by the Indonesian state as
*aliran kepercayaan* — streams of belief outside the official religions [S10][S11][S22].

## Findings

### What Subud is

Muhammad Subuh Sumohadiwidjojo was born **22 June 1901** near Semarang, Central Java, and died **23
June 1987** [S15][S17]. In 1925, on a late-night walk in Semarang, he experienced what Subud accounts
describe as a sphere of light descending into his body; he surrendered, believing he was dying, and was
then moved involuntarily through movements resembling Muslim prayer — experiences that recurred and
became the latihan [S15]. Others near him began to receive the same state spontaneously from around the
early 1930s [S14]. The movement remained an informal Javanese association until a congress in
**Yogyakarta in 1947**, where the name **Subud** — a contraction of **Susila Budhi Dharma** — was
adopted and the body was legally registered [S14]. Indonesian press coverage of the period records the
same essentials: founder a Javanese noble (Raden Mas), the name glossed from Susila, Budi and Dharma,
the practice held about twice a week for around half an hour, headquartered on Jl. Fatmawati in South
Jakarta, and by 2002 present in more than 78 countries [S7].

Bapak insisted that "Subud is neither a new teaching nor a religion" [S14]; members may belong to any
faith or none, and the latihan is said to incline practitioners more deeply toward their own religion
[S7][S14]. Scholarship reads this differently: Antoon Geels's standard monograph argues that, despite
the leadership's denials, Subud's conceptual apparatus is "firmly rooted in the cultural history of
Java," and a Brill handbook frames it as "an Indonesian interpretation of Sufism" [S19].

### The latihan

The **latihan kejiwaan** is Subud's only practice and its defining claim. Sessions are normally held
two or three times a week, last about thirty minutes, and are conducted with men and women in separate
rooms [S18]. Practitioners stand after a period of quiet; a helper announces the start; thereafter each
follows whatever spontaneously arises — involuntary movement, sound, walking, dancing, laughing,
weeping, or stillness — while remaining fully conscious and free to stop [S18]. The latihan is
explicitly **received, not taught**: it "cannot be either studied or taught… but is simply received,"
with surrender ("Thy will, Lord, not mine") as its only posture and no technique, mantra, scripture, or
doctrine [S14][S18]. Entry is through a one-time **opening**, in which a newcomer stands while
experienced "helpers" do their own latihan nearby; the contact is said to pass without instruction,
likened to one candle lighting another with no loss of flame [S14][S18]. **Helpers** coordinate
sessions and witness openings but are explicitly not clergy and not held to be spiritually superior;
selection is a service role [S14][S18]. There are no membership fees, only voluntary contributions
[S18]. Subud also runs affiliated "wings" — humanitarian (Susila Dharma International, with UN
consultative status), cultural, enterprise and youth bodies — reflecting Bapak's teaching that work
guided by inner awareness is itself a form of worship [S18].

### Who practises it and global spread

Subud stayed confined to Indonesia until the English linguist **Husein Rofé**, its first non-Indonesian
member, carried it abroad in the mid-1950s (Japan 1954, then Hong Kong and Cyprus) [S14]. The decisive
Western breakthrough came in **1957**, when Bapak visited Britain at the prompting of Rofé and stayed at
**John G. Bennett's** Coombe Springs centre near London, where Bennett's circle of several hundred
Gurdjieff/Ouspensky students were offered the opening [S14][S17][S27]. Publicity around the actress
**Eva Bartok** — whose difficult 1957 pregnancy and the birth of her daughter were attributed to the
latihan — drew a wave of new members; the often-repeated claim of cover stories in *Paris Match* and
*Time* is not independently confirmed and is best treated as one major publicity catalyst among
several, alongside Bennett's promotion to the worldwide Gurdjieff network [S28]. The first **World
Subud Congress** was held at Coombe Springs in 1959; the legally incorporated **World Subud Association**
(now a US non-profit registered in Washington, D.C.) dates from around 1989–1991, not from the 1959
congress [S24]. Bennett himself left Subud by around 1960–1962, concluding the latihan alone was
insufficient and returning to Gurdjieff's methods [S27]. Subud's reach touched establishment figures:
the Jakarta Post records that **Harvey Barnett**, a former Director-General of Australia's ASIO, was
"particularly committed to the Subud movement," described there as blending Islam, Christianity and
Javanese mysticism [S8] — though Barnett became ASIO Director-General in 1981 (not 1976, his year of
joining the service), retiring in 1985 and dying in 1995 [S26]. As of 2023 Subud reports roughly
**10,000 members across about 83 countries** [S14] — a membership that has been broadly flat or ageing
since the late 1980s, which the movement's own quietism (no proselytising, no advertising, a
non-reproducible experience) helps explain [S14].

### The Sumarah movement

**Sumarah** (Paguyuban Sumarah, "the fellowship of those who surrender") was founded in **1935** in
Yogyakarta through the revelatory experience (*wahyu sumarah*) of **R. Ng. Sukinohartono** ("Pak Kino,"
c. 1900–1970), a minor court attendant and bank clerk [S1][S21][S23]. Indonesian sources date the
*wahyu* to 8 September 1935 in the Wirobrajan district [S23]. The movement grew through the
revolutionary period and was formally organised as the Paguyuban Sumarah around **1950**, led by Dr.
Surono in Yogyakarta (1950–1966) and then by Arymurthy in Jakarta (1966–1997) [S1][S21]. Its membership
has remained small — estimates run from about **6,000 to 10,000**, with Stange giving ~6,000 in one
account and ~10,000 in another, and the Indonesian Wikipedia ~7,200 [S1][S2][S21][S23]. Its heartland
is Central and East Java; although the organizational seat (DPP) is in Jakarta, membership there is
small, and despite a few hundred international practitioners since the early 1970s there is no formal
international organisation [S1][S21].

The core practice, **sujud sumarah**, is a non-ritual meditation of total surrender to God's will,
conducted individually and in weekly group sessions [S3]. It uses no fixed technique, no sacred text,
and no guru hierarchy: the **pamong** (guide) is explicitly not a guru, deriving authority only from the
moment of attuned guidance through *rasa* (intuitive felt-knowing) [S2][S3]. Rather than forcing
stillness, the practitioner relaxes body, feeling and mind so the mind "settles like a tired horse,"
shifting attention from thought to *rasa* [S3]. Sources describe a progression through *tekad*
(intention), *iman* (faith) and *sumarah* (total surrender), and the expression *tapa rame* — practice
in the midst of ordinary working and family life — alongside *narimo* (acceptance) [S4][S5]. Most
members are Muslim, but the movement insists it is not a religion and includes Buddhist and Christian
practitioners; Sufi resonances (surrender, the "greater jihad" of inner purification) are acknowledged
but framed as incidental to an autonomous revelatory origin rather than a derivation from Sufism
[S1][S3]. The authoritative study is Paul Stange's 1980 University of Wisconsin–Madison dissertation,
*The Sumarah Movement in Javanese Mysticism*, later revised in Indonesian as *Kejawen Modern* (2009)
[S20].

### Kebatinan context, and Subud vs. Sumarah

Both movements sit within **kebatinan / kejawen** — the syncretic Javanese mysticism of the inner self
(*batin*) that blends animist, Hindu-Buddhist and Sufi-Islamic layers, mapped by Clifford Geertz onto
the *abangan / santri / priyayi* trichotomy of Javanese religious life [S6]. Geertz's *The Religion of
Java* (in the corpus in its 1976 University of Chicago edition) is the only source that surveys the full
sect landscape, listing five Modjokuto *kebatinan* sects — Budi Setia, Kawruh Bedja, Sumarah, Ilmu
Sedjati and Kawruh Kasunjatan — and characterising Sumarah as the anti-intellectual, emotionally
focused collective-meditation sect, numbering about forty clerks and teachers meeting weekly, with the
sects collectively called *kedjawen* or *ilmu djawi* [S6]. Stange treats the wider field as hundreds of
*kepercayaan/kebatinan* movements, of which Sumarah is among the dozen most prominent nationally [S2].

Subud and Sumarah are, in Stange's phrase, "close cousins" or branches of the same spiritual tree: they
emerged from the same early-1930s Yogyakarta circle, and Sukinohartono himself received an early
"opening" within Subud's milieu (sources name the helper variously as Wignosupartono or Pravirodisastra,
c. 1934) before his own independent revelation [S22]. Bapak confirmed in a 1953 letter that "the
practice of Sumarah (surrender) is much the same as that of Subud" [S22]. Their **divergences** are
nonetheless sharp: Subud's latihan is dynamic, audible and individually received in ~30-minute sessions
with no verbal guidance, and is performed identically worldwide; Sumarah's *sujud* is quiet and still,
runs to roughly three hours, is group-based and verbally guided by a pamong, and depends on local
Javanese-language transmission — a structural reason Subud globalised across ~83 countries while Sumarah
stayed largely Javanese [S22]. (A note of caution: a 1998 Jakarta Post review of Stange's work garbled
"Sumarah" as a *person* who introduced rasa-centred mysticism, rather than the organised movement —
illustrating how thinly even the regional press carried these distinctions [S9].)

The Indonesian state has regulated both as **aliran kepercayaan**. Press coverage documents the
contested status of such movements: a 1997 parliamentary battle over whether they should rank with the
official religions, their supervision by the culture ministry rather than religious affairs, and the
civil disabilities (unrecognised marriages, identity-card exclusion) borne by the ~400,000+ adherents
counted in the mid-2000s [S10][S11]. The landmark **2017 Constitutional Court ruling** (Decision No.
97/PUU-XIV/2016) finally allowed *penghayat kepercayaan* to record their belief on the national identity
card, ending decades of forced affiliation with one of the recognised religions [S25].

### The dharma connection

Subud is unusual among this project's cases in that *dharma* is built into its name. Bapak read the
three Sanskrit-derived words theistically: **Susila** as good human character in accordance with God's
will; **Budhi** as the inner life-force placed in every creature; and **Dharma** as "surrender, trust
and sincerity towards Almighty God" [S16]. This is a deliberate narrowing of the classical Sanskrit
*dharma* (the law that upholds cosmic order) into an Islamic-inflected idiom of submission, applied to a
Hindu-Buddhist vocabulary inherited through Javanese civilisation [S16]. Against the project's working
criteria ([[1.3-boundary-definition]]), both movements register as **named, identity-constituting,
practice-transmitted, telos-oriented** ways of life that are **reflectively self-aware** as distinct from
inherited custom: each took a specific name, codified and legally registered itself, and articulated a
defined soteriology of surrender. Subud's break from custom is the sharper — it presents itself as
universal and trans-cultural — while Sumarah remains more continuous with the kejawen inheritance, a
difference of degree rather than a disqualification. Like the Samin ([[4.1-samin-of-java]]), both are
**non-secular boundary cases**: this-worldly and ethics-first, yet grounded in explicit divine
submission (*Tuhan Yang Maha Esa*) rather than the naturalistic ethics the corpus usually foregrounds.
The prosocial literature offers a partial secular gloss on what surrender *does* — framing it as
"ego-extension" of the self into a wider group-self, and as the vertical-and-horizontal submission
encoded in the Arabic *aslama* — but explicitly does not treat ego-dissolution as a theistic practice,
so it illuminates the mechanism without capturing these movements' own self-understanding [S13].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

The shared **seculardharma / appliedanthropology** corpus is genuinely strong on **Sumarah** (eight-plus
dedicated documents: Stange's ethnography, a Melton-Baumann encyclopedia entry, Geertz, and
Indonesian-language transcripts) but **entirely silent on Subud** — four targeted queries returned zero
hits for Subud, Pak Subuh, or latihan [S12]. The Subud account in this note therefore rests almost
wholly on the *jawawa* news archive [S7][S8] and external web/scholarly sources [S14]–[S19], not on the
philosophy corpus. This is the predictable shape of the corpus's **secular / evolutionary /
Western-academic lean** (curated around Boehm, Sapolsky, Graeber, Batchelor, Bregman): it captures
Sumarah as an object of *anthropological* study but has no native interest in a living theistic
new-religious movement. The prosocial material on surrender [S13] is illustrative of this slant — it can
only render surrender in proximate/ultimate evolutionary or sociological terms, never in the movements'
own devotional register. **Honouring the non-privileging stance, this note treats Subud and Sumarah as
they describe themselves — theistic, mystical, oriented to "the Power of God" — not as covert secular
ethics.**

Several **numbers and facts are contested** and have been hedged accordingly. Subud's membership (~5,000
to ~13,000 across sources) is reported here as ~10,000 in ~83 countries on 2023 figures [S14]; Sumarah's
as ~6,000–10,000 [S1][S21]. The **World Subud Association** was *not* founded in 1959 (that was the first
World Congress); its legal incorporation came ~1989–1991 [S24]. The Western-expansion year is **1957**,
not 1956 [S14][S17]. The Harvey Barnett claim is **reportorially accurate** as a quotation from Rosihan
Anwar but not independently corroborated as biography, and the date of his ASIO leadership is corrected
to 1981 [S8][S26]. The Eva Bartok publicity is real but the specific *Paris Match*/*Time* attributions
and the "~500 openings in a month" figure are **unverified** [S28].

**Insider-versus-scholarly framing** is a live tension. Much accessible Subud material is **insider**
(official national-association sites, SubudVision); the movement's self-description as "not a religion"
is contradicted by scholars (Geels; the Brill Sufism reading) who classify it as religious under
standard frameworks [S19]. The identity of who "opened" Sukinohartono, and whether the two founders were
*equally* disciples of the Naqshbandi teacher Seh Abdurachman (Stange calls Sukino's link only
"indirect"), remain genuinely unsettled in the sources [S22]. There is also a **romanticisation /
appropriation** risk to flag: Western Subud was, by internal account, drawn heavily from
educated, "white, middle-class, liberal" Gurdjieff circles, and the movement's costly enterprise
ventures (the S. Widjojo building, Anugraha) collapsed with significant member losses — a corrective to
any purely edifying telling. Finally, the project brief's "Suwondo" could not be matched to the founding
trio (Kino, Hardo/Suhardo, Sutadi); it may be a variant of "Suhardo" or refer to a separate regional
figure, and Stange's full dissertation would be needed to resolve it.
