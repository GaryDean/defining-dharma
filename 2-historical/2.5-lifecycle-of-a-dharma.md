# 2.5 The Life-Cycle of a Dharma

**Question:** How does a dharma form, change, and end over time — is it carved from an
existing culture, does it carry that culture's "grain", and does it then die out, season
into a movement, or harden into a religion that becomes fresh material for the next dharma?

## Synthesis

A dharma, in the broad sense this project uses, has a life-cycle whose stages recur across
otherwise unrelated traditions. It begins not from nothing but as *bricolage*, carved from the
cultural materials already to hand [S8][A]. Whatever is cut carries the **grain** of its parent
stock: the new path inherits concepts it did not invent and cannot easily discard, as Buddhism
inherited and stayed locked into the Indian soteriological framework of karma and rebirth [S2].
Once named and cut loose, a dharma **drifts**, by selective transmission and by chance, the more
so once identity hardens around the name [S4][S6][S15]. What I have long called *the accretions
of ages* then works on it: a spare original gathers beliefs, rites, and commentary until little
of the founding simplicity shows, and "nothing is ever lost" along the way [A][S13]. Three fates
follow. Some dharmas **die out** — Manichaeism, and the near-vanished Shakers — when persecution,
competition, or mere drift removes them [S21][S22][S18]. Some **season** into movements and
subcultures, sliding along the sect-to-church continuum [S5][S10]. A few **harden** into whole
religions through what Weber called the routinisation of charisma and Whitehouse models as the
doctrinal mode: living insight congeals into orthodoxy, canon, and clergy, "dense, intertwined,
a bugger to split" [S9][S11][S3]. And a hardened dharma is fresh stock for the next carver — the
Reformation cut from Catholicism, Chan/Sŏn from formalist Buddhism, secular dharma from Buddhism
again [S20][S1][S10]. Throughout, the carver is not free: the cognitive and material grain of the
stock dictates where the cut will take, favouring transmissible, minimally counterintuitive,
credibility-backed forms [S12][S7]. The whole model is an [A] synthesis — the project's
"wood-carver" parable — that the evidence below supports at most points and complicates at four.

## Findings

### Carved from culture — nothing *ex nihilo*
No dharma is whittled from empty air; the carver only ever works pre-existing cultural material.
Claude Lévi-Strauss named the mode: the *bricoleur*, unlike the engineer, builds from whatever
lies to hand rather than from purpose-made parts [S8]. This is the anthropological default for how
religions and worldviews assemble — by selective reuse, not invention [A]. The one wrinkle is that
the "wood" is not always a *neighbouring* tradition: closely similar dharmas can arise in parallel
from similar material conditions (the Axial-Age case), so the substrate includes the economic and
ecological, not only the doctrinal [S13][A].

### Carries the grain — inheritance and path-dependence
What is carved shows the grain of its block: a new dharma inherits the assumptions of its parent
culture whether or not the carver intends it. The paradigm is Buddhism, cut from the Vedic-Brahmanic
milieu yet carrying karma, rebirth, and the goal of liberation with it — a framework "so embedded"
that later Buddhists could scarcely conceive of questioning it [S2]. This is what cultural-evolution
theory calls descent with modification under path-dependence: early commitments set a trajectory
later carvers cannot cheaply reverse [S6].

### Drift once named
Naming a dharma — cutting it loose into "something discrete and namable" — is itself a trigger for
change. James C. Scott's account of oral tradition is the sharpest evidence: absent a fixed text
"the concept of 'the original' simply does not make any sense", performances "permit a certain
'drift'", and once an identity is institutionalised it "acquires its own history", reproducing the
very difference the name was coined to mark [S4]. Some drift is adaptive tuning; some is the cultural
analogue of genetic drift, neutral variants rising and falling by chance [S6]. Dan Sperber's
epidemiology of representations explains the direction of travel: transmission is *reconstruction*,
not copying, so content slides toward forms the mind finds easy to hold [S15].

### The accretions of ages
Between the drift of a living tradition and the hardening of an old one sits the additive process I
described, decades ago, as *the accretions of ages*: a very basic philosophy gathers, generation on
generation, new beliefs, rites, and commentaries until the simple thing at its heart is all but buried,
and a few of these thickened structures swell into mega-religions [A]. This is drift with a direction.
It is why the grain grows dense — not one clean line but ring upon ring of later growth — and why in
cultural evolution "nothing is ever lost", each layer preserved beneath the next [S13][A]. The claim is
visible in the Buddhist case, where accreted doctrine became "so embedded" as to be inextricable from
the founding teaching [S2].

### Three fates: die, season, or harden
- **Die out.** Whole dharmas have gone extinct. Manichaeism, once spread from Rome to China, was
  persecuted into oblivion [S21]; the Shakers, at a peak of roughly 6,000 members in the 1840s, fell
  to about 1,000 by 1905 and survive today as a single village of a few members, undone largely by
  their own rule of celibacy [S22]. Selection removes maladapted variants, but so do drift and sheer
  bad luck; extinction is not always a verdict that a dharma "outlived its usefulness" [S18].
- **Season into movements/subcultures.** Between the living tree and the fossil lies the long middle
  life of a sect. Sociology since Troeltsch has mapped the slide of a high-tension cult or sect toward
  a lower-tension denomination and established church [S10]. The corpus catches traditions mid-season:
  Clifford Geertz watched Javanese mystical groups move from "loose, largely charismatic" bands toward
  a "more specific, almost denominational form", unsure whether they would crystallise [S5].
- **Harden into religion.** A few dharmas set into dense institutions. Max Weber called the mechanism
  the *routinisation of charisma*: a movement built on one founder's authority must, to outlive the
  founder, convert that charisma into doctrine, office, and ritual [S9]. Harvey Whitehouse models the
  hardened end-state as the *doctrinal mode* — frequent, routinised, orthodoxy-policing religion, the
  cross-linked structure that resists cleavage [S11]. Batchelor states the law in the parable's own
  terms: "no matter how radical the reform of a religious tradition, over time the new and vibrant
  school tends to coalesce into yet another orthodoxy and hierarchic institution", after which it "exercises
  its power most keenly by controlling the interpretation of its canonical texts" [S3]. That is Rupert's
  hardwood: dense, intertwined, a bugger to split.

### Fresh stock — the recursion
Hardening is not the end of the line: a dense, canonical religion becomes a fresh block for the next
carver. This is the standard shape of religious history. The Protestant Reformation was cut from
Catholicism in 1517, keeping the whole canon while splitting on authority [S20]. Within Buddhism the
Chan/Sŏn tradition arose as "a revolt against the institutional and doctrinal formalism" of the older
schools [S1]. The sect-formation literature treats exactly this recursion as its subject matter [S10],
and secular dharma is a live instance — a reform carved, once again, from Buddhism [S3][A].

### The material dictates the cut
The carver is not free; the grain has the last word. Cognitive science of religion is the study of that
constraint: Pascal Boyer argues that the concepts surviving transmission are *minimally counterintuitive*,
memorable precisely because they violate expectation in one or two respects and no more [S12]. Joseph
Henrich adds the social constraint: beliefs propagate when backed by *credibility-enhancing displays* —
the costly acts (vows, poverty, martyrdom) that make a transmitter believable [S7]. Sperber's attractors
supply the third: transmission drifts toward cognitively "sticky" forms whatever the carver intends [S15].

### The metaphor, and why it fits
The wood-carver image is not idle. Human abstract cognition is built on embodied, sensorimotor metaphor:
to understand is to *grasp*, to believe is to *hold* a view, to reason is to *grapple* — Lakoff and
Johnson's "Understanding Is Grasping" [S14]. The project's own first word carries the same freight, for
*dharma* derives from √dhṛ, "to hold, to support" (see [[1.1-etymology]]); the concept being carved is,
etymologically, *that which holds*. The carver holds the tool, the mind holds the idea, and a dharma is
what holds [A].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **"Carving" implies a discrete cut, but change is usually gradual.** Boundaries between traditions are
  continuously negotiated and fuzzy, not sliced at a moment; the "billiard-ball" model of bounded cultures
  is rejected in anthropology [S16]. The instant a dharma is "cut loose and named" is very often a story
  told afterward, not an event anyone lived through.
- **The arrow is not one-way.** The model runs toward hardening or death, but traditions also *fuse* and
  *soften*. Genuine mergers occur (syncretic religions that braid streams rather than split one off) [S17],
  and hardened religions liberalise as often as they ossify [A]. Wood cannot soften back once seasoned;
  this is where the metaphor is least faithful.
- **The family tree is too clean.** Influence is lateral and reticulate — like a braided river or horizontal
  gene transfer — so a strict branching "descent" understates borrowing [S19]. The grain weaves as much as
  it runs straight.
- **"Outliving usefulness" is functionalist.** Traditions persist or vanish by drift, hitchhiking, and
  by-product effects, not only by fitness; the phrase names one obituary among several [S18].
- **Corpus lean, and a verification catch.** The in-corpus Buddhism evidence reaches us mainly through
  secular-Buddhist reformers (Batchelor), who are partisan on the very question of what counts as *grain*
  versus removable *accretion* [S2][S3]; a traditionalist rebuttal (the "grain" is the heartwood, not
  baggage) is largely absent from the shared corpus and should be sought externally. Separately, a corpus
  explainer-transcript put the Shaker peak at 4,000–5,000 and a "1957 covenant closing"; the figures used
  above follow *Encyclopædia Britannica* instead (~6,000 in the 1840s; ~1,000 by 1905) [S22].
