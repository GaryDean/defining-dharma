# 2.4 Transmission & Institutionalization

**Question:** How did dharmas spread, ossify, and get carried across generations — by
priesthoods, monastic and philosophical schools, scribes, and states? What happens to a
dharma when it becomes institutionalized?

## Synthesis

Dharmas are carried across generations by a stack of mechanisms — **psychological**
(prestige- and conformist-biased social learning that makes humans high-fidelity cultural
copiers) [S1] and **institutional** (priesthoods, monastic Saṅghas, philosophical schools,
scribal/canon cultures, and states) [S2][S7][S8]. Two paradigm carriers bracket the range:
the Vedic priesthood's **verbatim oral transmission**, which preserved the Vedas with
extraordinary fidelity for twenty-plus centuries *without writing* [S8], and **Ashoka's state
dhamma**, broadcast across an empire by carved edicts and a cadre of "dhamma-officers" [S7].
Institutionalisation is **double-edged**. It is what lets a dharma outlive its founder, scale,
and stay faithful; but it triggers what Weber called the **routinisation of charisma** — the
founder's living insight hardens into codified doctrine, formal offices, and authority derived
"from the institution rather than from personal magnetism" [S6]. The predictable costs follow:
orthodoxy, heresy and schism; scholastic ossification (letter over spirit) [S4][S5]; and
capture by power, since institutionalised dharmas tend to legitimate the hierarchy that hosts
them [S3]. Hence the recurrent **reform cycle** — periodic returns to the "original," of which
secular dharma is itself one instance [S4]. The core paradox: institutionalisation is both what
preserves a dharma and what erodes what made it worth preserving.

## Findings

### How dharmas get carried — the transmission stack
- **The psychological substrate.** Humans are unusually high-fidelity cultural learners,
  acquiring norms through *model-based biases*: prestige bias (copy the admired/successful),
  conformist bias (copy the majority), and similarity/age cues [S1]. These biases are what make
  a dharma transmissible at all — and they *select* which elements persist: the dharma concept
  foregrounds "transmission fidelity and selection pressures: which elements are stable, which
  mutate, which get rejected" [S2]. Identity does the rest: dharmas tie rules to *who one is*
  ("we are the kind of people who…"), generating in-group cohesion and status systems, so a
  dharma is not merely learned but adopted and defended [S3].
- **Priesthoods + oral transmission.** The extreme case of fidelity is the Vedic priesthood,
  which preserved the Vedas through intricate mnemonic recitation systems (pada-pāṭha,
  krama-pāṭha, jaṭā-pāṭha, ghana-pāṭha), maintaining the *exact sonic form* "with extraordinary
  fidelity over more than twenty centuries" — and did so *deliberately without writing* (Staal's
  studies confirm near-perfect transmission) [S8]. Fidelity here is an institutional achievement,
  not a function of literacy.
- **Scribes and canon.** Writing (per [[2.3-pre-axial-ethics]] and Goody) adds fixity,
  accumulation, and standardisation — enabling scripture, canon, and commentary, but also the
  freezing of a particular version as authoritative.
- **Monastic communities and schools.** The Buddhist Saṅgha (governed by the *vinaya*), the
  Indian *darśanas*, and the Greek philosophical schools reproduced dharmas through apprenticeship,
  rule, and curriculum — institutional bodies that outlive individuals.
- **States.** Ashoka is the paradigm: ~13 years after his accession he created
  **dhamma-mahāmātras** (officers of dhamma) to propagate the ethic *and* keep the throne in touch
  with opinion, and broadcast his **dhamma** on rock and pillar **edicts** across India and beyond
  — notably a *trans-sectarian* civic ethic addressed to Buddhists, Hindus, Jains, and Ājīvikas
  alike [S7]. (Constantine's adoption of Christianity is the Western parallel.)

### What institutionalisation does to a dharma
- **Routinisation of charisma (Weber).** The founder's extraordinary personal authority is
  transformed into a stable, enduring structure: "teachings get codified, the organisation gets
  formalised, and successors derive their authority from the institution rather than from personal
  magnetism" — Muḥammad → the institutions of Islam, Jesus → the Church hierarchy [S6]. The living
  insight becomes an office.
- **The upside (why it's necessary).** Survival beyond the founder; scale and reach; fidelity;
  coordination; and cumulative scholarship/commentary. A dharma that is not institutionalised dies
  with the people who hold it.
- **The downside (ossification).** Batchelor names it precisely: institutions are "subject to human
  failings," and "ossification and corruption… tend to seep unnoticed into any establishment that
  has come to take its authority for granted" — yielding "inflexible dogmas," "charismatic priests,"
  patriarchal structures, and "polemics of exceptionalism" [S4]. Specific failure modes:
  - **Orthodoxy → heresy → schism**: boundary-policing of the "correct" version.
  - **Scholasticism / letter over spirit**: high fidelity can preserve the husk and lose the kernel —
    captured in the corpus principal's worry that "the Buddha's dharma is unfinished… accurate
    reproduction of dharmic discourse — gotama's greatest failure?" [S5].
  - **Power-capture**: dharmas are "interfaces between power and ethics" — they can legitimate,
    constrain, or challenge it [S3] — and institutionalised dharmas tend to *legitimate* the
    hierarchy that hosts them (developed in [[7.3-dharma-and-power]] and [[7.1-failure-modes]]).
- **The reform cycle.** Ossification provokes return-to-origin movements: the Protestant Reformation,
  Buddhist revival, and **secular dharma itself** — Batchelor's "Ten Theses of Secular Dharma," the
  "spiritual nomads" and "refugees" from lineages who practise "for the sake of this world alone"
  [S4]. Reform is the dharma's self-correction against its own institutionalisation — and, being a
  movement, is itself liable to re-institutionalise, so the cycle repeats.

### The deep tension
Institutionalisation is at once **necessary** (untransmitted, a dharma dies with its founder) and
**corrosive** (transmission routinises living insight into authority-backed dogma). High fidelity
preserves the *form* while risking the *spirit*; charisma routinised is charisma tamed. This is the
structural reason dharmas oscillate between consolidation and reform.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The corpus is itself a reform move, and reads institutionalisation accordingly.** The
  secular-dharma corpus (Batchelor especially) foregrounds ossification and corruption and valorises
  the de-institutionalised "spiritual nomad" [S4]. This risks under-crediting what institutions
  *achieve*: without the Saṅgha, the Brahmin mnemonic schools, the scribal canons, and even Ashoka's
  state, there would be no dharma left to recover. This note deliberately frames institutionalisation
  as *enabling-and-corrosive*, not merely corrupting. [bias flag]
- **"Routinisation of charisma" fits founder-dharmas better than emergent ones.** It is a Weberian
  ideal-type, not a law; diffuse dharmas without a single charismatic founder (the Vedas, customary
  ethics, Greek philosophy) institutionalise without a founding charisma to routinise — so the model
  is partial.
- **Selection bias in the exemplars.** A few paradigm carriers (Vedic priesthood, Ashoka's state,
  the Church) are developed; major others (the Confucian literati and examination system, the Islamic
  *ʿulamāʾ*/madrasa, Greek schools) are only gestured at.
- **Cited via summaries.** Weber, Ashoka's edicts, Vedic transmission (Staal), and Henrich's
  learning biases are cited via secondary/encyclopedic sources and corpus essays, not the primaries.
