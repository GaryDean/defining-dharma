# 2.4 Transmission & Institutionalization

**Question:** How did dharmas spread, ossify, and get carried across generations — by
priesthoods, monastic and philosophical schools, scribes, and states? What happens to a
dharma when it becomes institutionalized?

## Synthesis

Dharmas are carried across generations by a stack of mechanisms — **psychological**
(model-biased social learning that makes humans high-fidelity cultural copiers) [S1][S22] and
**institutional** (priesthoods, monastic Saṅghas, philosophical schools, scribal/canon cultures,
and states), an institution being a set of "socially created and culturally inherited
proscriptions on behaviour that define roles and set expectations about social interactions"
[S24][S7][S8]. The psychological layer is real but weaker and more conditional than the popular
account allows: in transmission-chain experiments prestige bias is the *smallest* measured
effect, with content biases dominating what survives [S18], and conformist transmission alone
**cannot sustain** a tradition — what stabilises one is norm reinforcement, the rewarding of
compliance and punishing of deviation [S21]. Two paradigm carriers bracket the institutional
range: the Vedic priesthood's **verbatim oral transmission**, which preserved the Vedas with
extraordinary fidelity for twenty-plus centuries *without writing* [S8], and **Ashoka's state
dhamma**, broadcast across an empire by carved edicts and a cadre of "dhamma-officers" [S7].
Institutionalisation is **double-edged**. It is what lets a dharma outlive its founder, scale,
and stay faithful; but it triggers what Weber called the **routinisation of charisma** — the
founder's living insight hardens into codified doctrine, formal offices, and authority derived
"from the institution rather than from personal magnetism" [S6]. Independently of Weber, the
same shape falls out of transmission modelling: dividing a repertoire among specialist
custodians raises the complexity a tradition can hold, but concentrates that knowledge in few
hands, so the body that preserves a dharma is also its single point of failure [S27]. The
predictable costs follow: orthodoxy, heresy and schism; scholastic ossification (letter over
spirit) [S4][S26]; and capture by power, since institutions can "enforce socially detrimental
behaviours" when "designed by a minority for a minority" [S24]. Hence the recurrent **reform
cycle** — periodic returns to the "original," of which secular dharma is itself one instance
[S4]. The core paradox: institutionalisation is both what preserves a dharma and what erodes
what made it worth preserving.

## Findings

### How dharmas get carried — the transmission stack
- **The psychological substrate.** Humans are unusually high-fidelity cultural learners, and the
  capacities that make this possible — transmission fidelity, teaching, innovation, recombination
  and ratcheting — are argued to be adaptations of the forager niche rather than by-products of
  general intelligence [S22]; the criteria for when such transmission counts as *cumulative* are
  set out by Mesoudi & Thornton [S23]. Norms are acquired through *model-based biases*: prestige
  bias (copy the admired/successful), conformist bias (copy the majority), and similarity/age
  cues [S1].

  ▲ **The experimental literature substantially qualifies this, and an earlier version of this
  note overstated it.** In a narrative transmission-chain experiment, "prestige bias as proxied by
  accent had the smallest effect on transmission, increasing the likelihood of a proposition's
  recall by only 15%" — what dominated survival were *content* biases: survival-relevant, social,
  emotionally negative and minimally counterintuitive material [S18]. And "conformist transmission
  alone cannot sustain traditions and variation in the face of cognitive biases"; what does
  sustain them is **norm reinforcement**, which also "creates a route by which historical cultural
  practices can influence behaviour long into the future," producing multi-generational lag as
  populations adapt to changed circumstances [S21]. The upshot is that a dharma's transmissibility
  depends less on *who* transmits it than on *what it says* and on *whether the community enforces
  it* — which relocates the explanatory weight from charisma to sanction [A].

  Prestige does carry normative content in the field: among young Tanzanian men, elders were
  prestigious "because of their age and position as preservers and teachers of societal norms,"
  their standing "culturally mandated" by customs of respect — though the same study found prestige
  "difficult to distinguish from dominance," which bears directly on the power-capture problem
  below [S19]. Identity does the rest: dharmas tie rules to *who one is* ("we are the kind of
  people who…"), generating in-group cohesion and status systems, and identity-cued learning of
  just this sort is modelled as an adaptive strategy under specifiable conditions [S20] — so a
  dharma is not merely learned but adopted and defended.
- **Priesthoods + oral transmission.** The extreme case of fidelity is the Vedic priesthood,
  which preserved the Vedas through intricate mnemonic recitation systems (pada-pāṭha,
  krama-pāṭha, jaṭā-pāṭha, ghana-pāṭha), maintaining the *exact sonic form* "with extraordinary
  fidelity over more than twenty centuries" [S8] — and did so *deliberately without writing* (Staal's
  studies confirm near-perfect transmission) [S9]. Fidelity here is an institutional achievement,
  not a function of literacy. The Vedic case is extreme but not sui generis: a coding of ethnographic
  records across 55 traditional societies found ethnoscientific experts were "religious or spiritual
  leaders, such as priests or shamans, in 19% of all of our text records," and sets two models of
  such expertise against each other — a *cultural transmission* model, in which experts pass
  knowledge on freely and prestige tracks competence, versus a *proprietary knowledge* model of
  rare, secretive know-how that "emphasises barriers to knowledge transmission" [S25]. That
  opposition is precisely the priestly tension this note is describing — custodianship shading into
  gatekeeping — stated as a testable alternative rather than an accusation [A].
- **Scribes and canon.** Writing (per [[2.3-pre-axial-ethics]] and Goody) adds fixity,
  accumulation, and standardisation — enabling scripture, canon, and commentary, but also the
  freezing of a particular version as authoritative [A].
- **Monastic communities and schools.** The Buddhist Saṅgha, governed by the *vinaya* [S10] and
  operating in the Mūlasarvāstivāda material as a property-owning juristic person with continuity
  independent of its members [S11]; the Indian *darśanas*, reproduced through commentarial lineage —
  the same root *sūtras* expounded by successive named teachers across a millennium [S12]; and the
  Greek philosophical schools, with the Stoa passing by scholarch succession from Zeno to Cleanthes
  to Chrysippus and on to Diogenes of Babylon, so that "the school of thought founded there long
  outlived the physical Athenian porch" [S13], the Lyceum and the Academy likewise persisting through
  their successors [S14][S15]. Rule, curriculum and commentary make institutional bodies that outlive
  individuals — a comparative pattern Cabezón's cross-cultural treatment of scholasticism sets out
  [S16]. The general principle these cases instantiate is **social ratcheting**: cultural products
  become "of such complexity that they become very unlikely to be recreated from scratch by naive
  individuals," and individual memory limits force the repertoire to be distributed across
  interdependent specialists [S28]. That is why a *vinaya*-governed corporate body is needed rather
  than merely a succession of gifted individuals — no one person carries the whole [A]. ▲ The "six
  schools" grouping of the *darśanas* is itself substantially a medieval
  doxographic construction rather than an ancient institutional fact [S17], so this row is about
  transmission mechanism, not a tidy institutional parallel [A].
- **States.** Ashoka is the paradigm: ~13 years after his accession he created
  **dhamma-mahāmātras** (officers of dhamma) to propagate the ethic *and* keep the throne in touch
  with opinion, and broadcast his **dhamma** on rock and pillar **edicts** across India and beyond
  — notably a *trans-sectarian* civic ethic addressed to Buddhists, Hindus, Jains, and Ājīvikas
  alike [S7]. (Constantine's adoption of Christianity is the Western parallel.)

### What institutionalisation does to a dharma
- **Routinisation of charisma (Weber).** The founder's extraordinary personal authority is
  transformed into a stable, enduring structure: "teachings get codified, the organisation gets
  formalised, and successors derive their authority from the institution rather than from personal
  magnetism" — Muḥammad → the institutions of Islam, Jesus → the Church hierarchy [S6]. The living
  insight becomes an office.
- **The upside (why it's necessary).** Survival beyond the founder; scale and reach; fidelity;
  coordination; and cumulative scholarship/commentary. Specialisation into custodial roles is what
  "allows populations to increase their cultural repertoire without requiring that members…
  increase their individual capacity" — a tradition can hold more than any of its holders [S27]. A
  dharma that is not institutionalised dies with the people who hold it [A].
- **The downside (ossification).** Batchelor names it precisely: institutions are "subject to human
  failings," and "ossification and corruption… tend to seep unnoticed into any establishment that
  has come to take its authority for granted" — yielding "inflexible dogmas," "charismatic priests,"
  patriarchal structures, and "polemics of exceptionalism" [S4]. Batchelor is a partisan reform
  voice, but the same costs now fall out of formal models built for other purposes, which is
  stronger evidence than his polemic alone. Specific failure modes:
  - **Orthodoxy → heresy → schism**: boundary-policing of the "correct" version — and this is a
    *predicted* dynamic, not merely a pathology. Norms driven by intra-group cohesion and
    inter-group differentiation are modelled as producing subgroup-differentiating markers [S29],
    and Manning et al. find that "when groups that are culturally similar come into contact the need
    to maintain clear cultural boundaries drives them apart," explicitly on analogy with character
    displacement [S21]. Doctrinal hair-splitting is therefore sharpest between *neighbouring* sects,
    which is what the historical record shows [A].
  - **Scholasticism / letter over spirit**: high fidelity can preserve the husk and lose the kernel —
    captured in the corpus principal's worry that "the Buddha's dharma is unfinished… accurate
    reproduction of dharmic discourse — gotama's greatest failure?" [A]. Modelling converts this
    from a value judgement into a predicted structure: sustaining a high-proficiency (as opposed to
    broad-repertoire) culture requires dense networks and "norms that enforce social learning at the
    expense of individual innovation," exemplified by Cameroonian potters whose "practitioners form
    tight communities that limit access to knowledge and reject departures from socially admitted
    norms," yielding "highly conserved methods and products" [S26]. Conservatism is the price of
    depth, not a failure of nerve.
  - **Fragility through concentration**: the same specialisation that raises attainable complexity
    means "domain-specific knowledge can be concentrated in small subsets of the population, making
    it more susceptible to loss" under demographic or environmental shock [S27]. A custodial
    priesthood is both the reason a dharma survives and the reason it can vanish in a generation [A].
  - **Power-capture**: dharmas are "interfaces between power and ethics" — they can legitimate,
    constrain, or challenge it [A] — and institutionalised dharmas tend to *legitimate* the
    hierarchy that hosts them, an instance of the general finding that institutions "can enforce
    socially detrimental behaviours if these institutions are badly designed, or designed by a
    minority for a minority" [S24] (developed in [[7.3-dharma-and-power]] and [[7.1-failure-modes]]).
- **The reform cycle.** Ossification provokes return-to-origin movements: the Protestant Reformation,
  Buddhist revival, and **secular dharma itself** — Batchelor's "Ten Theses of Secular Dharma," the
  "spiritual nomads" and "refugees" from lineages who practise "for the sake of this world alone"
  [S4]. Reform is the dharma's self-correction against its own institutionalisation — and, being a
  movement, is itself liable to re-institutionalise, so the cycle repeats.

### The deep tension
Institutionalisation is at once **necessary** (untransmitted, a dharma dies with its founder) and
**corrosive** (transmission routinises living insight into authority-backed dogma). High fidelity
preserves the *form* while risking the *spirit*; charisma routinised is charisma tamed. This is the
structural reason dharmas oscillate between consolidation and reform.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The corpus is itself a reform move, and reads institutionalisation accordingly.** The
  secular-dharma corpus (Batchelor especially) foregrounds ossification and corruption and valorises
  the de-institutionalised "spiritual nomad" [S4]. This risks under-crediting what institutions
  *achieve*: without the Saṅgha, the Brahmin mnemonic schools, the scribal canons, and even Ashoka's
  state, there would be no dharma left to recover. This note deliberately frames institutionalisation
  as *enabling-and-corrosive*, not merely corrupting. [bias flag] ▲ This flag is now **partly
  discharged**: the note's central paradox no longer rests on a reform partisan, because the same
  trade-off is derived independently in the cultural-evolution literature, which has no stake in the
  secular-dharma argument [S26][S27].
- **▲ A live contradiction: hierarchy may impede fidelity, not produce it.** This note's paradigm
  carriers of high-fidelity transmission — the Brahmin priesthood, the Saṅgha, the Mauryan state —
  are all steeply hierarchical. Yet Migliano & Vinicius argue the opposite for the hominin lineage:
  "reduction in social hierarchies was a central factor behind the increased efficiency of cultural
  transmission in humans," because dominance suppresses close observation and the free expression of
  learned behaviour [S22]. These may be reconcilable — an evolved *capacity* arising under
  egalitarian conditions, later harnessed by *historical institutions* operating at a different scale
  — but the note should not pretend the tension is settled, and the possibility that hierarchical
  custodianship buys fidelity at a cost this note has not measured stays open [A].
- **▲ If content beats prestige, the note's emphasis is misplaced.** Berl et al. found content
  biases outranked all model-based biases in narrative transmission [S18]. If that generalises, then
  *what a dharma says* — how memorable, emotionally charged and minimally counterintuitive its
  doctrines are — matters more for its survival than the prestige of those who teach it. That would
  shift the explanation of dharmic success toward doctrinal content and away from the transmission
  institutions this note is organised around. It is not pursued here and deserves its own pass [A].
- **"Routinisation of charisma" fits founder-dharmas better than emergent ones.** It is a Weberian
  ideal-type, not a law; diffuse dharmas without a single charismatic founder (the Vedas, customary
  ethics, Greek philosophy) institutionalise without a founding charisma to routinise — so the model
  is partial.
- **Selection bias in the exemplars.** A few paradigm carriers (Vedic priesthood, Ashoka's state,
  the Church) are developed; major others (the Confucian literati and examination system, the Islamic
  *ʿulamāʾ*/madrasa, Greek schools) are only gestured at.
- **Cited via summaries — reduced but not eliminated.** Henrich's learning biases are no longer
  carried by the trade book alone: prestige, conformity and similarity cues each now have primary
  experimental or modelling sources [S18][S20][S21], and this gap-flag is largely discharged for the
  psychological layer. **Weber, Ashoka's edicts and Vedic transmission (Staal) remain secondary or
  encyclopedic**, and targeted probing found no primary material in the corpus for state formation,
  bureaucracy, writing or literacy — so [S7] and the scribes-and-canon paragraph cannot currently be
  upgraded. Two book citations, [S11] (Schopen) and [S16] (Cabezón), are still consulted only at
  abstract or metadata level, though the claims they carry are now reinforced by full-text primaries
  [S28][S26].
