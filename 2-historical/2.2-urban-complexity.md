# 2.2 Urban Complexity

**Question:** How did the transition from kinship-based tribes to anonymous urban
societies necessitate explicit dharmic codes? What problems, individual and societal,
did these frameworks seek to solve?

## Synthesis

At small scale, cooperation runs on **kinship plus face-to-face reciprocity**; below
roughly Dunbar's ~150, everyone can monitor everyone and reputation is direct [S3][S6].
Scaling up — agriculture, settlement, the city — forces "one-off transactions with
anonymous strangers," and the small-group toolkit breaks down: I might help you and never
see you again, so the free-rider can win [S1][S3]. Cooperation among strangers therefore
needs a **scale-free substitute**: shared identity markers, indirect reciprocity, and
norms that are *monitored and sanctioned* — by the community, and, on one influential
account, by omniscient moralizing "Big Gods" acting as societal glue [S2][S3][S4]. An
explicit, codified, universalizable ethic — a *dharma* — is exactly this technology: it
converts a stranger into "one of us" (co-religionist, co-citizen) and makes conduct
predictable without personal acquaintance [S2][S4], solving **societal** problems
(free-riding, stranger-trust, coordination, conflict resolution, division-of-labour
legitimacy) and **individual** ones (moral orientation and meaning once kin-intuitions no
longer reach). But "necessitate" overstates the case: Graeber & Wengrow show that scale
neither requires coercive hierarchy nor presupposes prior kin-tribes [S5], and Henrich &
Schulz show that dharmic codes can *cause* stranger-society rather than merely respond to
it [S7]. The accurate verdict: scale made dharmic codes powerfully *advantageous*
(selected-for), and codes and stranger-society **co-produced** each other.

## Findings

### The mechanism that breaks down at scale
Robin Dunbar's "social brain hypothesis" ties stable-relationship capacity to neocortex
size, yielding a human cognitive limit of ~150 emotionally significant ties [S6]. Within
that range, kinship and direct reciprocity — plus the fact that everyone can watch everyone
— sustain cooperation. Pagel makes the structural point: **kinship caps group size** ("your
family's never going to be that huge"), and all other animal cooperation rests on kinship or
direct back-scratching; this is *why* no animal society lives in the billions. Humans needed
"a different mechanism… a scale-free mechanism" of shared identity and *indirect* reciprocity
to cooperate beyond kin [S3]. The transition to agriculture and settlement meant social life
was "no longer based exclusively upon face-to-face interactions": people must "frequently
engage in one-off transactions with anonymous strangers," and "traditional models of
cooperation break down at this scale" — the core "problem of cooperation" [S1]. Graeber & Wengrow counter that this premise is not universal: among modern hunter-gatherers (Hadza, Martu) residential groups are often not biological kin at all, and cities have existed without rulers or temples — so scale did not mechanically necessitate hierarchy or codified authority [S5].

### The dharmic solutions (what explicit codes supply)
- **Norms + monitoring + sanctions.** Henrich: the cultural evolution of social norms
  strengthens cooperation by "bring[ing] the community in as monitors"; sanctions for
  violations even reinforce kin altruism [S4]. Codified norms externalise and stabilise what
  face-to-face groups did informally.
- **Shared identity → indirect reciprocity.** A common code lets a stranger be treated as a
  co-member, so reputation and indirect reciprocity can operate among people who will never
  transact twice [S3].
- **Supernatural monitoring ("Big Gods").** Shariff & Norenzayan (2007) found priming god
  concepts increases prosocial behaviour in anonymous economic games; Purzycki et al. (2016,
  *Nature*) linked moralistic, punishing gods to the expansion of human sociality. Belief that
  an omniscient deity monitors and punishes acts as "societal glue," extending cooperation to
  distant co-religionists where human supervision is absent [S2].
- **Division of labour under competition/warfare** selected for these institutions (Turchin &
  Gavrilets) [S2].

Together these *are* an explicit dharma: a transmissible, universalizable ethic that makes
conduct predictable among strangers — the "social technology for cooperation at scale" of
[[1.3-boundary-definition]] and the universal pattern of [[1.2-universal-patterns]].

### What problems, individual and societal
- **Societal:** the free-rider/defection problem at scale; trust and impartiality among
  strangers; coordination; conflict resolution beyond kin ties; legitimacy of impersonal
  authority; a workable division of labour [S1][S2][S3][S4].
- **Individual:** moral orientation and meaning once kin-based intuitions no longer suffice;
  navigating anonymity; an internalised compass — the "new interiority" and emergence of the
  individual already noted at the moment of codification in [[2.1-axial-age]].

### A concrete historical mechanism — and a reversal of the causal arrow
Henrich & Schulz (2019, *Science*, "The Church, intensive kinship, and global psychological
variation") supply a documented case: from ~500 CE the Western Church's **Marriage and Family
Program** banned cousin marriage, polygamy, and concubinage, dissolving intensive kinship into
"small, nuclear households, weak family ties, and residential mobility," which "fostered
greater individualism, less conformity, and more impersonal prosociality" — a precursor of
civic society [S7]. This **reverses the naïve arrow**: rather than anonymous cities forcing new
codes, a *dharmic code* actively dissolved kinship and manufactured the stranger-society
psychology suited to large-scale cooperation. Codes can be cause, not just effect.

### The monitoring need is context-dependent (and decays)
Botero et al. (2014, *PNAS*) found contemporary societies grow *less* religious as they become
better off economically, politically, and environmentally; moralizing gods are more prevalent
under duress and volatility [S2]. As prosperity and stability rise — and impersonal institutions
(rule of law, bureaucracies, reputation systems) take over the monitoring role — the need for
divine surveillance declines. This is the bridge to **secular** dharma (see
[[6.1-secular-translation]]) and is consonant with the affluence finding in [[2.1-axial-age]]
once the two variables are separated: Baumard's affluence drives the historical *emergence* of an
ascetic, moralizing religious *type* as prosperity first rose, whereas Botero's tracks the
contemporary *prevalence and intensity* of belief as societies grow rich — the same underlying
shift, affluence retiring propitiatory, surveillance-driven religion in favour of an interiorised
ethic (Baumard) or its secular-institutional successor (Botero) [S2].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The question's framing is doubly contestable — and the corpus itself supplies the
  counter-evidence.** Graeber & Wengrow (*The Dawn of Everything*, in the corpus) attack both
  ends: (a) the **kin-tribe premise** — statistical work on modern hunter-gatherers (Hadza,
  Martu) shows residential groups "turn out not to be made up of biological kin at all," and
  many humans actively avoid their relatives; ancient HGs look similar; (b) the
  **scale→hierarchy inference** — "the mere fact of urban life does not, necessarily, imply any
  particular form of political organization, and never did"; early cities ran for centuries
  without temples, palaces, or a ruling stratum [S5]. So "anonymous urban societies necessitated
  explicit codes" overstates: scale made codes *advantageous*, not mechanically necessary, and
  did not require coercive hierarchy.
- **Dunbar's number is itself contested** — recent re-analyses put the stable-group figure far
  lower (≈42, or 70–107) or argue it cannot be cleanly estimated at all [S6]. The "150" should be
  treated as a heuristic, not a constant.
- **The Big Gods thesis is correlational and contested.** Even its proponents' data establish
  correlation (Purzycki) and strong context-dependence (Botero); and as flagged in
  [[1.2-universal-patterns]], Whitehouse et al. (2019) found complex societies *precede* moralizing
  gods (Letter later retracted on a coding critique, main finding largely upheld) — cutting against
  gods as the *prime mover* of scale. Moralizing religion looks more like one tool among several
  (alongside secular institutions) than the necessary cause.
- **Corpus bias.** This note is unusually corpus-rich (5 KB sources, real books — Pagel, Henrich,
  Graeber & Wengrow) — the inverse of the cognate/Axial notes. But the corpus leans functionalist
  ("religion built cities / societal glue"), a frame that risks the same teleology Assmann criticises
  in [[2.1-axial-age]]; the Graeber & Wengrow material (also in the corpus) is the internal
  counterweight, and is given full weight here.
- **Cited via summaries.** Dunbar, Shariff & Norenzayan, Purzycki, Botero, Turchin & Gavrilets, and
  Henrich & Schulz are cited via the corpus essays' reference lists and encyclopedic/news summaries,
  not read in the primary.
