# 2.3 Pre-Axial / Pre-literate Ethics

**Question:** What was the ethical baseline before codified dharmas? How did pre-Axial,
pre-literate societies regulate moral behaviour without explicit doctrine, and what does
that baseline tell us about what the Axial codifications actually added?

## Synthesis

The pre-Axial baseline was **moral, not pre-moral**: a robust ethical order operated for
tens of thousands of years with no writing and no doctrine, resting on an evolved
cooperative substrate (reciprocity, fairness, the near-universal Golden Rule — see
[[1.2-universal-patterns]]) and on **decentralised social enforcement** — gossip, ridicule,
shaming, ostracism, and, in the last resort, execution. Boehm's "reverse dominance hierarchy"
captures the mechanism: an egalitarian coalition actively suppresses would-be upstarts [S1],
backed by third-party/altruistic punishment [S9]. That enforcement worked because it had
*teeth*: a bad reputation in a small band converted reliably into reduced sharing, exclusion
and ostracism — and disapproval that carries no such material consequence decays, sustaining
cooperation only in the short run [S8]. The work that written doctrine would later
do was instead done by **ritual, taboo, and above all storytelling/myth**, which broadcast
the "rules of the game" and the fate of cheaters — tellingly, moralizing high-gods are "often
absent" among hunter-gatherers, so narrative serves as their functional analogue [S3]. It
follows that the Axial codifications added **not morality but a set of technologies layered on
it**: explicitness (codification), scope-expansion (universalising the in-group ethic to
strangers), second-order reflection/justification, and — via writing — fixity,
decontextualisation, and transmissibility at scale [S5]. The deep lesson is that ethics needs
neither scripture nor gods to exist; this is the historical ground of the secular-dharma claim.
Three honesty checks apply: the baseline was no utopia (egalitarianism is coercively enforced),
there may be no single baseline at all [S6], and the orality/literacy "great divide" is
contested as ethnocentric [S5].

## Findings

### The baseline was moral, not pre-moral
The cooperative substrate — reciprocity, fairness, sharing, the near-universal Golden Rule,
Curry's seven cross-cultural moral rules — predates writing and doctrine by tens of millennia
(documented in [[1.2-universal-patterns]]). Morality is thus the human *default*, not a
civilisational invention. Boehm's study of egalitarian hunter-gatherers shows how this default
is *organised*: although humans have an innate tendency toward social dominance, foragers
suppress it through "moral community practices," maintaining a deliberate **egalitarian ethos**
of sharing, cooperation, and mutual respect, in which leaders must be generous and unassuming
[S1].

### Enforcement without doctrine (the mechanisms)
- **Reverse dominance hierarchy.** The group collectively curbs "upstarts" who threaten the
  egalitarian ethos through ridicule, criticism, and ostracism — and, in rare cases of
  persistent domination, execution decided collectively to restore balance [S1].
- **Gossip and reputation.** Within small face-to-face groups, gossip and public opinion are
  the routine deterrents against deviance [S1] — the original, pre-doctrinal monitoring system
  (which works *only* at small scale; cf. [[2.2-urban-complexity]]). The mechanism has been
  measured directly in a contemporary experience-sampling study (309 people, 5,284 gossip
  events): everyday gossip is predominantly *about cooperativeness*, receivers update their
  evaluations of the target, and those updated evaluations shift help-and-avoid intentions — and
  gossip bites precisely *because* it is indirect, imposing reputational cost while sidestepping
  the retaliation risk of a face-to-face rebuke [S7]. ▲ That is a WEIRD contemporary sample, so
  it evidences the mechanism's human generality, not the pre-Axial past itself; Boehm's foragers
  remain the ethnographic instance [S1].
- **But disapproval alone is not enough — it needs teeth.** The crucial qualification the older
  version of this note lacked: expressed disapproval, on its own, "has no impact on an offender's
  fitness." In a 40-round experiment, communicated disapproval raised cooperation initially and
  then *decayed*, while tangible punishment held; disapproval sustains cooperation in the long run
  only when it is reliably backed by something fitness-relevant — "physical punishment, monetary
  fines or fewer cooperative interactions because of ostracism" [S8]. This is not a weakness in the
  pre-Axial account but its hidden strength: forager gossip worked *because* it was never merely
  talk. In a small band, a bad reputation reliably converted into reduced sharing, exclusion from
  cooperative ventures, and ultimately ostracism — the "teeth" were built in. The same finding
  predicts exactly where the mechanism fails: once groups scale past the point where reputation
  reliably tracks back to material consequence, disapproval loses its bite, which is the deep
  reason codified sanction and institutional monitoring had to take over ([[2.2-urban-complexity]]) [A].
- **Third-party / altruistic punishment.** Cooperation is stabilised by individuals who punish
  transgressors at a cost to themselves (Fehr & Gächter 2002); because "altruistic punishers are
  condemned to a lower evolutionary success within their group," the provision of a sanctioning
  system is itself "a second-order social dilemma," so the trait requires between-group selection
  [S9]. ▲ A qualifier the earlier note omitted: between-group competition selects *which* norms
  proliferate, but punishment and reputation can stabilise *any* norm, adaptive or maladaptive
  [S9] — which is part of why the baseline was no utopia (below). A further *nuance*: this punitive
  drive has a self-interested, reputation-signalling side — we punish partly to advertise our own
  trustworthiness, which is why we despise hypocrites (dishonest signallers) and sometimes punish
  disproportionately [S4]. But the signal is weaker and more ambiguous than the blog framing
  implies: in a direct experimental test, third-party punishers did *not* win the partner-choice
  competition that account predicts, and observers preferred generous helpers to punishers, so
  punishment is "a more ambiguous signal of cooperative intent compared to help" [S10].
- **Ritual, taboo, and storytelling.** Narrative is the pre-literate carrier of norms. Smith,
  Dyble et al. argue storytelling functions to "broadcast social norms, co-ordinate social
  behaviour and promote cooperation" — informing everyone of the rules *and that others know
  the rules* (solving coordination, not just free-riding). In their Agta fieldwork, stories
  encode sex-equality and cooperation (the sun and moon sharing the sky) and the punishment of
  cheats (a thieving bird impaled for stealing the shared catch). Crucially, they note that
  moralizing high-gods, which do this job in large agricultural populations, are "often absent"
  in hunter-gatherer societies — so storytelling is their analogue [S3]. This last point, the
  note's most load-bearing rhetorical claim, no longer rests on a blog aside: a phylogenetic
  reconstruction across a global hunter-gatherer sample finds animism the oldest reconstructed
  religious trait, belief in *active* ancestral spirits or high gods absent in the last common
  ancestor of present-day foragers, and hunter-gatherers the subsistence mode *least* likely to
  adopt morally punishing high gods — with the authors reading this as evidence of "a deep
  evolutionary past for the egalitarian ethos" [S11]. ▲ This is phylogenetic inference from
  *contemporary* foragers, so the note's "not living fossils" caveat below covers it too: it is a
  modelled inference about early modern humans, stronger than plain analogy but not direct
  pre-Axial evidence.

### What the Axial codifications actually added
Because morality already existed, the Axial/literate codes did not *create* it. On this project's
reading they added three things [A]:
1. **Explicitness / codification** — turning implicit practice into stated, examinable doctrine.
2. **Scope expansion / universalisation** — extending the ethic beyond the in-group toward
   strangers and, in principle, all humanity (the universal ethics of [[2.1-axial-age]]).
3. **Second-order reflection and justification** — theorised, argued ethics rather than merely
   enacted ones (Bellah's "theoretic culture"; the metacognitive turn of
   [[3.2-metacognitive-function]]).
4. **Fixity, decontextualisation, transmissibility — via writing.** Goody and Ong show that
   literacy introduces decontextualisation, reflection, metalanguage, and devices like lists and
   formulas, allowing doctrine to be fixed, scrutinised, accumulated, and transmitted across
   generations and distances [S5] — the basis of institutionalisation
   ([[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]]). Writing is not the *only* route to fixity, though:
   the Vedic priesthood preserved its corpus with extraordinary fidelity through institutional oral
   mnemonics alone (see [[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]]), so what literacy adds is a
   *cheaper, more scalable* fixity — not fixity as such.
5. **Detachment from face-to-face enforcement** — codified norms plus (super)natural or
   institutional monitoring substitute for the gossip-and-ostracism that only works in small
   groups (cf. [[2.2-urban-complexity]]). This transition now has a citable account rather than
   resting on this project's assertion alone: the shift from reputationally-enforced to
   institutionally-enforced commitment is dated to Neolithic sedentism, agriculture and hierarchy,
   its payoff being that institutional third-party punishment lets people "make commitments with
   strangers where reputational information about their past behaviour is irrelevant" [S12]. That
   is precisely the move the disapproval-needs-teeth finding [S8] predicts once groups outscale
   face-to-face reputation. ▲ Its author frankly labels it a narrative hypothesis, not established
   fact, and the note carries that caveat over.

In short, the Axial move took a small-scale, oral, enforcement-embedded morality and
**abstracted, universalised, fixed, and scaled it**.

### What the baseline tells us (the payoff)
That a full moral order ran on story, ritual, reputation, and reverse-dominance enforcement —
with no scripture and frequently no moralizing god — shows that doctrine is a *technology* on
top of an older cooperative nature, not its source. This grounds the secular-dharma position:
the *functions* religion later monopolised (norm-broadcast, enforcement, meaning) were met by
non-doctrinal means before, and could in principle be re-tooled without metaphysics
([[6.1-secular-translation]]) [A].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **No noble-savage utopia.** The egalitarian baseline is *actively and sometimes violently
  enforced* — ostracism and execution of upstarts [S1] — and the moral circle was bounded by a
  hard in-group/out-group line (out-group raiding and violence were common; moral norms are
  "usually highly group specific… not applied to out-group members" [S13]). Even the enforcement
  instinct has a self-interested, status-seeking side [S4]. And the same enforcement machinery is
  norm-neutral: punishment and reputation can stabilise a *maladaptive* norm as readily as a
  benign one [S9], so a functioning moral order is no guarantee of a *good* one. "Baseline" must
  not be read as "idyll."
- **Maybe no single baseline.** Graeber & Wengrow argue Pleistocene and early-Holocene societies
  were socially diverse and experimental (including seasonal shifts between egalitarian and
  hierarchical arrangements), not one uniform egalitarian band — so "*the* ethical baseline" is a
  problematic singular [S6].
- **The orality/literacy "great divide" is contested.** Goody and Ong's strong thesis is
  challenged by Brian Street's "ideological model," which rejects the inference that literate
  people are more rational or "advanced" and calls the autonomous model ethnocentric — a
  projection of Western academic textual practice [S5]. So "what writing added" should be stated
  as social-technological *capacities* (fixity, scale, decontextualisation), not cognitive
  superiority.
- **Evidence problem.** Pre-literate ethics leaves no texts; reconstruction leans on archaeology
  plus ethnographic analogy with modern foragers — who are not "living fossils" but peoples with
  their own histories, often shaped by (or fled from) states (a point James C. Scott develops,
  also in the corpus). Inferences from the Agta or Hadza to the Pleistocene are suggestive, not
  decisive.
- **Corpus bias.** This note is corpus-rich and well-sourced (Boehm; prosocial studies), but the
  corpus leans on the egalitarian/cooperative reading of foragers; the Scott and Graeber & Wengrow
  material (also in the corpus) is the internal corrective and is given weight here. Boehm's
  reverse-dominance summary was read via a chapter-summary document, not the full primary.
- **Cited via summaries — much reduced.** Three of the note's four knowledgebase citations were
  prosocial.world blog posts or a Boehm chapter-summary; the 2026 corpus expansion re-grounded them
  in peer-reviewed primary literature — the no-moralizing-gods claim in a global phylogenetic
  reconstruction [S11], altruistic punishment in a peer-reviewed treatment of the second-order
  dilemma [S9], gossip in an experience-sampling study [S7], and the punishment-as-signal nuance in
  a direct experimental test [S10] — and converted the previously-unsourced institutional-detachment
  claim into a citable one [S12]. **Still secondary:** Fehr & Gächter (2002) is named but read via
  [S9]; Smith/Dyble et al. on storytelling [S3] and Goody/Ong/Street [S5] remain corpus-essay or
  encyclopedic, as the corpus added nothing on orality/literacy.
- **The ethnographic-present hazard is now carried on more claims.** The gossip study [S7] is a
  contemporary Dutch sample and the forager-religion reconstruction [S11] is built from
  present-day hunter-gatherers; both evidence mechanism or modelled ancestry, not the pre-Axial
  past directly. The "not living fossils" caveat above must be read as covering them.
