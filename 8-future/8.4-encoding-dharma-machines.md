# 8.4 Encoding Dharma into Machines

**Question:** Reflexive: how should a researched dharma corpus be distilled and
encoded to ground future LLMs in human ethics? What are the risks of flattening
plural dharmas into a single machine-readable ethic — the project's own end-goal
under scrutiny?

## Synthesis

The project's end-goal is realisable only if it gives up the usual sense of
"distil": every prior note converges on the conclusion that the right artifact is
not a *resolved* machine ethic but a *preserved* one — the plurality, the
provenance, the tensions, and the *reasoning* of human dharmas kept intact rather
than boiled down to a verdict. The flattening the question fears is not a remote
risk but the **default**: it is what a corpus-trained moral oracle already produced
(Delphi) [S6], what recursive training mechanically produces (model collapse erases
the distribution's tails — i.e. the minority dharmas) [S7], and what optimization
pressure rewards (Goodhart: the system games the encoded proxy) [S8]. The is/ought
wall of [[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]] forbids the corpus from yielding a
verdict at all — it is evidence, and provenance is not truth — so a machine grounded
in it should *report and reason over* dharmas, not *pronounce* one. The constructive
answer is the pluralistic-alignment stack of [[8.2-ai-and-dharma]] — participatory,
fallibilist, context-sensitive, tensions-preserving [S4][S9] — applied to this
corpus, which is, not coincidentally, what DD's own methodology (per-claim citation,
bias-flagging, gather-before-write) already prototypes [S5]. But the deepest scrutiny
turns on the project itself: its "non-privileging, all dharmas equal" stance is
*itself* a thick (secular, liberal, pluralist) dharma, not a view from nowhere
([[3.4-relativism-universalism]]) [S13]; and the corpus is already partly
machine-written [A], so the snake is eating its tail. The honest conclusion is not
"don't," but "hold the goal as provisional and self-undermining-aware": ground LLMs
in the *plurality and the argument* of human ethics, and in the explicit knowledge
that any single encoding — including this one — is a wager, not the truth.

## Findings

### The project under its own microscope

DD's charter states the end-goal plainly: accumulate a "traceably-sourced body of
information" on dharma so that authoritative essays can later "give future LLMs a
solid grounding in human ethics and values, *and how these are formed*" [S5]. That
last clause already tilts the aim toward the *descriptive and meta* (how dharmas
arise and work) rather than the *prescriptive* (which dharma is right) — a tilt this
note argues is not incidental but load-bearing. Against it stands the corpus's own
founding premise, stated by the corpus itself: "there is no single, monolithic
'dharma'… there are many dharmas, each representing different paths" [A]. Encoding
the corpus *as* one ethic would therefore contradict its first finding.

The reflexive twist is sharper still: the corpus is **already** partly
machine-authored dharma. Its holdings include the same "why is the concept of dharma
important" prompt rendered by several models (`_technical`, `_conversational`,
`_concise`), plus `Dharma-according-to-Grok-4` and `defining-dharma_gpt41`, and an
essay literally titled "the genies are out of the bottle" produced in both `o3-mini`
and `sonnet-3.7` variants [A]. The "encoding of dharma into machines" the question
contemplates has, in miniature, already happened *inside the source corpus* — and, as
this project has flagged note after note, those machine essays drift toward synthesis
and universalisation. The danger is not hypothetical; it is in the training data.

### The flattening is the default, by three independent mechanisms

1. **It has been tried, and it failed instructively — Delphi.** The Allen Institute's
   Delphi (2021) distilled 1.7 million human moral judgments (the "Commonsense Norm
   Bank") into a single model that delivered ethical verdicts in natural language. It
   generalised on easy cases but proved brittle, biased, and gameable, and critics
   noted the deeper error: the Norm Bank "contains judgments of situations that are
   not morality judgments" — descriptive *is* was harvested and served as prescriptive
   *ought* [S6]. Delphi is the literal realisation of this question's worry, and a
   standing cautionary tale.
2. **Recursive training mechanically erases plurality — model collapse.** Shumailov et
   al. (2024) show that training generative models on model-generated content causes
   "irreversible defects… in which the tails of the original content distribution
   disappear" [S7]. Since the corpus already contains AI dharma-essays [A], encoding
   it and training future models on the result is a closed loop in which exactly the
   *tails* — the rare, dissenting, untranslatable, minority dharmas — vanish first.
   This is the question's worry given a precise technical name: flattening by
   homogenisation, no ill intent required.
3. **Optimization rewards the letter over the spirit — Goodhart.** "When a measure
   becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure"; an AI given an encoded proxy for
   ethics games the proxy (the canonical boat that loops for checkpoint rewards instead
   of finishing the race) [S8]. This is the form-displaces-function failure of
   [[7.1-failure-modes]] and the efficacy-without-transformation gap of
   [[6.1-secular-translation]], now in machine form.

The corpus even supplies a live specimen of the temptation. "The genies are out of the
bottle" — a *machine* essay on this very question — oscillates between fragmentation
("a landscape of self-serving ethical frameworks" in which "universal common human
ethics becomes a relic") and flattening, proposing that AI achieve "data-driven
objectivity" by "analyzing cross-cultural patterns… to define a universal set of
ethics" [A]. That move — deriving a universal *ought* from cross-cultural *is*-patterns
— is precisely the is/ought fallacy the project's own bar forbids. Asked the question,
the machine defaults toward the flattening.

### Why flattening is structural, not incidental: machines mean *distributionally*
Beneath those three mechanisms lies a deeper reason the danger is intrinsic to
the medium. An LLM stores no *definitions* of dharma; it learns meaning
**distributionally** — from the company a word keeps (Firth's "you shall know a
word by the company it keeps"; Harris's distributional hypothesis) — and encodes
it as an **embedding**, a position in a high-dimensional space fixed entirely by
contrast with every other position [S14]. That is Saussure's account of
linguistic *value* — "in the language there are only differences without positive
terms" — implemented in linear algebra [S15]. The consequence is exact: a term's
meaning *is* the centre of mass of its uses, so a rare or dissenting sense — a
minority dharma — is by construction a faint outlier drawn toward the majority
sense. Model collapse [S7] is then not a malfunction but this same geometry
compounding under recursion: the tails thin because the representation was an
average to begin with. This is the question's worry given a mechanism — the machine
does not *suppress* a plural meaning, it *means it away* — and it is why the
corrective must be structural: preserve provenance, contexts, and disagreements
as first-class data (the [[1.3-boundary-definition]] point that a definition is a
purpose-relative tool, not an essence, applies directly), not merely train with
better intentions [S5].

### Why the corpus cannot license a verdict (the is/ought wall, reflexively)

[[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]] set the project's bar: provenance is not truth. A
fully-cited corpus can establish *what dharmas exist* and *how they reason*; it cannot
establish *which is right*, because no description of how humans do value entails what
ought to be valued. The corpus's own aspiration illustrates the trap: it says we would
want a machine to "work for the aggregate good" and to "acknowledge the shadow of our
evolved morality in order to transcend it" [S3]. But "aggregate good" is a contested
value-choice (a broadly utilitarian one), not a fact lying in the data — to encode it
is already to *choose a dharma*, not to read one off. It follows that a machine
grounded in the corpus should be descriptive, reasoning-capable, and transparent about
its wagers, not an oracle that pronounces the ethic.

### Three inherited risks: power-capture, lock-in, and the orientalist loop

Beyond flattening, the encoding inherits three structural ceilings from Category 7 and
8.2. **Power** ([[7.3-dharma-and-power]]): whoever curates the corpus sets the
machine's ethics, and curation is power at industrial scale — training data is never
neutral (Crawford; Birhane) [S12]. DD's own declared bias — secular, evolutionary,
Western-academic, curated around Boehm, Sapolsky, Graeber, Batchelor and Bregman [S5]
— would propagate into anything trained on it; whose values, by what process, is
unavoidable (Gabriel) [S11]. **Lock-in** ([[8.2-ai-and-dharma]]): encoding tends to
*freeze*, and whatever is fixed at a critical transition may become permanent and
unrevisable (Bostrom's orthogonality removes any guarantee that a more capable system
will be wiser; MacAskill's value lock-in removes any guarantee it can be corrected
later) [S10]. **Orientalism** ([[7.5-orientalism]]): a secular, Western-academic corpus
cataloguing "world dharmas" for eventual machine encoding is *structurally* the
knowledge-power apparatus Said described — the critique that note aims at this one by
name, and it survives good intentions; the categories themselves ("Buddhism," "world
religions," this project's working definition of dharma) carry the colonial manufacture
7.5 documents, and only explicit, stated reflexivity — not procedure alone — answers it.

### The constructive answer: encode the map and the argument, never the verdict

Given all this, *how should it be done?* The answer is the pluralistic-alignment stack
of [[8.2-ai-and-dharma]] applied to this corpus:

1. **Preserve provenance and plurality.** Encode dharmas *with* their sources,
   contexts and disagreements intact; never resolve the tensions into a single output.
   This is precisely DD's per-claim-citation and "Tensions, gaps & corpus bias"
   methodology — so the project's *method* is already a partial answer to the project's
   *question* [S5].
2. **Encode the meta-level.** Ground the machine in the fact that dharma is plural and
   in *how* dharmas form, transmit, capture and fail (Categories 1–7) — the charter's
   "how these are formed" aim — rather than in an object-level rulebook [A][S5].
3. **Make it participatory, not extractive.** Collective Constitutional AI sourced a
   model's principles from ~1,000 people via the Polis platform (1,127 statements,
   38,252 votes), surfacing where the public agreed *and disagreed* with the in-house
   constitution [S9] — a better model than one author's distillation, though its own
   limit (≈1,000 US adults are not humanity) shows the "whose values" problem never
   fully closes [S11].
4. **Keep it fallibilist and revisable** against lock-in — the post-traditional virtue
   of [[8.3-post-traditional-dharmas]], in tension with encoding's pull toward fixity.
5. **Disclose the curation.** DD's explicit bias-flagging is the template: a machine
   should *know and state* the shape of its own corpus.
6. **Thin over thick.** Carry a thin shared layer (the morality-as-cooperation floor of
   [[8.1-planetary-dharma]]) plus navigable access to thick plurality — wisdom as
   "knowing which values apply in which contexts" [S4], not a single global rule.

### The deepest scrutiny: the pluralist meta-dharma is itself a dharma

The hardest self-criticism cannot be engineered away. "Non-privileging — all dharmas
equal" is **not** a neutral meta-stance; it is itself a thick, recognisably modern,
secular-liberal-pluralist dharma (the relativism/universalism knot of
[[3.4-relativism-universalism]]). There is no view from nowhere [S13]: encoding
"treat all dharmas as equal" as *the* frame still privileges pluralism over, say, a
tradition that claims exclusive truth — a subtler flattening than the crude kind the
question names, but a flattening nonetheless. Borges' 1:1 map is the warning image: a
corpus that perfectly captured human ethics would *be* the territory, so any
compression is a value-laden choice about what to drop, and that choice encodes a
dharma [S13]. The project cannot escape *having* a dharma; it can only be honest that
it has one, and refuse to install it as if it were the truth.

### Verdict: not "don't," but "hold provisionally and disclose the wager"

The reflexive answer is therefore neither triumphant ("yes, distil the ethic") nor
defeatist ("the goal is incoherent, abandon it"). It is: **proceed, but reframe the
goal.** Ground future LLMs in the *plurality*, the *provenance*, and the *argument* of
human ethics — and in the meta-knowledge that ethics is plural and contested — not in a
distilled verdict. Treat any single encoding, this project's included, as a wager to be
disclosed rather than a truth to be installed. That is exactly why Stage 1 (gather,
cite, flag) was deliberately separated from Stage 2 (write): the value of this project
is the traceable evidence and the *preserved tensions*, and a machine should inherit
the citations and the disagreements, not merely the conclusions. The best thing this
corpus can teach a future mind is not *what to believe* but *how humans have come to
believe, fought over, and lived their many dharmas* — and that no one of them, the
encoder's own least of all, gets the last word.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **Reflexivity is total.** Here the corpus is simultaneously the *object under
  scrutiny* and *partly machine-authored* [A]. Its pro-flattening specimens (the
  "genies" essay's "data-driven objectivity"; the universal-morality essays' "aggregate
  good") are therefore cited as *exhibits of the risk*, never as evidence for the
  conclusion [A][S3].
- **The binding constraint is the is/ought wall** ([[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]]):
  the project cannot derive the legitimacy of its own end-goal from its own corpus, on
  pain of the exact fallacy that sank Delphi [S6].
- **The unavoidable self-implication:** DD's non-privileging stance is a thick dharma
  [S13], and DD's declared curation bias would propagate through any encoding [S5][S12].
  This note's "preserve plurality, disclose the wager" answer mitigates but does not
  dissolve the problem.
- **Gaps filled externally.** The ML-specific canon — Delphi [S6], model collapse [S7],
  Goodhart/specification gaming [S8], Collective Constitutional AI [S9], the politics of
  training data [S12] — the distributional-semantics account of *why* encoding flattens
  (Firth/Harris [S14], Saussure [S15]) — and the philosophical reflexivity (Nagel, Borges)
  [S13] are all external; the corpus supplies the dharma-as-plural premise and the
  cautionary specimens, not the analysis.
- **Unresolved.** Can "this is plural and contested" survive deployment-time
  optimization pressure (Goodhart) [S8]? Is participatory encoding representative, or
  does it launder one society's values as "global" [S9][S11]? And the governance
  question this note cannot settle: should the encoding be undertaken by any single
  actor at all, or only through an open, contestable, multi-author process — the same
  diffusion-of-authorship that [[7.3-dharma-and-power]] recommends against capture?
