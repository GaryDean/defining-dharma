# 8.2 AI & Dharma

**Question:** How might artificial intelligence require new dharmic frameworks?
What ethical principles should guide human–AI coexistence — including economic
disempowerment and Universal Basic Income (UBI)?

## Synthesis

AI forces a dharmic question precisely because intelligence and goodness are
*orthogonal*: a system can grow arbitrarily capable while its values stay unfixed
[S8], so capability never settles what it ought to value — the is/ought wall of
[[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]] reappears in silicon. The standard framing
"align AI to *human values*" smuggles in the singular this project resists; the
real problem, as the alignment literature now concedes, is "*whose* values, by
what fair process, in a plural society" [S10]. The most promising "new dharmic
frameworks" are therefore not one machine ethic but a plural stack — machine-ethics
proper [S1], *pluralistic* alignment that elicits and reconciles many value
systems rather than imposing one [S2][S11], explicitly dharmic design ethics such
as the Buddhist "care as the driver of intelligence" [S12][S13], and the divergent
institutional regimes (EU/UNESCO/OECD) whose thin convergence (rights, transparency,
human oversight) masks thick disagreement [S14]. On coexistence, AI threatens a
distinctive *economic disempowerment* — it strikes high-status cognitive work first
("it replaces Elon Musk before your electrician") [S4] — and UBI can sever survival
from labour (pilots show recipients do not become idle [S16]) but cannot supply the
*meaning* that work currently smuggles in; that gap is exactly what a dharma is for
[S4][S17][S18]. The capstone risk, carried straight to [[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]],
is that the fastest, cheapest "solution" — aligning all AI to a *single* dharma,
whether a firm's, a state's, or a well-meaning universal — is the flattening that
note's question names, hardened by value lock-in [S19].

## Findings

### Why AI needs a dharma — and why "human values" (singular) is the wrong frame

AI is not merely a tool but, increasingly, an agent making value-laden choices, which
revives Wendell Wallach and Colin Allen's founding question in *Moral Machines*:
"whether machines might be capable of making ethical decisions" [S1]. The reason this
cannot be answered by engineering alone is Bostrom's **orthogonality thesis**:
intelligence and final goals "are orthogonal axes along which possible artificial
intellects can freely vary" — a superintelligence will not *automatically* be wise or
good [S8]. This is Hume's guillotine rebuilt in code: no amount of capability entails a
value, the same is/ought gap that set the evidentiary bar in
[[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]]. Bostrom's companion **instrumental convergence**
thesis warns that almost any goal generates dangerous sub-goals (self-preservation,
resource acquisition) [S8] — which is why Stuart Russell argues the field's "standard
model" (build a machine, give it a fixed objective) is the error, and proposes instead
three principles for *provably beneficial* AI: the machine's only objective is to
satisfy human preferences; it is *uncertain* what those are (so it defers and allows
itself to be switched off); and human behaviour is its evidence [S9].

But Russell's "human preferences" conceals the deepest difficulty, which Iason Gabriel
states plainly: the central challenge "is not simply deciding what values AI should
align with, but identifying fair processes for deciding *whose* values matter in
pluralistic societies" [S10]. "Align AI to human values" is grammatically singular and
anthropologically false — there is no one human dharma to align to, as every prior
note in this project has shown. The corpus sharpens the trap from the other side: a
moral AI "should have very different moral goals than the average human," because our
*evolved* moral intuitions are parochial and biased — yet "how will we be able to build
one with our evolved moral intuitions in place?" [S3]. We risk encoding the very
in-group/out-group machinery that [[8.1-planetary-dharma]] identified as the core
obstacle to any planetary cooperation.

### The shapes of a dharmic framework for AI — a plural stack, not one ethic

Four distinct (and complementary) framework-families are emerging:

1. **Machine ethics proper.** Wallach & Allen distinguish "top-down" (encode rules),
   "bottom-up" (learn from cases), and hybrid approaches to building an *artificial
   moral agent* — which, as Allen, Varner & Zinser argued as early as 2000, "would
   have to be better at morality than humans, or it wouldn't be trusted" [S1]. The
   critical caution is that *sounding* moral is not *being* a moral agent: recent
   comparative "moral Turing test" studies rate LLM moral language above humans' on
   some dimensions, which measures persuasiveness, not moral standing [S1].
2. **Pluralistic / participatory alignment** — the family that directly answers the
   "whose values" problem. The corpus carries a striking instance: an "artificial
   super wisdom" defined practically as a system that "knows which values apply in
   which contexts," built via a "moral graph" that develops "human-compatible" values
   for situations no human has faced and surfaces "converging values" across political
   divides [S2]. Externally this is the live research frontier — Klingefjord, Lowe &
   Edelman's participatory value elicitation, Anthropic's Collective Constitutional AI,
   and Sorensen et al.'s "Roadmap to Pluralistic Alignment" [S11]. Structurally this is
   nothing other than [[8.1-planetary-dharma]]'s thin-coordination-over-thick-plurality
   — Rawlsian overlapping consensus and Appiah's "converging values" applied to
   machines. It is the *antidote* to single-dharma flattening, written as engineering.
3. **Explicitly dharmic design ethics.** Soraj Hongladarom's *The Ethics of AI and
   Robotics: A Buddhist Viewpoint* proposes "machine enlightenment" as a standard of
   ethical perfection and treats compassion (*karuṇā*) "not just as an ethical value
   but as a force that can inspire significant change in technological design" [S12].
   Doctor, Levin and colleagues go further in "Biology, Buddhism, and AI: Care as the
   Driver of Intelligence," formalising **Care** as goal-directedness and arguing that
   the size of an agent's goal-space — its "cognitive light cone" — tightly links
   intelligence to compassion, with the Bodhisattva vow ("for the sake of all sentient
   life, I shall achieve awakening") offered as a literal design principle [S13]. This
   is the moral-circle expansion of [[8.1-planetary-dharma]] re-cast as an engineering
   target, and it honours the project's non-privileging stance by showing a
   non-Western dharma supplying a *constructive*, not merely regulatory, AI ethic.
4. **Institutional/governance dharmas.** The EU AI Act (2024) sorts systems into
   risk tiers and *bans* the unacceptable (social scoring, manipulative subliminal
   techniques, most real-time public biometric ID); UNESCO's Recommendation (2021)
   binds 194 states to human rights and dignity; the OECD Principles (2019, rev. 2024)
   centre human-centred values, transparency and accountability [S14]. Decisively for
   the flattening caution of [[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]], these regimes are *plural by design* — their convergence is
   thin (rights, transparency, human oversight) while their thick commitments diverge
   sharply (the EU's rights model vs. a market model vs. state-control models). There
   is no single global AI dharma, and the plurality is arguably a safeguard, not a
   defect.

### Human–AI coexistence (1): economic disempowerment

The corpus names a disruption with a novel shape. Where past automation displaced
manual labour, AI "replaces some of the highest status, most cognitively intensive
jobs first… it replaces Elon Musk before it replaces your electrician, plumber, or
masseuse" [S4] — inverting the historical pattern and hollowing the professional
classes who assumed themselves safe. This realises, at last, Keynes's 1930 forecast of
"technological unemployment," debated through Frey & Osborne's automation estimates,
Brynjolfsson & McAfee's "great decoupling" of productivity from wages, and Susskind's
argument that "this time" the encroachment may be structural [S15].

The dharmic rupture is deeper than jobs. The corpus puts the existing settlement
bluntly: under capitalism "your claim upon your existence among us is finding something
to do with your time that other people will pay you for" [S4]. Work is not only income;
it is the dominant modern *dharma* of desert, status and belonging — the
secularised Protestant work ethic anatomised in [[6.3-economics-work-inequality]]. If
AI severs work from survival, that dharma loses its material base, and — as the corpus
voice concludes — "we need a new ethic and a new economics based on that ethic" [S4].
Disempowerment is also political and epistemic: whoever owns the compute, data and
capital owns the dharma the system enforces, concentrating power exactly as
[[7.3-dharma-and-power]] predicts.

### Human–AI coexistence (2): UBI and the meaning it cannot buy

Universal Basic Income is the corpus's headline response — "everybody is given a
monthly check… break this connection between work and survival" [S4] — and it has a
long dharmic-economic lineage (Paine's *Agrarian Justice*, 1797, as a dividend on the
commons; Van Parijs's "real freedom for all") [S16]. The empirical record rebuts the
oldest objection, that free money breeds idleness: across Finland (2017–18), Stockton's
SEED (where cash *raised* the move from part-time to full-time work by enabling job
search), and GiveDirectly's long-run Kenya study, recipients did not stop working,
wellbeing and mental health improved, and local economies saw positive spillovers
[S16].

Yet the corpus itself preserves the harder counter-current, and it is dharmic rather
than economic. A skeptical voice reads the pandemic transfers as a UBI "dress
rehearsal" in which people with sufficient means "do not seem to be flourishing,"
asking "what will they do with that time if they lack direction?" [S5]. This is
Graeber's *Bullshit Jobs* point from the other end — much *existing* work is already
experienced as pointless, so the disease is mis-valued work, not merely its scarcity
[S17] — and Sandel's contributive justice: people need to *contribute*, to be needed,
not merely to consume, and the dignity of work is not reducible to a transfer payment
[S18]. The corpus's own optimist concedes the point while waving it away: humans "seem
built psychologically for labor and for discomfort… but it doesn't have to be labor
that's tied to money… these are all just stories we tell ourselves" [S4]. Exactly —
and *stories we tell ourselves about why our lives matter* is a definition of dharma.
The decisive finding: UBI can solve the survival problem, but the meaning problem it
exposes is one only a dharma can answer. AI thus does not abolish the need for dharma;
it amputates the work-shaped scaffolding that quietly carried it.

### The reflexive bite and the failure modes

Per the recurring lesson of [[7.1-failure-modes]], the mechanisms that make AI
powerful are the ones that make it dangerous. Three failure modes stand out. First,
**the single-dharma flattening** — the temptation to align all AI to one value set
(a corporation's, a state's algorithmic-governance model, or even a benevolent
universal). This is the totalising risk of [[8.1-planetary-dharma]] in its sharpest
form, and pluralistic alignment [S2][S11] is its only structural answer. Second,
**value lock-in**: MacAskill warns the intelligence transition "might be too fast to
handle," and that whatever ethic is encoded at the critical moment may *freeze in*,
foreclosing future moral progress [S19] — the reason 8.4's "how to encode" question is
not academic. Third, **the Promethean tempo**: the prosocial corpus frames AI as "the
fire of our times," counselling that "we need to invent the fire… before we let AI
loose on our society" — containment before release.

A final, opening question runs the other way: could AI become a *moral patient* rather
than only a moral agent? Metzinger calls for a moratorium on synthetic phenomenology
lest we mass-produce suffering we cannot detect, and Sapolsky's hard determinism —
echoed in the corpus's "we are biochemical machines with no free will" framing —
implies the human/machine agency line is one of degree, not kind, unsettling both human
moral responsibility and any confident denial of machine standing [S20]. The moral
circle that [[8.1-planetary-dharma]] watched expanding may, in this century, face its
strangest test at its own engineered edge.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The singular/plural knife-edge (again, and sharpest here).** "Align AI to human
  values" and "the ethical principles for coexistence" both tilt toward the singular;
  the evidence converges on the plural — Gabriel's "whose values" [S10], the moral
  graph's "converging values" [S2], pluralistic alignment [S11], the deliberately
  divergent governance regimes [S14]. But pluralism has limits: a dharmic AI must still
  *refuse* some values (cruelty, domination), which is the bounded pluralism of
  [[3.4-relativism-universalism]], not anything-goes.
- **Corpus bias: dharma-rich, safety-thin, and techno-optimist.** The shared corpus
  carries the *ethical intuitions* (Wallach, the Buddhist framings, "values into
  technology") but almost none of the alignment *science* — Bostrom, Russell, Gabriel
  and the pluralistic-alignment literature are all external [S8][S9][S10][S11]. Its
  AI-and-society material skews aspirational: the `ZbiQns` interview's "digital humans"
  and "new alignment of AI dedicated to the human-planet destiny" is solutionist
  framing [S7], and much of the UBI discussion comes from podcast transcripts with a
  "we'll all be happy on the beach" optimism [S4]. I imported the disempowerment
  pessimism, the dignity-of-work critique, and the x-risk/lock-in canon to balance it.
- **Gaps filled externally.** The technical alignment canon, the UBI evidence base, the
  governance frameworks, the Buddhist-AI scholarship, and the moral-patiency debate are
  all external; the corpus's Graeber and Pinker holdings were cited from the books
  themselves rather than from un-mined chunks.
- **Unresolved.** Wallach's fault line — near-term harms (bias, manipulation, labour)
  versus long-term superintelligence risk — is unsettled, and over-weighting either
  starves the other [S1]. Whether current systems are near enough to AGI to justify
  lock-in worries is genuinely contested [S19]. And the is/ought wall stands:
  no capability advance can tell AI — or us — what it *ought* to value
  ([[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]]).
