# 8.1 Planetary Dharma

**Question:** As humanity faces global challenges (climate change, inequality,
technological disruption), what elements of a 'planetary dharma' are emerging?

## Synthesis

A "planetary dharma" is best read not as a single global ethic replacing local
ones, but as a *thin coordination layer* — commitments narrow enough to be
affirmed from inside many thick, divergent traditions — sitting atop preserved
plurality. The driving pressures are real and now measurable (six of nine
planetary boundaries transgressed [S9]; climate as a Tragedy of the Commons with
no out-group to organise against [S1]), but the obstacle is our evolved parochial
altruism [S1][S17], and the deepest finding is structural: because there is no
competing planet, a planetary dharma cannot arise by blind group selection — it
must be *consciously authored* [S3][S4]. The elements already crystallising are an
enlarging moral circle [S12][S5], a "morality-as-cooperation" universal whose
*means* are shared while its *ends* stay plural [S6][S7], polycentric rather than
monolithic governance [S2], cross-tradition convergence in real documents
(secular Earth Charter [S14]; religious *Laudato Si'* [S15]), and an
intergenerational duty to those not yet born [S13]. The very same forces generate
a signature failure mode — the totalising temptation to impose one thick planetary
ethic top-down, of which China's state "ecological civilization" is the live
cautionary case [S16] — so the project's own end-goal ([[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]])
inherits a warning this note's own title courts: a planetary dharma worth
having *coordinates* dharmas; it does not dissolve them into one.

## Findings

### The pressures are real, and for the first time the clock is empirical

The case for *some* shared planetary response no longer rests on moral
exhortation alone; it has an instrument panel. The planetary-boundaries framework
(Rockström et al. 2009; first full assessment Richardson et al. 2023) defines a
"safe operating space for humanity" across nine Earth-system processes and finds
**six already transgressed** — climate change, biosphere integrity, land-system
change, freshwater change, biogeochemical (nitrogen/phosphorus) flows, and novel
entities — with ocean acidification approaching its limit [S9]. The corpus frames
the same moment through the *Nature* editorial "Welcome to the Anthropocene," an
epoch "defined by the massive planetary impact of human behavior," and quotes the
Obama-era science adviser John Holdren's stark menu: "adaptation, mitigation and
suffering" [S1]. The three challenges named in the question are best treated as one
interlocked predicament: E.O. Wilson's much-quoted diagnosis — that we carry
"Paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, and god-like technology" [S17] —
locates climate, inequality, and technological disruption in a single mismatch
between an evolved psychology and the scale of its tools.

Crucially, the boundaries are an *is*, not an *ought*. Earth-systems science can
describe a safe operating space; it cannot by itself generate the obligation to
stay inside it — that step is a value-wager, not a measurement, and it sits
exactly on the is/ought wall mapped in [[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]]. The
science raises the stakes of the question; it does not answer it.

### The core obstacle: a cooperative ape with a tribal switch

Human cooperation is real but *parochial*: we are "altruistic in the sense that
members of a tribe, team, or platoon are primed to cooperate and even sacrifice
for others in the group" and "parochial in the sense that altruism tends to exist
only in a context that helps us compete with outsiders" [S1]. This is the
in-group/out-group machinery that drives the lethal-boundary failure mode in
[[7.1-failure-modes]] and that Boehm's reverse-dominance account in
[[7.3-dharma-and-power]] traces to coalitions formed *against* a threat.

Climate exposes a cruelty specific to planetary problems. Ordinary cooperation
free-rides on an enemy — an out-group sharpens in-group bonds. But "climate
management via effective mitigation is a global problem, which by definition
excludes nobody. This means there aren't out-groups to compete against and thus
encourage in-group cooperation" [S1]. The mechanism that *built* human cooperation
is therefore structurally unavailable to *scale* it to the species. Worse, "the
power dynamics are skewed because the high polluters also carry the most global
clout" [S1] — the authoring of any planetary response is captured in advance by
those least incentivised to author it.

### Why a planetary dharma must be authored, not evolved

This asymmetry is the note's pivot. Within the corpus's own multi-level-selection
idiom, some argue "the welfare of the whole earth system must be the ultimate unit
of selection" — but a sympathetic critic immediately objects that "there is no
sense in which our planet is competing with other planets," so planet-level
selection looks impossible [S3]. The resolution is decisive: planetary selection
becomes possible only through "a decision-making process that makes planetary
welfare the target of selection… This is how conscious cultural evolution often
takes place at smaller scales… and can also take place at the global scale" [S3].
The evolutionary biologist Athena Aktipis makes the same point about scaling
cooperation: "all of humanity… is one system that has not had the kind of
evolutionary history that would allow selection to be operating on the level of
the entirety of humanity," so reaching it "is going to take very deliberate and
very intelligent measures" [S4].

A planetary dharma, then, is not an emergent inevitability we can wait for; it is
a *design problem*. That reframing matters because it relocates the danger: where
selection is blind, no one is responsible for the outcome, but where a dharma is
deliberately authored, the authoring power is precisely what
[[7.3-dharma-and-power]] warns can be captured.

### The shape the evidence points to: a thin shared layer over thick plurality

If a planetary dharma must be authored, what should it author? The convergent
answer across the corpus and the external philosophy is a **thin universal over
thick particulars** — and this directly honours the caution that "dharma" in this
note's title is grammatically singular but anthropologically plural.

- *From evolutionary morality.* The corpus's "universal moral principle" — "Solve
  the cooperation/exploitation dilemma without exploiting others" — is explicitly
  thin: it "defines only moral 'means'… and is silent on moral 'ends'… societies
  are free to define what those benefits of cooperation ought to be and change
  them as circumstances change" [S6]. Oliver Scott Curry's morality-as-cooperation
  predicts "variation on a theme": seven cooperative moral domains found across 60
  societies, but ranked and coloured differently everywhere — "moral values will
  reflect the value of different types of cooperation under different social and
  ecological conditions," *not* an identical global code [S7].
- *From political philosophy.* This is Rawls's **overlapping consensus**: a thin
  shared public conception that diverse comprehensive doctrines each affirm *from
  within their own framework*, without anyone surrendering their thick worldview
  [S11]. And it is Appiah's **partial cosmopolitanism**: two commitments held in
  tension — "universal concern for all human beings" *and* "respect for legitimate
  difference" — where "partial loyalties to family, nation, culture are
  legitimate, provided they coexist with concern for all," and where
  "cosmopolitanism is the name not of the solution but of the challenge" [S10].
- *From the corpus's own prosocial frame.* Between the global scale and the small
  group there is "a veritable tropical rain forest of meso-scale groupings and
  social identities… nationality, ethnicity, gender, religion, political
  affiliation," and "in many cases they deserve to remain strong — as long as they
  are coordinated with the global good in mind" [S3]. That last clause *is* the
  planetary dharma in one line: not the abolition of plural identities but their
  coordination.

The moral circle can widen — Singer's "expanding circle" runs from kin to tribe to
nation to humanity (and beyond, to sentient life) with reason as the escalator
[S12] — but the corpus insists this expansion is *bounded*: "we probably won't
discover a single perfect way of life for either individuals or societies… [yet]
not just any set of proposed norms can form a viable moral system" [S6]. This is
the same anti-relativist, anti-monist middle reached in
[[3.4-relativism-universalism]].

### Elements already crystallising

What, concretely, is emerging?

1. **An enlarging moral circle / nascent "species identity."** Researchers point
   to the slow emergence of "species identity along with an appreciation of the
   mutual benefits of collective cooperation at a global scale," provoked by "the
   first bio-ethical-ecological crisis in human history" [S5]. The cultural-MLS
   programme calls the operative move "blurring the line between ourselves and 'the
   other'" [S5]. The corpus's cosmopolitanism and "supertribe" material articulates
   the same intuition in dharmic register, though as advocacy rather than evidence
   [A].
2. **Polycentric, not monolithic, governance.** Ostrom's Nobel-winning work showed
   communities can self-govern shared resources and "avoid the tragedy of the
   commons" via eight core design principles, generalised across contexts by
   Wilson, Ostrom & Cox (2013) [S2]. Decisively for a *planetary* dharma, Ostrom
   herself warned these principles "didn't easily apply to global collective action
   problems" — global commons carry "massive complexity and cultural diversity"
   and "no room exists for major error because experimentation occurs on a single
   and fragile planet" [S1][S2]. Her remedy was *polycentricity*: many locally
   tuned "policy experiments," not one global rule [S2]. Polycentric coordination
   is the institutional form of thin-over-thick.
3. **Cross-tradition convergence in real documents.** The **Earth Charter** (2000)
   is a secular soft-law declaration — respect for the community of life,
   ecological integrity, social and economic justice, democracy/non-violence/peace
   — whose legitimacy comes from "the most inclusive and participatory process of
   its time," i.e. an overlapping consensus, not enforcement [S14]. *Laudato Si'*
   (2015), a religious encyclical, reaches strikingly convergent commitments via
   "integral ecology," "our common home," and the claim that "intergenerational
   solidarity is not optional, but rather a basic question of justice" [S15]. Two
   incompatible comprehensive frameworks arriving at overlapping public commitments
   is the overlapping-consensus mechanism observed in the wild — and it satisfies
   this project's non-privileging stance (cf. [[4.4-abrahamic-frameworks]]).
4. **An explicit intergenerational duty.** Hans Jonas's imperative — "act so that
   the effects of your action are compatible with the permanence of genuine human
   life" — extends the moral circle forward in time to people who cannot reciprocate
   [S13]. The contemporary "longtermism" literature presses the same intuition
   harder [S13]; its hazards are noted below.
5. **A re-embedding of economics within limits.** Kate Raworth's "doughnut" places
   a social foundation (the inner ring no one should fall below) inside the
   planetary-boundaries ceiling, defining "a safe and just space for humanity" —
   the planetary-scale expression of the moral-embedding of economic life argued in
   [[6.3-economics-work-inequality]] [S18].

### The failure mode these same forces generate

Per the recurring lesson of [[7.1-failure-modes]] — the mechanism that makes a
dharma work is the same mechanism that makes it dangerous — the drive toward a
planetary dharma generates a characteristic pathology: the **totalising
temptation** to impose one *thick* planetary ethic top-down.

The live case is China's "ecological civilization" (*shengtai wenming*),
constitutionally entrenched (CCP 2012, state 2018) and producing measurable gains
(forest cover from ~12% to >23%) — but as a "top-down, party-state project" widely
analysed as **authoritarian environmentalism**, in which "the authority to enact
and enforce environmental policy resides primarily with centralized, technocratic
elites… frequently without public consultation" [S16]. It demonstrates that a
*singular*, state-authored planetary dharma is not hypothetical, can be effective,
and is exactly the flattening [[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]]
exists to resist. The corpus supplies its own brake: Aktipis warns that "there are
a lot of things that a multicellular body does to regulate the cells inside of it
that nobody would think are ethical for humans to do to one another" [S4] — you may
scale cooperation by studying the body, but you must not govern a planet like an
immune system culling defectors.

Two further failure modes follow. First, the **manufactured out-group**: because
planetary problems lack a natural enemy, a planetary movement may invent one
("deniers," "the unsustainable," a demonised nation) to recover the tribal
cohesion described above — re-importing the lethal boundary of
[[7.1-failure-modes]]. Second, **capture by the authors**: Garrett Hardin's
canonical "Tragedy of the Commons" (1968) framed the problem in terms that licensed
coercion and his own Malthusian, exclusionary politics — a reminder that *who
frames* the planetary emergency shapes which solutions look thinkable, and that
Ostrom's whole career was the empirical refutation of Hardin's pessimism [S19].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The singular/plural knife-edge (the central tension).** This note treats the
  section's grammatically singular "a planetary dharma" as shorthand for a thin,
  shared *coordination layer*, never a one-size-fits-all comprehensive ethic. The
  evidence converges hard on this reading — morality-as-cooperation's plural ends
  [S6][S7], Appiah's respect for legitimate difference [S10], Rawls's overlapping
  consensus [S11], the prosocial "rainforest of identities… coordinated with the
  global good" [S3] — yet the *pull* of the singular is strong, and the
  ecological-civilization case [S16] shows the thick-monoculture version is the
  default outcome when one author has the power. The plurality is not a hedge; it
  is the finding.
- **Corpus bias toward engineerable optimism.** The shared corpus is curated around
  the cooperation-scaling programme (Wilson, Ostrom, Curry, the prosocial.world
  posts), which is genuinely its strongest material here — but that strength tilts
  the answer toward a hopeful "we can deliberately engineer global cooperation"
  framing [S2][S3][S4]. The harder counter-currents had to be imported: political
  realism and the "ebb and flow" of voluntary agreements (Paris 2015, with the US
  withdrawal/return) [S5]; North–South climate justice and the skewed power of high
  polluters [S1][S16]; and the degrowth-vs-ecomodernism dispute over what the
  planetary dharma's economics should *be* [S18]. The corpus under-weights the
  possibility that no planetary dharma arrives in time.
- **Genuine gaps filled externally.** The corpus is thin on Earth-systems science
  (planetary boundaries, external [S9]), on intergenerational ethics (Jonas /
  longtermism, external [S13]), on the philosophical instrument (Rawls/Appiah,
  external [S10][S11]), and on non-secular planetary ethics (Earth Charter,
  *Laudato Si'*, external [S14][S15]). The corpus's most on-topic documents —
  `cosmopolitanism.expanded` and `we-are-a-supertribe-called-human` — are curated
  framing essays and AI-generated advocacy respectively, used here only to show how
  the corpus *articulates* the aspiration, with the substantive claims anchored on
  Appiah and the planetary-boundaries science [A].
- **Unresolved.** Is "species identity" [S5] actually emerging, or is that hopeful
  projection reading a trend into noise? The is/ought wall ([[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]])
  means the planetary boundaries cannot, alone, oblige anyone. And **longtermism's
  own totalising risk** [S13]: an ethic that weights vast hypothetical future
  populations can discount present and particular people into invisibility — another
  flavour of the flattening this note warns against, carried forward to
  [[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]].
