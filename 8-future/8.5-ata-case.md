# 8.5 Dharma from Scratch: The 'Ata Case

**Question:** When the inherited scaffolding is stripped away, do humans rebuild a
functioning ethical order or collapse into "Lord of the Flies" savagery? What does the
real 1965 'Ata shipwreck show, and what are its limits as evidence?

## Synthesis

The claim that dharmas are *made* — assembled by particular people out of the materials to
hand — has a rare real-world test case. In 1965 six Tongan schoolboys were marooned on the
uninhabited island of 'Ata for fifteen months and, rather than descending into the savagery
of Golding's fiction, built a small working society: rostered labour, a fire kept alight for
over a year, a dispute-resolution ritual, daily song and prayer, and mutual care including
the setting of a broken leg [S1]. Reconstructed by Rutger Bregman as the real-life answer to
*Lord of the Flies*, the case is a vivid instance of a cooperative order rebuilt from scratch
under maximum pressure [S1]. It should be read as *evidence, not proof*: it is a single case,
and the boys arrived already carrying Tongan communal norms and Catholic-school routine, so it
shows a cooperative inheritance *remembered* rather than cooperation invented from nothing
[A]. Nicholas Christakis's comparative work on stranded and shipwrecked groups supplies the
wider pattern — a recurring "social suite" of mild hierarchy, friendship and cooperation — and
its honest counter-case, Pitcairn, where a colony collapsed under racism, alcohol, competition
over women, and failed leadership [S3]. The case also carries a methodological payload for this
project: it *replaces* the discredited Robbers Cave experiment as the manuscript's evidence
that the lethal in-group boundary is movable, since that experiment's hostility was itself
manufactured by the researchers ([[7.1-failure-modes]]) [S2][S4].

## Findings

### The case: fifteen months on 'Ata

In June 1965 six pupils of a Catholic boarding school in Nuku'alofa, Tonga, took a fishing
boat for an adventure, were caught by a storm that shredded the sail and broke the rudder,
and drifted eight days without food, rationing rainwater caught in coconut shells, before
washing up on 'Ata, an island uninhabited since an 1863 slave raid [S1]. They survived there
until their rescue on 11 September 1966 [S1]. Captain Peter Warner, who found them after spotting
unexplained burn-marks on the cliffs, recorded that "the boys had set up a small commune with
food garden, hollowed-out tree trunks to store rainwater, a gymnasium with curious weights, a
badminton court, chicken pens and a permanent fire, all from handiwork, an old knife blade and
much determination" [S1].

The order they built has the recognisable features of a dharma in miniature [A]:

- **A division of labour.** They "agreed to work in teams of two, drawing up a strict roster
  for garden, kitchen and guard duty" [S1].
- **A dispute-resolution ritual.** Quarrels were solved by a time-out: the disputants "would
  go to opposite ends of the island to cool their tempers," and after about four hours "we'd
  bring them back together. Then we'd say 'Okay, now apologise.' That's how we stayed friends"
  [S1].
- **Daily ritual and shared meaning.** "Their days began and ended with song and prayer," to a
  makeshift guitar built from driftwood, half a coconut shell and salvaged wire [S1].
- **Care for the weakest.** When one boy fell from a cliff and broke his leg, the others
  climbed down, carried him up, and set the leg with sticks and leaves; it had healed cleanly
  by rescue, to the local physician's astonishment [S1].

Where the boys of the *make-believe* *Lord of the Flies* "come to blows over the fire," those
in the real one "tended their flame so it never went out, for more than a year" [S1].

### The comparative frame: the "social suite" and its failures

The 'Ata case is one data point in a broader pattern. Christakis's survey of shipwreck and
stranded societies finds that the groups that "fared best were those that had good leadership
in the form of mild hierarchy (without any brutality), friendships among the survivors, and
evidence of cooperation and altruism — all key elements of the social suite" [S3]. The pattern
holds across intentional communities too: those that "respected the exigencies of the social
suite fared better than ones that did not" [S3].

Crucially for honesty, the pattern includes its own failures. Pitcairn Island — settled by the
*Bounty* mutineers and their Polynesian captives in 1789 — did *not* cohere, collapsing into a
"centuries-long culture of sexual predation." Christakis attributes the failure not to
isolation as such (other isolated groups "coped well in similar circumstances") but to specific
corrosive factors: "explicit racism," "intoxication with locally distilled alcohol," "the
competition among the men for the smaller number of women," and "notably ineffective
leadership" [S3]. Collapse is possible; it has identifiable causes; and those causes are the
absence or poisoning of the very cooperative machinery 'Ata expressed [S3].

### The methodological payload: 'Ata replaces Robbers Cave

This project previously leaned, in [[7.1-failure-modes]] and Part 6 of the essay series, on
Muzafer Sherif's 1954 Robbers Cave experiment as evidence that in-group/out-group hostility can
be dissolved by a superordinate goal — that "the fence can be moved." That citation does not
survive scrutiny. Sherif had run the same design a year earlier at Middle Grove, New York
(1953), where the boys "remained the best of friends" no matter how hard the staff tried to set
them against each other, and he buried the failure in a footnote as "various difficulties and
unfavorable conditions" [S2]. At Robbers Cave the hostility was largely *manufactured*: the
experimenters, not the boys, insisted on the week of zero-sum competition, manipulated the
scores, and acted as agents provocateurs; Gina Perry, the first researcher into Sherif's own
archives, describes the study as "a choreographed enactment" and "an exercise in confirmation
bias that says nothing about behavior in the real world" [S2][S4]. Because the "cure"
(superordinate goals) was applied to hostility the experimenters had engineered, the study is
not clean evidence for the movable-fence claim [A].

The 'Ata case is the stronger replacement: a *real* group under *real* survival pressure that
built and sustained a cooperative order — and Middle Grove itself, where boys refused to be
turned into enemies, is arguably better evidence for the underlying optimism than the
manufactured brawl it was buried to hide [S2][A]. The movable-fence claim proper (that the
lethal boundary is installed, not innate) now rests in [[7.1-failure-modes]] on Wrangham's
chimpanzee contrast alone, which does not depend on any staged experiment.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **Single case; not a blank slate.** 'Ata is *n* = 1, six adolescent boys of one culture over
  fifteen months. They were not culturally naked: they carried Tongan village communal norms,
  Catholic-school religious routine (the song and prayer), and pre-existing friendships. The
  honest reading is therefore *cultural cooperation surviving stripped conditions*, not
  cooperation emerging ex nihilo. Presented as proof of an innate "default to goodness," it
  overreaches; presented as evidence that the cooperative inheritance is deep and readily
  re-expressed, it holds [A].
- **Corpus bias toward hopeful anthropology.** Both anchor sources — Bregman's *Humankind* and
  Christakis's *Blueprint* — sit squarely in the shared corpus's optimistic,
  cooperation-first lean (Bregman is one of the corpus's named curators). This is exactly the
  material the corpus is strongest on and most inclined toward, so the "hopeful" verdict is
  partly an artefact of the library. The counter-weight is retained *from within the same
  sources*: Pitcairn and the cannibalism cases are Christakis's own, and Bregman's own reporting
  supplies the Robbers Cave debunking. The bias is named rather than neutralised.
- **Golding as a rhetorical foil.** *Lord of the Flies* is a novel, not data [S5]; using it as the
  contrast case is a device of Bregman's polemic. The real argument is not "fiction was wrong"
  but "the pessimistic experimental canon (Robbers Cave, and by extension the staged view of
  human nature) was unreliable, and the observational record is more cooperative than the
  canon claimed" [A].
- **Selection and survivorship.** We hear about 'Ata because it ended well and was found;
  strandings that ended in death leave fewer narrators. Christakis's sample partly corrects for
  this by including the failures, but the vividness of 'Ata should not be mistaken for
  representativeness [A].
