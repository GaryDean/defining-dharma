# 8.3 Post-Traditional Dharmas

**Question:** What would a post-traditional dharma look like — one that honours
wisdom traditions while embracing scientific understanding and cultural pluralism?

## Synthesis

A post-traditional dharma is not *anti*-traditional but *post-conventional*: it has
passed through tradition and kept its practices and "ultimate concern" while loosening
its metaphysics, institutional authority, and identity-labels [S1][A]. The question's
honour-versus-embrace framing is softer than it looks, because the "wisdom traditions"
we inherit were *already* remade by modernity — detraditionalized, demythologized,
psychologized — so post-traditional dharmas are the honest continuation of a
150-year-old process, not a clean break, and they multiply in what Charles Taylor calls
the "nova effect" [S9][S11]. Their constructive features are fallibilism,
naturalism-without-scientism, practice and community over doctrine, and critical
reflexivity [S3][S4][S13]. Two opposite failure modes bound them — and both are forms
of the flattening this project guards against ([[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]]): at one end, collapse into shallow
DIY consumerism ("Sheilaism") [S14]; at the other, the sharper danger of collapsing
distinct traditions into one perennial "deep unity," which the corpus's own
Interspirituality courts [S6] and which Steven Katz's constructivism shows to be false
[S12]. Two further findings cut against easy optimism: a *secular* dharma is not
automatically safer or more rational than a religious one — it can become a "stealth
religion," dogmatic in its own right [S4] — and the community/belonging that traditional
religion delivered remains the unsolved problem, with secular substitutes so far
under-performing [S5]. There will be many post-traditional dharmas, not one; this note
and the project that contains it ([[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]]) are themselves an
instance of the genre.

## Findings

### What "post-traditional" means — and what it does not

"Post-traditional" is easily misread as "anti-traditional." It is better understood as
*post-conventional* in Kohlberg's and Habermas's sense (cf [[5.3-moral-psychology-development]]):
a stance reached by internalising a tradition and then moving beyond its unquestioned
authority, not by skipping it. The corpus's central exemplar, Stephen Batchelor's
*After Buddhism*, supplies the structural move. Batchelor distinguishes two senses of
"religious": (1) the wish "to come to terms with… our own birth and death" — Tillich's
"ultimate concern" — and (2) "whatever formal means are employed — adherence to sacred
texts, submission to the authority of monastics and priests, performance of rites" — to
enact that concern [S1]. A post-traditional dharma *keeps the first and loosens the
second*: one can be driven by ultimate concern "without ever engaging in any overtly
religious behaviour," which is why, as Batchelor notes, "those who describe themselves
as 'devout atheists' are not entirely joking" [S1]. The corollary stance — "moving
beyond a Buddhist identity while still engaging with dharmic practices," extracting
insight "without adhering to religious dogma or identity labels" — is the secular-dharma
programme in one sentence [A].

The deepest reframing, though, dissolves the question's premise. David McMahan shows
that the "wisdom traditions" available to us are *already* products of modernity:
Buddhist modernism was produced by **detraditionalization** (privileging "reason,
experience, and intuition over tradition"), **demythologization** (aligning with
science for its "halo-effect of reliability"), and **psychologization** (reframing
meditation in the idiom of Western psychology), through a continual "decontextualization
and recontextualization" [S9]. So "honouring wisdom traditions while embracing science"
is not the fusion of two pristine things — the fusion happened generations ago.
"Post-traditional" is simply the honest name for what has been underway since the
nineteenth century, and what Taylor's "immanent frame" and "nova effect" — the
explosion of meaning-options once exclusive humanism became available — made
inevitable [S11].

### The constructive features

Across the exemplars, five family resemblances recur:

1. **Fallibilism / revisability.** A post-traditional dharma holds its claims
   provisionally and updates them against evidence, rather than treating them as fixed
   revelation — the answer it offers to the grounding problem of
   [[3.3-grounding-problem]] and the evidentiary discipline of
   [[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]]. (The corpus's secular dharma *claims* this
   virtue; Evan Thompson's critique, below, is that it often fails to deliver it.)
2. **Naturalism without scientism.** Religious naturalism (Ursula Goodenough, Loyal
   Rue) treats the sacred as *emergent from* nature — "the mystical dimension as
   emergent from one's mind and heart" rather than access to a separate spiritual
   realm — reverence without the supernatural [S13]. Crucially this is *not* the
   reduction of value to science that sinks Harris's project (cf
   [[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]]); it keeps the is/ought wall intact. The corpus's
   minimalist version is the Dalai Lama's "no need for temples… our own brain, our own
   heart is our temple; the philosophy is kindness" [S3].
3. **Orthopraxy over orthodoxy.** What is preserved is practice — meditation, ethics,
   ritual, community (cf [[5.4-ritual-habituation]], [[6.1-secular-translation]]).
   Ritual matters because, as costly-signalling theory shows (Sosis & Bressler 2003),
   it builds cooperation precisely through non-rational, hard-to-fake commitment [S7].
   But this exposes a signature difficulty: once a ritual is understood to be "just" a
   bonding mechanism, it risks the scrupulosity Sapolsky describes — performing "the
   ritual for its own sake… without thinking of the content," i.e. form displacing
   function ([[7.1-failure-modes]]) [S7].
4. **Critical reflexivity.** A post-traditional dharma is, in principle, aware of its
   own failure modes, power dynamics, and appropriation risks (cf [[7.1-failure-modes]],
   [[7.2-appropriation]], [[7.3-dharma-and-power]]). The corpus's best instance is
   D.S. Wilson's own warning that secular belief systems must be tested lest they
   "go the way of stealth religions" [S4].
5. **Community without authoritarianism.** The proposal for "forward-looking,
   quasi-religious groups that provide the benefits of traditional religious communities
   while eschewing supernatural beliefs" — fellowship-first, face-to-face,
   intergenerational [S5].

### The exemplars are plural by design

The nova effect is visible in the spread of actual post-traditional dharmas: secular
Buddhism (Batchelor's *Buddhism Without Beliefs*, recasting the four truths as tasks
rather than propositions) [S1][S15]; Sam Harris's secular-meditation *Waking Up* [S17];
religious naturalism [S13]; modern Stoicism (cf [[6.1-secular-translation]]); Alain de
Botton's "religion for atheists" and Comte-Sponville's "atheist spirituality," which
borrow religion's practices, calendars and architecture without its creed [S17]; humanist
congregations such as the Sunday Assembly [S5]; and prosocial / interspiritual
movements [S6]. No single one is "the" post-traditional dharma — which is exactly the
point, and exactly where the dangers live.

### The two flattening failure modes (the flattening caution, doubled)

A post-traditional dharma can betray the plural in two opposite directions.

- **The shallow end — "Sheilaism."** Robert Bellah's *Habits of the Heart* named the
  archetype: Sheila Larson's private faith, "Sheilaism," following her "own little
  voice," co-opting "strands of multiple religions… without much theological
  consideration" [S14]. This is the buffet/consumerist degeneration — the same
  hollowing diagnosed as McMindfulness in [[6.1-secular-translation]] and as
  decontextualised borrowing in [[7.2-appropriation]]: maximal personalisation, minimal
  depth, no community, no accountability.
- **The deep end — perennialism.** The sharper danger, because it masquerades as
  respect, is the claim that all traditions are surface expressions of one underlying
  truth. The corpus advances exactly this: its Interspirituality literature holds that
  "contemplative practices unearth the mystical roots of the world's religious
  traditions, revealing a deep unity" (Wayne Teasdale, Kurt Johnson, in a Teilhardian
  lineage) [S6]. But this perennialism (Huxley) is precisely the single-dharma
  flattening this project resists — and Steven Katz's constructivism shows it is also
  false: there are "no pure or unmediated mystical experiences"; each tradition
  *constructs* the experiences it produces, so a Christian's union and a Buddhist's
  realisation "are not two encounters with the same reality but two fundamentally
  different experiences," and a forced global synthesis ends up "stripped of much of its
  meaning" [S12]. Honouring traditions means honouring them as genuinely *different*,
  not as interchangeable masks of one essence — the same thin-over-thick logic as
  [[8.1-planetary-dharma]].

Behind both lies a meta-failure: the assumption that a *secular* dharma is automatically
rational and safe. D.S. Wilson's test exposes this — any belief system, religious or
secular, that "sacrifices factual realism on the altar of practical realism" by
portraying "a world without trade-offs" is functioning as a stealth religion; his
Exhibit A is not a church but Ayn Rand's Objectivism, "rationalist" yet as
trade-off-free as any fundamentalism [S4]. Secular ≠ undogmatic.

### The unsolved problems: community and disenchantment

Two difficulties remain genuinely open. First, **community**: despite long effort,
secular substitutes "have not come close to matching the popularity of traditional
religious communities" [S5], even as the decline of religion correlates with an epidemic
of loneliness whose health toll rivals smoking and obesity [S5]. Second,
**disenchantment**: the corpus's *Secondo Natura* mourns a modern "mercantile" order
that has "exiled ritual," replacing the communal and sacred with "the cult of
consumption" [S8] — Taylor's buffered self may be freer but also lonelier and flatter
[S11]. Whether a naturalised dharma can carry the existential weight that transcendence
once bore is the question post-traditional dharmas have not yet answered.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The singular/plural caution, doubled.** "A post-traditional dharma" is plural in
  reality; the two ways to wrongly singularise it are Sheilaism (atomise) and
  perennialism (homogenise) [S14][S12]. This note treats both as failure modes, not
  resting points.
- **Corpus bias: this is the corpus's home turf — and its advocacy.** The shared corpus
  does not merely *describe* post-traditional dharma; it *is* the secular-dharma /
  secular-Buddhism movement (Batchelor, the P-SAGE programme, the secular-community
  campaign), and the Templeton-funded P-SAGE is advocacy with a funder (cf the same
  flag in [[6.1-secular-translation]]) [A][S6]. So the corpus argues *for* the genre;
  the rigorous internal critique had to be imported — Thompson's charge of "Buddhist
  exceptionalism" (that secular Buddhism cherry-picks and claims a false scientific
  warrant) [S10], McMahan's deflation ("it was all already modernist construction")
  [S9], and Bellah's Sheilaism [S14]. Credit where due: D.S. Wilson's "stealth religion"
  essay is a genuine *internal* self-critique [S4].
- **Gaps filled externally.** Taylor, McMahan, Thompson, the Huxley/Katz debate,
  religious naturalism, and Bellah are all external; Pinker's *Enlightenment Now* (the
  reason-science-humanism creed) is cited from the book, with the caveat that it
  under-rates the meaning/disenchantment gap [S16].
- **Unresolved.** Can ritual keep its binding power once everyone knows it is "just"
  bonding (the sincerity/transparency problem) [S7]? Can secular community ever match
  religious depth [S5]? And does fallibilism quietly undercut the wholehearted
  commitment that makes a dharma transformative — the efficacy-is-not-transformation
  tension of [[6.1-secular-translation]]?
