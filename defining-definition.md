# Defining Definition

*A discussion piece on the problem of definition — semantic instability, contested
meaning, and what it implies for grounding machines in human ethics. This is a
reflective essay, not a Stage-1 cited research note: it carries no `[S#]` provenance
and sits outside the question registry. It connects most closely to notes
[[1.3-boundary-definition]] (Boundary & Definition) and
[[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]] (Encoding Dharma into Machines).*

---

You've put your finger on something that turns out to be one of the deepest problems
there is — and framing it reflexively ("defining definition, perhaps") is exactly
right, because the problem bites its own tail. What follows works through it, returning
throughout to *dharma*, which happens to be a near-perfect specimen: it exhibits every
pathology of meaning at once.

## The request contains its own answer

Ask "what is a definition?" and you immediately discover there isn't *one* thing called
definition. There are at least a dozen distinct operations we file under that word:

- **Lexical/reportive** — describes how a word *is* used (can be right or wrong about usage).
- **Stipulative** — assigns a meaning by decree ("let *X* mean…"); can't be false, only useful or useless.
- **Precising** — sharpens a vague term for a purpose (the law deciding "adult = 18").
- **Ostensive** — pointing: "*that* is red."
- **Operational** — defined by a measuring procedure ("intelligence is what the test measures").
- **Theoretical/real** — claims to state the *essence* ("water is H₂O").
- **Persuasive** — smuggles a value in under cover of neutrality ("*real* freedom is…").

So "definition" is *itself* a family-resemblance word with no shared essence — which
means defining definition fails by its own standard before we even start. That's not a
clever paradox to be cleared away; it's the first real datum. The thing we reach for to
fix meaning is itself unfixed.

## Why the classical model breaks

The dream is Aristotle's: a definition states genus plus differentia — the necessary and
sufficient conditions for membership. "A triangle is a three-sided polygon." Clean,
eternal, true. And for *invented* concepts — mathematical, legal, technical — it works,
because we **stipulated** them into existence and own the boundary.

It fails for almost everything else. Wittgenstein's hammer-blow was the word *game*:
board games, ball games, solitaire, ring-a-ring-o'-roses, war games. Try to state the
feature common to all — competition? no (solitaire). skill? no (snakes and ladders).
fun? no (professional drudgery). There is no common thread; there is only "a complicated
network of similarities overlapping and criss-crossing" — *family resemblance*. The
concept is held together like a rope, whose strength comes from many overlapping fibres,
none of which runs the whole length.

Cognitive science later confirmed this empirically: we don't store definitions, we store
**prototypes** (Rosch). A robin is a "better" bird than a penguin; membership is
*graded*, boundaries are fuzzy, categories have centres rather than edges. And Waismann
added **open texture**: even a good definition can't anticipate every future case —
there's always a possible instance the rule never saw coming.

`★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────`
*Dharma is the paradigm case.* Across its life the same orthographic shell has carried:
"that which holds/supports" (the root √dhṛ), cosmic order, caste-and-role-specific duty
(*svadharma* — the warrior's dharma is to kill, the monk's is not to), law,
righteousness, the Buddha's teaching, *and* — in Abhidharma — the smallest momentary
atoms of experience (*dharmas*, plural). One word means both "ultimate reality" and "a
fleeting mental particle." No genus-differentia survives that. It is rope, not thread —
which is why DD's note 1.3 had to ask "what makes something *a dharma*" rather than
"what *is* dharma."
`─────────────────────────────────────────────────`

## Meaning isn't *in* the word — and that's why no one owns it

If definition is hard, it's because we keep looking for meaning in the wrong place. There
are really three rival locations, and the truth is that meaning is smeared across all
three:

**In the head.** The intuitive view (Locke): words are signs of private ideas. But Locke
himself saw the trap — if meaning is my private idea, I can never be sure yours matches,
and "the cheat of words" follows. Meaning can't be *merely* mental or communication
would be impossible.

**In the world.** Putnam and Kripke pushed the other way: "meaning ain't in the head."
Whether "water" means H₂O was settled by the world, not our beliefs; we defer to a
**division of linguistic labour** (I use "elm" correctly without being able to tell an
elm from a beech — I'm trusting botanists). Reference gets fixed by an initial baptism
and a causal chain of transmission, not by descriptions in anyone's mind.

**In use, within a system.** Wittgenstein: "the meaning of a word is its use in the
language." Saussure: a sign has no positive content — its value is purely its
*difference* from neighbouring signs (which is why "*mouton*" ≠ "sheep/mutton" and
translation always leaks). Firth: "you shall know a word by the company it keeps."

The upshot is that meaning is *relational and social* — distributed across a community,
the world, and a whole web of contrasts. That is precisely why definition can never be a
private act of fixing: **no individual stands outside the practice with the authority to
freeze it.** Including us, with our corpus.

## Meaning moves — and now it moves fast

Even if you pinned a word today, it would slip. Historical linguistics catalogues the
drift: broadening ("dog" once meant one breed), narrowing ("meat" was any food),
amelioration and pejoration ("nice" went *ignorant → foolish → precise → pleasant*;
"awful" went *awe-inspiring → terrible*), metaphor, bleaching, metonymy. "Literally" now
also means its opposite. And the internet has collapsed the timescale from centuries to
months — "viral," "based," "woke," "cancel" mutated in a few years, often into contested
or reversed senses.

Lexicography long since surrendered to this. Since the *Webster's Third* controversy of
1961, serious dictionaries are openly **descriptive, not prescriptive** — the OED is a
*history* of meanings, not a verdict on the right one. The authority everyone imagines
fixes words has publicly admitted it only photographs a river.

*Dharma* is a three-thousand-year time-lapse of this: "to hold" → cosmic order
(inheriting *ṛta*) → social duty → the Buddha's *Dhamma* → Jain *dharmāstikāya* (a
literal *substance*, the medium of motion!) → Western "Buddhism, the Dharma" →
Batchelor's secular dharma → this project's "dharma as any ethical operating system."
Each step is a real semantic event, lawful in mechanism, unpredictable in direction.

## Definition is an act of power — and of forgetting

Two more things the textbooks underplay.

First, **to define is to forget.** Nietzsche: every concept arises "through the equation
of the unequal" — no two leaves are identical, yet "leaf" discards their differences.
Borges dramatised the limit in *Funes the Memorious*, who remembered every instant so
vividly he couldn't form a single general concept — for him "dog at 3:14 seen from the
side" and "dog at 3:15 from the front" were unrelated. Abstraction *requires* throwing
information away. A definition is a useful, deliberate impoverishment.

Second, **to define is to exercise power.** Drawing a boundary includes and excludes, and
the boundary-drawer holds symbolic authority (Bourdieu's "power to name"; Foucault on
discourse). Watch how much hangs on the contested definition of "marriage," "refugee,"
"terrorist," "person," "woman" — these aren't lexical curiosities, they're battlegrounds
because the definition allocates rights and obligations. Gallie called such terms
**essentially contested concepts**: ones where endless, rational disagreement about the
correct use is *part of the concept itself*, not a flaw to be fixed. "Democracy,"
"justice," "art" — and yes, "dharma," whose varṇa-bound senses encoded a whole social
hierarchy. DD's note 7.3 (dharma & power) is this point in the specific.

## So what does this mean for us — and for the machines?

Here's where it turns from a seminar into the actual problem, because the DD corpus
exists to ground future LLMs in human ethics, and this names the obstacle in its purest
form.

`★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────`
An LLM is, secretly, the most thoroughgoing anti-essentialist about meaning ever built.
It learns words **distributionally** — purely from the company they keep — which is
Firth, Saussure, and the use-theory implemented in linear algebra. An embedding *is* a
Saussurean value: a position defined entirely by contrast with every other position. The
model holds *no definitions at all*, only usage geometry. So when DD worried (note 8.4)
about "flattening plural dharmas into a single machine-readable ethic," the danger is
concrete and mathematical: training **averages over usage**, and averaging collapses the
long tail — minority and dissenting senses get pulled toward the majority sense (the
documented "model collapse" / mode-seeking). The machine doesn't *suppress* the plural
meaning; it *means it away*.
`─────────────────────────────────────────────────`

That's why the methodology this project settled on is the philosophically correct
response to the problem of definition — and it arrived there almost by instinct. Faced
with an essentially contested, drifting, family-resemblance word, the wrong move is to
*define* dharma; the right move is to **map its uses, with their contexts, provenance,
and tensions preserved** — which is exactly what 32 cited notes do instead of one
authoritative entry. We built a *descriptive lexicon of a practice*, not a *real
definition of an essence*. That instinct (don't resolve to a verdict; hold the plurality;
disclose the wager) is the only honest stance once you accept that meaning lives in use.

Which leaves the genuinely useful reframing. The classical question — "what does *dharma
really* mean?" — has no answer and never did. The tractable question is the **pragmatic**
one: *for what work, for whom, do we need to stabilise this word, and what plurality do
we destroy by doing so?* A definition is not a truth-claim about an essence; it is a
**tool**, judged like any tool by fitness for a purpose, and every act of defining is a
wager that the coordination gained is worth the difference forgotten. Maps, not
territory; and you choose the map by where you intend to walk.

The danger, with words like *dharma* — and with any ethics we hand to a machine — is
forgetting that we drew the map at all, and mistaking our convenient stipulation for the
shape of the world.
