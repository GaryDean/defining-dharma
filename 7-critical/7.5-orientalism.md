# 7.5 Orientalism

**Question:** What is the concept of Orientalism (especially Edward Said's) — its core thesis, intellectual roots and method, and the major critiques of it? How does it bear on the study, translation and appropriation of "dharma" and "Eastern wisdom", and on this project's own standing as a Western-curated, secular corpus of "world dharmas"?

## Synthesis

Edward Said's *Orientalism* (1978) argues that "the Orient" is not a place encountered but a Western discursive construct — produced and managed through a dense apparatus of scholarship, literature and administration, and inseparable from imperial power [S11]. Said folds three senses into the term: an academic discipline, a "style of thought" resting on an ontological divide between Orient and Occident, and a "corporate institution" for dominating the East [S11]. His method grafts Foucault's discourse theory onto Gramsci's cultural hegemony, which is why he holds that the West keeps a "flexible positional superiority" even when individual scholars are sympathetic [S11]. The book is contested on every front — for selective evidence (Lewis, Kerr, Irwin), for a Foucault/humanism incoherence (Clifford, Ahmad), and for essentialising "the West" in the very gesture that condemns essentialising "the East" (al-'Azm) [S14][S15][S16]. For dharma this matters twice over: the categories "Buddhism," "Hinduism" and even "world religions" were partly nineteenth-century Western inventions [S4][S17], and the secular, text-centred, "rational" Buddhism the West admires is itself a colonial-era co-production [S6][S17]. The sting for this project is that a secular, Western-academic corpus cataloguing "world dharmas" for eventual machine encoding is *structurally* the kind of knowledge-power apparatus Said described — a risk the corpus's own lean makes acute, and which only explicit reflexivity (not good intentions) can answer [S9].

## Findings

### Said's thesis: the Orient as construct

Said's *Orientalism* was published by Pantheon Books in 1978 [S11]. Its claim is not that Western writers were rude about the East but that European culture "was able to manage — and even produce — the Orient politically, sociologically, militarily, ideologically, scientifically, and imaginatively" (p. 3), such that knowledge "in a sense creates the Orient" (p. 40) [S11]. The Orient is a textual space stabilised by representation rather than a region described from life — what Said calls the "textual attitude" [S11]. He distinguishes three overlapping senses (pp. 2–3): Orientalism as an academic field ("anyone who teaches, writes about, or researches the Orient"); as "a style of thought based upon an ontological and epistemological distinction made between 'the Orient' and … 'the Occident'"; and as "the corporate institution for dealing with the Orient … a Western style for dominating, restructuring, and having authority over the Orient" [S11]. To explain how this endured across centuries he separates "latent" Orientalism — an almost unconscious, stable deep structure of assumed Eastern backwardness and passivity — from "manifest" Orientalism, the variable explicit scholarship of any given era; only the latter changes (pp. 201–211) [S11].

The corpus's own clearest statement of Said gets the substance right but the attribution wrong, and the error is worth quarantining. A lecture transcript defines Orientalism as "the tendency of thought in which the so-called East is portrayed as Europe's other," distinguishes a romanticising "positive Orientalism" from the denigrating kind, and offers Kerouac's *The Dharma Bums* as a dharma-specific case of the former [S1]. But the same source calls Said "the philosopher Edward Said" who "coined" the term — both claims false. Said was a literary and cultural critic (University Professor of English and Comparative Literature at Columbia), not a philosopher, and "Orientalism" long predated him as a label for Western scholarship on the East; his 1978 book *redefined and popularised* it as a critical concept rather than inventing the word [S11].

### Roots and method (Foucault, Gramsci, precursors)

Said's method is explicitly Foucauldian: he adopts the notion of *discourse* from Foucault's *Archaeology of Knowledge* and *Discipline and Punish*, and the *episteme* from *The Order of Things*, to treat Orientalism as a power-saturated system that constitutes its object rather than a neutral tradition — "knowledge of subject races … is what makes their management easy" (p. 36) [S11]. He then borrows Gramsci's *cultural hegemony* to explain Orientalism's durability: it operates through consent secured in civil society (universities, presses, literature) rather than coercion, and so produces a "flexible positional superiority" that "puts the Westerner in a whole series of possible relationships with the Orient without ever losing him the relative upper hand" (p. 7) [S11]. Crucially Said *modifies* Foucault: against the "death of the author," he insists on "the determining imprint of individual writers," naming sympathetic Orientalists (Massignon, Berque) a pure discourse theory could not accommodate [S11][S13]. He later defended the resulting eclecticism as deliberate — "*Orientalism* is theoretically inconsistent, and I designed it that way" (interview, *Edward Said: A Critical Reader*, ed. Sprinker, 1992) — and called the book "a partisan book, not a theoretical machine" in his 1994 Afterword [S13].

*Orientalism* was not without precursors, a point its critics press. Anouar Abdel-Malek's "Orientalism in Crisis" (*Diogenes* 11, no. 44, Winter 1963: 103–40) had already charged the field with treating the Orient as passive object and fixing peoples into essences ("homo Arabicus," "homo Sinicus"); Said cites it [S12]. A.L. Tibawi's "English-Speaking Orientalists" (*Islamic Quarterly* 8, 1964) launched a parallel Islam-focused critique, which Said cites only glancingly [S12]. Behind both stand the anti-colonial thinkers Frantz Fanon (the colonizer/colonized Manichean division) and Aimé Césaire, whom Labelle (2022) places among several formative influences on Said's decolonial formation — though Césaire is not directly cited in the 1978 text [S12].

### Orientalism and religion: constructing "the mystic East" and "dharma"

Postcolonial scholars of religion extended Said into precisely the terrain that concerns this project. Philip Almond's *The British Discovery of Buddhism* (1988) showed that "Buddhism" coalesced as a discrete object in the Victorian imagination, constituted largely as a textual corpus in European libraries rather than as living Asian practice [S17]. The corpus independently confirms the linguistic core of this: the term "Buddhism" was "an early-nineteenth century European invention" with "no equivalent in any Asian language," not in Pali, Sanskrit, Chinese or Tibetan [S4]. Richard King's *Orientalism and Religion* (1999) generalised the move — "Hinduism," "Buddhism" and "mysticism" are Western-shaped constructions, and "defining the nature of 'religion' remains a political exercise, even in a modern secular academic context" — while stressing that "Hinduism" was *co-produced* by Western Indologists and elite Brahmin reformers, not simply imposed [S17]. Tomoko Masuzawa's *The Invention of World Religions* (2005) argued that the very "world religions" taxonomy preserved European Christian universalism "in the language of pluralism," with Buddhism elevated partly by being reclassified as an "Aryan," Indo-European tradition [S17]. Donald Lopez (*Curators of the Buddha*, 1995; *Prisoners of Shangri-La*, 1998) traced how Buddhist studies "curated" its object under colonialism, privileging scripture over practice and trapping Tibetans in a Western "Shangri-La" fantasy [S17].

The concept "dharma" sits at the sharp end of this. Sir William Jones, founder of the Asiatic Society of Bengal (1784) and first European translator of the *Manusmriti* (1794), helped fix the flexible *Dharmaśāstra* tradition as static "Hindu law" for colonial courts, displacing the living interpretive authority of pandits [S18]. The Monier-Williams *Sanskrit-English Dictionary* (1872/1899) — compiled under the Boden chair, endowed expressly to aid Christian conversion — offered dozens of inadequate glosses for *dharma* (law, duty, religion, righteousness, nature) but no equivalent; Geldner's Rig-Veda translation alone used some twenty renderings [S18]. That a single concept once translated by Ashoka into Greek as *eusebeia* (piety) could be compressed into the Western category "religion" is the kind of epistemic narrowing Said's heirs analyse — though, as King insists, the reframing of dharma as "religion" belongs more to the comparative-religion discourse than to Jones's juridical project alone, and was co-produced rather than unilaterally imposed [S17][S18]. David McMahan's *The Making of Buddhist Modernism* (2008) and Gombrich and Obeyesekere's "Protestant Buddhism" (*Buddhism Transformed*, 1988) complete the picture: the rational, meditation-centred, science-friendly Buddhism the modern West receives is a hybrid co-created by Western Romantic-Protestant currents and Asian reformers under colonial pressure [S17]. The corpus shows this from inside: Batchelor names his own reading "a Protestant perspective," and the Vipassana movement that seeded Western mindfulness was invented by anti-colonial Burmese reformers responding to British Christianity [S6]. Secular Buddhism's own "projection screen" problem is documented too — Kantians found Kant in the Dharma, positivists found Russell, "theosophists, fascists, environmentalists, and quantum physicists alike" each found themselves [S5] — and the romantic image of Buddhism as a "religion of nirvanic tranquillity" is flagged in the corpus as a "pious caricature" reinforcing "the colonial assumption that it took a European to shake up the passive Burmese" [S7].

### The major critiques and their force

Said's book drew sustained fire from sharply different camps, and the critiques are strong enough that the note must give them their due. The most persistent empirical charge — pressed by Bernard Lewis (*NYRB*, 24 June 1982; *Islam and the West*, 1993), Malcolm Kerr (*IJMES*, 1980) and Robert Irwin (*For Lust of Knowing* / *Dangerous Knowledge*, 2006) — is selective evidence: Said excluded German, Austro-Hungarian and Russian Orientalism (Nöldeke, Goldziher, Snouck Hurgronje), traditions that flourished without the British-French imperial nexus central to his thesis [S14][S15]. Lewis also alleged specific philological errors and called Said's claim that Silvestre de Sacy "doctored" texts a libel; Irwin called the book "malignant charlatanry" [S14][S15]. (Lewis carries his own freight: he coined "clash of civilisations" and advised the Iraq war hawks, and Said's 2003 Preface named him and Fouad Ajami as suppliers of intellectual cover [S13][S15].)

From within critical theory, James Clifford (*History and Theory*, 1980) and Aijaz Ahmad (*In Theory*, 1992, "Orientalism and After," pp. 159–220) identified the canonical methodological paradox: Said deploys Foucault's anti-humanist discourse analysis while appealing to humanist authorial agency, which Clifford judged an unresolved incoherence rather than a strength [S15]. Sadik Jalal al-'Azm's "Orientalism and Orientalism in Reverse" (*Khamsin* 8, 1981) made the structurally sharpest left critique: by tracing Orientalism to Homer and treating "the Western mind" as a unified formation, Said himself slides into the "Ontological Orientalism" he set out to demolish — and al-'Azm named the mirror-image phenomenon (Arab nationalists and Islamists essentialising East and West with the value-signs reversed) as no less "reactionary, mystifying, ahistorical and anti-human" [S16]. Later book-length accountings by Daniel Martin Varisco (*Reading Orientalism*, 2007) and the polemicist Ibn Warraq (*Defending the West*, 2007) extended the charge that Said "rhetorically opposed but intellectually promoted" binary East/West thinking, and Vivek Chibber (*Catalyst*, 2020) argued Said's two theses — that imperialism produced Orientalism, and that a deep Orientalist mindset made imperialism possible — are mutually inconsistent [S15]. Ernest Gellner's Ottoman counter-example (armies at Vienna in 1683) questions the long-domination chronology [S15]. Even hostile reviewers conceded the book's importance: Kerr called it one that "in principle needed to be written," and it is widely credited with revolutionising Middle Eastern studies [S15].

### Afterlives: postcolonialism, self-orientalism, neo-orientalism

*Orientalism* seeded postcolonial studies, with Said, Spivak and Bhabha its "holy trinity" [S19]. Said himself extended the framework — *The Question of Palestine* (1979), *Covering Islam* (1981) and *Culture and Imperialism* (1993), where the "contrapuntal reading" method properly appears [S19]. (The common claim that *The Question of Palestine* was the *first* English work to centre the Palestinian case overstates it; Rodinson's *Israel: A Colonial-Settler State?* appeared in English in 1973 [S19].) The concept then mutated. Al-'Azm's "Orientalism in Reverse" (1981) named the inverted essentialism of the colonised [S16]; Lisa Lau's "re-orientalism" (*Modern Asian Studies*, 2009) named diasporic South Asian writers self-exoticising for Western markets; Arif Dirlik's "self-orientalization" named the same compliance more broadly [S19]. Post-9/11, "neo-Orientalism" (Gol, 2010) described the media-saturated re-racialisation of Islam [S19]. "Occidentalism" became a contested keyword across three programmes — Buruma and Margalit's anti-Western demonology (2004), Xiaomei Chen's counter-discourse in post-Mao China (1995), and Hasan Hanafi's earlier proposal for an academic "science of the West" (1991) [S19]. Linda Nochlin's "The Imaginary Orient" (1983) carried the critique into art history [S19]. On the dharma front the afterlife is commercial: Ron Purser's *McMindfulness* (2019) documents a multi-billion-dollar wellness industry that claims Buddhism's prestige while stripping its ethics, and Vijay Prashad's *The Karma of Brown Folk* (2000) analyses the Western *demand* for an essentialised, marketable "Eastern wisdom" [S20]. The Vatican supplied an external religious version of the same complaint: Cardinal Ratzinger's 1997 *L'Express* description of Western Buddhism as "spiritual autoeroticism" targeted a consumerist, solipsistic spirituality rather than cultural appropriation as such [S8].

### Reflexive: orientalism and THIS project

The Graeber-anchored corpus supplies a parallel critique of "the West" as an incoherent category — a colonial "Orientalism" footnote distinguishing "decadent" rival civilisations from improvable "savages," and a section on "the slipperiness of the Western eye" that mirrors Said's structure without citing him [S2] — reinforced by Quinn's argument that "Western Civilisation" was a nineteenth-century invention built on racialised mapping [S3]. The *prosocial* corpus adds the psychological substrate orientalism exploits: automatic essentialism (treating outgroups as homogeneous and immutable) and Fiske's "low-warmth/high-competence" stereotype — cold, devious, clannish — historically applied to merchant diasporas and mapping neatly onto the "inscrutable, crafty East" [S9]. None of these name Said; the postcolonial canon proper (Said's primary text, Bhabha, Spivak) is essentially absent from the corpus, reaching it only through anthropological and secular-Buddhist secondary channels [S10]. This is the project's own orientalist exposure made concrete. A secular, evolutionary, Western-academic corpus that catalogues "world dharmas" risks four things: *essentialising* dharma into a unified "Eastern ethics" genus; adopting a *connoisseur-collector* stance in which the Western researcher decides what counts as a dharma; *secular supersessionism*, treating religious dharmas as pre-scientific raw material for an evolutionary account; and *text-and-corpus privilege*, marginalising oral and living traditions exactly as colonial philology did. The project's mitigations are real but procedural: the non-privileging stance directly counters supersessionism, per-claim sourcing constrains the authoritative voice, and a mandatory bias section breaks the unreflective neutrality Said identified as Orientalism's dangerous core [S11]. Yet the working definition of dharma remains a Western analytic construct, and the ultimate aim — encoding dharma into machine training data ([[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]]) — makes the knowledge-to-power loop structurally explicit. The honest position is Said's own distinction: cross-cultural knowledge cannot escape some Orientalist residue, but the unreflective, power-serving variety can be fought, and is fought only when the residue is named in the notes themselves [S13].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

This is the most self-implicating note in the set, and the bias runs three ways. **First, by omission in the corpus.** Said's *Orientalism* is present only as a single (inaccurate) YouTube-transcript summary; Bhabha, Spivak, Almond, King, Masuzawa, Lopez and the entire critical apparatus had to be imported from the web [S1][S17]. The corpus reaches the topic through Graeber's anarchist anthropology and Batchelor's secular Buddhism — the very lenses most congenial to this project — so its native material tilts toward "exchange is normal, the West is incoherent" rather than toward the postcolonial critique that would indict the project itself [S2][S6]. **Second, the critiques of Said deserve their force and are not a rhetorical foil.** The German-Orientalism omission genuinely weakens the universal knowledge-power claim; the Foucault/humanism incoherence is a real fault Said conceded rather than resolved; and al-'Azm's charge that Said essentialises "the West" applies with equal weight to any project that speaks of "Western-secular framing" as a unit [S13][S15][S16]. Several of the web sources on the dharma-compression seam are advocacy-oriented rather than peer-reviewed; the specialist Indology (Olivelle, Halbfass) that would firmly ground the "dharma reduced to religion" claim was not directly consulted, and that claim should be treated as well-motivated but not fully verified [S18]. **Third, what remains contested.** Whether Said's framework even applies cleanly to South Asia (his focus was the Arab-Islamic Near East) is itself disputed; the extension to dharma is King's and Almond's, not Said's, and inherits the same Foucault/humanism tension [S15][S17]. The deepest unresolved question is not answered here: whether a reflexive, well-sourced, non-privileging apparatus genuinely escapes the trap or merely performs an awareness of it while continuing to install a Western-secular taxonomy as the ground on which future machines will "understand" dharma. The corpus cannot adjudicate that; only the conduct of Stage 2 can.
