# 7.7 Dharma by Decree: The Engineered-Dharma Failure Mode

**Question:** Dharmas can be built on purpose — the series' definition includes "named,
questioned, chosen, reformed, and even built from scratch," and Part 8 proposes doing exactly
that deliberately. What happens when this capacity is exercised by a state, at speed, against
the inherited substrate — the Jacobin cults, the New Soviet Man, the Khmer Rouge's Year
Zero? What distinguishes catastrophic construction from benign construction, and what licence
(and warning) does the answer leave for Part 8?

## Synthesis

The deliberate construction of dharmas has been tried at full state scale at least three
times in the modern era, and the record is a gradient of catastrophe ordered by how much of
the inherited substrate each attempt tried to erase. The Jacobins decreed a replacement
religion outright — the Cult of the Supreme Being, complete with festival, liturgy and a
rebuilt calendar — and it evaporated within months, dying with Robespierre seven weeks after
its inaugural festival [S6][S7][S8]. The Bolsheviks aimed deeper, at the human material
itself: Bukharin defended "compulsion in all its forms, beginning with summary execution" as
"a method of reworking the human material of the capitalistic epoch into Communist humanity"
— the New Soviet Man project, pursued for seventy years and abandoned [S2][S3]. The Khmer
Rouge ran the limit case: Year Zero, a total blank-slate reset that abolished money,
markets, schools, religion and the family within days of taking Phnom Penh, and killed
roughly a quarter of the population in under four years [S4][S9][S10]. The analytical spine
that unifies the three is James C. Scott's account of authoritarian high modernism: disaster
requires the *combination* of a rational blueprint for society, contempt for inherited
practical knowledge (mētis), an authoritarian state able to impose the design, and a
civil society too prostrate to resist [S1][S6]. The payload for this project is
double-edged. The warning: the failure mode is not *building* — every dharma was built
([[2.5-lifecycle-of-a-dharma]]) — but building with a boot, at speed, on a slate believed
blank. The licence: the same era supplies engineered dharmas that did not end in terror —
Ambedkar's Navayāna (renovation without a state), and the Communist Party of Australia,
which unmade its own dogma from the inside and dissolved itself ([[7.6-cpa-case]]) — so the
capacity Part 8 proposes to exercise is dangerous, not damned [A]. And one case sharpens the
diagnosis into something closer to a natural experiment: Tan Malaka held Scott's blueprint *and*
his contempt for the inherited substrate, yet lacked and refused the coercive state — and produced
a textbook rather than a killing field, isolating that third element as the decisive variable
[S11][A].

## Findings

### The Jacobin decree: a dharma by legislation, dead in a season

The French revolutionaries attempted the most literal version of dharma-construction on
record: a new religion by decree. On 7 May 1794 (18 Floréal Year II) the National
Convention, at Robespierre's urging, decreed the Cult of the Supreme Being — a deistic
civic religion intended to instil republican virtue and moral order, replacing Catholic
worship with public festivals honouring nature, reason and a distant creator [S7]. The
inaugural Festival of the Supreme Being (8 June 1794) drew hundreds of thousands to the
Champ de Mars: a procession led by Robespierre, a statue of Atheism burned in effigy, an
artificial mountain representing nature [S7]. The installation machinery was rebuilt from
scratch to match: the Republican calendar restarted time at Year I, renamed the months, and
replaced the seven-day week — with its Sunday — with a ten-day *décade* [S8]. Nor was this
the first attempt even within the Revolution: the rival, atheistic Cult of Reason
(Chaumette, the Hébertists) preceded it, and Robespierre destroyed its authors before
launching his own [S5][S7].

The failure was as fast as the construction. Distaste for the cult, and for Robespierre's
central position in it, is thought to have contributed to his fall; he went to the
guillotine on 28 July 1794 — seven weeks after the festival — and the cult lost all official
sanction and disappeared from public view with him [S7]. The calendar limped on until
Napoleon abolished it, effective 1 January 1806 [S8]. The lesson in the wreckage: the parts
of a dharma that take generations to install — the felt reality, the habitual observance,
the below-consciousness grip that Part 5 described — cannot be decreed in a season, and a
dharma that exists only as legislation dies with its legislator [A].

### The New Soviet Man: reworking the human material

The Bolshevik project went a layer deeper than the Jacobin: not new rituals for old people
but a new people. The corpus carries a primary, contemporaneous witness — the anarchist
Alexander Berkman, who supported the revolution and watched the party capture it. He
records the doctrine in its own words: Bukharin, "the foremost Communist theoretician,"
wrote that "Proletarian compulsion in all its forms, beginning with summary execution and
ending with compulsory labor is, however paradoxical it may sound, a method of reworking
the human material of the capitalistic epoch into Communist humanity" [S2]. Berkman's
framing supplies the diagnosis: the Bolsheviks "had no confidence in the people… no faith
in the initiative and creative ability of the toilers," distrusting them as a
"multi-colored mob which has to be forced into liberty" [S2]. The self-organising
revolution — peasants taking land, workers' soviets running factories, the co-operative
and committee life Berkman calls "the actual Revolution" — was precisely the mētis the
blueprint then suppressed [S2].

The corpus's Graeber material adds the aesthetic register of the same project: under
Stalin the state's aim became the creation of the "new man," with the leader as "the
individual heroic genius reshaping life itself according to a single aesthetic vision" —
and Todorov's generalisation that revolutionary remakers of humanity, once obliged to rely
on politicians, end with those politicians "inspired to carve out their own aesthetic
visions on the flesh and sinews of humanity," doing "absolutely terrible things" [S3]. The
same passage preserves the road not taken: Malevich's rival vision of the revolution as
*removing impediments* to an already-creative human nature, rather than reshaping that
nature — the in-corpus statement of the alternative premise on which non-catastrophic
construction rests [S3].

### Year Zero: the blank slate at its purest

The Khmer Rouge ran the limit case of the engineered dharma: not reform of the inherited
order but its total erasure. On 17 April 1975, the day Phnom Penh fell, the regime declared
Year Zero — a rebirth of Cambodian history from nothing [S9]. Within roughly 72 hours the
capital's two million inhabitants were emptied into the countryside at gunpoint, hospitals
included; money, markets, schools, religion and the family unit were abolished; banks were
dynamited, the worthless currency raining on the streets [S9]. The name and the first
documentation reached the world through François Ponchaud's *Cambodia: Year Zero* (1977),
built from 94 refugee interviews [S10]. The corpus's own witness is Paul Stange, who calls
it "a virtually incomprehensible effort to eliminate everything that had come from
outside," aiming "to eliminate cities, the middle class and Buddhist religious
institutions, to return the population to villages and concentrate the economy on rice
cultivation" — a drive he reads as both ideologically Maoist and a warped purification of
indigenous identity, "if as warped as Hitler's" [S4]. The cost of the purest blank slate
ever attempted: nearly two million dead, about one quarter of the population, in under four
years [S9]. The gradient across the three cases is the finding: the more of the inherited
substrate the builders treated as rubble, the faster and higher the body count [A].

### The diagnosis: Scott's four elements, and mētis

The unifying analysis is James C. Scott's *Seeing Like a State* (1998). The great disasters
of state-initiated social engineering require a combination of four elements: the
administrative ordering of nature and society (the state's simplifications); a
"muscle-bound" high-modernist confidence in the rational design of social order; "an
authoritarian state that is willing and able to use the full weight of its coercive power
to bring these high-modernist designs into being"; and "a prostrate civil society that
lacks the capacity to resist these plans" [S6]. What the blueprint destroys, on Scott's
account, is *mētis* — the local, practical, situational knowledge that actually keeps
societies running, which cannot be centralised or decreed [S6]. The corpus carries the
schema at second hand through Pinker: authoritarian high modernism "denied the existence
of human nature, with its messy needs for beauty, nature, tradition, and social intimacy,"
starting from a "clean tablecloth" on the theory that "mankind will be reborn" [S1].

Translated into this project's terms: the inherited cooperative substrate — the evolved
toolkit of Parts 1–5, and the accumulated cultural expression of it — *is* the mētis of
ethical life. The engineered-dharma catastrophe is what happens when a builder treats that
substrate as the obstacle rather than the material [A]. This is the exact inverse of the
'Ata case ([[8.5-ata-case]]), where six boys rebuilt a functioning micro-dharma precisely
*from* remembered materials — rostered work, ritualised dispute-resolution, song and prayer
— rather than from a blueprint [A].

### The counter-cases: construction without catastrophe

The failure mode does not condemn construction as such; the same modern era supplies
deliberate dharma-building that did not end in terror, and the differences map cleanly
onto Scott's elements [A]:

- **Ambedkar's Navayāna** (treated in [[7.3-dharma-and-power]] and Part 6's authority twin):
  a consciously *engineered* Buddhism — doctrine deliberately edited, karma excised — built
  by persuasion among volunteers, with no state, no coercive power, and a mobilised rather
  than prostrate community [A].
- **The Communist Party of Australia** ([[7.6-cpa-case]]): a party-form constructed dharma
  that ran the failure mode in reverse — cut its funding tie to Moscow (1967), condemned
  the invasion of Czechoslovakia (1968), democratised its practice, and ended by voting to
  dissolve itself (1991) rather than hardening. It demonstrates that an engineered ethical
  order can also *unbuild* its own dogma — the capacity the catastrophic cases
  conspicuously lacked [A].
- **Secular dharma itself** ([[8.3-post-traditional-dharmas]]): construction by voluntary,
  gradual, reversible adoption — renovation of inherited materials, not erasure of them [A].

The licence this leaves Part 8 is conditional: build, but without the boot — no coercive
enforcement, no contempt for the inherited substrate, no blank-slate premise, and the
power to unbuild kept in the hands of the built-for [A].

### The natural experiment: blueprint and contempt, minus the state (Tan Malaka)

The three benign cases above withhold *all* of Scott's dangerous elements, which makes them
weak controls: they show construction can be safe when nothing about it is high-modernist, but
they cannot isolate *which* element does the damage. The Indonesian revolutionary Tan Malaka is
the sharper test, because he supplies the first two elements and pointedly lacks the third. On
Oliver Crawford's study of his thought, Tan Malaka's *Madilog* (1943) was an explicit
mentality-reconstruction programme — "the declared aim of this book was to educate the Indonesian
people in correct methods of thought" [S11] — resting on the full high-modernist premise,
including contempt for the inherited substrate: Crawford documents Tan Malaka's view that the
Indonesian mind "remained trapped in 'mystical' patterns of thought" and that Indonesia lacked an
indigenous tradition of "logical analysis and scientific investigation," with the Hindu-Buddhist
inheritance blamed for a "backward mentality" [S11]. That is Scott's blueprint (element 1) plus
high-modernist confidence and mētis-contempt (element 2) in full.

What he did *not* have — and, decisively, *refused* — was Scott's third element, the coercive
state. *Madilog* spread not by decree but as a manuscript circulated among the *pemuda* in
1943–46 [S11]; and across his career Tan Malaka consistently countermanded coercive shortcuts,
opposing the premature 1926–27 PKI insurrection and later condemning the 1948 Madiun uprising as
a repetition of "the folly of 1926" [S11]. Blueprint contempt for the inherited mentality,
*minus* the coercive apparatus to impose it, produced a textbook, not a killing field. This is the
case the other three cannot supply: it isolates Scott's third element as the decisive variable —
the boot, not the blueprint or the contempt, is what turns engineered dharma catastrophic [A].

▲ Benign in *means*, not sanitised in *content*. Crawford also documents the racialised "*bangsa*"
theory in Tan Malaka's thought and the expansionist "Aslia" (Asia-Australia) fantasy [S11], and
characterises his growing self-image as "a kind of revolutionary prophet" — the much-repeated
"Jesus left the Bible, Mohamad the Koran, and I leave only Madilog" is one Crawford himself relays
only as what Tan Malaka "is supposed to have said," so it is reported here at that remove, not as
established fact [S11]. The case feeds, rather than answers, the note's standing caveat that no one
knows what any of these builders would have become with an army [A].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The corpus's witnesses are all anti-authoritarian.** The in-corpus sources on the
  Soviet case are anarchists (Berkman, Graeber) and a liberal rationalist (Pinker) — the
  corpus's curated lean supplies no sympathetic account of the constructivist projects,
  and no Marxist-Leninist defence of them. The verdict here (catastrophe) is also the
  overwhelming verdict of the external record, so the lean does not distort the
  conclusion; but the *texture* — what it felt like to believe in the New Soviet Man — is
  underrepresented, and a fuller treatment would draw on sympathetic primary voices. The Tan
  Malaka case rests on a single scholarly source (Crawford's dissertation [S11]); it is used here
  only for the structural point (blueprint + contempt, minus state) and would benefit from
  corroboration (Benedict Anderson's *Java in a Time of Revolution* is in the corpus and confirms
  the 1926 countermand), and Crawford is scholarship *about* Tan Malaka — his characterisations are
  not Tan Malaka's own words.
- **Scott's schema is itself contested.** Critics note that *Seeing Like a State* undercounts
  high-modernist successes (public health, universal schooling, the green revolution's
  gains) and that "mētis" can romanticise local knowledge that is itself oppressive.
  The note uses the four-element combination as a diagnostic for *catastrophe*, not as a
  general indictment of planning [A].
- **The gradient claim is an ordering, not a measurement.** "More erasure, faster
  catastrophe" fits Jacobins → Soviets → Khmer Rouge, but three cases cannot establish a
  law; confounds (war, invasion, famine, external pressure — American bombing in the
  Cambodian case, which Stange himself flags [S4]) contributed to each collapse and are
  not partialled out [A].
- **The counter-cases are smaller in every dimension.** Navayāna, the CPA and secular
  dharma operated at community-to-party scale without state power — which is the point
  (Scott's third element withheld), but it also means the comparison is not
  scale-controlled: no one knows what any of them would have become with an army [A].
- **Quotation provenance.** The Bukharin and Stange quotations are from full in-corpus
  primary texts [S2][S4]; the Scott four-element wording is carried via the cited external
  summaries rather than a full-text read of *Seeing Like a State* [S6]; the Pinker
  paraphrase of Scott is in-corpus [S1].
