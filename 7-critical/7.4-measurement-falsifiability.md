# 7.4 Measurement & Falsifiability

**Question:** Can the efficacy of a dharma be empirically assessed? What would count as evidence that one dharmic framework 'works' better than another — and what does answering this set as the evidentiary bar for this project itself?

## Synthesis
Efficacy splits in two, and keeping the halves apart is the whole answer. **Instrumental
efficacy** — does a dharma achieve its *stated* ends (wellbeing, cooperation, group survival)?
— is genuinely measurable, and the cultural-evolution literature already measures it: Big Gods
that scaled stranger-cooperation [S1][S7], practices that modestly raise wellbeing
([[5.1-neurological-basis]]), communes that outlast their secular twins
([[5.2-evolutionary-psychology]]). But **whether those ends are the right ends** is *not*
empirically decidable: it runs into the is/ought wall ([[3.3-grounding-problem]]), and every
candidate yardstick — flourishing/capabilities [S6], harm-reduction, non-domination
([[7.3-dharma-and-power]]), the expanding circle — is itself a normative commitment, not a
finding. Sam Harris's attempt to close that gap by science fails on its own terms (it
*presupposes* that good = wellbeing) [S3], and the corpus's own analysis shows the
universal-welfare measure goes non-operational under coalitional conflict [S2]. Three further
limits bite: **fitness ≠ goodness** (a dharma can succeed by being good at *spreading*, not at
being good); the measurable evidence is itself shaky (replication crisis, WEIRD samples)
[S5]; and metaphysical claims are unfalsifiable (Popper) — but so are the *value-axioms* of
secular dharmas, so falsifiability does not cleanly favour the secular [S4]. The honest bar
for this project follows: DD can establish **provenance** and report **instrumental** efficacy
where evidence exists, but it must not pretend to empirically crown a "best" dharma — that is a
normative argument to be made transparently, with its wagers named, not disguised as a result.

## Findings

### What can be measured: instrumental efficacy
A dharma's success *at its own stated task* is open to ordinary social science. The clearest
case is functional/cultural-evolutionary fitness: a corpus source lays out Ara Norenzayan's
thesis that religion supplied "group cohesion and large-scale cooperation," with "a common
belief in divine supervision and punishment… [that] can strongly influence cooperation, even
between distant strangers," solving the scaling problem when agriculture pushed humans beyond
face-to-face groups [S1]. Norenzayan's "Big Gods" — "morally interested gods with an
observational reach beyond an immediate locality" — are selected precisely because they grow
cooperation and group size [S7]; this is measurable by cohesion, group size, and longevity
(cf. Sosis's communes, [[5.2-evolutionary-psychology]]). Wellbeing and health outcomes are
likewise measurable, with the bounded, modest effects catalogued in
[[5.1-neurological-basis]]. So "does this dharma work?" has a real empirical answer — *once you
fix what "work" means*.

### The fitness trap: success at spreading is not success at being good
The cultural-evolution metric measures the wrong thing for the evaluative question. Fitness
tracks how good a dharma is at *cohering and propagating*, not whether its ends are worth
having. A maximally "successful" dharma — one that maximises group size, retention, and
transmission — could achieve that through fear, conquest, or in-group/out-group hostility
([[7.1-failure-modes]]); it could be, in effect, a successful parasite. Transmission success
and moral worth are orthogonal, so even a perfectly executed fitness measurement cannot tell
us which framework "works *better*" in the sense the question intends.

### The is/ought wall blocks the deep measurement
This is why the deepest comparison is not empirical. You can measure *efficacy-toward-X*; you
cannot empirically establish *that X is good* — the is/ought gap ([[3.3-grounding-problem]]).
"Works better" therefore always presupposes a standard, and each proposed standard is a value
choice in disguise: Nussbaum and Sen's **capabilities approach** offers "a universal measure of
human flourishing while… respecting religious and cultural differences," but Nussbaum's list is
grounded in an Aristotelian conception of dignity and Sen's in freedom — each a normative
framework, not a neutral instrument [S6]. Choosing the yardstick smuggles in a dharma
([[3.4-relativism-universalism]]).

### The Sam Harris foil: can science cross the wall?
Sam Harris's *The Moral Landscape* makes the strongest contrary case: treat "the well-being of
conscious creatures" as peaks and valleys on a measurable landscape, and science can, in
principle, tell us "what we ought to do" — explicitly challenging Hume [S3]. The objection is
decisive and double. Philosophically, Harris "is no more successful in deriving 'ought' from
'is' than anyone," because his science "presupposes… that what is good is that which supports
well-being of conscious creatures" — the value is assumed, not discovered [S3]. And the corpus
supplies the operational version of the same point: the welfare-of-all-conscious-beings rule
works only "where there are no conflicts of interest between competing groups," but "a primary
reason people cooperate in groups is so they can compete more effectively against other
groups, and moral disputes tend to arise out of these coalitional conflicts" — in the loggers
-vs-hikers case, "you can't resolve moral debates by identifying the solution that would
benefit all conscious beings, because that won't be the goal for which either side… will be
fighting" [S2]. The universal measure dissolves exactly where morality is actually contested.

### The measurable part is shakier than it looks
Even instrumental claims deserve humility. The Open Science Collaboration's 2015 study found a
**36% replication rate** across 97 psychology experiments [S5]; much contemplative and
positive-psychology evidence is underpowered, WEIRD-sampled ([[5.3-moral-psychology-development]]),
and publication-biased, and key structural neuroscience claims failed to replicate
([[5.1-neurological-basis]]). So the "evidence-based" confidence often attached to dharmic
efficacy should be discounted accordingly.

### Falsifiability cuts both ways
Popper's demarcation holds that a claim is scientific only if it is "incompatible with possible
empirical observations"; a theory "compatible with all such observations is unscientific"
(though, Popper insisted, not therefore meaningless) [S4]. Metaphysical dharmic claims — karma,
rebirth, God — are typically unfalsifiable. But the move that matters here is reflexive: the
*core value-axioms of secular dharmas* ("flourishing is good," "reduce suffering") are **also**
unfalsifiable, because they are values, not hypotheses. Both religious and secular dharmas thus
have an unfalsifiable core; what is falsifiable in either is only their *instrumental* claims.
And recall from [[5.2-evolutionary-psychology]] that the unfalsifiable anchor is itself
functionally adaptive (a commitment device) — so falsifiability is not straightforwardly a
virtue for a dharma's *working*, only for its honesty.

### The evidentiary bar for this project
Answering the question sets DD's own standard, in three tiers:

1. **Empirical/instrumental claims** — held to evidentiary standards: sourced, ideally
   replicated, reported with uncertainty, and discounted for the replication and WEIRD caveats
   [S5].
2. **Normative/interpretive claims** — named *as* normative, with the value-commitment made
   explicit (the function the "Tensions, gaps & corpus bias" section performs in every note).
3. **The project's own frame** — dharma-as-plural and the secular, non-privileging stance — is
   itself a normative *wager*, flagged as such, not a finding.

Two consequences. **Provenance ≠ truth:** the project's traceable-sourcing methodology is
necessary but not sufficient — a cited claim can still be false, and the corpus contains
AI-generated essays [A], so citing one is not confirming it (the reason every note fences such
material off). And the honest deliverable is **"here is the evidence, here are the
value-commitments, here is where they meet,"** never "science shows dharma X is best." A
multi-dimensional scorecard — wellbeing + prosociality + non-domination + sustainability,
assessed *net of failure rate* ([[7.1-failure-modes]]) — is buildable and useful, but the
*weighting across those dimensions is itself a value choice that must be declared*. This is the
bar the constructive Category 8 inherits, and especially [[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]],
where an unexamined weighting would be silently frozen into a model.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias
The corpus leans toward the **measurable, naturalistic** side of this question. It is stocked
with the science-of-cooperation literature (Norenzayan [S1], the ProSocial "evidence-based"
framing) and science-of-morality essays, so its native register is closer to Harris's optimism
than to Humean caution; it is primed to believe efficacy is measurable and to under-flag the
is/ought wall, which I had to import along with the philosophical brakes (Popper [S4], the
is/ought critique of Harris [S3], Nussbaum/Sen read as *normative* rather than empirical [S6]).
That said, the corpus is not naive: it contains its own correction — the essay showing Harris's
welfare rule fails under coalitional conflict [S2] — which is a genuine internal check.

Two deeper cautions. **First**, the replication crisis [S5] means the "evidence-based"
confidence the corpus projects (especially around contemplative practice and ProSocial's own
programmes, [[6.1-secular-translation]]) is weaker than presented — a systematic bias toward
over-claiming efficacy. **Second**, and most reflexively, *this note's own framing* — that
efficacy cleanly splits into a measurable instrumental half and an unmeasurable normative half
— is itself a philosophical position (a broadly Humean, naturalist one), not a neutral fact;
a strong moral-realist would contest the split, and Harris does [S3]. The note cannot escape
the very problem it describes: drawing the measurement boundary is already a normative act.
What remains genuinely unresolved is whether *any* non-question-begging universal yardstick
exists ([[3.4-relativism-universalism]]); if none does, "works better" is permanently
relative-to-a-chosen-good, and the project's non-privileging stance must own that it, too, is a
stance and not a neutral vantage.
