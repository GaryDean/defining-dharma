# 7.6 Dogma Undone: The CPA Case

**Question:** Constructed ethical-political orders — dharmas built on purpose — are usually
studied through their catastrophic failures (the Jacobins, the New Soviet Man, Year Zero). Is
there a documented counter-case: a party-form communist movement that *unmade* its own dogma
from the inside — de-aligned from its doctrinal centre, democratised its practice, and ended
by dissolving itself rather than hardening? The Communist Party of Australia (CPA) is the
candidate. This note fact-checks six claims about it (the author of this series was a member
in the late 1970s, and the preface characterises the party in these terms): (1) the CPA was
unique and completely independent of the Soviet Union; (2) the pro-Soviet line was instead
represented by the Socialist Party of Australia (SPA), which split from the CPA "in the early
60s"; (3) the pro-China line by the CPA (Marxist–Leninist), also a 1960s split; (4) the CPA
was a *moderating* force on the Australian Left, sometimes railing against "ultra-leftists"
within the ALP, particularly the Victorian branch; (5) its embeddedness in union-movement
leadership grounded it in reality and in a commitment to representative democracy and
diversity; (6) it folded and merged into the Greens around 1990. Which of these does the
external record support?

## Synthesis

The external record supports the author's characterisation **in substance while correcting it
in two particulars**. The CPA of the era he joined (1977) genuinely was independent of both
Moscow and Beijing: it stopped accepting Soviet money in 1967, condemned the Warsaw Pact
invasion of Czechoslovakia in August 1968, and expelled or shed its pro-Soviet wing by 1971 —
an unusually early Western break with Moscow, though "completely independent" is true only of
the post-1968 party, not of the loyally Stalinist CPA of 1920–1967, which had refused to
condemn the invasion of Hungary in 1956 [S2][S3][S8]. The two rival lineages are real but the
first date is wrong: the pro-China CPA (Marxist–Leninist) did split in the 1960s (Ted Hill,
founded March 1964), but the pro-Soviet SPA was formed on 4–5 December 1971, not the early
60s — and, in a closing irony, the SPA renamed *itself* the Communist Party of Australia in
1996, so today's "CPA" is the pro-Moscow lineage, not the author's party [S5][S6]. The
"moderating force" claim finds real, if contested, corroboration: a Communist-influenced ALP-left
Steering Committee backed the 1970 federal intervention against the "too radical" Victorian
branch, though a hostile analyst reads the motive as factional hegemony rather than maturity,
and the CPA's own pro-Soviet dissidents simultaneously denounced the Aarons leadership as
"ultraradicals" — moderation and ultra-leftism were epithets in motion, not fixed positions
[S4][S7]. The union embeddedness and the democratic turn are well documented (the Aarons-era
"new unionism," rank-and-file democracy, the Mundey BLF and the green bans) [S3][S9][S10].
The ending needs its correction: the CPA dissolved at its 31st Congress on 2–3 March 1991
into the short-lived New Left Party (disbanded 1992), with its assets passing to the SEARCH
Foundation; there was **no organisational merger with the Greens**, but the migration of
ex-members into the Greens — Jack Mundey among them — is real and documented, which is
presumably what the compressed memory records [S2][S10][S11][S12].

## Findings

*The shared corpus is silent on Australian communist history — retrieval returns only this
project's own note 0.2 and unrelated material — so, unusually for a DD note, every substantive
finding below rests on external sources [S1].*

### Claim 1 — "Unique and completely independent of the Soviet Union": ✓ for the party he joined; ✗ as a whole-history claim

The independence is real and datable. In 1967 the CPA ceased receiving payments from the
Communist Party of the Soviet Union, following general secretary Laurie Aarons's argument in
the Soviet Union itself that "ideas require free contest, not confined in a framework of
established dogmas" [S2]. When Warsaw Pact tanks entered Czechoslovakia in August 1968 the
party condemned the invasion officially — Aarons had welcomed Dubček's Prague Spring, and at
the 1969 Moscow conference of world communist parties he delivered a speech strongly critical
of the invasion and of Soviet policy under Brezhnev generally [S2][S3]. The CPA's published
reading of the invasion was structural, not incidental: evidence that within the Soviet system
"bureaucracy has developed, and with it an overconcentration of power" [S3]. The pro-Soviet
faction (Edgar Ross, Alf Watt) was expelled in September 1970, and the remaining pro-Moscow
unionists walked in 1971 [S3]. Cutting the money in 1967 — *before* the invasion — and
breaking publicly in 1968 places the CPA among the earliest Western communist parties to break
with Moscow, several years ahead of the mid-1970s West-European "Eurocommunist" wave it is
usually grouped with [A].

The fence matters, though. The CPA of 1920–1967 was the opposite of independent: founded in
Sydney on 30 October 1920, affiliated to the Comintern from 1921 to 1943, opposed to
Australia's war effort during the Molotov–Ribbentrop period as "a war between imperialist
nations," suppressing internal discussion of Khrushchev's Secret Speech in 1956, and refusing
to condemn the Soviet invasion of Hungary that year — a loyalty that cost it a wave of
resignations and roughly a quarter of its membership [S2][S8][S9]. So the claim is true of the
party the author actually joined in 1977, and false projected backwards [A]. "Unique" also
wants softening to "unusually early and thoroughgoing": the CPA's trajectory paralleled, and
anticipated, the broader Eurocommunist family rather than standing wholly outside it [A].

### Claim 2 — The SPA as the Soviet-aligned rival, "splitting off in the early 60s": ✓ substance, ✗ date

The Socialist Party of Australia was indeed the pro-Soviet party — but it was formed on
**4–5 December 1971**, not in the early 1960s [S3][S5]. It emerged from the cumulative
conflicts of 1968–71: the Czechoslovakia condemnation, the September 1970 expulsions, and Pat
Clancy's resignation in October 1971; its founding resolution condemned the CPA's "departure
from Marxist theory and practice" [S3][S4]. Its leadership was a roll-call of veteran union
officials — Clancy (NSW secretary of the Building Workers' Industrial Union, ACTU executive)
as chairman, Peter Symon (Port Adelaide Waterside Workers' Federation) as general secretary
[S3][S5]. The likely source of the date error is compression of the two splits into one
decade: the *pro-China* split really was the early-to-mid 60s (Claim 3) [A]. A detail worth
recording: in 1996 the SPA renamed itself the Communist Party of Australia, making the
pro-Moscow lineage the legal bearer of the name today [S2][S5].

### Claim 3 — The CPA (M-L) as the pro-China 1960s split: ✓

Confirmed. When the Sino-Soviet split opened, Ted Hill — leader of the party's pro-China
faction and a Victorian party heavyweight — sided with Beijing while the CPA majority stayed
with Moscow; he was expelled (1961 by one account, 1963 by another — see Tensions) and in
March 1964 founded the Communist Party of Australia (Marxist–Leninist) at a conference of
roughly two hundred, taking many militant Victorian members with him [S2][S6]. The CPA
leadership's own path here was wobblier than the clean version suggests: Lance Sharkey
flirted with the Chinese position after visits to China in 1959 and 1961 before swinging the
party back behind Moscow at the November 1961 Moscow conference [S8].

### Claim 4 — A moderating force, "railing against ultra-leftists in the ALP, particularly the Victorian branch": ✓ partially corroborated, interpretation contested

There is a documented instance of exactly the configuration the author describes. In the
lead-up to the October 1970 federal intervention that sacked the Victorian ALP executive (the
crucible of the Hartley-era Socialist Left), the ALP-left Steering Committee — which the
historian Corey Oakley describes as operating under Communist Party influence and on Popular
Front unity principles — *backed the intervention*: "The real reason the Steering Committee
backed intervention was the same reason Whitlam did: the Victorian Party was too radical"
[S7]. That is the CPA's orbit siding against Victorian ultra-leftism, on the record. The
contest is over motive: Oakley, writing from the party's left, reads it not as maturity but
as "a threat to their claim to hegemony over the ALP left" [S7]. Both readings fit the same
facts; "the adult in the room" and "the incumbent defending its franchise" are the
sympathetic and hostile descriptions of one behaviour [A].

Two complications keep the claim honest. First, "ultra-left" was a contested epithet, not a
fixed address: at the very moment the CPA's orbit was disciplining Victorian radicals, the
party's own pro-Soviet dissidents were resigning over the leadership's perceived
"ultraradicals" and Trotskyist influence [S4]. Second, the CPA's moderation was political and
strategic — coalition-of-the-left, broad alliances, parliamentary democracy — not industrial:
the same party championed the militant Mundey BLF as its "new concept of unionism" [S3][S10].
Moderate in method and pluralism, militant in industrial practice: both are documented, and
the claim holds if it means the former [A].

### Claim 5 — Union embeddedness as ballast; commitment to representative democracy and diversity: ✓ in substance, with the causal story an interpretation

The embeddedness is beyond dispute. At its 1945 peak the party had slightly above 22,000
members and controlled major unions — ironworkers, miners, waterside workers [S2]; into the
1970s its unionists included Laurie Carmichael and John Halfpenny in the metalworkers and
Jack Mundey's NSW Builders Labourers' Federation [S9][S10]. The democratic turn is equally
documented: the Aarons leadership's "new unionism" emphasised rank-and-file workplace
democracy and workers' control against Clancy's top-down model [S3][S4]; Mundey's BLF ran on
rank-and-file democracy and limited tenure of office, and its green bans (1971–74) fused
union power with resident action groups, heritage and environmental campaigns — the
diversity-of-alliances politics the author remembers [S10]. Whether the unions *caused* the
grounding is less clean: the party's most embedded union heavyweights — Clancy's building
workers, the pro-Soviet union officials who founded the SPA — went the *other* way in 1971,
so union embeddedness per se did not uniformly moderate; it was the Aarons-era political
leadership that chose the democratic course, with part of the union base and against another
part [S3][A].

### Claim 6 — "Folded and merged into the Greens Party in ~1990": ◐ half right

The folding is right to within a year; the merger is not what happened. In 1990 the party
established the SEARCH Foundation to preserve its assets and legacy "to further democratic
socialist and progressive aims," and at its 31st Congress in Sydney on 2–3 March 1991 the
CPA dissolved itself into the New Left Party, a deliberately broader formation that failed to
attract the hoped-for membership and disbanded in 1992 [S2][S12]. There was no organisational
merger with the Greens. What *did* happen — and what the compressed memory presumably records
— is a documented migration of people: ex-CPA and Labor-left members, many shaped by the
green-ban alliance politics, joined the Greens NSW (formed as a party in 1991), with the New
Left Party interlude acting as a bridge; Jack Mundey himself, a CPA member until the 1991
dissolution and its president in the 1970s, later joined the Australian Greens [S10][S11].
The distinction matters for Stage-2 use: "the CPA's people and green-alliance politics flowed
into the Greens" is defensible; "the CPA merged into the Greens" is not [A]. The formulation
that best fits the documented sequence: the party dissolved itself *by fits and starts* into
the new paradigm — the SEARCH Foundation to hold the legacy, the New Left Party as the failed
bridge, and then the migration of its people and its alliance politics into the Greens — an
ending read most naturally as response to a changed world (the waning of union influence from
the late 1980s) rather than as simple defeat [A][S2][S11][S12].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The corpus is silent; the note is external by necessity.** The shared
  `seculardharma`/`appliedanthropology` corpus returns nothing on Australian communist
  history beyond this project's own note 0.2 reflected back [S1]. The project's
  knowledgebase-primary policy is here inverted by necessity — exactly the "web-supplemented
  where the corpus is thin" case the source policy anticipates.
- **The historiography has its own leans.** Most accessible sources are labour-history and
  Marxist publications. The one directly corroborating source for the Victorian claim
  (Oakley) writes from a *further-left* position hostile to the CPA's popular-front politics,
  and reads the same behaviour the author calls moderation as factional self-interest [S7];
  sympathetic institutional sources (SEARCH Foundation, the ANU exhibition) read the same
  arc as maturation [S4][S12]. The note records the behaviour as fact and the motive as
  contested.
- **A primary-source date discrepancy.** Hill's expulsion is given as 1961 in the general CPA
  article and 1963 in the biographical one [S2][S6]; the March 1964 founding of the CPA (M-L)
  is solid in both. Not resolved here; immaterial to the claim being checked.
- **The author's two errors are ordinary memory compression, not confabulation.** Placing the
  SPA split in the "early 60s" merges the two schisms into one decade; "merged into the
  Greens" compresses dissolution-plus-migration into a single event. Both have a true claim
  standing immediately behind them [A]. The characterisation needs no retraction, only these
  two corrections when drawn on in Stage-2 prose.
- **Privacy boundary maintained.** Per the project's standing constraint, this note checks
  the *organisation's* history only; nothing here searches or corroborates the author's
  personal role, which remains self-reported by design in the project's private Part-0
  research notes (note 0.2, Politics & Anarchism — not published).
- **Tooling caveat.** Web verification was done via fetched page extracts (accessed July
  2026), not full-text reads of the underlying books (Macintyre's histories, Mallory's full
  paper). Quotations are as carried on the cited pages.
