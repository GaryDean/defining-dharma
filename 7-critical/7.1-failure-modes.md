# 7.1 Failure Modes

**Question:** What are the common failure modes of dharmic systems? How do they become corrupted, co-opted by power structures, or turn exclusionary?

## Synthesis
Dharmic systems fail in patterned, recurring ways, and the first methodological point is
that these failures are *internal* to dharma, not external corruptions to be waved away
with a "no true Buddhist/Christian" move: religions are internally diverse, and the
hate-preaching monk is as authentic an expression of Buddhism as the peace activist — "peace
or violence… cannot be inherently baked in" [S1]. The failure modes cluster into four
families. **(1) Form displaces function:** the dharma ossifies into doctrine, ritual, or
commodity while its transformative core dies (Weber's routinization of charisma; the
professionalisation of compassion; spiritual bypassing) [S3][S4][S7]. **(2) The boundary
turns lethal:** the in-group cohesion that *makes* a dharma work curdles into exclusion and
sacred violence — on the parochial-altruism reading, the same machinery aimed outward [S2][S6];
though, as the Findings show, whether the inward and outward faces are really *one* mechanism is
empirically contested [S11]. **(3) Capture by
power:** the dharma becomes legitimation ideology for domination, or is seized by a
charismatic authoritarian (Marx's "opium"; cults and totalism) [S5][S8]. **(4) The
self-undermining success:** the dharma's own virtues turn against its ends (moral licensing,
hypocrisy, purity spirals) [S9]. Reading the four together, the deep pattern I draw is that
**the very mechanisms that make a dharma effective — strong identity, costly commitment,
transcendent authority, in-group trust — are the mechanisms that, unchecked, make it
dangerous**, which would make failure-proneness intrinsic rather than incidental [A]. That
thesis is strongest for Clusters 1, 3 and 4, where the same feature plainly does both jobs; it
is weakest, and now partly challenged, for Cluster 2, where field evidence suggests outward
hostility can arise *without* the inward cohesion the thesis assumes is its source [S11]. And
every one of these applies reflexively to a machine-encoded dharma
[[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]].

## Findings

### Methodological preliminary: failures are authentic, not "not-real-dharma"
A critical account must first refuse the **No True Scotsman fallacy**. Buddhism is widely
called peaceful, yet Ashin Wirathu "inflamed hatred against the Muslim population of Myanmar"
— and answering "well, he is not a true Buddhist" is precisely the fallacy [S1]. The Harvard
Religious Literacy Project's response is that "religions are internally diverse": a tradition
"complex enough that both Ashin Wirathu and a Hollywood actor can fall under the label of
Buddhist," and "while some of these expressions may be morally abhorrent, they should not be
denied as authentic" [S1]. Trying to "reduce a particular religion to an essential true
Buddhism or true Islam is neither helpful nor accurate" — the essentialising move is mostly a
device for "belief perseverance" [S1]. This matters for the whole note: the failure modes
below are expressions of a dharma's own potentials, not betrayals of a pure essence — which
is also why the essential-vs-cultural question ([[4.3-indigenous-oral-dharmas]],
[[3.4-relativism-universalism]]) is unavoidable when deciding what even counts as "failure."

### Cluster 1 — Form displaces function (the dharma hollows out)
The commonest failure is the survival of the shell after the fire dies. Max Weber named the
mechanism: the **routinization of charisma**, by which a founder's "spontaneous,
extraordinary" insight is "transformed into a stable, enduring, and institutionalized
structure" — teachings codified into texts, a priesthood established, laws and rituals
fixed, personal authority "succeeded by bureaucracy or tradition" [S4] (the pathology of
[[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]]). The same hollowing recurs through
**commodification** — clinical mindfulness stripped of ethics into "McMindfulness"
([[6.1-secular-translation]]) — and through the "professionalisation of compassion," where,
as one corpus source puts it, "the profession of compassion has become professionalized,
creating structures and hierarchies that often mirror corporate dynamics, yet without
assuming responsibility for the outcomes" [S3]. At the individual scale the same inversion is
**spiritual bypassing** — John Welwood's term (1984) for "using spiritual ideas and practices
to sidestep or avoid facing unresolved emotional issues," a "premature transcendence" that
deploys "absolute truth to disparage or dismiss relative human needs" [S7]. In each case the
practice persists while its function inverts.

### Cluster 2 — The boundary turns lethal (cohesion → exclusion → violence)
The mechanism here is the dark side of the very thing that makes dharmas cohesive. **Parochial
altruism** — "costly-to-self behavior to protect group members from non-group members" —
responds to perceived outgroup threat with "fear, withdrawal… overt hostility and aggression,"
and now misfires through mismatch; the corpus argues this "normal psychological mechanism"
underlies radicalization itself, since "terrorist grievances… are really tapping into
sensitivity to parochial altruism" [S2]. The result is **sacred violence**: "any religion —
including Buddhism known for its principles of non-violence — can be instrumentalised to
justify violence" [S6]. Wirathu's anti-Rohingya campaign drove over a million people from
Myanmar; Sri Lanka's interfaith coexistence shattered; and Juergensmeyer's *Terror in the
Mind of God* maps the same pattern across every major tradition [S6]. Crucially, the
evolutionary story is contested on two fronts. First, Richard Wrangham argues battlefield
self-sacrifice "should be regarded as a culturally induced behavior rather than an evolutionary
product of selection" (chimpanzees share our war-death rates but show no battlefield altruism;
hunter-gatherer battles stop after first injuries) [S10]. Second — and this bears directly on
the note's own thesis — the assumption that the *same* machinery does both jobs is empirically
challenged. Silva & Mace's naturalistic field experiments across the Catholic/Protestant divide
in Northern Ireland (charity and school donations, lost-letter returns) found that conflict
"was associated with reduced donations to out-group schools and the return of out-group
letters, but we found no evidence that it influences in-group cooperation. Rather,
socio-economic status was the major determinant of cooperative behaviour" [S11]. The outward
hostility appeared *without* the inward cohesion the parochial-altruism model treats as its
source — which the authors frame as "a challenge to dominant perspectives on the origins of
human cooperation" [S11]. So the cohesion→exclusion coupling this cluster relies on may be
looser than the framing suggests: out-group hostility and in-group solidarity can move
independently, and structural factors (here, deprivation) can drive the boundary hostile on
their own [A]. That contestation is *good* news on both counts — if the lethal boundary is
cultural rather than hardwired [S10], and if it is not welded to in-group love but can be
decoupled from it [S11], then it is malleable: it has been enlarged repeatedly across history,
from band to city to nation, the seed of a still wider in-group ([[8.1-planetary-dharma]]), and
stripped-down groups reliably rebuild cooperation rather than collapse into it
([[8.5-ata-case]]).
Either way, the exclusion is not a malfunction but the *over-extension of a feature*.

### Cluster 3 — Capture by power (the dharma harnessed to domination)
Dharmas are repeatedly captured by power in two directions. **Outward**, as legitimation
ideology — Marx's "religion is the opium of the people," which "serves to placate society"
and sanctify the existing order [S5]: *varṇa* sacralising caste, Calvinism-to-meritocracy
sanctifying inequality (both [[6.3-economics-work-inequality]]), divine right, established
state religion. **Inward**, as charismatic-authoritarian capture — Robert Jay Lifton's
*Thought Reform and the Psychology of Totalism* catalogues the "eight criteria" (beginning
with Milieu Control — control of information and communication) by which a movement becomes a
closed control apparatus, and names the "quest for absolute or 'totalistic' belief systems"
as "the most dangerous direction of the twentieth-century mind" [S8]. This is the dark twin of
the installation machinery of [[5.4-ritual-habituation]]: the same ritual, community, and
transcendent authority that install virtue can install obedience (Arendt's banal cruelty,
seeded there). Capture by power is examined in depth in [[7.3-dharma-and-power]].

### Cluster 4 — The self-undermining success (virtue turned against itself)
The subtlest failures are the ones the dharma's own success produces. **Moral licensing**
(Monin & Miller, 2001) is the finding that "people who establish moral credentials in one act
feel licensed to act less morally in the next" — their experiment showed that endorsing a
disadvantaged-group candidate made participants *more* willing to express subsequent prejudice
[S9]. A dharma's accumulated moral capital becomes permission to transgress; the
profession–practice gap and self-congratulatory hypocrisy follow. I would extend the same logic
to **purity spirals** — the commitment-signalling that binds a community
([[5.2-evolutionary-psychology]]) running away into escalating orthodoxy competition and schism;
this is my extrapolation from the licensing and signalling literatures, not a finding either
reports [A]. Here the failure is not that the dharma is abandoned but that it is taken *too*
seriously in the wrong dimension.

### The reflexive turn: failure modes of a machine-encoded dharma
Each cluster maps onto this project's own end-goal ([[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]]). A machine
dharma can **hollow out** (Cluster 1: frozen training data, fluent mimicry without
transformation); **exclude** (Cluster 2: flattening plural dharmas into one machine-readable
ethic *is* a boundary that erases the outgroup of excluded traditions); **be captured**
(Cluster 3: whoever controls the model controls the dharma — the most total capture yet); and
**license** (Cluster 4: an "aligned" label as moral cover for whatever the system does). Naming
the failure modes is the precondition for the project not enacting them.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias
Unusually, the corpus serves this question *well*: a knowledgebase curated around Boehm,
Sapolsky, Graeber, and Wrangham is primed to see power, tribalism, and institutional capture,
and it supplied two of the strongest anchors here — the No True Scotsman / internal-diversity
framing [S1] and the parochial-altruism account of radicalization [S2], plus self-critical
material on commodified compassion [S3] and (from [[6.1-secular-translation]]) on McMindfulness
and Buddhism-becoming-religion. The secular/critical lean is, for once, a feature. ▲ One
provenance caveat: [S2], the parochial-altruism anchor for the whole of Cluster 2, is a
bylineless magazine article; the 2026 corpus expansion now lets the same mechanism be read
against peer-reviewed primary work — Rusch's war-conflict analysis (in-group love without
necessary out-group hate) and, as the counter-weight, Silva & Mace's Northern Ireland field
experiments [S11], which is why Cluster 2's coupling is stated more cautiously above than the
magazine framing alone would license.

But that lean still shapes *which* failures are salient. The corpus foregrounds
**political/structural** failures (co-optation, violence, tribalism) and is thinner on the
**interior/spiritual** corruptions that traditions name from the inside — spiritual pride,
attachment to attainment, the "stink of Zen," the corruption of the teacher–student bond —
for which I imported external vocabulary (Welwood [S7]; Lifton [S8]). It also frames
*religious* failure far more readily than *secular*-dharma failure; I balanced this by
stressing that secular dharmas fail the same four ways (McMindfulness, ideological purity
spirals, the scientism/essentialism flagged in [[6.4-gender-and-dharma]], wellness- and
tech-guru cults). Two deeper cautions: **first**, the No True Scotsman point cuts at the
project itself — if failures are authentic expressions, the project's secular-dharma advocacy
cannot exempt itself by saying "real secular dharma wouldn't do that" [S1]. **Second**, the
evidence that the same mechanisms underlie both a dharma's effectiveness and its failures
suggests failure-proneness may be *intrinsic* — the necessary price of the bonding,
commitment, and authority that make dharmas work at all. If so, the right question is not
"which dharma avoids failure" but "which dharma fails least and recovers best," a bar that
[[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]] must operationalise and that should temper the constructive
proposals of Category 8. ▲ This "same mechanisms" claim should be held with more care than an
earlier draft did. It holds most cleanly for Clusters 1, 3 and 4 (routinisation, capture,
licensing all run *on* the effectiveness-machinery directly), but for Cluster 2 the coupling is
now empirically contested: Silva & Mace found the outward-hostility face without the inward-
cohesion face, with deprivation the real driver [S11]. Where the two faces can be *decoupled*,
failure-proneness is less "the necessary price of what makes dharmas work" and more a
contingent, structurally-conditioned hazard — which, if anything, is more hopeful for the
constructive project, since a contingent coupling can be engineered apart in a way an intrinsic
one cannot [A].
