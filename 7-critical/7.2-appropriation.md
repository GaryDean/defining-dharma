# 7.2 Cultural Adaptation & Appropriation

**Question:** How can Western adoption of Eastern dharmic concepts avoid superficial appropriation (such as orientalism) while enabling genuine cross-cultural learning?

## Synthesis
The line between appropriation and genuine learning is **not** "did you borrow?" — cultures
have always exchanged, and dharmas spread precisely *by* adaptation (Greco-Buddhism, the
Sinification of Buddhism into Chan/Zen, the "pizza effect" of yoga re-imported to India in
Westernised form) [S1][S6]. The line is a cluster of conditions: **power asymmetry, consent,
reciprocity, context retained, and whether the borrower is transformed or merely consumes**
[S3]. Edward Said's *Orientalism* supplies the deep structure (a framework that is itself contested on every front; see [[7.5-orientalism]]) — the problem is borrowing
inside a colonial power gradient that constructs the East as an exotic, inferior Other, where
"knowledge is a form of power" [S2]. Appropriation operates at three levels: the **category**
("Buddhism," "Hinduism," "world religions" are themselves partly 19th-century Orientalist
constructions) [S4]; the **market** (the commodified "spiritual marketplace" that "colonizes
Eastern beliefs") [S5]; and the **image** (the palatable "Oriental Monk" packaged for
consumption) [S7]. Genuine cross-cultural learning is possible — it is the engine of dharmic
history — but it requires reversing each: relationship over extraction, reciprocity over
profit, **indigenisation in one's own idiom** over decontextualised lifting, and being changed
over shopping [S1][S3]. The reflexive sting is that the project's own secular-translation
programme ([[6.1-secular-translation]]) is itself a prime suspect, and cannot exempt itself.

## Findings

### Borrowing is not the problem: exchange is how dharmas live
Cultures have never been sealed, and dharmas have always travelled by mixing. The
Greco-Buddhist world is a vivid case: the Indo-Greek king Menander (Milinda) appears in
Buddhist dialogue, his successor Strato issued coins styling himself *Dharmakasa* ("follower
of the Dharma"), and a coin of Menander II sets the Greek Dharmachakra beside Zeus and Nike
[S1]. Buddhism later Sinified by absorbing Taoist and Confucian elements on its way to becoming
Chan/Zen [S8]. So the baseline is anti-essentialist: a tradition is not a sealed monolith with an
owner, and "syncretism" itself is "often oversimplified," implying "two distinct monolithic
entities… merging like a Venn diagram," when in reality cultural forms "combine and influence
each other in complex and asymmetrical ways" [S1]. The healthy form of this is **indigenisation
or localisation** — "individuals in a local culture actively seek to make sense of another
culture in their own terms and idioms" [S1]. The "pizza effect" (Agehananda Bharati, 1970)
shows the flow runs both ways: yoga enjoyed a renaissance *in India* after its Western
adoption, re-imported with new status [S6]. Borrowing, in short, is normal; the question is
the *terms* on which it happens.

### Orientalism: the deep structure that turns exchange into appropriation
Said's *Orientalism* (1978) names what makes some borrowing pathological: not the borrowing
but the **power gradient** it occurs within. Western representation "constructed the Orient as
an exotic 'other,' producing a body of literature that exaggerated, simplified, and
dehumanised Eastern societies," and "this knowledge was a form of power" — "a dynamic system
of knowledge production that maintains power asymmetries" [S2]. The corpus furnishes a small
worked example: the French scholar Alfred Foucher coined "Greco-Buddhism" for Gandhāran art on
the assumption that "only Greeks could produce such masterpieces" — an orientalist denial of
local artistry that later scholars reject [S1]. Even admiration is not exempt: the romance of
the "mystic East" is itself an orientalist trope.

### Appropriation at the level of the category
The most consequential appropriation is invisible because it shaped the very containers.
Philip Almond's *The British Discovery of Buddhism* dates the coining of "Buddhism" to around
1800 — "the 'invention' of Buddhism" as a Western mental object, only then separated from
"Hinduism" [S4]. Richard King argues "the notion of 'Hinduism' as a single world religion is a
nineteenth-century construction, largely dependent upon the Christian presuppositions of the
early Western Orientalists" [S4]. Tomoko Masuzawa's *The Invention of World Religions* shows
the whole "world religions" schema encoded European universalism "despite the appearance of
pluralism" [S4]. The West did not only borrow practices; it built the frame through which
"Eastern religion" is perceived — and that frame is itself an artefact of colonial power.

### Appropriation at the level of the market and the image
Carrette and King's *Selling Spirituality* describes how spirituality "has become a powerful
commodity in the global marketplace… a corporate hijack" that "colonizes Eastern beliefs,"
from feng shui to yoga weekends — the same hollowing as "McMindfulness"
([[6.1-secular-translation]], [[6.2-digital-age-dharma]]) [S5]. At the level of the image,
Jane Iwamura's "Oriental Monk" — the nonsexual, solitary sage — functions as a "figure of
translation," making "Asian religiosity and spirituality… palatable for dominant culture
consumption" [S7]. And the dynamic includes **self-orientalising**: Asian teachers and
institutions sometimes package an exoticised product for Western buyers, so appropriation is
not always one-directional [S7].

### How to tell them apart — and how to do it well
The appropriation/appreciation literature converges on a usable test. **Appropriation**: a
dominant group adopts elements from a marginalised culture "without understanding or respecting
their original meaning, often while profiting from these elements or contributing to harmful
stereotypes" [S3]. **Appreciation**: "building relationships of respect and reciprocity where
consent and active participation" occur, "recognising the importance of obtaining permission,
participating with respect, and valuing the contributions of the culture" [S3]. "Unequal power
dynamics can transform exchange into appropriation" [S3]. From this, five conditions for
genuine cross-cultural learning:

1. **Reckon with power and direction of flow** — who holds the power, who profits, who is
   credited (the Said axis) [S2].
2. **Reciprocity over extraction** — relationship and return to source communities, not
   one-way taking [S3].
3. **Context over decontextualisation** — keep the ethical-communal "bundle," do not lift the
   technique alone (the [[6.1-secular-translation]] lesson) [S5].
4. **Indigenise honestly, in your own idiom** — adapt openly as the Greco-Buddhist localisers
   did (translating *dharma* as *eusebeia*), naming it as a new thing rather than passing it
   off as "the authentic original" [S1].
5. **Be willing to be changed** — genuine learning transforms the learner; consumption leaves
   the consumer intact while extracting a trophy [S3].

### The reflexive test: secular dharma in the dock
Apply the test to the project's own master move. Secular Buddhism (6.1) strips Buddhism of its
Asian cultural and cosmological context and rebrands a "universal core" — which is textbook
*decontextualisation*, and its defence ("recovering what was always universal") is exactly what
an appropriator would say. Whether it is genuine indigenisation (a Western culture honestly
making the dharma its own, in its own idiom, as the Greeks did) or extractive appropriation
(a dominant culture lifting a decontextualised fragment for a self-help market) is not settled
by intention; it turns on power, reciprocity, honesty about novelty, and whether the ethics
travelled with the technique. The same test indicts the present corpus (below) and is handed
forward to the constructive programme of [[8.3-post-traditional-dharmas]].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias
The corpus is **thin and skewed precisely where it most threatens itself.** The survey for
orientalism/appropriation returned mostly secular-Buddhist advocacy material plus an
AI-generated essay on "exoticization," and essentially none of the critical apparatus; the
entire critique — Said, Almond, King, Masuzawa, Carrette & King, Iwamura — had to be imported
[S2]–[S7]. The one genuinely strong corpus anchor, the Greco-Buddhism transcript [S1], is
excellent but cuts toward the *anti-essentialist, exchange-is-normal* side of the ledger — the
side most congenial to a translation movement. A corpus curated by that movement under-stocks
the postcolonial critique that would put its own project in the dock: the bias here is one of
**omission**.

Three substantive tensions remain. **First**, the anti-essentialist move can be weaponised:
"cultures always borrow" and "it's all hybrid anyway" can excuse extraction and erase power, so
the two truths must be held together — borrowing is normal *and* power can make particular
borrowings unjust [S1][S2]. **Second**, over-strict appropriation-policing risks the opposite
error: freezing living traditions as static, owned, and unable to adapt — which the pizza
effect [S6] and the No True Scotsman point ([[7.1-failure-modes]]) both refute. **Third**,
"whose consent?" has no clean answer: traditions are internally plural ([[7.1-failure-modes]]),
with no single authority empowered to grant or withhold permission, so "the tradition" cannot
consent as a unit — which makes the appreciation test [S3] genuinely hard to operationalise and
pushes the weight onto power, reciprocity, and honesty rather than formal permission. The
underlying scholarly version of this problem — comparing concepts across cultures at all — is
the methodological risk flagged in [[1.4-cross-cultural-cognates]] and underlies the
non-privileging stance of [[4.4-abrahamic-frameworks]].
