# 7.3 Dharma & Power

**Question:** How do dharmic frameworks both challenge and reinforce existing power structures? When do they liberate vs. oppress? Are they inclusive or exclusive?

## Synthesis
Dharma has a double face toward power because its core asset — **transcendent normative
authority** — is a weapon available to whoever can wield it. Rulers capture it to sanctify
domination (varṇa, divine right, karma-as-desert, Calvinist election); the oppressed wield it
to indict that domination (the prophets, liberation theology, Gandhi, Ambedkar) [S4][S6][S7][S8].
The deep irony, grounded in Christopher Boehm's evolutionary anthropology, is that morality
*originated* as an **anti-domination device** — egalitarian foragers' "reverse dominance
hierarchy" leveling would-be tyrants through ridicule, ostracism, and worse — so dharma's
liberatory face is its primordial function, and its legitimating face is a later capture by the
very hierarchies it evolved to prevent, a reversal Boehm dates to "after the invention of
agriculture" [S1][S3]. The hinge between the two faces is whether the transcendent standard
sits *above* the throne (chastising power, as the Axial breakthrough made possible) or *below*
it (sanctifying power) [S5]. On inclusion: every dharma draws a boundary
([[7.1-failure-modes]]), and both universalist dharmas (open to all) and bounded dharmas
(guarding a people) have liberatory and oppressive modes — universalism can erase difference
imperially even as it includes. Dharma is therefore neither inherently liberating nor
oppressing but **contested, power-laden terrain**; and, as Foucault insists, even its
liberations install new disciplines, because there is no power-free dharma [S9].

## Findings

### The deep origin: morality as anti-domination
Boehm's *Hierarchy in the Forest* (in the corpus) argues that egalitarianism among
hunter-gatherers "arises from a collective commitment to personal freedom and autonomy,"
maintained through a **reverse dominance hierarchy**: "while humans have an innate tendency
toward social dominance," foragers "acting as moral communities" curb upstarts through
"ridicule, criticism, and ostracism," with leaders "expected to be generous and unassuming"
and serious domination episodes punished, "including execution, if necessary" [S1].
Egalitarianism, in Boehm's phrase, is thus "a hierarchy in which the weak combine forces to
dominate the strong" — and crucially "this political direction was somehow reversed after the
invention of agriculture, and an 'orthodox' version of social dominance hierarchy reappeared"
[S3]. The implication for dharma is profound: the moral capacity dharmas formalise *began* as
a leveling check on power, so dharma's anti-domination face is its original function, not a
later humanitarian veneer.

### Capture I — legitimation by consent (the reinforcing face)
Once societies stratified, that moral authority was captured to sanctify the new hierarchies.
The mechanism is not mainly coercion but **consent**: Gramsci's hegemony names how a ruling
class "secures the voluntary consent of subordinate groups" by controlling the institutions —
"religion, the media and the education system" — so that its rule appears "natural, inevitable,
and beneficial to all"; "religious institutions… provide moral legitimacy to the ruling class
and reinforce social hierarchies" [S4]. This is Marx's "opium" ([[7.1-failure-modes]]) given a
finer mechanism. The worked instances run through the whole project: *varṇa* and karma-as-desert
sacralising caste, Calvinism-to-meritocracy sanctifying inequality (both
[[6.3-economics-work-inequality]]), divine right, the Constantinian turn of a persecuted sect
into an imperial church, and the sacralisation of gender hierarchy ([[6.4-gender-and-dharma]]).

### Capture II — the productive face (Foucault)
Foucault complicates the simple "repression" picture: power is not only top-down prohibition
but **productive**, making subjects. His **disciplinary power** produces "docile bodies," and
his **pastoral power** — the shepherd who "cares for his flock from birth to death" — is "an
individualising power" that requires "detailed knowledge of the mental and physical attributes
of its subjects" and directs them toward prescribed "self-knowledge" via "quasi-therapeutic
authorities" [S9]. On this reading the confessional, the spiritual director, and the meditation
hall are technologies of power that *form* the self, not merely cages that constrain it — which
means even a liberating dharma installs a regime of self-governance. There is no view from
outside power.

### Capture III — the power to name
Power over a dharma is also power over its *definition*. Bourdieu's **symbolic power** is
precisely "the power of legitimate naming… the official imposition of the legitimate view of the
social world" — the authority to fix what counts as orthodox and what as heresy, what is *dharma*
and what is *adharma* [S11]. Whoever controls the definition controls the boundary of
[[7.1-failure-modes]], and thus who is inside the community and who is cast out: ecumenical
councils fixing the canon and naming heretics, Brahminical authority over who may even hear or
interpret *śruti*, the modern state's monopoly on legitimate classification. The corpus puts the
question in exactly these terms — *"Daring to define. By whose right?"* — noting that defining is
inseparable from "linguistic baggage… and etymological evolution," yet is needed so that a
community can "speak the same language" [A]. So definition itself wears the double face: a
shared dharmic vocabulary enables cooperation, but the right to set that vocabulary is a prize the
powerful seize — this note's whole argument, run at the level of meaning (whose semantics belong
to [[1.3-boundary-definition]]).

### The return of the repressed: the liberating face
Yet the same transcendent authority is a weapon the powerless can seize, and the Axial
breakthrough ([[2.1-axial-age]]) made this structural. Bellah and Eisenstadt argue that Axial
transcendence "implied a certain relativization of politics… an assertion of the inherent
subordination of earthly government to higher objectives" — "not a secularization of power…
but rather a certain potential for its disenchantment or chastisement" [S5]. A higher law above
the king is a standing indictment of any king. From this vantage flow the resistance traditions:

- **Liberation theology** — Gutiérrez's *A Theology of Liberation* (1971) and its "preferential
  option for the poor," binding salvation to the material liberation of the oppressed [S6].
- **Ambedkar's Navayāna Buddhism** — the 1956 mass conversion of ~500,000 Dalits to a Buddhism
  recast "as a tool for social equality, justice, and empowerment," which pointedly *denies
  karma and reincarnation* and reframes suffering "from internal to societal" [S7]. This is the
  thesis made surgical: to turn the dharma against caste oppression, Ambedkar deleted the exact
  doctrine (karma-as-desert) that *legitimated* it.
- **Gandhi's satyagraha** — "truth-force" / "soul-force" nonviolent resistance, where dharma
  ("duty, religion, and ethics," measured by "love, compassion, truth") is mobilised against
  empire [S8].
- And the everyday register — Scott's "weapons of the weak" and hidden transcripts
  ([[4.1-samin-of-java]]), and Ubuntu mobilised in post-apartheid reconciliation via Tutu's Truth and Reconciliation Commission ([[4.2-ubuntu]]).

The believer's engine here is often a **providential faith** that, in King's words, "the arc of
the moral universe is long, but it bends toward justice" — a "potent source of hope… for people
struggling against injustice" [S2]. But Sandel notes the catch: this is providentialism, and
"the right side of history" is claimable by tyrants too (Putin, Assad "prevailed") — so the
moral arc is a motivating *wager*, not a guaranteed fact [S2].

### The hinge, and inclusion vs. exclusion
Why the double face? Because institutions are usually held by the powerful, legitimation is the
default and resistance the harder act of wresting the tradition back; but the moment a
transcendent standard is posited, it judges the powerful too. As for inclusion: universalist
dharmas (Buddhism, Christianity, Islam — "neither Jew nor Greek") include across ethnic lines
but carry an imperial edge (convert-or-erase; the universal that flattens difference, cf.
[[7.2-appropriation]]), while bounded/ethnic dharmas can oppress internally yet also *protect*
a minority's identity against absorption. Every dharma draws a boundary
([[7.1-failure-modes]]); the liberatory question is where it is drawn and how permeable it is.

### Reflexive turn: the machine dharma as a power question
A dharma encoded into machines ([[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]]) is the purest version of this
note's problem: whoever controls the model controls the encoded ethic. Will a machine dharma
function as a Boehmian *reverse-dominance leveler* — a higher standard that chastises the
powerful — or as the new legitimation ideology of a tech aristocracy, sanctifying whoever owns
the compute? The answer is not in the ethics encoded but in who holds the institution.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias
The corpus is **power-attentive and serves this question well** — Boehm, Graeber, Scott, and
Sandel are all alert to domination, and Boehm's reverse-dominance thesis [S1] is a genuinely
strong, in-corpus spine. But that same roster tilts **anti-authority and egalitarian** (Boehm's
and Graeber's leanings especially), so the corpus reads dharma-as-resistance more readily than
dharma-as-legitimate-order, and under-weights the conservative case that dharma rightly
*stabilises* society — that some hierarchy and authority are legitimate and necessary (the
Confucian and Burkean defence of order, and the dharma-as-social-glue established in
[[5.2-evolutionary-psychology]] and [[6.2-digital-age-dharma]]). I have stated that
counter-case explicitly rather than inheriting the corpus's tilt.

Three further cautions. **First**, Foucault's point that power is productive and ubiquitous
[S9] dissolves any clean liberate-vs-oppress binary: "liberation" installs its own disciplines,
so the honest question is *which* regime of power-formation, not power vs. freedom. **Second**,
the resistance strands were typically marginalised by their own institutions — the Vatican's
doctrinal office disciplined liberation theology; Ambedkar's movement still fights caste [S6][S7]
— a reminder that the institutional centre usually sides with order, so the liberating face is
the harder-won exception. **Third**, Boehm's "reversal after agriculture" is a powerful but
contested thesis: the egalitarian-forager baseline is not uniform (some foragers were
hierarchical, and Graeber & Wengrow's *Dawn of Everything* disputes any single original
condition) [S12], so the clean arc from leveling to capture should be held as a strong hypothesis,
not settled fact. The deepest unresolved question — handed to Category 8 — is whether a dharma
can check power *without itself becoming a power*: Boehm's leveling had no permanent office, but
every institutionalised dharma becomes a new hierarchy ([[7.1-failure-modes]] routinization).
