# 5.6.2 The contemplative vocation: atypical minds and the religious, mystical and shamanic niche

**Question:** Are atypical (neurodivergent) minds genuinely drawn to, and over-represented in, the contemplative vocations — monastic, mystical, shamanic, eremitic and ascetic? If some temperaments do fit some religious niches, which temperaments fit which, and how much of the popular "monks and mystics were neurodivergent" claim survives the evidence?

## Synthesis

The sourceable core is narrow and must be held three ways at once. First, certain temperaments plausibly fit certain religious niches, and cultures have demonstrably institutionalised atypical inner states into sanctioned specialist roles [S1][S4][S5]. Second, there are no base-rate data — no prevalence study with a numerator or denominator — establishing that neurodivergent people are *over-represented* in contemplative vocations; the popular claim is inference from temperament-fit plus anthropological anecdote, not an observed rate [S1][S2]. Third, "neurodivergence" hides a split the corpus blurs: the temperament most linked to mystical *experience* is high absorption / positive schizotypy — the trance-prone, magical-thinking pole [S26][S27] — whereas autistic/systemising cognition tends, on the measured average, toward the secular, the literal and the doctrinal [S15][S16][S8]. So the two atypical profiles pull in opposite religious *directions*, and for autism specifically the naive "natural mystic" inference runs against the evidence. The difference-not-deficit reframing is real, but its mirror-error — "every contemplative was a hidden atypical genius" — is the deficit model inverted, not overcome: the strongest sentence the evidence bears is that cultures have *channelled* certain atypical temperaments into valued roles, not that atypical minds are a reservoir of holiness [S2][S7].

## Findings

### The claim, and why the honest middle is narrow

The claim under examination is that atypical minds — what modern usage would call neurodivergent — are drawn to, and over-represented in, the contemplative vocations: monastic, mystical, shamanic, eremitic and ascetic roles. This is the least-settled node in [[5.6-neurodivergence]] because the shared corpus supplies its strongest, most-cited material precisely here, and that material is the seductive kind. The defensible core is threefold. Certain temperaments plausibly fit certain religious niches; cultures have demonstrably institutionalised atypical inner states and experiences into valued specialist roles [S1][S4][S5]; but — cutting the other way — the strong claim that "monks and mystics were neurodivergent" is largely unproven. There is **no prevalence study supplying a numerator or a denominator** for neurodivergent people in contemplative vocations, so "over-representation" is unmeasured inference from temperament-fit plus anthropological anecdote, not an observed base rate [S1][S2]. The direction of effect is contested, and for autism specifically the measured tendency runs the *other* way (see below). This note deliberately holds the difference-not-deficit reframing apart from its mirror-error: the story that "every contemplative was secretly an atypical genius" is the deficit model inverted, not overcome. Tellingly, the best defence against the romance comes from inside the very corpus source that tells the seductive story — Sapolsky's own brakes that traditional societies were "no more tolerant of the mentally ill," that the sanctioned niche is scarce, and that the same traits "would spectacularly fail in other cultural settings" [S2].

### The corpus's strongest thread — and its source quality

Anthropology long identified a category of "half crazy" specialists — shamans, medicine men and women who, in Paul Radin's 1930s framing, "make a living being metamagical," with Anthony Wallace and Alfred Kroeber calling shamans "healed madmen" [S1]. Robert Sapolsky sharpens this into the corpus's most-cited version of the story: the shaman is *not* a full schizophrenic but a "controlled, socially contextual version of metamagical schizotypalism" who "hears voices at the right time," sits among "the most powerful members of society," and has "been passing on their genes just fine" [S1]. **Source quality has to be foregrounded here, not deferred:** this thread is a single popular-science lecture recounting 1930s–40s ethnology and layering just-so evolutionary inference on top — the very pathologising tradition that Richard Noll's phenomenological work (1983) later dismantled [S19] — so it is a contested interpretive lens, not a settled corpus "finding," and it must ride alongside Noll from first mention rather than be treated as an established hub. With that caveat in place, the *convergent* material is genuine: James C. Scott "deexoticizes" prophetic and shamanic activity as "continuous with traditional healing practices," "different in degree but not necessarily in kind" from ordinary village decision-making [S4]; Javanese ethnography frames mystics as valued "receptors" or "barometers" whose unusual clarity registers what stays "confused or unclear to most" [S5]; and agent-based modelling suggests the specialist's *form* tracks social structure — spirit-possession in stratified societies, out-of-body journeying in egalitarian ones (speculative) [S12]. The archetype has a name outside the corpus too: Jung's "wounded healer" (1951), "only the wounded physician heals" [S21], and Eliade's shaman as "a sick man who has been cured, who has succeeded in curing himself" [S20]. But the same sources brake the story — the niche is scarce (Kroeber's Winnebago informant: "Thank God we don't have two of them"), traditional societies peripheralise the genuinely ill as readily as the West, and identical traits fail catastrophically in the wrong setting [S2]. The defensible reading is a narrow, context-dependent, culturally sanctioned niche — not a general upgrade.

### The distinction "neurodivergence" hides: schizotypy is not autism

This is the note's spine, and the point the corpus most blurs. The shaman evidence is about **positive schizotypy** — metamagical thinking, loose associations, trance-proneness, believing "strange things" — which Sapolsky explicitly frames as a mild genetic variant of schizophrenia identified via Kety's Danish adoption studies [S3][S6]. That is not autism. The temperament most predictive of mystical and spiritual *experience* is **absorption** (Tellegen & Atkinson, 1974), which loads onto Openness to Experience and onto positive schizotypy and fantasy-proneness — not onto the autistic profile [S26]. Tanya Luhrmann's anthropology supplies the mechanism: higher Tellegen absorption scores predict more vivid, sensory experiences of God's presence, and religious communities "kindle" invisible others into felt reality through disciplined practice [S27]. This is the peer-reviewed, non-romantic version of "cultures institutionalise atypical inner experience into religious roles," and it locates the mystically-prone temperament at the absorption / positive-schizotypy pole. Autistic cognition, on the measured average, leans toward almost the opposite religious *outcome*: reduced mentalising, literal interpretation, systemising, and a tendency toward secular or doctrinal rather than ecstatic belief [S15][S16][S8].

Two honesty brakes must ride with this split, or it hardens into a false dichotomy. **First, the "opposite poles" framing is a claim about religious outcomes, not about underlying cognition.** It draws on Crespi and Badcock's *diametric* model of autism and psychosis as opposite disorders of the social brain [S30] — a model that has received only mixed empirical support. At the level of measured *traits*, autistic and schizotypal tendencies in fact correlate **positively**: a meta-analysis finds autistic traits correlate most strongly with negative schizotypy (r ≈ 0.54) and, more weakly but still positively, with positive schizotypy (r ≈ 0.26), the diametric (inverse) pattern surfacing only in some partialled or network analyses [S31]. So the clean opposition holds for the mystical-versus-literal *outcome*, not as a categorical split in the cognition beneath it. **Second, the split is a tendency, not a wall:** some exploratory work reports that anomalous/psi experiences correlate with the broad autism phenotype [S28]. With those brakes, the substantive point stands — lumping schizotypy and autism under one unnamed "neurodivergent" umbrella obscures that the shaman-temperament and the autistic-temperament lean in opposite religious directions, which is why 5.6.2 is read *against* [[5.6.1-autism]] rather than folded into it. (The corpus's balancing-selection framing — heterozygote-advantage genetics → schizotypy → reproductively successful shaman [S6] — belongs to the schizotypy pole, and any advantage lands only there: schizophrenia proper is, in Sapolsky's own words, "by definition maladaptive," with "no hidden benefit" [S7]. Both the mechanism [S6] and its evolutionary logic are themselves contested — see Tensions.)

### The sharpest counter-evidence: for autism, the data point the other way

The naive inference — reduced social cognition equals natural mystic — does not merely lack support; for autism it contradicts the central tendency of the evidence. Norenzayan, Gervais and Trzesniewski (2012) found that reduced mentalising (theory of mind) predicts *less* belief in a personal God, and that mentalising — not systemising — statistically mediates the autism-to-lower-belief link [S15]. The corpus carries its own echo in Michael Blume's "mind-blind atheism," where "weaker mentalizing tendencies, more often found among (mostly male) people with autistic tendencies, correlate to lower levels of religiosity" [S8]. Caldwell-Harris and colleagues (2011) found high-functioning autistic adults more often atheist (about 26% versus 17% of controls) or agnostic and, when religious, more likely to build an idiosyncratic "own-construction" belief system than to join an organised religion [S16]. So autistic-type cognition empirically tracks *toward* secular, systematic worldviews — the opposite of intuitive theistic or mystical belief.

The brake cuts here too, against over-claiming the reversal. The corpus itself flags that the mentalising-to-atheism correlation is "small, inconsistent across countries, and easily overshadowed by other cultural factors," and that the analytic-thinking-to-atheism link is "cross-culturally weak and fickle" [S9]. A 2025 review of the empirical literature (Burnos and Kopacz) finds it genuinely mixed: several studies confirm lower religiosity in autism, but one (Reddish) finds no group difference, a large study (Ekblad and Oviedo) reports *higher* religious practice among autistic respondents, and Visuri's qualitative work shows autistic people forming coherent, meaningful relationships with supernatural beings despite mentalising differences — the review's own conclusion is that "a consistent characterization of the religiosity of individuals on the autism spectrum has not been established" [S17]. The defensible statement is a population *tendency* toward the secular with wide individual exceptions — not a law, and certainly not evidence for the autistic mystic.

### Autistic spirituality: the systemising–ritual affinity, not mysticism

Where autistic people *are* religious, limited qualitative evidence suggests the fit is often with structure rather than with open-ended contemplation or mysticism — though this must be stated as a tentative, small-sample affinity, not an established pattern. Van Ommen and Endress (n = 13) found autistic Christians drawn to service predictability, pattern recognition and structured community, with barriers arising from unexpected stimuli, loud music and loss of control [S17]; less-rigorous religious-education observations suggest fixed liturgical forms such as the Divine Office and the Rosary offer more scope for autistic practitioners than extemporary or contemplative prayer [S17]. **No systematic structured-versus-contemplative comparison has been done** [S17]. This dovetails with the corpus's ritual thread: Sapolsky maps the daily bits-and-pieces of religious ritualism onto a mild, OCD-adjacent register of repetition and intrusive rule-following [S10], and prosocial.world's ritual literature — Sosis and Bressler's costly-signalling account, Henrich's credibility-enhancing displays, and Legare and Souza's finding that perceived ritual efficacy rests on procedural repetition plus a supernatural observer — explains why rule-bound, repetitive ritual is socially load-bearing rather than incidental [S14] (connecting to [[5.4-ritual-habituation]]). Crucially, this points the trait-to-niche link *away* from mysticism: Caldwell-Harris's "systematizing, literal interpretation" [S16] and Sapolsky's observation that schizotypal believers tend to extremely concrete, fundamentalist readings ("the seven-day creation… measured down to the minute") [S3] both indicate that atypical-cognition religiosity, where it appears, leans doctrinal and literal, not ecstatic. The honest formulation is therefore *systemising → doctrinal/ritual structure*, not *systemising → mystical experience* — and the trait must be kept distinct from the condition throughout: Baron-Cohen's systemising is a normally-distributed, self-report-measured cognitive style, not a diagnosis, and high-systemising vocations enrich for autistic traits only modestly [S29].

### Temperament, solitude, and the eremitic niche

There is a real temperament dimension in play, though it maps onto the eremitic vocation by analogy, not diagnosis. Autism research finds atypically reduced responsiveness to *social* reward specifically, and personality research finds that introverted, high-sensitivity individuals proactively seek solitude for self-regulation and reflection — a self-chosen "unsociable" solitude that can be well-adjusted rather than pathological [S25]. Cultures have valued the hermit; the corpus's Batchelor material frames solitude as a cultivated art ("the cell of true self-knowledge"), distinct from being "consumed and paralyzed by… private anxieties," and notes that "there are ways of failing in solitude as in society" [S13]. But three brakes apply. First, much religious life is intensely *communal* (cenobitic) rather than solitary (eremitic), so the fit is partial at best — the monastery is often a crowd. Second, the temperament-to-vocation link is inference, not a measured pathway [S25]. Third, pathological withdrawal is not vocation: *hikikomori* — Tamaki Saito's home-bound reclusion of six-plus months, clustering around 1.2% in Japanese community surveys (the widely-quoted 2.3% is a Korean figure), now documented well beyond Japan — is a distress syndrome, the pathologised mirror of the culturally-valued hermit, and must not be romanticised as contemplative [S24]. The contrast is itself the lesson: identical withdrawal reads as sanctity or as sickness depending on cultural framing — the same "you have to get it just right" logic Sapolsky draws for the shaman [S2]. The temperament may be continuous across the valued ascetic and the pathologised recluse; what differs is whether a culture supplies a sanctioned role to receive it.

### Shaman-as-ill, retrospective diagnosis, and the romance in the wild

The reductive thesis that the mystic or shaman is simply mentally ill is largely rejected. Julian Silverman (1967) argued that shamanic and acute-schizophrenic experiences share an underlying sequence, differing mainly in cultural framing [S18]; Richard Noll (1983) dismantled this with a state-specific phenomenological analysis showing shamanic altered states are systematically *different* — entered and exited voluntarily, controlled, and organised around a coherent cultural narrative — making the "schizophrenia metaphor" untenable [S19]. Mircea Eliade's shaman as "master of ecstasy," a "sick man who has cured himself," is the counter-position [S20] — but it should be flagged as itself a contested, romantic and universalising move whose comparative method is widely criticised: it is a position in the debate, not neutral fact, and it can slide from *pathologising* romance to *sanctifying* romance without a scrap of added data. Any invocation of shaman-as-neurodivergent must carry Noll's refutation alongside it [S19].

Retrospective diagnosis of historical mystics and saints as autistic (or epileptic, or schizophrenic) is methodologically fraught and belongs in the speculation column every time it appears: it is anachronistic, typically unfalsifiable from surviving sources, and ethically questioned [S22]. Yale's Fred Volkmar warned, of the fashion for diagnosing historical figures, that "there is unfortunately a sort of cottage industry of finding that everyone has Asperger's" [S23]. The romance is live in the wild: popular neurodiversity-and-shamanism writing recasts autism as "the shaman's disease" and the "autistic shaman" as naturally complementary [S36] — an appealing story with no peer-reviewed support, and precisely the move the corpus's own retrospection invites, hosting an "Is rationalism a form of autism?" provocation [S11] and a Radin/Sapolsky schizotypal retrofit of historical shamans [S1]. Even Sapolsky hedges: schizotypy, not schizophrenia; advantage only if you "get it just right" [S2]. No specific historical mystic, monk or saint can be said to "have been" autistic or schizotypal on this evidence; such attributions are speculation, never a premise an argument may lean on.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **Zero base-rate for "over-representation."** The single biggest gap: there is no quantitative prevalence study — no numerator, no denominator — for neurodivergent people in contemplative vocations. Every "over-represented" or "drawn to" claim in this literature is inference from temperament-fit plus anthropological anecdote, and should be read as such, not as an observed rate [S1][S2].
- **The corpus is Sapolsky-weighted and frames the whole question through an evolutionary, pathology-derived lens.** Its strongest, most-citable thread — the shaman/seer as "socially channelled schizotypalism" (Radin's "half crazy," Kroeber and Wallace's "healed madmen") — is genuinely rich and cross-disciplinary [S1][S4][S5], but it actively *leans into* retrospective diagnosis, the exact move this note exists to brake, and it hosts an "Is rationalism a form of autism?" provocation [S11]. It never uses the words "neurodiversity" or "neurodivergence," and engages neither disability studies nor autistic self-advocacy at all — so the whole answer had to be re-centred using external, peer-reviewed sources.
- **What the corpus does not supply.** Three loads had to be carried from outside it: (1) the *direction-of-effect* counter-evidence — reduced mentalising predicts *less* theistic belief (Norenzayan and Gervais [S15]; only lightly present in-corpus via Blume [S8]) — which is the note's spine and cuts against the corpus's flattering "atypical mystic" pull; (2) the schizotypy-versus-autism distinction and the absorption / "cultural kindling" mechanism (Tellegen [S26]; Luhrmann [S27]); and (3) the anthropological refutation of shaman-as-ill (Silverman → Noll [S18][S19]), the wounded-healer archetype [S20][S21], the empirical autistic-spirituality literature [S16][S17], and every prevalence figure.
- **"Neurodivergence" is a fuzzy, contested umbrella.** There is no clean bimodal line between neurodivergent and neurotypical; the dilution critique — "if everyone is neurodivergent, no one is" — is live, and the category has drifted from Singer's original Asperger's-centred coinage into a diluted "neurodiversity lite" [S32]. Hold *difference* and *disability* together: the umbrella spans profound, minimally-verbal, high-support autism (the Lancet Commission's own — and itself contested — "profound autism" category), so "every neurodivergent person is secretly a mystic or a genius" is the deficit model's mirror-error, to be resisted as hard as the deficit model itself [S33].
- **Prevalence and rate figures are definition-, method- and era-dependent — ranges, not facts.** Autism surveillance among US 8-year-olds reached roughly 1 in 31 (about 3.2%) in the 2022 ADDM data, with a male-to-female ratio near 3.4:1 and roughly five-fold variation across surveillance sites (about 9.7 to 53.1 per 1,000, i.e. ~1%–5%) — a spread that tracks ascertainment and broadened criteria, not a fixed biological rate [S34]. *Hikikomori* clusters near 1.2% in Japanese community surveys (the 2.3% often quoted is Korean) [S24]. And the "~26% atheist" figure for high-functioning autistic adults comes from a *single* small, self-selected survey (n ≈ 61) and must never be read as a population rate [S16].
- **The deepest genetic brake, and a contested mechanism.** Any advantage sits at the *mild* / schizotypal pole; schizophrenia proper is, in Sapolsky's own words, "by definition maladaptive," with "no hidden benefit" [S7]. And the corpus's balancing-selection framing (heterozygote-advantage genetics → schizotypy → reproductively successful shaman [S6]) is itself contested in modern psychiatric genetics: schizophrenia shows *reduced* fecundity and a highly polygenic architecture that Keller and Miller argue fits polygenic mutation-selection balance, not balancing selection — so the evolutionary "well-rewarded niche" story should be held as a hypothesis, not an established mechanism [S35].
- **Citation integrity (assembly note).** Two source attributions in the research draft were corrected here for traceability. The 2025 Frontiers in Psychiatry review is authored by Burnos and Kopacz — not by the Ekblad/Oviedo study it reviews [S17]. And the Volkmar "cottage industry" quotation, sometimes attributed loosely, is sourced to its origin in Erica Goode's 2001 *New York Times* piece [S23]; Muramoto 2014 (whose case study is Socrates and temporal-lobe epilepsy, and which does *not* contain the Volkmar line) is used only for the general ontological/epistemic/ethical critique of retrospective diagnosis [S22].
