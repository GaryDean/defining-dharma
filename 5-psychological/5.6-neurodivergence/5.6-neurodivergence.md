# 5.6 Neurodivergence: the contested category and its place in the human group

**Question:** What is "neurodivergence" — a natural kind or a contested umbrella — and what place do atypical minds (autistic, ADHD, dyslexic, gifted, schizotypal, and the psychopath at the exploitative pole) hold in the human group? Do cultures channel cognitive variation into sanctioned specialist niches, and is that best read as an individual-level fact or a group-level feature of human sociality? *(Index note, framing [[5.6.1-autism]], [[5.6.2-contemplative-vocation]], [[5.6.3-technical-vocation]], [[5.6.4-gifted-wider-spectrum]] and the sibling [[5.5-psychopaths]].)*

## Synthesis

"Neurodivergence" is a contested umbrella, not a natural kind. Nick Walker's widely-used gloss splits it into three things that are routinely conflated: a biological fact (the ordinary variation in human neurocognition), a civil-rights / self-advocacy movement, and a paradigm that rejects a single "normal" brain and relocates much disablement in social barriers [S36]. The category has fuzzy edges and no sharp biological boundary — there is no clean bimodal split between autistic and non-autistic people — which is the source of both its explanatory power and the "dilution" critique: if everyone is neurodivergent, no one is [S40]. Its main forms carry prevalence figures that are ranges, not constants, because they track definition, instrument, method and era: autism ~1 in 31 US eight-year-olds (2022 surveillance, up from ~1 in 150 c.2000, the rise driven largely by broadened criteria and ascertainment) [S42][S43]; ADHD ~5% of children worldwide with no evidence of a true rise across three decades [S45]; dyslexia ~5–17% [S47]; psychopathy ~1% ([[5.5-psychopaths]]) [S48]; giftedness (IQ≥130) the top ~2% [S49] — and co-occurrence is the rule, not the exception [S50]. The note's dharma thesis is that human groups have always contained a spread of cognitive and temperamental types, and many cultures built sanctioned specialist niches — seer, shaman, systemiser, ascetic — that channelled atypical minds into valued roles while policing the exploitative pole (the psychopath) [S1][S2][S9]. That is best read as a *plausibly* group-level feature of human sociality, grounded in the same balancing-selection logic as [[5.2-evolutionary-psychology]] — but it inherits 5.2's hedge exactly: the individual-level maintenance of variation is well-evidenced, whereas a group-level "purpose" is contested and must not be overclaimed [S4][S9][S11]. Three brakes hold against the romance the topic invites: the niche is narrow, scarce and context-dependent, not a general upgrade [S2]; the severe, minimally-verbal, high-support end is real, and the "every neurodivergent is secretly gifted" story is the deficit model's mirror-error [S16][S61]; and retrospective diagnosis of historical shamans, saints or geniuses is speculation, not evidence [S59][S60]. This is an index note: it frames [[5.6.1-autism]], [[5.6.2-contemplative-vocation]], [[5.6.3-technical-vocation]] and [[5.6.4-gifted-wider-spectrum]], and positions [[5.5-psychopaths]] as the exploitative instance of the same underlying fact of cognitive variation.

## Findings

### 1. What "neurodivergence" means — three senses, one contested history

The umbrella term is younger than the phenomena it names, and its history is genuinely disputed. The cleanest available disambiguation is Nick Walker's: "neurodiversity" can mean (a) a biological fact — the near-infinite variation in human neurocognition; (b) the neurodiversity *movement*, a civil-rights and self-advocacy project; and (c) the neurodiversity *paradigm*, a theoretical stance that denies there is one correct "normal" brain and locates much disablement in person–environment mismatch and social barriers. Conflating the three is the most common error in the debate [S36].

The standard popular story — "Australian sociologist Judy Singer coined 'neurodiversity' in her 1998 honours thesis" — is real but incomplete. Singer did model the word on "biodiversity" and used it in her 1998 thesis and a 1999 book chapter [S32]; the term appeared in print contemporaneously in journalist Harvey Blume's 1998 *Atlantic* piece (with "neurological diversity" already in his 1997 *New York Times* reporting), attributing the ethos to the online autistic community [S33]; and the deeper root is Jim Sinclair's 1993 manifesto "Don't Mourn for Us," which reframed autism as a way of being rather than a tragedy to be cured [S34]. A 2024 peer-reviewed correction by six leading (mostly autistic) scholars argues the concept was "developed collectively" through distributed discussion on 1990s mailing lists (notably InLv, founded by Martijn Dekker in 1996), so crediting any single founder is a historical oversimplification — the provenance itself is contested [S35]. The satirical community coinages "neurotypical" and, later, "neurodivergent" (attributed to activist Kassiane Asasumasu c.2000) entered serious use from the same self-advocacy milieu [S37].

This matters for corpus honesty. The shared corpus barely speaks this vocabulary at all: it discusses autism, schizotypy, Williams syndrome and ADHD-adjacent traits in clinical or older anthropological terms, and the *word* "neurodiversity" and the movement's history arrive almost entirely from outside. The one clear exception found in the corpus is a first-person autistic self-account (a video transcript) that both uses "neurodiversity" and draws the key trait/disorder line: "the symptoms of Autism are best described as simple neurodiversity until they cause unavoidable pathological outcomes" [S25] — a rare lived-experience voice in a corpus otherwise reliant on Temple Grandin and Naoki Higashida quoted second-hand inside a neuroscience book [S15].

### 2. Deficit model vs social / identity-first framing

The framing debate pits the *medical / deficit model* (a disorder located in the individual, to be treated or cured) against the *social model of disability* (disablement arises largely from societal barriers and person–environment mismatch); the neurodiversity paradigm builds on the social model. Neither is purely correct — the paradigm reframes disablement without denying that impairment and support needs are real [S38].

Strikingly, the corpus supplies a strong *difference-vs-deficit* apparatus of its own, but from developmental psychology and critical psychiatry rather than from disability studies. Kline, Shamsudheen & Broesch name "the deficit assumption" — reading population-level differences as caused by "something lacking" — as carrying "a value judgement that goes beyond a scientific approach" and "obscur[ing] the science itself," with the overarching thesis that variability is "one of the broadest human universals of all" [S19]; the same paper dissolves the nature/nurture framing behind deficit-talk ("there are not two kinds of things, the innate and the non-innate, but only one, the developmental process itself"; "culture is a part of human biology") [S20]. A critical-psychiatry piece makes the logic sharp: studies comparing a diagnosed group to controls "could not distinguish differences from deficits, even in principle" — biological correlates of a condition do not by themselves prove pathology [S21]. That argument is philosophically clean, but it comes from a contested minority school (critical psychiatry) and should be flagged as a partisan position, not settled consensus, when it is carried into the neurodivergence debate. Sapolsky states it directly for autism: "the old model of viewing autism as a pathological neurological disorder is outdated; it exists on a continuum," and "the distinction between a disorder that impairs functioning and a variation of normal human diversity is arbitrary" [S17]; he elsewhere notes autism is not inherently linked to cognitive impairment, nor necessarily to impaired empathy or theory of mind [S26].

On language, large surveys find most autistic adults prefer identity-first ("autistic person") over person-first ("person with autism") — Keating et al. (2023) found ~80% of a global English-speaking autistic sample endorsed "autistic person" vs ~24% "person with autism," consistent with Kenny et al. (2016) — but preference is not unanimous and varies by respondent and culture [S39]. This note therefore defaults to identity-first for autism while respecting person-first where individuals or contexts prefer it, and does *not* universalise identity-first to constructs such as psychopathy that have no pride or self-advocacy movement ([[5.5-psychopaths]]).

### 3. Fuzzy edges and the dilution critique

There is no sharp biological line around "neurodivergence." Ginny Russell notes there is "no clear bimodal distribution" cleanly separating autistic from non-autistic people, so an unbounded umbrella risks losing discriminating meaning — the "if everyone is neurodivergent, no one is" worry — and a diluted corporate "neurodiversity-lite" can strip the disability-rights substance in favour of "superpower" branding [S40]. The corpus never names this critique, but Sapolsky's "the boundary is arbitrary / it's a continuum" [S17] in fact cuts *toward* the dilution worry; balancing that required external critical-psychiatry and disability-studies sourcing.

The umbrella's *membership* is itself unsettled: it usually covers autism, ADHD and the specific learning differences (dyslexia, dyspraxia, dyscalculia), often Tourette's and OCD, sometimes bipolar, schizophrenia or intellectual disability, and usually excludes acquired conditions such as Parkinson's or MS. Singer's original coinage was narrower still — about Asperger's / high-functioning autism [S32].

A distinct representativeness critique holds that the movement speaks poorly for the most severely affected. Jaarsma & Welin (2012) argue the broad claim covering "low-functioning" as well as "high-functioning" autism is problematic and that only a narrow version is reasonable; groups such as the National Council on Severe Autism argue the paradigm downplays profound, non-speaking, intellectually disabled autism (though many advocates counter that "functioning" labels are themselves misleading) [S41]. The honest position holds *both* sides: "difference not deficit" is a legitimate reframing, and the profound end is real [S16][S61].

### 4. The main forms and their prevalence ranges

Every figure here is a range with a reason it varies; single point estimates should never be quoted as fixed, and administrative / ever-diagnosed rates must be distinguished from epidemiological point prevalence.

**Autism.** US surveillance among 8-year-olds has risen steeply *as a surveillance figure*: ~1 in 150 (2000) → 1 in 44 / 2.3% (2018) → 1 in 36 (2020) → 1 in 31 / 3.22% (2022 surveillance year; CDC/ADDM, MMWR published April 2025), with a male-to-female ratio of 3.4:1 and a roughly 5-fold spread across sites (9.7 to 53.1 per 1,000) — the site spread alone shows the number tracks ascertainment, not a fixed biological rate [S42]. That scope must travel with the headline number: CDC and mainstream researchers attribute the rise primarily to broadened DSM criteria, greater awareness, earlier / better screening and improved access for previously under-identified groups, so "rising diagnosis" is warranted while "rising occurrence / epidemic" is not — though "purely artefactual" would overclaim [S43]. The male-to-female ratio (and its narrowing from ~4.2:1 toward 3.4:1) is itself partly a diagnostic artefact: a meta-analysis of prevalence studies found the often-quoted 4:1 ratio falls to roughly 3:1 under active case-finding, evidencing a diagnostic gender bias whereby girls who meet criteria are disproportionately missed (camouflaging; historically male-normed criteria) [S63]. That caution bears directly on the Extreme-Male-Brain material below (5.6.3), which the raw ratio superficially appears to corroborate [S55]. Adult figures are model-based and uncertain: UK ~1.1% (Brugha / APMS), US modelled ~2.2% (Dietz) [S44].

**ADHD.** Childhood prevalence pools to ~5% worldwide (Polanczyk et al. 2007: 5.29%), and a 2014 update across three decades found *no* evidence of a true increase over time — most variation is methodological; US administrative rates (~11% ever-diagnosed) reflect diagnostic practice, not a different underlying rate [S45]. Adult ADHD is ~2.5% (persistent) to ~6.8% (symptomatic) [S46].

**Dyslexia.** ~5–17% depending entirely on cutoff and definition; there is no biomarker, so the prevalence is inherently criteria-dependent [S47].

**Psychopathy.** Commonly cited as ~1%, but instrument-dependent: ~1.2% by the gold-standard PCL-R and ~0.6% in a UK household survey, vs ~4.5% under looser instruments. It is a clinical / forensic construct, not a self-advocacy identity — the exploiter limit-case of [[5.5-psychopaths]] [S48].

**Giftedness.** No single agreed definition; the common threshold IQ≥130 is the top ~2–2.5%, but the concept has moved from Terman's unitary IQ toward multi-domain models that are themselves empirically contested, so any "prevalence" is definition-driven [S49].

**Overlap is the norm.** Autism and ADHD co-occur heavily (~30–70% of autistic people have co-occurring ADHD; ~20–50% of ADHD show autistic traits), and DSM-5 (2013) was the first edition to permit a simultaneous diagnosis, so pre-2013 figures undercount the overlap [S50]; dyslexia and ADHD show ~25–40% bidirectional comorbidity [S51]. The corpus itself illustrates jaggedness rather than uniform deficit or gift: Williams syndrome pairs hypersociality and musical / verbal facility with cognitive impairment, showing cognition is modular and atypical profiles are spiky, not uniformly up or down [S24].

### 5. The dharma thesis: atypical minds and the specialist niche

The heart of why this belongs in DD is a claim about groups, not individuals: human societies have always contained a spread of cognitive and temperamental types, and many cultures built sanctioned specialist roles — shaman, seer, healer — that plausibly channelled atypical temperaments into valued niches [S1][S2][S6][S7]. That much is solid anthropology. Identifying the temperament in question as "schizotypy," however, is a contested modern heuristic and must be flagged inline as speculation, not evidence — the corpus's single strongest, most citable thread makes exactly this identification, so the reader should hold it lightly from the outset. Anthropology long identified a category of "half crazy" specialists — shamans, witch doctors, medicine men and women who "make a living being metamagical"; Paul Radin named the category in the 1930s, and Wallace and Kroeber called shamans "healed madmen," framing shamanism as "a very controlled, socially contextual version of metamagical schizotypalism" [S1]. Sapolsky presses the point: shamans are not full schizophrenics but people who "hear voices at the right time" — "some of the most powerful members of society" who "have been passing on their genes just fine," i.e. a well-rewarded, reproductively successful niche for an unusual temperament [S2]. The psychiatric half of that identification is precisely what a later brake dismantles: Silverman's shaman-as-schizophrenic thesis was rebutted by Noll on phenomenological grounds [S59], so the niche shows that cognitive variation can be *given* a social role — it does not show that the variation is schizophrenia, nor that it exists "for" that purpose. The niche claim is reinforced across disciplines: James C. Scott "deexoticizes" prophetic / shamanic activity as "continuous with traditional healing practices," "different in degree but not necessarily in kind" from ordinary village decision-making, rather than a "collective derangement" [S6]; Javanese ethnography frames mystics as valued "receptors" or "barometers" whose unusual clarity of consciousness registers what remains "confused or unclear to most" [S7]; and an agent-based-modelling account suggests the niche's *form* tracks social structure (spirit-possession in stratified societies, out-of-body "journeying" in egalitarian ones), though this is flagged speculative [S8].

The biological engine proposed for this is *balancing selection*. Using sickle-cell, Tay-Sachs and cystic fibrosis, Sapolsky shows a gene catastrophic when fully expressed can be protective in a partial / heterozygous form — "there's no such thing as a bad gene, only a bad gene–environment interaction" [S4]; schizotypal personality is read as a mild genetic version of schizophrenia (identified via Kety's 1970s Danish adoption studies), the sub-clinical pole that plausibly became the shaman [S5]. Two brakes belong right here, not later. First, any "advantage" lands only at the mild / sub-clinical pole: schizophrenia proper is "by definition maladaptive" with "no hidden benefit," so balancing selection must never be read as "the disorder is secretly a gift" [S4]. Second, the niche depends on getting the ratio right — schizophrenia holds at roughly 1–2% across cultures, and the sanctioned role works only because such individuals stay scarce (Wallace's observation that our own culture is unusual in outlawing the irrational) [S3]. Evolutionary psychology supplies the general template: personality differences modelled as frequency-dependent "behavioral morphs" / polymorphisms — a "cheater" strategy advantageous only while rare — so a population retains a *mix* of types rather than converging on one [S9][S10], with Christakis adding heterozygote advantage and disassortative ("compatible-genes") mating as maintenance mechanisms [S11].

The correct framing is therefore that neurodiversity is *plausibly a group-level feature* of human sociality, not only individual pathology — the same polymorphism logic that DD 5.5 uses for psychopaths ("frequency-dependent selection at the individual limit") and that DD 5.2 uses for diverse temperaments as group-level insurance. But it must inherit 5.2's hedge exactly: the *individual-level* maintenance of variation is well-evidenced, whereas a *group-level* adaptive "purpose" is contested at the level of selection theory and must not be overclaimed. The prosocial.world material on the payoff of mixed minds is suggestive but the *wrong kind* of evidence for a direct claim: it concerns cultural / demographic diversity feeding the "collective brain" (Henrich — the 1924 Johnson–Reed immigration cuts reduced US patenting by roughly two-thirds) [S28] and organizational-culture mix (a "team of average players who collaborate brilliantly can outperform a group of talented individuals") [S29], not neuro-cognitive diversity. Its built-in nuances are the honest version of the thesis: "uncoordinated diversity will not help" [S29], and "purely flat, self-organising egalitarian structures tend to fall apart, as do structures which are only hierarchical or only individualistic" [S30] — a spread of minds pays off only *with* coordinating structure, and cognitive-diversity-as-group-resource remains a live modelling question, not settled fact [S31].

One further brake the group-level reading cannot dodge: a "useful diversity / insurance" frame cannot absorb the profound, minimally-verbal, high-support end of the range — 24-hour care, IQ<50, absent speech (the Lancet Commission's contested "profound autism") [S61]. Whatever adaptive story is told about sanctioned seers or systemisers says nothing for these presentations, and a note that leant on "group-level insurance" without saying so would be quietly selecting the mild pole and calling it the whole. The group-level claim is therefore plausible-but-unproven, scoped to the mild end, and explicitly *not* a theory of the profound end.

### 6. Honesty brakes: why this is a romance-magnet

This topic pulls hard toward a flattering story — the misunderstood genius, the neurodivergent shaman, the systemiser who built the modern world — and the corpus conveniently supplies its own brakes against it.

**The niche is narrow, not a general upgrade.** The very source that celebrates the shaman also insists traditional societies are "no more tolerant of the mentally ill than we are in the West," that the niche is scarce (Kroeber's informant: "Thank God we don't have two of them"), and that the *same* traits that make a sanctioned shaman "would spectacularly fail in other cultural settings" [S2].

**Advantage lands only at the mild pole.** Sapolsky is explicit that schizophrenia proper is "by definition maladaptive" with "no hidden benefit"; any advantage sits at the schizotypal pole, so balancing selection must not be read as "the disorder is secretly a gift" [S4].

**Gene-to-temperament effects are tiny.** Dopamine-related variants (DRD4, DAT, COMT) and NPY map weakly onto novelty-seeking and anxiety — a DRD4 variant explains only ~3–4% of novelty-seeking variance, inconsistently [S12] — and Sapolsky stresses there is "nothing remotely resembling" a world where one allele makes you a sensation-seeker and another a stamp-collector [S13]. The disorder / positive-trait genetic correlations behind "mental-illness-as-hidden-gift" may have been overstated (Amodei on recent GWAS) [S14]. Tidy single-mechanism explanations fail too: the popular "broken mirror" (mirror-neuron) theory of autism is largely unsupported [S27].

**Trait is not condition.** Systemising (a normally-distributed cognitive style) is not autism (a clinical, often disabling condition); they overlap but are not identical, and the corpus's own "Is rationalism a form of autism?" provocation is exactly the trait-conflation move to critique, not to build on [A]. The corpus also holds the severe end: autism includes "a constant stream of ambiguous sensory input," disrupted interoception, and difficulty learning basic concepts and language [S15][S16], and even where reframing is valid, neuroplasticity-based social training has real limits — trained skills stay "forced and not intuitive" [S18]. The "every neurodivergent is secretly gifted" story is the deficit model's mirror-error; the Lancet Commission's contested "profound autism" category (24-hour care, IQ<50 and/or minimal speech) exists precisely because "difference not deficit" does not describe this group [S61].

**Retrospective diagnosis is speculation, not evidence.** Retrofitting historical shamans as "schizotypal" [S1][S5], or saints and geniuses as autistic, is methodologically fraught — anachronistic, typically unfalsifiable, and ethically questioned (Volkmar's "cottage industry" [S64]; Muramoto's ontological / epistemic / ethical objections) [S60]. The older shaman-as-mentally-ill thesis (Silverman 1967) was dismantled by Noll (1983), who showed shamans enter and exit trance voluntarily and maintain a coherent cultural narrative, phenomenologically unlike schizophrenia; Eliade's "master of ecstasy" rehabilitation is itself a contested, arguably romantic move [S59]. Any note invoking the shaman-as-neurodivergent must carry this refutation.

### 7. Index: how 5.6 frames its children (and 5.5)

This umbrella positions four child notes and one sibling.

**[[5.6.1-autism]]** — the paradigm case: autism as a highly variable, "degenerate" (many-to-one) neurodevelopmental profile [S15], spanning the fusiform-face-area and prediction findings at one end and profound, minimally-verbal presentations at the other [S16][S61]; the site to work through the continuum / "arbitrary boundary" claim [S17] and the dilution critique [S40].

**[[5.6.2-contemplative-vocation]]** — the "autistic mystic / neurodivergent shaman" thesis, which the note must present against the grain: the evidence runs the *other* way. Reduced mentalizing (the autism-linked profile) predicts *less* belief in a personal God and more atheism, and mentalizing — not systemising — mediates the link [S58]; but that cognitive-style-and-belief literature is itself modest and contested — the same research group's companion claim that analytic thinking promotes religious disbelief failed a large pre-registered direct replication (d≈0.07, in the wrong direction, N=941), so [S58] gives a suggestive direction, not a settled result [S62]. The shaman-as-ill thesis is in any case largely rejected [S59], and chosen contemplative solitude must not be medicalised as pathological withdrawal — the same retrospective-diagnosis caution that forbids reading historical mystics as autistic or schizotypal [S60].

**[[5.6.3-technical-vocation]]** — the "systemisers built the modern world" thesis: Baron-Cohen's Empathizing–Systemizing and Extreme-Male-Brain theory [S52], with its strongest support (PNAS 2018, ~600k) *and* the authors' own brake that the differences "apply only to group averages, not to individuals" [S54]; the engineering link confounded by general professional-class over-representation (Jarrold & Routh) [S53]; measurable but modest STEM over-representation among the minority of autistic people who reach college [S56]; and the "geek syndrome" / assortative-mating cluster story as journalism and diagnosed-prevalence with unexcluded ascertainment bias, not established cause [S57]. EMB itself is influential but embattled — weak independent replication, a retracted 2017 neuroimaging paper, disputed prenatal-testosterone link [S22][S55].

**[[5.6.4-gifted-wider-spectrum]]** — giftedness and the wider spectrum, where the definition problem [S49] and Gardner-style multiple-intelligence claims are influential but empirically contested, and the trait / condition and jagged-profile cautions apply [S24].

**[[5.5-psychopaths]]** is the exploitative instance of the *same* underlying fact of cognitive variation: where the seer / systemiser / ascetic niches channel atypical minds into pro-social specialist roles, the psychopath is the frequency-dependent cheater the cooperative order evolved to *police* [S9][S10]. 5.6 and 5.5 are two settings of one mechanism — the maintenance of a spread of temperaments — read once toward sanctioned specialism and once toward the exploiter pole. Connections to [[5.1-neurological-basis]] (the neural substrate of atypical cognition), [[5.2-evolutionary-psychology]] (balancing selection / diverse temperaments as insurance) and [[5.3-moral-psychology-development]] (how cognitive variation meets moral development) anchor the psychological category.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

**The corpus lean, named.** The YaTTi shared corpus is curated around Boehm, Sapolsky, Graeber, Batchelor and Bregman — a secular / evolutionary / Western-academic slant — and it shaped this answer heavily and unevenly. The whole frame is evolutionary / biological and pathology-derived, and strongly Sapolsky-weighted: the "difference not deficit" idea arrives via developmental psychology (Kline et al.'s "deficit assumption"; "variation is the universal") [S19][S20] and critical psychiatry [S21], and via Sapolsky's autism-as-continuum line [S17] — *not* via disability studies, the neurodiversity movement, or autistic self-advocacy. The very word "neurodiversity" / "neurodivergence" and its history are essentially absent from the corpus; the umbrella concept, its coinage and its politics were sourced entirely externally (Singer, Blume, Sinclair, Botha et al. 2024, Walker) [S32]–[S37]. The single in-corpus exception is one first-person autistic video-transcript [S25], so first-person lived-experience voices are near-missing (Grandin and Higashida appear only second-hand inside a neuroscience book) [S15].

**The ableism brake this most needs (and the corpus cannot supply).** Stated plainly: a secular / evolutionary / Western-academic, largely *non-disabled* corpus (Sapolsky, Boehm) is here narrating disabled people's lives, and that standpoint is prone to romanticising conditions it does not experience — precisely the flattering "adaptive niche" reading this note leads with. The corpus is thin on disability studies and thin on identity-first / lived-experience perspectives; its sole first-person source is a single video transcript [S25]. Every generous inference (the sanctioned seer, the heroic systemiser) therefore had to be bound with brakes imported from outside, and the reader should treat the non-disabled provenance of the flattering story as itself a bias to discount.

**What is genuinely contested.** (1) *The specialist-niche-as-schizotypy identification* — the anthropology of sanctioned seer / shaman / healer roles is well-sourced [S1][S2][S6][S7], but naming the temperament "schizotypy" is a contested heuristic that Sapolsky himself hedges, and the older shaman-as-mentally-ill thesis (Silverman 1967) was rebutted phenomenologically by Noll (1983) [S59]; it is flagged inline as speculation, not evidence [S60]. (2) *Neurodiversity as a group-level feature* — the individual-level maintenance of variation is well-evidenced (balancing / frequency-dependent selection) [S4][S9][S11], but a group-level adaptive "insurance" function is contested selection theory; the "collective brain" evidence marshalled for it is cultural / demographic, not neuro-cognitive, and holds only "with coordinating structure" [S28][S29][S30][S31] — so group-level benefit is suggestive, not established, and cannot absorb the profound end [S61]. (3) *Hidden-advantage / "secretly gifted"* — any advantage lands only at the mild pole; the clinical disorder confers "no hidden benefit" [S4]; gene-to-temperament effects are tiny and inconsistent (DRD4 ~3–4% of variance) [S12][S13]; recent GWAS suggest the disorder / positive-trait correlations may be overstated [S14]. (4) *The prevalence "rise"* — the autism figures are surveillance / ascertainment rates (a ~5-fold spread across US sites proves it), so "rising diagnosis" is warranted but "epidemic" is not, while "purely artefactual" would overclaim [S42][S43]; the male-to-female ratio is itself partly a diagnostic artefact (girls under-ascertained; true ratio ~3:1, not 4:1) [S63]. (5) *The vocation theses* — Baron-Cohen's E–S / Extreme-Male-Brain (5.6.3) is influential but embattled (weak replication, a retracted 2017 paper, disputed prenatal-testosterone link), and its own authors insist group averages "say nothing about an individual" [S52][S54][S55]; the contemplative thesis (5.6.2) actually reverses under scrutiny — reduced mentalizing tracks toward atheism, not mysticism, and even that literature is modest and partly unreplicated [S58][S62]. (6) *Coinage* — the lone-founder "Singer 1998" story is contested by a 2024 peer-reviewed collective-origins correction [S32][S35]. (7) *Retrospective diagnosis* of historical mystics / geniuses is anachronistic, typically unfalsifiable and ethically questioned — speculation to flag, never evidence to build on [A][S60].

**Method note.** Because the corpus gives essentially no clean prevalence figures and repeatedly conflates a trait (systemising) with the clinical condition (autism), every prevalence range here is external, peer-reviewed where possible, and given as a range with the reason it varies [S42]–[S51][S63]. Where the corpus is genuinely strong — the shaman / seer specialist-niche arc and the balancing-selection mechanism [S1]–[S9][S11] — the note led with it, but bound it with the corpus's own anti-romance brakes (niche scarcity; "no hidden benefit"; tiny gene effects; the debunked "broken mirror" theory) [S2][S3][S4][S12][S13][S27], so the group-level reading is presented as plausible-but-contested, inheriting 5.2's hedge rather than asserting an adaptive "purpose."
