# 5.6.3 The technical vocation: systemisers, programming, and Silicon Valley

**Question:** Are systemising and autistic minds genuinely over-represented in programming and Silicon Valley — what does the evidence actually show, how measurement-fragile is it, and what is the ethical double-edge of treating the technical niche as a home for such minds?

## Synthesis

"Silicon Valley is autistic" is part real over-representation and part cultural hype, and the safest anchor is that *systemising* — the drive to analyse rule-governed systems — is a normally-distributed cognitive trait, not the clinical, often disabling condition of autism [S12][S21]. Software is about as pure a rule-governed system as exists, so an affinity between systemising minds and code is intelligible in principle [S21], and the data do show a real tilt: people in STEM score modestly higher on the Autism-Spectrum Quotient than non-STEM (Cohen's d ≈ 0.26), autistic college students enter STEM majors more than any other disability group (34% vs 22%), and an IT-rich Dutch region records two-to-four times the autism prevalence of comparison towns [S14][S15][S18]. But every one of these signals is small, measurement-fragile and easily inflated: the largest study explains only 3.6% of AQ variance and never asked about diagnosis, the STEM-college tilt describes a selected minority who are themselves the least likely of any disability group to reach college, and the "Geek Syndrome" assortative-mating cluster story (Silberman, *Wired* 2001; the Eindhoven study) rests on *diagnosed*-prevalence data with unexcluded ascertainment bias and a mating mechanism that was only ever hypothesised [S14][S15][S16][S18][S19][S20]. The theory linking trait to condition — Baron-Cohen's Empathizing-Systemizing / Extreme-Male-Brain account — is influential but embattled, and its strongest supporters concede their sex-brain findings apply "only to group averages, not to individuals" [S21][S22][S23]. Ethically the niche is double-edged in the DD sense, echoing 5.5's exploiter logic: tech's rule-bound, object-mediated, nominally meritocratic structure genuinely accommodates many systemising minds and has spawned real corporate hiring programmes [S25], yet it excludes the autistic majority (unemployment estimates ~40–85%), taxes the included through masking-driven burnout, and is criticised as "neurodiversity lite" tokenism — the niche helps and uses in the same motion [S24][S26][S27][S28]. The gravest pull is the flattering one, voiced inside the corpus itself as autism being "the technology genes" without which we would "still all be socializing around that campfire" [S1]: that is the deficit model's mirror-error, speculation not evidence, and the honest reading is deflationary, trait-not-condition and double-edged throughout [S4][S14][S24].

## Findings

### 1. The honest core — a cognitive trait, not a diagnosis, and a real signal wrapped in hype

The core to hold throughout is that "Silicon Valley is autistic" is part real over-representation and part hype, and that *systemising* is a cognitive trait, not a diagnosis. Baron-Cohen's Empathizing-Systemizing (E-S) framework defines systemising as the drive to analyse and build rule-governed systems [S21]; software is about as pure a rule-governed system as exists, so an affinity between systemising minds and code is intelligible in principle [S21]. But the popular slide from "high-systemising occupations contain somewhat more autistic traits" to "programmers are autistic / autistic people are natural coders" crosses from a modest, measurement-fragile statistical signal into a cultural myth [S14][S15]. The corpus itself hosts that myth in a first-person register: a diagnosed-autistic YouTuber calls autism "the technology genes" and repeats Temple Grandin's speculation that without "the genes for autism" humanity "might still all be socializing around that campfire" [S1] — exactly the flattering story this note must both explain and resist. (The campfire line is Grandin's folk-speculation relayed second-hand through the transcript; it is flagged as such and is never adopted in the note's own voice.)

### 2. What the data show — and how fragile the measurement is

The empirical seed is Baron-Cohen et al. (1997): fathers and grandfathers of autistic children were engineers roughly twice as often as those of non-autistic children [S10] — the origin of the "autism runs in engineering families" idea. That specific engineering signal was quickly contested: Jarrold & Routh's reanalysis (1998) found accountancy, science and medicine over-represented among autism fathers about as strongly, implying a general professional-class sampling bias rather than an engineering- or systemising-specific effect [S11]. The trait-continuum evidence is sturdier but must be read carefully. Using the Autism-Spectrum Quotient (AQ) they devised, Baron-Cohen et al. (2001) found Cambridge mathematics and physical-science students, and Mathematics Olympiad winners, scored higher on the AQ than humanities controls, while diagnosed Asperger / high-functioning-autistic adults averaged AQ 35.8 versus 16.4 for controls [S12] — establishing that autistic traits shade continuously into the typical population *without* implying the mathematicians were autistic [S12]. The largest self-report occupational test, Ruzich et al. (2015, n = 450,394), found people in STEM careers scored higher on the AQ (mean 21.92, SD 8.92) than non-STEM (18.92, SD 8.48), and males higher than females (21.55 vs 18.95) [S14]. Crucially the effects are *small* — Cohen's d ≈ 0.26 for STEM, ≈ 0.3 for sex — and the regression model explained only 3.6% of AQ variance; the sample self-selected (younger viewers of a medical TV programme) and autism diagnosis was never asked [S14]. This is the measurement-fragility the topic demands we flag: a real but modest tilt, easily inflated. Educational over-representation is similarly real-but-bounded: Wei et al. (2013), on the nationally representative US NLTS-2, found 34% of college students with an autism diagnosis enrolled in a STEM major versus 22% of the general population — the highest STEM rate of any disability group [S15] — but the same data show autistic students were among the *least* likely to reach college at all (roughly a third, against around half of peers with learning or speech disabilities), so the STEM tilt describes a selected minority, not autistic people in general [S15]. A separately reported diagnosis-linked finding, that Cambridge maths undergraduates self-reported autism at 1.85% versus 0.24% in controls, rests on only eight autistic individuals total and on self-report — a genuine but tiny-sample signal, easily over-read [S13].

### 3. "The Geek Syndrome" and *NeuroTribes* — the cultural story and its origins

The phrase that fused autism with Silicon Valley was journalism, not a study. Steve Silberman's "The Geek Syndrome" (*Wired*, December 2001) reported rising autism diagnoses around Silicon Valley and floated the idea that tech clustering brings sub-clinical systemisers together, compounding heritable autistic traits in their children [S16]. It framed a hypothesis; it did not test one [S16]. Silberman later expanded the theme into *NeuroTribes* (2015), a Samuel Johnson Prize-winning narrative history of autism and the neurodiversity movement — culturally important and humane, but a work of history and advocacy, not an empirical test of the assortative-mating or vocation theses [S17]. The corpus supplies the surrounding cultural imagery from a critical angle rather than the data: sociologist Judy Wajcman's "How Silicon Valley Sets Time" (via Kate Crawford) describes an elite engineering workforce "younger, more masculine and more fully committed to working all hours," building efficiency tools premised on a winner-takes-all ideal [S5], and Max Fisher's reporting characterises the Valley's "contrarian, brash, almost millenarian engineering subculture" of "misfit twentysomethings" [S6]. These are the raw materials of the "Silicon Valley is autistic" trope — but they are portraits of a *culture and a labour regime*, not evidence about autism, and conflating the two is precisely the move to avoid [S5][S6].

### 4. Assortative mating and the tech-hub clustering hypothesis

The clustering hypothesis has one much-cited datum. Roelfsema et al. (2012) surveyed 62,505 Dutch schoolchildren and found autism-spectrum prevalence in IT-rich Eindhoven of 229 per 10,000 — roughly two-to-four times the rates in Haarlem (84) and Utrecht (57) — while control conditions such as ADHD and dyspraxia were similar across the three regions [S18]. That specificity is striking and is the strongest single piece of evidence for a tech-hub autism concentration [S18]. But it does not establish the assortative-mating mechanism, and the study's own limits are decisive: it measured *diagnosed* prevalence, not true prevalence, and did not test or confirm assortative mating — the authors only hypothesised it [S18][S19]. Co-author Rosa Hoekstra explicitly flagged the live alternative that autistic children "may more often remain undetected" in the comparison regions, i.e. ascertainment and awareness bias is an unexcluded explanation for the gap [S19]. On the mating mechanism itself the evidence is real but cuts two ways. Assortative mating is genuine and, for *diagnosed* autism, not trivial — Nordsletten et al. found autistic adults roughly ten-to-twelve times more likely to partner with another autistic person (spouse correlation r ≈ 0.47, ≈ 0.28 after general-population correction) [S20]. But assortative mating on the *systemising / empathising traits* the Geek-Syndrome mechanism actually requires is weaker and mixed, and — decisively — neither trait-level nor diagnosis-level assortative mating has ever been shown to produce the geographic prevalence differences [S20]. So the vivid story — "systemisers migrated to tech hubs, paired up, and bred an autism cluster" — remains an unproven hypothesis and should be presented as one, not as an established cause [S18][S19][S20].

### 5. Trait is not condition; the theory behind the link is contested

Two disciplines of caution are mandatory. First, trait is not condition: systemising and a high AQ are a normally-distributed cognitive style, whereas autism is a clinical, frequently disabling condition; the studies enrich high-systemising fields for autistic *traits and diagnoses* modestly, they do not show that engineers "are autistic" [S12][S14]. Second, the theoretical scaffolding — Baron-Cohen's E-S and Extreme-Male-Brain (EMB) account, which frames autism as a "masculinised" cognitive profile of low empathising plus intact or high systemising [S21] — is influential but embattled, not consensus. Its strongest support, Greenberg et al. (PNAS 2018, n = 671,606, with a further 14,354-person replication sample), found the predicted directions — systemising-minus-empathising "D-scores" larger in males, larger still in autistic people — and is real, quantitative support [S22]; but the authors themselves impose the key brake: the differences "apply only to group averages, not to individuals" and "say nothing about an individual based on their gender, autism diagnosis, or occupation," and using them to do so "would constitute stereotyping" [S22]. Beyond Baron-Cohen's own group the theory has weak independent replication (Meng-Chuan Lai), the self-report EQ/SQ/AQ instruments are accused of circular, gender-stereotyped design, a prominent 2017 neuroimaging paper supporting EMB was retracted for an analysis error, the prenatal-testosterone link is disputed, and the theory fits autistic women poorly [S23]. The corpus transmits EMB in its *approving* register — Sapolsky relays Baron-Cohen's "analytical focus at the cost of social empathy" and prenatal-androgenisation story as a "convincing argument" that a masculinised profile "taken to its extreme, results in autism" [S2] — even while, in the same lectures, conceding that in humans the prenatal-testosterone evidence is only "maybe just some hints of this effect, if any" [S2]. A frequently-cited neuroscience anchor beneath the caricature is narrower and, on inspection, contested rather than hard: a dated corpus lecture reports the fusiform face area — strongly face-selective in most people — as showing uniformly low activation in autistic individuals ("mother… stranger… armchair" all low) [S3], but the primary literature is mixed and that hypoactivation is substantially attenuated by attention, familiarity and eye-fixation, so it illustrates the "objects not faces" idea rather than proving it (properly a 5.1 / neurological-basis question). What the first-person corpus material more robustly supports is milder: autistic accounts describe effortful, rule-based *compensation* for a conceptual system that does not run automatically [S4] — a jagged, variable profile, "a menu of different possibilities," not a uniform upgrade [S4][S9].

### 6. The double edge — accommodation and exploitation in the same motion

Here is the DD-relevant ethical point, echoing 5.5's double-edge logic and the 5.6 umbrella: the same niche that helps also uses. On the accommodation side, tech's structure is a genuine fit for many systemising minds — work that is rule-bound, object- rather than face-mediated (a claim the contested fusiform finding illustrates rather than proves [S3]), and at least nominally meritocratic in its output. Corporate "neurodiversity hiring" has institutionalised this: Specialisterne (founded in Denmark by Thorkil Sonne), SAP's "Autism at Work" (which committed to 1% autistic staffing and redesigned interviews away from social performance toward work-sample tasks, with post-hire support), Microsoft's autism hiring, and DXC's Dandelion programme all treat autistic cognition as talent and restructure the environment around it [S25]. That is a real accommodation, and it matters. But the same motion excludes and taxes. First, the flattering niche coexists with catastrophic underemployment: autistic-adult unemployment estimates range widely by definition and source — roughly 40% up to 80–85%, with Bernick & Holden (2015) estimating ~60–70% for autistic Americans, only about 21% of employed autistic people in full-time work, and US young adults leaving school the least likely of any disability group to have worked — while in the UK the National Autistic Society found only ~16% of autistic adults in full-time paid work and ~32% in any paid work, with 43% reporting they had lost a job because of autism-related discrimination [S24]. The STEM-hero image therefore describes a fortunate slice while most autistic people are locked out of the labour market entirely [S24]. Second, even the included pay a hidden tax: the accommodation is often conditional on *masking* — suppressing autistic traits to meet neurotypical workplace norms — which the occupational-health literature links directly to "autistic burnout": extreme fatigue, withdrawal and loss of functioning [S27]. Third, critics argue the programmes themselves are frequently "neurodiversity lite": symbolic inclusion that channels a cherry-picked, "high-functioning" minority into narrow, stereotyped technical roles while leaving structural barriers intact, at times shading into tokenism [S26][S28]. The preference for "high-functioning" profiles is itself questionable, since there is no established link between that former medical category and greater professional competence, and disability advocates (e.g. Josef Schovanec) note most autistic people are neither interested in nor able to make a living from IT [S24]. The corpus's own critical-theory strand supplies the ambient exploitation frame — the masculinised, all-hours Valley labour regime [S5], its "millenarian" engineering subculture [S6], and, by analogy, tech's reliance on hidden, psychologically taxed and disposable labour elsewhere in the stack (content moderators, not autistic engineers) [S7] — while its retreat-dialogue register supplies the dignity test that should govern any accommodation: autonomy versus powerlessness, and the insistence that workers "speak for themselves about what work is" rather than be spoken for [S8]. Held together, the niche is real, narrow and double-edged: it accommodates some systemising minds and, in the same structure, overworks the ones it admits and excludes the majority it will not.

### 7. Resisting the corpus's romance (and the mirror-error)

The gravest pull in this topic is the flattering one, and the corpus leans into it. "Difference not deficit" is a legitimate reframing — the corpus's own Sapolsky material calls the disorder/diversity boundary "arbitrary" and places autism on a continuum [S9] — but the reframing has a mirror-error: the story that "every autistic person is secretly a gifted coder" is the deficit model inverted, and it is contradicted by the same evidence that grounds the modest over-representation [S14][S15][S24]. Autism spans profound, minimally-verbal, high-support presentations that no "technology genes" narrative describes [S4], and the "campfire" / "technology genes" trope reproduced in the corpus [S1] is speculation, not evidence — of a piece with the wider, methodologically fraught habit of retrospectively diagnosing historical or fictional "geniuses" as autistic. The honest formulation is deflationary and dual: systemising minds are somewhat over-represented in software and STEM, by a real but small and measurement-fragile margin [S14][S15]; the "Silicon Valley is autistic" and assortative-mating-cluster stories are part signal and part hype, and the mating mechanism is unproven [S18][S19][S20]; the trait is not the condition [S12][S21][S22]; and the technical niche accommodates and exploits in the same gesture [S24][S26][S27].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

The YaTTi shared corpus — curated around Boehm, Sapolsky, Graeber, Batchelor and Bregman — shaped this answer by supplying the flattering frame and almost none of the discipline. Its only substantive material on the tech-vocation question is (a) the romantic "autism = the technology genes" trope, voiced first-person by a diagnosed-autistic YouTuber and echoing Grandin's "campfire" speculation [S1]; (b) Sapolsky relaying Baron-Cohen's Extreme-Male-Brain account in its approving register as a "convincing argument" [S2]; and (c) a critical-theory picture of Silicon Valley's masculinised, all-hours labour regime (Wajcman via Crawford [S5]; Fisher's "millenarian" engineering subculture [S6]; the hidden exploited data-labour of the AI stack [S7]). The corpus is heavily Sapolsky- and evolutionary-biology-weighted, pathology-derived, and contains essentially *no* empirical STEM-autism data, no Geek-Syndrome / assortative-mating literature, no autistic-employment statistics, and nothing from disability studies, autistic self-advocacy, or the neurodiversity-at-work critique. Every load-bearing empirical claim therefore came from external, mostly peer-reviewed sources [S10]–[S28]. Without that external balancing the note would have inherited the corpus's pull toward the "systemisers built the modern world / autism is the technology gene" hero narrative; the external sourcing is what supplies the deflationary, double-edged, trait-not-condition reading the topic requires.

What is genuinely contested, and must not be presented as settled: **(1) the "Silicon Valley is autistic" cluster** — the one strong datum (Eindhoven, 229 vs 84/57 per 10,000) measured *diagnosed* prevalence with unexcluded ascertainment bias, the mating mechanism was only hypothesised (Silberman's *Wired* piece is journalism), and while assortative mating on *diagnosis* is strong it has never been shown to produce geographic clustering — an unproven hypothesis, not an established cause [S16][S18][S19][S20]. **(2) The strength of the over-representation** — the occupational AQ effect is small (d ≈ 0.26; 3.6% of variance; self-selected sample), the engineering-specific signal is confounded by general professional-class over-representation (Jarrold & Routh), and the clearest STEM tilt (Wei, 34% vs 22%) holds only among the minority of autistic people who reach college: real but modest, easily inflated [S11][S14][S15]. **(3) E-S / EMB theory** — the strongest support (PNAS 2018) is directional but applies only to group averages, replication beyond Baron-Cohen's group is weak, the instruments are accused of circular gender-stereotyped design, a 2017 EMB neuroimaging paper was retracted, and the theory fits autistic women poorly: influential but embattled [S21][S22][S23]. **(4) Trait vs condition** — the studies enrich high-systemising fields for autistic traits and diagnoses; they do not equate a normally-distributed cognitive style with a clinical, often disabling condition that includes profound, minimally-verbal presentations [S4][S12][S14]. **(5) The "technology genes" / retrospective-genius story** is speculation reproduced inside the corpus, the deficit model's mirror-error, and belongs to the fraught genre of retrospectively diagnosing historical or fictional geniuses — flagged as speculation, never built on [S1][S4]. **(6) Every rate figure varies** by definition, instrument, era, country and clinical-versus-community sampling, and is given here as a range with its reason (autistic unemployment ~40–85%; AQ gaps a few points with small effect sizes; Eindhoven rates flagged as *diagnosed*, tracking ascertainment), never a single point estimate [S14][S24].

Four structural caveats bound the whole evidence base. **First and strongest, single-lab dependence:** nearly the entire empirical backbone of the vocation-link — Baron-Cohen 1997 (engineering fathers), 2001 (AQ), 2007 (Cambridge maths), Roelfsema 2012 (Eindhoven), Ruzich 2015 (AQ × STEM) and Greenberg 2018 (E-S/EMB) — is the output of *one* research group, the Cambridge Autism Research Centre, with a prior theoretical commitment to E-S/EMB; the genuinely independent data points are a critique (Jarrold & Routh [S11]) and one national-cohort study (Wei / NLTS-2 [S15]), so researcher-allegiance bias is a live confound across the whole literature, not just the EMB theory [S10][S12][S13][S14][S18][S22]. **Second, gendered ascertainment:** the male skew of *both* autism diagnosis and tech employment is partly an artefact of historical female under-diagnosis and masking, which mechanically inflates the "extreme male brain" and "systemising is male" story — the sex-difference data are themselves confounded by who gets counted, and the theory's poor fit for autistic women is a symptom of this, not a footnote [S23]. **Third, reverse causation:** the occupational AQ correlations are cross-sectional self-report and cannot distinguish "systemisers self-select into tech" from "tech training and culture cultivate systemising" from "a geek identity inflating self-reported AQ" — the direction of causation is unresolved [S14]. **Fourth, the fusiform anchor is over-stated:** the "uniformly low activation… mother… stranger… armchair" characterisation [S3] comes from a dated Sapolsky lecture and is far more uniform than the primary literature supports (FFA hypoactivation in autism is attenuated by attention, familiarity and eye-fixation, and the findings are mixed), so it can illustrate the object-not-faces idea but cannot ground "tech is a genuine cognitive fit" — treat it as contested, a 5.1 / neurological-basis matter.

- [S21] Baron-Cohen (2009), *Autism: The Empathizing-Systemizing (E-S) Theory*, Annals of the NY Academy of Sciences 1156:68–80 — https://nyaspubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1749-6632.2009.04467.x — defines systemising (analysing rule-based systems) as an independent dimension and frames autism as an extreme-male / masculinised profile: a *theory* of autism, and the intelligibility basis for the systemising–code affinity.
- [S22] Greenberg, Warrier, Allison & Baron-Cohen (2018), *Testing the Empathizing-Systemizing theory of sex differences and the Extreme Male Brain theory of autism in half a million people*, PNAS 115(48):12152–12157 (n = 671,606, plus a 14,354-person replication) — https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1811032115 ; with the authors' "group averages, not individuals" brake (Univ. of Cambridge / EurekAlert, 2018) — https://www.eurekalert.org/news-releases/875062 — strongest quantitative support for E-S/EMB *and* the authors' own brake that the differences say nothing about an individual and using them so would be stereotyping.
- [S23] The Transmitter (Spectrum), *The extreme male brain, explained* — https://www.thetransmitter.org/spectrum/extreme-male-brain-explained/ — EMB contestation: weak independent replication beyond Baron-Cohen's group (Lai), a retracted 2017 EMB neuroimaging paper, disputed prenatal-testosterone link, and poor fit for autistic women.
- [S24] *Employment of autistic people*, Wikipedia (aggregating National Autistic Society 2016/2021 surveys, Roux/Shattuck NLTS-2, Bernick & Holden 2015, and Josef Schovanec) — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Employment_of_autistic_people — autistic-adult unemployment ~40–85% (Bernick & Holden ~60–70% US; ~21% of employed in full-time work; UK NAS ~16% full-time / ~32% any paid work; 43% lost a job to autism-related discrimination); Schovanec that most autistic people are not interested in or able to make a living from IT; programmes favour "high-functioning" despite no proven competence link — a secondary aggregator, with underlying primaries attributed inline.
- [S25] Specialisterne USA (founded in Denmark by Thorkil Sonne) — https://us.specialisterne.com/ ; SAP "Autism at Work" (1% staffing commitment; work-sample interviews) and DXC "Dandelion" programme, per US DOL/ODEP autism page — https://www.dol.gov/agencies/odep/program-areas/autism — concrete corporate neurodiversity-hiring programmes that restructure hiring and work around systemising cognition (the genuine-accommodation side of the double-edge).
- [S26] Dasborough (2026), *Neurodiversity at Work: A Hiring Revolution or Just "Lite" Inclusion?*, Journal of Organizational Behavior, doi:10.1002/job.70045 — https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/job.70045 — critique that many neurodiversity-hiring initiatives are "neurodiversity lite": symbolic inclusion channelling a cherry-picked minority into narrow, stereotyped technical roles while structural barriers remain (title/abstract-level; full text paywalled).
- [S27] *The workplace masking experiences of autistic, non-autistic neurodivergent and neurotypical adults in the UK*, Autism (2023) — https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10482295/ ; and Hazlett et al.
