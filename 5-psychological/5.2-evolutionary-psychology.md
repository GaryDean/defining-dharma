# 5.2 Evolutionary Psychology

**Question:** From an evolutionary psychology perspective, how do dharmic frameworks exploit
cognitive biases and social instincts to promote cooperation? What are the adaptive advantages?

## Synthesis

Evolutionarily, dharmas are best understood as cultural systems that solve the **cooperation /
free-rider problem** by recruiting two evolved resources and adding a third device. They recruit
(1) **cognitive biases** — hyperactive agency detection, a taste for "minimally counterintuitive"
concepts, confirmation bias — that make supernatural and sacred ideas *catchy* and transmissible
[S5]; and (2) **social instincts** — reciprocity, reputation, and kin-feeling extended by
pseudo-kinship — that make cooperation feel obligatory [S1][S3]. To these they add (3) **hard-to-fake
commitment devices**: costly ritual and *credibility-enhancing displays* (CREDs) that filter
free-riders and forge trust [S6][S3][S7]. The headline adaptive evidence is Sosis & Bressler's
finding that 19th-century **religious communes outlived secular ones (≈25 vs ≈5 years)**, and that
*stricter* communes lasted longer — but **only** when the strictness was religiously framed [S6].
Two cautions are essential. First, the *framing* matters: dharmas don't "exploit" biases by design;
**blind cultural selection** favours whichever variants happen to leverage them, and the result is
"neither adaptation nor exaptation — it's both" [S4]. Second, **adaptive ≠ true ≠ good**: the
mechanisms that build in-group solidarity are *often* implicated in **out-group hostility** (parochial
altruism) [S8] — though how tightly the two are coupled is now contested (a natural-groups field test
found conflict depressed out-group cooperation while leaving in-group cooperation untouched [S11]) —
and survival-value says nothing about truth or rightness ([[3.3-grounding-problem]]). And the
reflexive sting for a *secular* dharma: the durability engine Sosis identified — costly signalling
anchored to *unfalsifiable* supernatural claims — is exactly what secularism renounces (→
[[6.1-secular-translation]]).

## Findings

### The framing: adaptation, by-product, or exploitation?
Evolutionary accounts of religion/dharma sort into a menu of hypotheses: a **group-level adaptation**
(Durkheim's "secular utility" — religions "forge human groups into cooperative units"); an
**exploitation** device (some members profit at others' expense — Marx); a **cultural parasite**
(memes spreading for their own sake — Dawkins); a **by-product** of cognition; an **adaptation now
mismatched** to modern environments; or **drift** [S1]. D.S. Wilson's verdict — that "most enduring
religions are impressively adaptive at the group level" [S1] — is the corpus's house position, but
the honest synthesis is pluralist: a large tradition contains elements of *all* of these, and the
adaptation-vs-by-product question is, as one corpus critic insists, "very naively framed" — religion
is "neither an adaptation nor an exaptation; it's both" [S4].

### Cognitive biases dharmas leverage (the by-product machinery)
Several evolved quirks make supernatural/sacred concepts cognitively "sticky":
- **Hyperactive agency detection (HADD).** We over-attribute events to intentional agents — Guthrie's
  anthropomorphism, formalised by Barrett as a "hypersensitive" agency-detector biased to find minds
  from minimal cues — predisposing us to gods, spirits, ancestors [S5].
- **Minimally counterintuitive concepts (Boyer).** Ideas that violate *just one or two* intuitive
  expectations (an invisible but mind-having agent) are "unusual enough to capture attention yet
  simple enough to be transmitted across generations" — proposed as optimal cultural survivors [S5].
  ▲ The experimental support is narrower than the theory: Berl et al.'s transmission-chain test of six
  content biases found the minimally-counterintuitive effect held for *biological* violations only, and
  not for mental or physical ones — so the bullet should be read as partly confirmed, not settled [S12].
- **Confirmation bias.** We notice the hits and forget the misses (Bacon: "men observe when a thing
  hits, but not when it misses"), which entrenches the inference that prayer or ritual "works" [S5].

Atran's synthesis: religion is "a converging by-product of several cognitive and emotional mechanisms
that evolved under natural selection for mundane adaptive tasks" [S5]. On this *content-bias* (EP)
view, the catchiness of the ideas explains their spread [S3].

### Social instincts dharmas harness
Dharmas plug into the cooperation toolkit catalogued in [[1.2-universal-patterns]]: **reciprocity and
reputation** (do-as-you-would-be-done-by, enforced by gossip — reputation functioning, in Milinski's
review of the experimental literature, as "a universal currency for human social interactions" [S14]);
**kin selection extended by pseudo-kinship** (co-religionists as "brothers and sisters," lineage
invoked in oaths); and **conformist and prestige biases** that make norms copy faithfully
([[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]]). ▲ The kin-selection limb needs the heaviest hedge. A
preregistered Bayesian meta-analysis of real-world microfinance repayment — a genuine cooperation
dilemma, largely non-WEIRD — found only *punishment* robustly associated with cooperation; kin
relatedness came out weakly **negative** (a 65% posterior probability that more-related loan groups
repay *less*), amid high heterogeneity the authors read as pointing to "non-generalisable findings
and/or reproducibility issues" [S13]. So "kin-feeling extended by pseudo-kinship" is a plausible
proximate story, not an established driver of real cooperation, and this note should not lean on it
harder than the evidence allows [A].
A recurrent amplifier is **supernatural monitoring and punishment**: belief in watchful, punitive
agents deters cheating and stabilises cooperation [S3] (the "Big Gods" hypothesis — but note the
serious empirical challenge that moralising gods tended to *follow* social complexity rather than
cause it, flagged in [[2.2-urban-complexity]]).

### The commitment engine: costly signalling and CREDs (the adaptive core)
The distinctive evolutionary mechanism is **costly signalling**. Because any cooperative group is a
target for free-riders, groups that demand *expensive, hard-to-fake* behaviours filter out the
uncommitted: Iannaccone's "**strict churches are strong**" — dietary rules, dress codes, time-costly
ritual act as a screening deposit [S6]. The cleanest test is **Sosis & Bressler (2003)**: of 83
19th-century American communes, the religious ones had a median lifespan ≈25 years vs ≈5 for secular,
and *within* the religious set more costly requirements predicted greater longevity — an effect
**absent** for secular communes [S6]. The Thaipusam Kavadi ritual (Mauritius) has become the standard
field test of costly signalling, and it can now be cited to preregistered fieldwork rather than a
secondary summary: Klocová et al.'s study of Tamil Hindu (in-group) and Christian (out-group)
participants (N = 369) tested whether committed members simply estimate ritual *costs* as lower — the
classic Sosis account — or whether something else keeps the ritual persistent [S9]. Their result
complicates the tidy version: in-group members estimated *physical* costs as lower but *material*
costs as **higher**, so the cost story is mixed, and what separated in-group from out-group cleanly
was the estimation of *benefits* — including supernaturally induced benefits (healing, blessing,
protection) that only committed members perceive. The authors conclude that "differential estimation
of ritual benefits, not costs, are key to the persistence of extreme rituals," and that "this
connection between supernatural benefits, costly signals, and social norms may explain why religions
are powerful adaptive systems regulating collective action" [S9]. This is a *sharper* mechanism than
the old "costly ritual forges solidarity" gloss: free-riders self-exclude not because the ordeal is
expensive but because, not sharing the belief, they never perceive the pay-off that would justify it
[A]. Henrich (2009) sharpened "costly signals" into **credibility-enhancing displays (CREDs)**:
beliefs spread when adherents act in ways that would be costly *if they were faking*, so "walking the
walk" makes even improbable beliefs *imitable* — and, crucially, this solves *transmission to the
next generation*, which strictness-as-filter alone cannot [S7]. That mechanism now has a direct field
test outside the usual Abrahamic/industrialised samples: Singh & Henrich's experiment among Mentawai
shamans in Siberut pitted three accounts of self-denial against each other — cooperative signalling,
CREDs, and "supernatural otherness" — and found evidence for **all three**, observers inferring that
self-denying shamans are more cooperative, more sincere in belief, and more supernaturally powerful,
with the strongest effect on perceived belief [S10].

### Adaptive advantages
The pay-offs: **durability** (Sosis's longevity gap [S6]); **trust and solidarity** within the group;
**cooperation scaled beyond the face-to-face band** ([[2.2-urban-complexity]]); robust **common-pool-
resource management** (Wilson's test links religion to Ostrom's design principles —
[[3.1-tension-resolution]]) [S1]; and, over history, **cultural group selection** — groups with
better cooperation-enhancing dharmas out-compete and out-last their rivals [S1][S3].

### The costs, the limits, and the reflexive bite
- **Parochial altruism — cooperation's dark twin.** Choi & Bowles show in-group altruism and
  out-group hostility can *coevolve*: "neither parochialism nor altruism would have been viable singly,"
  but together they win intergroup conflicts [S8]. The very instinct that bonds the in-group arms it
  against outsiders (the oxytocin "tend-and-defend" of [[5.1-neurological-basis]]; the dharmic
  tribalism of [[3.1-tension-resolution]]). ▲ But the coevolution thesis is a model, and the empirical
  picture is more equivocal than the note previously implied. Silva & Mace's naturalistic field
  experiments with Catholics and Protestants in Northern Ireland (donations, lost-letter returns)
  found that conflict reduced *out-group* cooperation but — against the theory's central prediction —
  showed "no evidence that it influences in-group cooperation," with socio-economic status the dominant
  factor; the authors frame this explicitly as "a challenge to dominant perspectives on the origins of
  human cooperation" [S11]. Rusch adds a modelling critique: the symmetric n-person-prisoner's-dilemma
  underlying Choi & Bowles makes *all* war participation altruistic by definition, and once loot,
  status and mate motives are separated the historical data show in-group altruism clustering in
  **defence** rather than attack — "call[ing] for a refinement of theories" [S18]. The bonding/hostility
  link is real but neither automatic nor symmetric — which matters directly for [[7.1-failure-modes]],
  whose "same machinery, aimed outward" framing this counter-evidence should temper [A].
- **Strictness has limits.** Push costs too high and the group cannot reproduce — the Shakers'
  celibacy made them maximally committed *and* drove them to near-extinction [S6].
- **The secular challenge.** Sosis argued religious communes outlasted secular ones because religious
  ritual is anchored to *unfalsifiable* supernatural claims, whereas secular utopias make testable
  claims experience can refute [S6]. So a *secular* dharma forgoes the strongest durability mechanism
  evolution discovered — a structural problem it must solve some other way ([[6.1-secular-translation]],
  [[5.4-ritual-habituation]]). The Klocová mechanism sharpens the sting: if what filters free-riders is
  the *perceived supernatural benefit* only believers see [S9], a dharma that renounces supernatural
  claims removes the very quantity that made the filter work [A].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The corpus's adaptationist / group-selection lean.** Much of the corpus's evolution-of-religion
  material flows from David Sloan Wilson and **ProSocial World** (of which Wilson is president), a
  Templeton-funded, explicitly group-selectionist, "religion-is-adaptive" programme [S1][S3]. It
  favours the Durkheimian group-functional reading and can under-weight the **by-product** camp
  (Boyer, Atran, Dawkins) — which is why that camp is anchored here from outside [S5]. Group selection
  itself, though revived as multilevel selection, remains contested.
- **The just-so-story problem — flagged from *inside* the corpus, now with quantitative backing.** The
  corpus contains a blistering critique ("evolutionary psychology is neither"): adaptive stories (mate
  preferences, waist-to-hip ratios) repeatedly collapse once confounds are controlled; "an evolutionary
  explanation is [wrongly assumed] ipso facto an adaptive explanation"; the human mind shows
  "adaptability, not adaptedness"; and EP's methods "would not meet the standards of any other science"
  [S4]. That hedge no longer rests on a polemic alone: Foster et al.'s preregistered meta-analysis of
  73 effect estimates across a real-world cooperation dilemma found most mechanisms mixed or
  non-generalisable, with only punishment robust and kin selection weakly negative [S13]. When the
  cooperation mechanisms this note invokes are tested at scale in the field, most do not replicate
  cleanly — so adaptationist claims here are hedged as plausible, not proven.
- **"Exploit" is loaded.** Dharmas don't *intend* to exploit biases; blind cultural (and genetic)
  selection retains variants that happen to leverage them. The question's verb smuggles in a designer.
- **Adaptive ≠ true ≠ good.** Evolutionary success is amoral; supernatural beliefs need not be *true*
  to be adaptive (the by-product point), and adaptiveness grounds no *ought* (the is/ought wall,
  [[3.3-grounding-problem]]). A maximally cohesive dharma can be maximally cruel to outsiders [S8].
- **Most evidence is "religion," not "dharma" broadly.** The costly-signalling/CREDs literature studies
  *religious* groups; extending it to secular or philosophical dharmas is an inference — and Sosis's
  data actively warn that the extension may fail [S6].
- **The WEIRD complaint, partly answered.** The note's evidence used to sit overwhelmingly in
  Abrahamic and industrialised populations. Two of the new anchors move it out: the Klocová Thaipusam
  study is Tamil Hindu / Mauritian [S9], and the Singh & Henrich CREDs test is among Mentawai
  shamans in a small-scale animist setting [S10]. The costly-signalling machinery is now attested well
  beyond the WEIRD frame, which strengthens the cross-cultural generality the note claims.
- **Provenance repaired at the core.** An earlier version rested its two headline empirical claims —
  the Thaipusam result and Henrich's CREDs — on a YouTube video transcript (a ReligionForBreakfast
  episode, now removed from the note) and a paywalled abstract [S7]. Both now carry full-text
  peer-reviewed anchors [S9][S10], several of the
  new sources *qualify* rather than reinforce (kin selection [S13], parochial altruism [S11][S18], the
  MCI effect [S12]), and the note is deliberately more hedged as a result. What remains soft: the
  supernatural-monitoring/"Big Gods" line still leans on a prosocial.world post [S3], and the
  group-selection reading is still carried by Wilson's own programme [S1].
