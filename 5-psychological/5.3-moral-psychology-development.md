# 5.3 Moral Psychology & Development

**Question:** What does moral psychology (e.g. Haidt's moral foundations, Kohlberg's stages of moral
development) and the WEIRD-sample critique tell us about how dharmic intuitions form and vary across
populations?

## Synthesis

Moral psychology reframes a dharma as a **cultural recipe written over a small set of evolved moral
intuitions**. Haidt's Moral Foundations Theory holds that judgment is *intuition-first* — "the
emotional dog and its rational tail," with reasoning largely post-hoc — and rests on a handful of
innate-but-tunable "foundations" or "taste buds of the moral mind": care, fairness, liberty, loyalty,
authority, sanctity (and likely more) [S1][S2][S7]. Cultures *tune* these differently, so different
dharmas are different *settings* over a shared palette — and, tellingly, **WEIRD-liberal morality
fires mainly on care + fairness + liberty, while most of humanity's traditions weight the "binding"
foundations (loyalty, authority, sanctity) just as heavily** [S1][S3]. Kohlberg's developmental
ladder (pre-conventional → conventional → post-conventional) captures real sequencing but is dated
and skewed — Gilligan showed it was androcentric (justice over care), and cross-cultural work shows
the stages don't progress inevitably [S4][S6]. The **WEIRD critique** (Henrich, Heine & Norenzayan) is
the hammer: behavioural science built "human nature" from Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich,
Democratic samples that are in fact global *outliers* on fairness, cooperation and moral reasoning
[S5] — so dharmic intuitions **vary systematically across populations**, and the project's own thin
floor (suffering-reduction = the care foundation) may be a WEIRD-parochial projection
([[3.4-relativism-universalism]], [[7.2-appropriation]]). On *formation*: intuitions come first, but
they are not fixed — they are "the end products of learning," our guts trained by past reasoning
([[5.4-ritual-habituation]]).

## Findings

### Haidt: morality is intuition-first, built on tunable foundations
Haidt's two core claims. First, the **social intuitionist model**: most moral judgment is fast,
affective intuition, with conscious reasoning arriving afterward as justification — the "emotional
dog and its rational tail" [S7]. Second, **Moral Foundations Theory**: humans share a few evolved
"foundations" — care/harm, fairness/cheating, liberty/oppression, loyalty/betrayal,
authority/subversion, sanctity/degradation — which Haidt calls "the evolved taste buds of the moral
mind," explicitly open-ended ("how many foundations are there?... property, honesty, [dislike of]
wastefulness" are further candidates; "evolution didn't give a damn about parsimony") [S2]. A dharma,
on this view, is a *culturally specific cuisine* built from universal tastebuds — which is exactly
why dharmas can be both recognisably moral and wildly variable [S2].

### The "binding" foundations and the WEIRD-liberal split
Both experimental and real-world data show **liberals weight the first three foundations (care,
fairness, liberty); conservatives weight all six**, especially the "binding" trio loyalty, authority,
sanctity [S1][S3]. These binding foundations have a real *function* — they "increase the benefits of
living in [an] in-group" (family, tribe, religion) — and a real *cost*: they license demonising
out-groups and dissenters [S1]. There is a live interpretive fight: conservatives read liberals as
"morally impoverished, with half their foundations atrophied"; Jost and Greene read liberals as
*refined*, having "jettisoned the less important, more historically damaging" foundations [S3]. That
this fight is unresolved is itself the point — *which* foundations a dharma elevates is a value
choice, not a fact (the is/ought wall, [[3.3-grounding-problem]]).

### Kohlberg's stages — and why they fracture
Building on Piaget's developmental sequences, Kohlberg proposed six stages of moral *reasoning*,
grouped as **pre-conventional** (self-interest: avoid punishment, seek reward), **conventional**
(conformity to group norms, law and order), and **post-conventional** (universal principles that can
*override* law — the realm of principled civil disobedience) [S4]. Three serious critiques: (1)
**gender bias** — Kohlberg's 1960s samples were largely male, and Gilligan's *In a Different Voice*
showed the scoring ranked women's more relational, care-based reasoning as "less mature," when it is a
*different, equally valid orientation* ("ethic of care" vs "ethic of justice") [S4][S6]; (2)
**cultural bias** — cross-cultural studies find the stages do *not* progress in an inevitable
sequence, and "post-conventional/principled" reasoning skews toward WEIRD, individualist populations
[S4]; (3) the **intuitionist challenge** — Haidt argues Kohlberg over-rationalised morality, mistaking
post-hoc justification for the engine [S7]. (Later work qualifies Gilligan: both orientations appear
across genders, with context and culture outweighing sex [S6].)

### The WEIRD critique: the variation is systematic
Henrich, Heine & Norenzayan (2010) showed that behavioural scientists routinely generalise about
"human" psychology from **WEIRD** samples that are, across domain after domain — fairness and
cooperation (Ultimatum/Dictator games), analytic vs holistic cognition, self-concept, *moral
reasoning* — among the *least* representative humans on Earth, "frequent outliers"; yet 90%+ of
subjects remain WEIRD [S5]. The upshot for dharma: cross-population moral variation is not noise but
*structured* — Henrich's later work ties it to **kin-intensity** (societies built on dense kin
networks score differently from individualist ones; the West became WEIRD partly via the Church's
dissolution of clans, [[2.2-urban-complexity]]). So "how dharmic intuitions vary across populations"
has a real answer: along an autonomy-vs-embeddedness axis that tracks social structure.

### How dharmic intuitions *form*
Three mechanisms, in tension and combination:
- **Intuition-first, reason-after** (Haidt) — the dharma's doctrines often *rationalise* pre-existing
  gut intuitions rather than generating them [S7].
- **Innate "first draft," revised by culture** — the foundations are "organised in advance of
  experience" but *tuned* by upbringing; the moral/conventional distinction children draw early
  ([[1.3-boundary-definition]]) shows some structure is precocious [A].
- **Intuitions are learned automaticities** — crucially, Sapolsky (with Woodward & Allman) insists
  moral intuitions "are neither primordial nor reflexively primitive... they are the end products of
  learning." Our gut conviction that slavery, child labour or cruelty is wrong is *recent*, built by
  "the fierce moral reasoning and activism of those who came before us" — "our guts learn their
  intuitions" [S3]. This dissolves the false either/or of innate-vs-cultural and grounds dharma's
  *training* function ([[5.4-ritual-habituation]]).

### How they *vary* — Shweder's three ethics and the Golden Rule's underdetermination
Shweder's cross-cultural framework — the ethics of **autonomy** (harm, fairness), **community**
(loyalty, authority), and **divinity** (purity, sanctity) — maps directly onto Haidt's foundations and
names the main axes of variation [S3]. Against this variety there *are* near-universals (condemnation
of some murder/theft; the Golden Rule), but the Golden Rule is famously underdetermined — it founders
on the masochist who says "beat me" — so the durable universal is a *generalised reciprocity* (give
others the concern you'd want in their place) rather than a fixed content [S3]. Shermer's
"provisional morality" captures the working stance: treat widespread norms as presumptively important,
"but watch your wallet" [S3].

### Greene's two tragedies: why dharmas *clash*
Joshua Greene's *Moral Tribes* distinguishes the **tragedy of the commons** (Me vs Us — selfishness
within a group, which dharmas solve) from the **tragedy of commonsense morality** (Us vs Them — two
groups with *internally coherent but mutually incompatible* moral systems) [S3]. The second is the
dharma-clash problem of [[3.4-relativism-universalism]]: each side experiences its parochial intuition
as a self-evident "right," when "right" is often "enough post-hoc rationalisation slathered on a
shapeless, self-serving, parochial moral intuition" [S3].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The reflexive WEIRD bite (the headline).** The secular-dharma corpus is a WEIRD-liberal product,
  and it *shows*: a corpus essay proposes "increasing flourishing and reducing harm" as morality's
  *ultimate goal* [S1] — i.e. the **care/harm** foundation, the WEIRD-liberal 2–3-foundation palette —
  and treats the binding foundations (loyalty/authority/sanctity) chiefly as pathology (Pinker: the
  source of "most of the violence in human history") [S1]. MFT + WEIRD give empirical teeth to
  [[3.4-relativism-universalism]]'s worry: the project's thin floor may be a parochial projection, and
  "non-privileging" requires seeing the *function* of the binding foundations most dharmas rely on, not
  only their dangers ([[7.2-appropriation]]).
- **The theories themselves are contested.** Kohlberg's stage model is dated; **MFT's foundation count
  and even its innateness are unsettled** — Haidt himself asks "how many foundations are there?" [S2],
  and the Moral Foundations Questionnaire has faced replication and structure critiques. The
  liberal/conservative result is partly WEIRD-bound. Claims here are "best current maps," not settled
  fact.
- **Innate modules vs learned automaticity.** Haidt's "taste buds" imply innate modules; Sapolsky's
  "guts learn their intuitions" [S3] implies learned automaticity. The truth is probably gene–culture
  coevolutionary (Haidt's "tweak settings and timings" in the Holocene [S2]) — but the note should not
  reify the foundations as fixed hardware.
- **Describing ≠ justifying.** None of this tells us which intuitions are *right*; intuitionism can
  slide toward relativism, and adaptiveness grounds no *ought* ([[3.3-grounding-problem]]).
- **Cited via popular syntheses.** Kohlberg, Shweder, Greene and much of Haidt are read here through
  Sapolsky's *Behave* and corpus essays; Haidt's primary work, the WEIRD paper, and Gilligan are
  anchored externally.

## Open threads (non-registry)

- **Reader-raised seed (Rupert, correspondence, June 2026):** a reader described his late
  brother's rapid, whole-hearted swings between worldviews — atheist, then committed Christian,
  then another creed — each time declaring the previous "one true path" wholly mistaken and the
  new one the real thing. It is a vivid single case of *moral/worldview lability*: how plastic
  are an individual's dharmic commitments across a lifetime, and what distinguishes a lifelong
  "belief-hopper" from a steadfast adherent? Connects to conversion/deconversion research and to
  the mechanism-and-plasticity thread parked in [[5.1-neurological-basis]]. Not yet researched —
  parked here so it is not lost. [A]
