# 5.5 Psychopaths: the exploiter limit-case that cooperative orders evolved to police

**Question:** Examine the role of what we call *psychopaths* in human development, history, and evolution. What is this human type, what evolutionary and neural dynamics produce it, how have cooperative/dharmic systems policed it, and what does it reveal about the limits of moral psychology?

## Synthesis

"Psychopath" names the limit case of moral psychology: the individual in whom the affective machinery that underwrites cooperation — empathy, fear, guilt, remorse, reciprocal adjustment — is constitutionally muted, leaving a charming, manipulative, conscienceless exploiter of others [S31][S45]. Clinically the construct descends from Cleckley and was operationalised by Hare's PCL-R; it is *not* a DSM diagnosis, is best modelled as a dimension rather than a discrete type, and sits at roughly 1% of the general population (and far higher in carceral and some leadership samples) [S31][S32][S33][S35][S37]. Neurobiologically the corpus locates it in an under-reactive amygdala (low fear, blunted empathy) paired with weak prefrontal regulation, producing cold *instrumental* rather than hot reactive aggression [S13][S15][S16]. This is precisely the free-rider / intraspecies-predator that cooperation theory says cheater-detection and punishment evolved to contain [S23][S25], and that Christopher Boehm's "reverse dominance hierarchy" describes egalitarian bands coalition-suppressing through gossip, ridicule, ostracism, expulsion and, in the extreme, execution [S1][S4][S5]. So dharma is not only a code but an *enforcement coalition*, partly explained by the perennial presence of exploiters — yet the same construct is historically moralised, cross-culturally unstable, and demonstrably abusable as an instrument of exclusion [S46][S47][S49], making the policing of exploiters and the scapegoating of deviants two settings of one mechanism. Yet suppression is only half the story: the same low-fear temperament that yields the exploiter also, on its **boldness** face — fearless dominance, shared with heroism, leadership and exploration — produces the rare individual a group may have reason to value, so evolution appears to have kept the type as a stable, double-edged, low-frequency niche rather than merely a defect to be policed [S54][S55][S57]. But that persistence is an individual cheater-equilibrium requiring no group benefit, its plausibly-useful edge is boldness rather than the callous core, and any genuinely *group*-level function is real-but-unproven, contested at the level of selection theory, and in any case outstripped by the scale at which modern predators now operate [S66][S71][S81].

## Findings

### 1. What the type is — a contested clinical construct, not a clean natural kind

The modern concept derives from psychiatrist Hervey Cleckley's *The Mask of Sanity* (1941), which distilled psychopathy into sixteen clinical criteria — superficial charm and good intelligence, lack of remorse or shame, untruthfulness and insincerity, pathological egocentricity, poverty of major affective reactions (an inability to love), and failure to learn from experience [S31]. Robert Hare operationalised this into the Psychopathy Checklist-Revised (PCL-R), now the field's "gold standard," adding items such as grandiosity, impulsivity and juvenile delinquency, and resolving the construct into a two-factor / four-facet structure: an Interpersonal/Affective factor (glib charm, manipulation, pathological lying, shallow affect, callousness, lack of remorse) and a Lifestyle/Antisocial factor [S32]. Hare frames psychopaths as conscienceless "intraspecies predators who use charm, manipulation, intimidation and violence to control others and to satisfy their own selfish needs" [S45].

Crucially, "psychopathy" is not a DSM diagnosis: the manual codes the behaviourally-defined Antisocial Personality Disorder (ASPD), with which psychopathy is highly comorbid but not synonymous — not all with ASPD meet PCL-R criteria [S33]. Psychopathy is one leg of the "Dark Triad" (with narcissism and Machiavellianism), introduced by Paulhus and Williams (2002) [S34]. A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis (15 studies, 11,497 people) put pooled general-adult prevalence at 4.5% (95% CI 1.6–7.9%) across all instruments but only 1.2% (95% CI 0–3.7%) when restricted to the PCL-R — wide intervals, not population constants [S35]; carceral samples run far higher (commonly ~15–25%) [S35]. Wrangham, citing a UK household survey, likewise reports psychopathy in under 1% of the population, more common in men and younger adults and associated with violence, imprisonment, drug dependence, ASPD and homelessness, with lack of conscience the key feature [S15]. Taxometric work (Edens et al. 2006, "Psychopathic, not psychopath," 876 inmates/patients) found no support for a discrete taxon and argued the trait is **dimensional** — a continuum, not a category [S37]. This matters for the dharma frame: exploiter tendencies are normally distributed across the population, not a rare alien defect, which is exactly why cooperative ordering must *continuously* monitor for them.

### 2. Neurobiology — the cooperation hardware selectively offline

The corpus grounds the type in a dissociable neural profile drawn mainly from Sapolsky and Wrangham. In violent psychopaths the amygdala is relatively unresponsive to fear-evoking stimuli and smaller than normal, so their violence is not aroused reaction but "purely instrumental, using others as a means to an end with emotionless, remorseless, reptilian indifference" [S13]; imaging shows blunted amygdala activity especially during moral decision-making, fear recognition and social cooperation, with low fear plus low empathy jointly supporting *proactive* aggression [S15]. The corpus distinguishes two neurally-dissociable aggression types: in proactive (psychopathic) aggression the amygdala underperforms, while in reactive/impulsive aggression the prefrontal cortex underperforms and low brain serotonin predicts impulsive rather than premeditated violence [S16][S20]. A vivid window onto the cheater-detection circuit: patients with damage restricted to the amygdala fail to lower cooperation in an economic game even when a partner "is robbing them of everything" — the signal that "that was awful, I'll shift my behaviour" is absent [S17].

Sapolsky's neuroimaging summary (Raine, Kiehl) reports that criminal psychopaths show decreased frontal-cortex activity and less coupling of the PFC to other regions than non-psychopathic criminals and controls — a secondary popular-science gloss on a well-established finding, not the primary papers [S14]. Damage to the ventromedial PFC (the Phineas Gage / Damasio lineage) leaves intelligence intact but cripples emotionally-grounded social decision-making; such patients give the same detached answer to "kill a stranger to save five?" and "kill a loved one to save five?", a response "unlike any normal person or primate on Earth" [S14][S18]. This rests on Damasio's somatic-marker hypothesis, in which choices are scored by internal limbic simulations reported to the vmPFC — decision-making as "an emotion experiment," not pure calculation [S19]. Sapolsky presses these findings toward hard determinism, arguing that "evil," "soul," "blame" and free will are incompatible with neuroscience and should be discarded [S20]; he states (in one transcript) that 25–75% of men on US death rows have a history of concussive trauma to the prefrontal cortex — a deliberately wide, soft figure best quoted as that source's wording, since he more commonly cites ~25% [S21]. A useful contrast case is Williams syndrome, where absent amygdala response to scary faces yields not predation but extreme trust and gregariousness — blunted fear can produce over-trust *or* exploitation depending on the rest of the brain [S22].

### 3. Evolutionary models — adaptation, balancing selection, or disorder (genuinely unsettled)

The most dharma-relevant theory treats psychopathy not as a breakdown but as an evolved, low-frequency "cheater" strategy. Linda Mealey's "The sociobiology of sociopathy" (1995) reconceived sociopaths as deceptive cheaters held at equilibrium by **negative frequency-dependent (balancing) selection**, distinguishing genetically-based "primary" (obligate) from environmentally-induced "secondary" (facultative) sociopaths [S38]. Frequency-dependence means cheating pays only while *rare and undetected*: as cheaters become common they grow detectable and increasingly meet other cheaters, pushing the strategy back down to a low stable frequency [S39]. Book and Quinsey (2004) split this into a deceptive "cheater" strategy (PCL-R Factor 1) versus an aggressive "warrior-hawk" strategy (Factor 2) [S41]. Krupp, Lalumière, Harris and colleagues (2013) argue psychopathy fails Wakefield's "harmful dysfunction" test for a disorder — "there is no evidence that psychopathy is the result of dysfunctional rather than merely different psychological mechanisms" — and cite preserved kin-protection (psychopathic offenders showing lower violence against genetic relatives) as evidence of an alternative life strategy rather than a defect [S40].

This adaptation thesis is **contested, not established**. Glenn, Kurzban and Raine (2011) explicitly weigh the adaptive-strategy models against a mutation-accumulation (deleterious by-product) account [S39], and reviewers note that the *dimensional* distribution of psychopathic traits undercuts the assumption that psychopaths form "a distinct class of individuals," weakening any single-strategy reading [S52]. Heritability estimates are real but range-bound: twin studies put 36–78% of variance in callous-unemotional traits down to genes, and Viding et al.'s (2005) study of 3,687 seven-year-old twin pairs found ~67% heritability of CU traits — but that figure comes from an extreme-scoring selected sample, which inflates it, so the honest claim is the range, not a point estimate [S36]. A Stage-2 essay must therefore present the cheater-strategy account as a powerful but disputed model, never as settled fact.

### 4. Deep-time policing — Boehm's reverse dominance and the suppression of free-riders

The corpus's deepest mechanism is Christopher Boehm's *Hierarchy in the Forest* (1999): egalitarian band society is not the absence of hierarchy but a **"reverse dominance hierarchy"** in which the rank-and-file form a vigilant moral coalition keeping would-be alphas "under their collective thumbs" [S1]. Boehm's own catalogue of sanctioned "upstarts" reaches pathological types — "an upstart can also be a recidivist murderer or a homicidal psychotic," alongside bullies, meat-hoarders and manipulative shamans — the corpus's closest pre-clinical analogue to "psychopath" [S2]. He describes a "bullying recidivist killer, possibly a psychotic, who in effect intimidates his group," handled by group-sanctioned capital punishment "often delegating to a relative of the deviant the role of executioner" to forestall revenge [S3]. Social control runs as an escalating ladder — gossip, cool greeting, criticism, ridicule, ostracism, expulsion, and "the ultimate form of social distancing: execution" [S4]. Boehm's limited ethnographic survey (1993) "turned up five instances of execution among hunter-gatherers on three continents, and five instances among tribesmen on three continents," strictly political executions of upstarts, leading him to call domination episodes "rare, yet widespread" [S5]; a later 50-society Late-Pleistocene-appropriate sample reports capital punishment in nearly half, the leading cause being a male trying to physically or supernaturally intimidate fellow hunters (this proportion reaches the corpus only via a secondary paper and should be treated as such) [S11].

Boehm broadens the target to "free-riders" — those "born with fewer altruistic genes" or carrying "opportunistic genes," including "camp bullies as well as free-loaders and cheaters" [S6] — and advances a multilevel argument in which phenotypic suppression of free-riders is one crucial mechanism: if free-riding "could be eliminated or heavily suppressed at the level of phenotype," between-group selection "would not need to rise to nearly so high a level for altruistic genes to be supported," directly linking the policing of exploiters to the evolution of cooperation [S7]. Lethal weapons help: universal possession of hunting weapons gives "all men, however physically weak, cowardly, unskilled or socially inept," the means to kill a perceived threat, "a powerful leveling mechanism" against coercers, documented by Boehm across 339 ethnographic studies [S12]. The prosocial corpus supplies the complementary game theory: cooperation faces a universal "cooperation/exploitation dilemma" in which exploitation is "almost always a winning short-term strategy," solvable only by pairing risky cooperation with punishment of cheaters [S23]; layered punishment (by victims, by third parties, and "secondary" sanction of those who fail to punish) sustains it [S26], and punishing cheaters can act as a costly honest signal of trustworthiness, "like a peacock's tail" [S27]. Critically, the exploiter is never eliminated, only held down: simulated populations cycle toward Always-Cooperate "until a mutation reintroduces an exploitative strategy that spreads like wildfire, a wolf among Always Cooperate sheep, starting the cycle all over again" [S25].

### 5. History and society — the niche, the leadership channel, and the anonymity problem

A prosocial essay states outright that hunter-gatherer groups sustained cooperation "despite the fact that roughly 1% of the people are psychopaths," handling free-riders and would-be dictators by shaming, then ostracism, then (rarely, "when a serial killer was in their midst") execution, because "social transparency" held bullies accountable [S24]. Extending the 1% rate to ~7 billion people, it reasons there are "roughly 70,000,000 psychopaths," and argues internet anonymity and disposable avatars defeat the shame/ostracism mechanism that worked when "everyone knows your name" — a theoretically motivated but empirically unproven claim, echoed in the wider literature on complex anonymous societies widening the "always-defect" niche [S24][S51]. Psychopathic traits also cluster in leadership: Babiak, Neumann and Hare (2010) found 3.9% of 203 management-program executives scored at/above the PCL-R clinical threshold — though from a non-random sample that should not be read as a population base rate [S42]. Dutton argues a "successful" sub-syndrome (fearlessness, focus, charm, ruthlessness under pressure) is functionally adaptive in surgeons, special forces and CEOs [S43], and Lilienfeld et al. (2012) found the psychopathy-linked trait "fearless dominance" predicted better-rated US presidential performance while "self-centered impulsivity" predicted negative outcomes — boldness as "a double-edged sword" [S44]. Retrospective diagnoses of historical tyrants are popular but methodologically weak and should be flagged as speculative.

### 6. The reflexive turn — the construct as moralised and abusable

The same evidence that grounds the dharma-defence also warns against it. There is a recurrent cross-cultural folk category of the uncooperative cheat-liar-bully — the Inuit *kunlangeta* and Yoruba *arankan* — and a recurrent coalitional remedy: asked what was done with a *kunlangeta*, Murphy's (1976) informant said "somebody would have pushed him off the ice when nobody else was looking" [S46]. That is Boehm's suppression operating across cultures — but Murphy's two-community sample equally supports a "culturally-drawn-but-locally-policed" reading rather than proof of a universal natural kind. The construct descends from an explicitly moralised lineage (Pinel's *manie sans délire*, 1801; Prichard's "moral insanity," 1835, where "moral" meant *affective*, not *ethical*) [S50], and critics argue it is a culturally produced category offering "a comforting neurobiological solution to the mystery of evil" rather than a validated biological entity [S49]. The label carries punitive force: in a controlled experiment, calling a capital defendant "psychopathic" produced 60% mock-juror support for death versus 30% for "psychotic," and the construct compounds racialised "dangerousness" stereotypes [S47]; PCL-R scores also travel poorly across cultures and shift with which side retains the examiner [S48][S53]. The dharma double-edge is exact: a cooperative order's defence system against genuine predators is also its most dangerous instrument of conformity-enforcement and moral exclusion.

### 7. The functional minority — and why "function" overreaches

Everything above treats the type as an exploiter to be policed. But biological and cultural evolution both appear to have kept a small, low-frequency stock of it, which forces a harder question: does the type do anything *for* human groups, or only *to* them? The honest answer is that almost the entire "functional" signal in the literature attaches to *one* dimension of the construct, and even there the benefit is individual, modest, and easily overstated. Christopher Patrick's *triarchic* model decomposes psychopathy into **disinhibition** (impulse-control failure), **meanness** ("aggressive resource seeking without regard for others"), and **boldness** ("the nexus of social dominance, emotional resiliency, and venturesomeness"), and singles out boldness as "of unique importance in understanding so-called successful psychopaths" who "achieve success as military, political, or corporate-industrial leaders" [S54]. The callous-exploitative core the earlier sections describe is meanness; the functional signal rides on boldness alone — the load-bearing brake for all that follows. And the split cannot be cleanly cashed out: the model holds that boldness and meanness "share a key etiologic substrate (i.e., diminished fear capacity)," so one low-fear temperament forks into adaptive boldness *or* callous meanness, and a population cannot be assumed to keep the one and shed the other [S54].

Operationalised as Lilienfeld's **fearless dominance** — fearlessness, social potency, stress-immunity, resilience — boldness is the part of the syndrome carrying adaptive correlates. In a community survey of 3,388 adults, fearless dominance "appears to be associated largely with adaptive functioning" while the impulsive-antisocial factor tracks maladaptive outcomes, and psychopathic traits "especially those linked to fearless dominance" were moderately associated with holding leadership and high-risk occupations — the leadership link stronger for boldness than for the antisocial factors [S55]. But the design is cross-sectional (the authors cannot exclude that high-risk jobs *build* fearlessness rather than select for it), the "high-risk" category is mixed rather than cleanly prosocial, and every payoff is to the bearer's own standing, not a group's [S55]. This converges with the presidency finding already in the note: estimated fearless dominance predicted rated performance and crisis leadership while the antisocial factor tracked impeachment and unethical conduct — the "double-edged sword" [S44].

The one channel in which boldness benefits anyone but its bearer is heroism, and it is where the case is simultaneously made and deflated. The framing descends from David Lykken's conjecture — an untested hypothesis, not a finding — that heroes and psychopaths are "twigs in the same genetic branch," a shared fearless temperament steered toward rescue or crime by upbringing [S56]; testing it, Smith, Lilienfeld and colleagues found fearless dominance modestly, positively tied to "everyday heroism" across community and presidential samples [S57], and Patton and colleagues replicated the heroism link in first responders — while finding the *antisocial* edge does **not** ride on boldness (workplace deviance "was not significantly associated with boldness or fearlessness"), so prosocial and antisocial conduct co-occur at the level of the whole syndrome, not as two clean faces of one trait [S58]. Even this is thin: the head-on test of Lykken returned "decidedly mixed support" (heroism r ≈ 0.31, self-report, online) [S59], and the "successful psychopath" construct is inconsistently defined and, its own proponents concede, backed by a research base "dwarfed" by speculation [S60]. Worse for the thesis, whether boldness even belongs *inside* psychopathy is disputed: two meta-analyses find fearless dominance only weakly related to core psychopathy [S61][S62], Lynam and Miller call tying psychopathy to adaptive traits "conceptually incoherent" and rate the dimension "at best … a diagnostic specifier … rather than an essential feature" [S63], and Crego and Widiger argue heroes and psychopaths overlap only on fearlessness — "a normal, adaptive trait" — while heroes lack the antagonism that *defines* psychopathy, placing the two on "very different genetic branches" [S64]. The deflation is sharp: the functional signal may ride on boldness precisely because boldness is the *least* psychopathic part of the construct, so "functional psychopathy = boldness minus the antisocial factor" threatens to collapse into "boldness minus psychopathy."

The strongest *group*-level version comes not from psychiatry but from behavioural ecology — and it imposes the decisive brake. Negative frequency-dependent and fluctuating selection robustly maintain a bold, risk-taking minority in animal populations, but at the *individual* level: a rare bold type persists for the same reason a rare parasite does, by rare-type advantage, with "no unconditionally optimal value" for the trait and predation often selecting *against* boldness [S66][S68]. Persistence is therefore not group-function — a *stable exploitative niche* (well-evidenced, individual) is not the claim that *the group is better off for having a few* (weak, contested, group-level). A genuine group-level reading does exist — Wolf and Weissing's "insurance" effect, whereby diverse populations better harbour types that cope with novelty — but the same review concedes the direct evidence is "still scarce," and the step to a *human* population keeping a few bold rule-breakers as insurance is analogy, not demonstration [S71].

Where the trait's usefulness is best evidenced it is, once more, individual and *ecology-contingent*. The preference for dominant leaders is not admiration of the exploiter but a threat-toggled followership response: it rises under intergroup conflict, is driven by the intuition that dominant leaders enable *aggressive* responses, and is confined to the leader role — for friends, people prefer non-dominant cooperators [S73][S74]. Beneath it sits a fast, short-horizon life-history strategy that maps specifically onto psychopathy [S75] and is pushed by harsh, unpredictable childhood ecologies — a facultative response to adversity (Mealey's "secondary" sociopathy) rather than a fixed defect [S76][S38]. The **warrior** thread — which rides on aggression, not boldness — is weakest and most contested: Chagnon's finding that Yanomamö killers out-reproduced non-killers [S77] is confounded by age [S78] and reversed outright in Beckerman's Waorani data [S79], with any reliable payoff routing through culturally rewarded, low-risk raiding — individual reward, not group defence [S80]. On the mismatch question: ancestral war-leadership was situational and *reversible*, the aggressive leader losing influence once the threat passed (Boehm's containment); modern entrenched hierarchies and anonymous scale strip out that reversibility, so institutions now substitute for the individual function while the ancestral leash is outstripped by the scale at which modern predators operate, and the fast-life-history niche keeps paying in harsh, low-rule-of-law ecologies (collapsed states, war zones) but not in rule-of-law ones [S81][S51]. The balanced frame the corpus's own contested-adaptation source and a 2026 synthesis both reach: several evolutionary models can make psychopathic traits fitness-enhancing in adverse environments, but older adaptationist assumptions are increasingly flagged as outdated, leaving by-product / mutation-load a live alternative [S52][S82][S39]. The honest middle, then, is neither rehabilitation nor pure pathology: a double-edged human type that evolution kept as a stable, low-frequency niche; its persistence an individual cheater-equilibrium needing no group benefit; its plausibly-useful edge boldness — shared with heroism, leadership and exploration — and any *group*-level function real-but-unproven, contested at the level of selection theory, and in any case largely superseded by institutions and outstripped by scale.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

The YaTTi shared corpus is curated around Boehm, Sapolsky, Graeber, Batchelor and Bregman, a secular / evolutionary / Western-academic slant that frames psychopathy as a *suppressible exploiter-type* and leans hard determinist (Sapolsky). The deep-time-policing material here is almost entirely **single-author Boehm**, and the corpus contains essentially **no primary clinical-psychopathy literature** — its only bridge to the construct is Boehm's loose pre-clinical phrases ("homicidal psychotic," "recidivist killer, possibly a psychotic") and a prosocial blogger's uncited "1% psychopaths" gloss. Every claim about Cleckley/Hare/PCL-R, prevalence, heritability, dimensionality, the adaptation-vs-disorder debate, and the critical-psychiatry critique was therefore supplied by **external sourcing** to balance the lean.

What is genuinely contested: (1) **Adaptation vs disorder** — the frequency-dependent "cheater strategy" (Mealey; Krupp et al.) is a serious model but disputed by mutation-accumulation accounts and undermined by the trait's dimensional distribution; do not present it as fact [S38][S39][S40][S52]. (2) **Boehm's small-sample tallies** — the 5+5 executions and "nearly half of 50" capital-punishment figures are tentative ethnographic counts Boehm himself flags as limited and likely under-reported, and the "nearly half" figure reaches the corpus only through a secondary paper (zoonpolitikon) citing Boehm 2014 [S5][S11]. (3) **The 1%/70M and "anonymity widens the niche" claims** are blog-essay reasoning, not epidemiology or a clean empirical test [S24]. (4) **Cross-cultural validity** — PCL-R validation rests on a WEIRD-plus-carceral base; Murphy's folk-category evidence is two communities and is routinely over-cited as proof of universality [S46][S48]. (5) **Retrospective diagnosis** of historical figures is methodologically weak. (6) Several KB citations carry locator caveats: line-anchor corrections flagged in verification include the death-row figure (transcript ~263, not :30), the cheater-detection passage (~26095, not :1214), the Raine/Kiehl summary (~358), and the Boehm survey passage (~1157). Two claims were corrected on substance: the cheater-detection "75/25 + chimps show the same bias" is a single uncited lecture aside supporting the *debated* cheater-detection-module hypothesis (critics: Sperber, Cara & Girotto), so it is hedged here, not asserted [S29]; and a "two men with identical frontal damage, one a murderer one a rude guest" anecdote attributed to Sapolsky was **dropped** as unverifiable in the corpus. Unresolved overall: whether the exploiter is best read as adaptation, by-product, or disorder; and whether "psychopath" is a discoverable kind or a moralised, abusable label — the answer the dharma frame most needs to keep open.

The **functional-minority** reading is almost entirely external to the corpus. The shared YaTTi corpus supplies the *individual*-level persistence story (frequency-dependence), the anonymity/mismatch argument and every one of the honesty brakes — but where it touches the group-benefit idea at all it leans *against* it (when the aggressive males of Sapolsky's baboon troop died off, the troop grew durably more peaceful and healthier [S84]), and it contains no Triarchic model, no successful-psychopathy literature and no behavioural-ecology "diversity as insurance" work, so the functional-minority case here rests almost wholly on external sourcing [S54]–[S82]. Two cautions bound how far it can go. First, **persistence is not group-function**: negative frequency-dependent selection already explains a stable exploiter niche at the individual level — a parasite persists the same way [S38][S66] — so the step from "a stable niche" to "the group is better off for having a few" is a *contested multilevel-selection* claim, and the corpus's own David Sloan Wilson warns against exactly the "naïve group selectionism" it would require [S83], while even behavioural ecology's strongest group-level statement concedes the direct evidence is "still scarce" [S71]. Second, the **mismatch** is best read not as the trait turning useless but as *containment outstripped by scale*: ancestral face-to-face transparency and reversible war-leadership held exploiters in check [S81], modern institutions now substitute for that individual policing function while the ancestral leash cannot reach predators operating at anonymous, planetary scale [S51][S81], and the fast-life-history niche still pays in harsh, low-rule-of-law ecologies (collapsed states, war zones) but not in rule-of-law ones — the same trait at lower marginal usefulness and far greater potential harm [S68][S76]. Throughout, every functional claim rides on **boldness**, never meanness — the callous-exploitative core carries none of the upside, and whether boldness even belongs inside "psychopathy" is itself disputed [S54][S63][S64].
