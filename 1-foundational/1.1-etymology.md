# 1.1 Core Etymology

**Question:** Trace the etymology of 'dharma' beyond Sanskrit to Proto-Indo-European
roots. What cognitive and social functions do these linguistic origins reveal about
humanity's need for ethical frameworks?

## Synthesis

*Dharma* descends from the Sanskrit verbal root **√dhṛ** ("to hold, support, sustain"),
itself reflecting Proto-Indo-European **\*dʰer-** ("to hold"), giving the literal sense
"that which holds / upholds" [S1][S7]. Its earliest attested form is the Vedic *n*-stem
**dhárman-** ("bearer, supporter"; "something established or firm"), first conceived as
an aspect of **ṛta**, the cosmic order, before the thematic noun *dharma-* broadened into
duty, law, righteousness, and proper conduct [S8]. The etymology therefore encodes one
deep metaphor — *order as that which holds reality together* — applied successively to
cosmos, society, and self [S1][S2]. Read through the corpus's secular / evolutionary
lens, that "holding" names a recurring human need: ethical frameworks are social
technologies that hold groups together by solving the cooperation-without-exploitation
problem, which is why convergent moral patterns recur across unrelated cultures [S3][S4].
The linguistic origin and the social function thus point at the same fact — humans need
*something to hold* behaviour predictable and cooperative under impermanence and anonymity.

## Findings

### The Sanskrit root and its literal meaning
*Dharma* (धर्म) derives from the Sanskrit verbal root **√dhṛ** (धृ), "to hold," "to
maintain," "to support," "to sustain" [S1][S7]. The literal nominal sense is therefore
"that which holds / upholds / sustains" — whether the referent is the cosmos, society, or
an individual life [S1][S2]. The term passes into Pali and Prakrit as **dhamma** [S1][S8].

### Proto-Indo-European depth
The root traces to Proto-Indo-European **\*dʰer-** ("to hold, support") → Proto-Indo-Iranian
**\*dʰar-** → Sanskrit class-1 root √dhṛ [S7]. Documented cognates include Latin *firmus*
("stable, strong"; whence English *firm*) and *frēnum* ("rein"); Avestan and Old Persian
*dar-* ("to hold"); Lithuanian *derė́ti* ("to be suitable, to fit"); and Old Church
Slavonic *drъžati* ("to hold, possess") [S7]. The authoritative documentation of this
derivation is Manfred Mayrhofer's *Etymologisches Wörterbuch des Altindoarischen* (1992),
cited here via Wiktionary [S7].

### Vedic dhárman- and ṛta
The earliest form is the Vedic *n*-stem **dhárman-**, with a literal sense of "something
established or firm" (in the concrete sense of props or poles) and a figurative sense of
"sustainer / supporter" of the deities — semantically parallel to Greek **themis**
("fixed decree, statute, law") [S8]. In the religious cosmology it functions as an aspect
of **ṛta**, the impersonal cosmic order; *dharma* is in effect the *application of ṛta to
human action* — a more human-centred, conduct-focused version of the cosmic law [S8].
Joel Brereton documents **dharman** occurring 63 times in the Rigveda (alongside forms
such as *dharmakṛt*, *satyadharman*, *dharmavant*, *dhariman*); Paul Horsch argues that in
the Atharvaveda the term gains a "dynamic functional sense," becoming a "cosmic law that
links cause and effect"; and the *Monier-Williams* dictionary later glosses the thematic
noun across "that which is established or firm... decree, statute, law, practice, custom,
duty, right, justice, virtue, morality, ethics, religion" [S8].

That widening — from a concrete prop, to cosmic order, to law/duty/virtue — is a textbook case
of **semantic broadening** (generalisation), one of the regular mechanisms (with narrowing,
amelioration, and pejoration) by which word meanings drift over time [S10]. The corpus's own
"defining definition" essay names this "evolution over time" as intrinsic to meaning — instancing
the redefinition of *planet* that reclassified Pluto — and stresses that "language is fluid" and
cultural difference adds further layers [S9]. So dharma's protean range is not an exotic
peculiarity but an ordinary linguistic process observed in slow motion across three millennia.

### What the origins reveal: cognitive & social functions
**Cognitive (the root metaphor).** A single embodied image — *holding / supporting /
making-firm* — is projected from physical stability onto moral and cosmic order. Ethics is
conceptualised spatially, as a structure that keeps things from collapsing; this is why the
same word can mean a literal prop, the order of the universe, and a person's duty [S1][S2].

**Social (dharma as coordination technology).** Anthropologically, a dharma is a "bundle"
of values, norms, stories, practices, and institutions [S2] that operates as a "social
technology" solving enduring coordination problems — trust, conflict, belonging, and
meaning — by making behaviour predictable and enabling cooperation at scale [S3].

**Evolutionary (why the need is universal).** In the prosocial corpus, morality is
"always and everywhere a cooperative phenomenon" that evolved to capture the benefits of
cooperation without being destroyed by exploitation [S4][S5]. Oliver Scott Curry's
"morality as cooperation" analysed ethnographic accounts from 60 societies (>600,000 words)
and found seven recurrent moral rules — love your family, help your group, return favours,
be brave, defer to authority, be fair, respect others' property — with *no* counter-examples
across cultures [S4]. Cross-cultural moral diversity is then explained largely by *whom* one
must cooperate with and the in-group/out-group markers that bound it, not by the absence of
a shared cooperative core [S6]. This is why "dharma-shaped" frameworks recur independently:
they are cultural elaborations on a common cooperative substrate. The linguistic need
("something to hold us") and the evolutionary need ("something to hold cooperation
together") are two readings of one fact.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The corpus is partly self-referential / AI-generated.** Several shared-corpus
  "sources" (`etymology-origins-of-dharma.md`, `defining-dharma_gpt41.md`,
  `Dharma-according-to-Grok-4.md`, `combined_dharma_keep_notes.txt`) are themselves
  LLM-authored essays — some apparently produced by the sibling *DefiningDharma* pipeline —
  not primary philology. They agree on the √dhṛ root but cannot count as *independent*
  authorities. This is precisely why METHODOLOGY mandates an external philology pass; the
  PIE reconstruction and Vedic detail above rest on Wiktionary/Wikipedia (which cite
  Mayrhofer, Horsch, Brereton), not on the corpus.
- **Secular / evolutionary lean.** The corpus frames dharma functionally (social
  technology, cooperation) and explicitly de-privileges religious/metaphysical readings.
  That lens is productive here but underweights the *internal* self-understanding of dharma
  in Hindu and Buddhist traditions, where it is experienced as revealed or transcendent
  rather than merely adaptive. The Vedic *ṛta*-as-cosmic-order framing [S8] partially
  restores that dimension.
- **A philological caveat the corpus missed.** The *word* is securely PIE (√dhṛ < \*dʰer-),
  but the religious/ethical *concept* dharman looks substantially **Vedic-internal**: the
  only Iranian counterpart, Old Persian *darmān*, means "remedy" — semantically divergent —
  which suggests the ethical sense developed after the Indo-Iranian split [S8]. So "PIE
  roots" ground the etymon, not the concept of cosmic/ethical order; the corpus's confident
  cognate lists (*firmus*, *derė́ti*) risk implying more *conceptual* continuity than the
  evidence supports.
- **A verify-don't-trust instance.** An automated search initially reported the Greek
  parallel as *ethos*; the primary article gives *themis*. Corrected above. Minor, but it
  is exactly the kind of slip the citation discipline is meant to catch.
- **Unconsulted primaries (open gap).** Mayrhofer (*EWAia*), Horsch, and Brereton are cited
  second-hand. A Stage-2 essay leaning hard on the philology should consult these directly.
