# 1.2 Universal Patterns

**Question:** How does dharma represent a universal cognitive pattern that emerges
independently across cultures? What evolutionary pressures drive humans to create
dharmic frameworks?

## Synthesis

Dharma is best understood not as a concept that diffused across the world but as one
*culturally particular expression* of a *universal* underlying capacity: the human
"normative sense" — the disposition to represent, internalise, and enforce shared rules
about right conduct [S4][S7]. The cleanest way to hold these two facts together is
Tinbergen's distinction between a trait's *function* and its *mechanism*: morality has a
near-universal **function** (sustaining group cooperation), but a one-to-many set of
proximate **mechanisms** (duty, divine command, honour, dharma, Ubuntu) — so dharma-shaped
frameworks recur independently wherever the same social problem recurs, without any shared
origin [S4]. The evolutionary pressure that drives this is the cooperation-without-
exploitation dilemma: cooperation yields large gains but exposes cooperators to free-riders,
so humans evolved — genetically *and* culturally — a suite of adaptations (reciprocity,
reputation, norm-enforcement, in-group solidarity) to capture cooperation's benefits at
ever-larger scales [S2][S5][S6]. Comparative anthropology confirms the output: cooperation,
reciprocity, and "constant ethical patterns" appear in *every* documented culture [S6][S7].
The honest caveat — visible in the contested moralizing-gods literature — is that the
*direction* and *mechanism* of these pressures remain scientifically live, so the universality
claim is strong for the function and weaker for any single causal story [S8].

## Findings

### Two claims, kept distinct
The question bundles two things that must be separated to answer it well: (a) a **universal
cognitive pattern**, and (b) **dharma** as a specific instance of it.

- **The universal is the capacity, not the content.** Evolutionary psychology holds that
  "behaviors or traits that occur universally in all cultures are good candidates for
  evolutionary adaptations," cooperation among them [S1]. What is panhuman is the *machinery* —
  the moral/normative sense and the drive to create and enforce norms — not any particular
  code [S1][S4].
- **Dharma is one mechanism the machinery produces.** In the corpus's own framing, dharma is
  treated as plural and adaptive — a "way, path, culture, or outlook" that recurs in many
  forms (personal, communal, professional, ecological) because each is a local solution to
  the same orientation-and-coordination problem [A]. ⚠ This framing is from an AI-authored
  corpus essay (see bias note), but it aligns with the scholarly picture.

### Why the pattern is genuinely universal (the comparative evidence)
- Donald Brown's *Human Universals* catalogues features "for which there are no known
  exceptions," explicitly including **cooperation, reciprocity, exchange, kinship,
  ethnocentrism, and gossip**, and finds "constant ethical patterns present in every single
  culture"; reciprocity in particular is a "universal cornerstone of morality" [S7].
- Oliver Scott Curry's "morality as cooperation" operationalises this: seven moral rules
  (love family, help group, return favours, be brave, defer to authority, be fair, respect
  property) appear across 60 societies with no counter-examples [S6].
- Christakis's *Blueprint* names a "social suite" of universals (cooperation, friendship,
  in-group bias, social learning) and argues humans have "genetically internalised universal
  social axioms" — convergent behaviour arising "primarily as a consequence of our species'
  shared evolutionary past" [S2].

### Why function is universal but form varies (the key resolution)
The apparent tension — "universal" yet "emerges independently across cultures" — dissolves
under Tinbergen's four questions applied to morality [S4]:
- **Function:** in virtually all cultures most people have a sense of right and wrong tied
  to group welfare, and create/enforce norms accordingly [S4].
- **Phylogeny:** this is the product of *between-group selection* (Darwin's conjecture —
  moral groups out-compete fractious ones) opposed by *within-group selection* (self-serving
  individuals do well locally), which is why everyone behaves immorally sometimes and why
  specific moral content is so variable [S4].
- **Mechanism:** there is a "one-to-many relationship between the function of a trait and the
  proximate mechanisms that evolve to cause it" — duty, pleasure in helping, or "a ticket to
  heaven" can all drive the same cooperative act [S4]. **Dharma is one such proximate
  mechanism.** This is the crux: the same function recruits different cultural software.
- **Development:** core moral agency may be universal, but particular systems follow
  particular developmental paths — which is why Wilson reads Kohlberg's "highest stage" as a
  rare evolved *moral system*, not a universal developmental stage [S4].

### The evolutionary pressures, named
1. **The cooperation/exploitation dilemma.** Cooperation produces far more than solo effort
   but exposes one to exploitation; the adaptations that solve this (reciprocity, guilt,
   gratitude, punishment) are the raw material of morality [S6] (and see [[1.1-etymology]]).
2. **Deep time in social groups.** For ~50 million years humans and ancestors lived in
   groups, making the *social* environment "as powerful as any predator" in shaping the
   genome — a feedback loop that internalised prosocial axioms [S2].
3. **Scaling cooperation to non-kin.** Getting along at large scale required more than
   kin-selection. Handley & Mathew's field study of four northern-Kenyan pastoralist groups
   (Turkana, Samburu, Borana, Rendille; 793 individuals across 9 clans) gives empirical
   evidence that, under intergroup competition, **cultural group selection** — not genetic
   relatedness or geography — best explains cooperation with non-kin [S5].
4. **Norm psychology.** A capacity to acquire, internalise, and police shared norms (backed
   by gossip and reputation, both Brown universals) is what lets a group run as a "moral
   system" even when many individuals defect [S4][S7].

These pressures jointly predict exactly what we observe: independent cultures repeatedly
inventing comprehensive normative frameworks — dharmas — that bind values, duties, stories,
and practices into a cooperation-sustaining whole [S2][A][S5].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **Corpus self-reference / AI-authored sources.** The shared-corpus survey for *dharma
  specifically* returned mostly LLM-generated essays (`define-dharma-in-its-broadest-sense`,
  `why-is-the-concept-of-dharma-important_scholarly`, `Dharma-according-to-Grok-4`) plus
  AI-written aphorism commentaries. The substantive evidence here therefore rests on the
  corpus's *real* books and studies (Christakis, Curry, Wilson, Handley & Mathew) and on
  external scholarship (Brown), **not** on the dharma-essays — which are used only for the
  project's framing [A]. [bias flag]
- **Adaptationist lean, and its live contestation.** The corpus leans hard on the
  "morality/religion evolved for cooperation" narrative. That narrative is *contested* in
  exactly the place it is strongest-sounding: Whitehouse et al. (2019, *Nature*) found that
  complex societies **precede** moralizing gods (i.e. prosocial religion looks more like a
  consequence than a cause), and after a coding/missing-data critique by Beheim et al. (2021)
  the original Letter was **retracted by its authors** — who nonetheless argue the corrected
  analysis preserves the main finding [S8]. The lesson for this project: the *function*
  universal (morality tracks cooperation) is robust; any single *causal* story (e.g. "big
  gods built civilisation") is not, and should be cited cautiously.
- **Universality is statistical, not exceptionless.** Even the strongest sources hedge:
  morality is universal "in virtually all cultures," with psychopaths and chronic defectors
  as real exceptions [S4]. "Universal cognitive pattern" should be read as *species-typical
  and reliably developing*, not *invariant in every individual*.
- **Evolutionary psychology's own methodological critiques** (testability, the modularity
  assumption, uncertainty about ancestral environments) apply and are acknowledged even by
  the corpus's evo-psych source [S1]; "good candidate for an adaptation" is an inference, not
  a proof.
- **Gaps for later/primary work.** Tomasello's *shared-intentionality* account of how
  obligation arises ("I" → "we" cognition), Boyer/Atran's cognitive-science-of-religion
  explanation for why supernatural-normative concepts recur, and Henrich's gene-culture
  coevolution are all relevant and only lightly touched; Brown (1991) and the Whitehouse/
  Beheim exchange are cited via summaries, not the primary texts.
