# 1.3 Boundary & Definition

**Question:** What makes something a *dharma* as opposed to mere custom, law, etiquette,
or ideology? Where are the boundaries of the concept, and what is the minimal set of
features a framework must have to count as dharmic?

## Synthesis

A dharma is distinguished from its neighbours not by *content* but by a *combination* of
four features: (1) **comprehensiveness** — it bundles values, norms, justifying stories,
cultivating practices, and institutions into a single architecture of how to live, rather
than governing one slice of conduct [A]; (2) **internalisation** — it binds through
conviction, identity, and meaning rather than external coercion (unlike law) or mere social
expectation (unlike etiquette) [S5][S8][S10]; (3) **ethical orientation toward the good** —
it concerns the "ought," not pragmatic advantage (the classical Indian split between
*dharma* and *artha*) [S10]; and (4) **transformative telos** — it tells you not just what
to do but why reality is so and who to become, with practices to get you there [A].
By these criteria, **custom/etiquette are too thin** (single-domain, authority-contingent,
no transformative aim), **law is too coercive and external**, and **ideology overlaps most**
but is typically defined by its service to power and lacks dharma's self-cultivation core.
Crucially, the boundary is **graded, not sharp**: there is no single necessary-and-sufficient
mark, the line between "moral/dharmic" and "merely conventional" is itself drawn differently
across cultures [S7], and the corpus's own analysts admit the concept risks becoming "too
broad to be analytically discriminating" [A]. Dharma is best treated as a **family-resemblance
category** with a prototypical core — in the vocabulary of philosophy of language, a
*family-resemblance* and *essentially contested* concept whose boundary is a **precising
stipulation chosen for a purpose**, not an essence to be discovered [S13][S16][S17].

## Findings

### The positive core: what a dharma *is*
The corpus's anthropological working definition treats a dharma as an evolving constellation
of five elements — **values** (what is good), **norms/duties** (what one should do),
**stories/justifications** (why life is so; what counts as meaning), **practices/disciplines**
(habits, training, rituals, contemplative methods), and **institutions/reinforcement** (roles,
communities, sanctions) [A]. The decisive point is that a dharma is *enacted*, not merely
believed: "humans rarely just believe ethics; they perform ethics" [A]. It is therefore both
**embodied and institutionalised**, and functions as a "meso" unit linking individual
psychology to macro-level culture, subject to cultural selection [A].

**Proposed minimal feature set (a checklist).** For a framework to count as dharmic it needs,
at minimum: (a) a **normative core** — an account of how one ought to live / what is good;
(b) **comprehensive scope** — it orients a whole life or whole domain-of-life, not a single
act-type; (c) **cultivation practices** — training that forms disposition, so its rules are
"rules of training" rather than mere commandments [S5]; (d) a **legitimating narrative** —
an account of why this is so and what it means [A]; and (e) **internalisation + transmission**
— it is carried by identity and community and reproduced across generations [A]. Frameworks
with only some of these sit at the blurry edge (below).

### Drawing the boundaries (the contrastive analytics)

**vs Custom (folkways & mores).** In Sumner's classic typology, *folkways* are implicit
usages learned by immersion and *mores* are the morally-weighted norms of a group; both arise
spontaneously from repeated practice without deliberate enactment [S9]. Custom is thus a
*component* of a dharma — its norm layer — but lacks the integrating narrative, the
cultivation practices, and the reflective telos. A custom tells you *what is done*; a dharma
tells you *what is good, why, and how to become it*. Dense mores shade into a dharma only once
they acquire justification, practice, and a transformative aim.

**vs Law.** Sumner calls laws "codified mores" — deliberately enacted by the state and
enforced by organised coercion, in contrast to spontaneous, uncodified custom [S9]. The
classical Indian distinction is sharper still: *daṇḍa* (punishment, derived from *dama*, "to
restrain") is the coercive instrument, whereas "dharma is beyond law and the process of
determining what is good is self-oriented rather than coercive" [S10]. This matches Turiel's
empirical finding that moral norms are judged **authority-independent** (wrong regardless of
what any authority decrees) and **general in scope**, whereas legal/conventional norms are
authority-contingent [S8]. Net: law is external, institutional, coercive, and changeable by
fiat; a dharma is internalised, binding by conviction, and not abolished by repealing a statute.

**vs Etiquette.** Etiquette is the paradigm *conventional* domain in Turiel's scheme:
context-dependent, authority-contingent, and harmless when violated (wearing pyjamas to
school) [S8]. Etiquette governs surface coordination — manners — and supplies only
"if-you-want-to-be-polite" reasons; a dharma claims to govern the good life as such. Etiquette
can be a *stylistic expression* of a dharma (monastic decorum, professional bearing) but is
never the whole of one.

**vs Ideology.** This is the hardest boundary, because both are comprehensive meaning-systems
fusing values and narrative — which is exactly why the corpus extends "dharma" to cover "any
lived ethical-cultural system (environmentalism, punk, military ethos, humanism)" [A].
Distinguishing marks: (i) *ideology* is typically theorised as serving or legitimating power,
and potentially distorting reality (the critical/pejorative sense), whereas a dharma centres
self-cultivation and the reduction of suffering; (ii) dharma's **practice-and-discipline layer**
(contemplative training, ritual) is essential to it but inessential to ideology; (iii) dharmas
aim at personal transformation and flourishing, ideologies at social-political mobilisation.
But the boundary is *functional, not absolute*: a dharma can operate ideologically (legitimating
caste, hierarchy, or conquest) — see [[7.3-dharma-and-power]].

**vs Artha (the tradition's own boundary).** The four *puruṣārthas* separate **dharma**
(righteous conduct, the ethical ends) from **artha** (material prosperity and statecraft, the
pragmatic means), **kāma** (desire/pleasure), and **mokṣa** (liberation) [S10]. Kautilya's
*Arthaśāstra* deliberately brackets morality — "it does not address the moral dimensions of
sovereign power... those are subjects for separate texts concerned with dharma" — while Manu's
*Dharmaśāstra* supplies the ethical ends [S10]. This is the tradition's own **is/ought (means/ends)
boundary**, and a clean template: dharma is precisely the part of life that is *not* reducible to
efficacy, prosperity, pleasure, or coercion.

### Where the boundary blurs (and why that is expected)
No single feature is necessary and sufficient; dharma is a **family-resemblance** concept.
Prototypical cases (Buddhism, Stoicism, Ubuntu) exhibit all five features; marginal cases
(a professional code, a fandom, a political movement) exhibit some. The corpus's habit of
calling punk rock, environmentalism, and "democratic dharmas" dharmas [A] is analytically
useful for comparison but courts the **over-extension failure** its own analysts flag: the
more things are dharmas, the less the term discriminates [A]. Two principled blur-sources:

1. *Graded membership.* The edge cases are real, not errors — they are frameworks with a
   subset of the features, and where one draws the cut-off is a stipulation, not a discovery.
2. *The moral/convention line is itself cultural.* The Turiel distinction was built largely on
   Western samples and is contested cross-culturally: Shweder, Mahapatra & Miller found Indians
   moralise much that WEIRD subjects treat as mere convention, and Fessler et al. (2015) and
   Barrett et al. (2016) document wide cross-cultural variation in moral parochialism and the
   role of intentions [S7]. So the very boundary between "dharma" and "mere custom" shifts by
   culture — and, tellingly, the tradition that coined *dharma* casts the moral net wider than
   the Western moral/conventional distinction assumes. It also strains a *second* Turiel
   criterion. The "vs Law" boundary above leans on Turiel's mark that moral norms are **general
   in scope** [S8]; yet the Gita makes paradigmatic dharma **agent-relative** — one's own duty,
   indexed to one's nature and station, is better than another's, "however imperfect" its
   performance (the argument at 3.35 and 18.47) [S11]. *Svadharma* is thus dharmic *and*
   agent-indexed at once, which the "general in scope" test would misclassify. ▲ Cited for what
   the text argues, by chapter.verse only; the anonymous corpus translation (register

### Naming the apparatus: a family-resemblance, essentially contested concept
The blur just described is not a local quirk of *dharma*; it is what twentieth-century
philosophy of language predicts for any rich human concept, and naming the apparatus sharpens
the analysis. Wittgenstein showed that *game* has no feature common to all its instances — only
"a complicated network of similarities overlapping and criss-crossing" — so such concepts cohere
by **family resemblance**, not by a shared essence [S13]. Cognitive psychology made this precise
as the **prototype** model: membership is graded around central exemplars (a robin is a "better"
bird than a penguin), with fuzzy edges rather than necessary-and-sufficient criteria [S14]. And
Waismann's **open texture** shows that even a well-made definition cannot anticipate every future
case, so an empirical concept is never fully closed [S15]. The corpus reaches the same place in
its own idiom, contrasting "sharp and homogenous" definitions (the triangle) with the "borderless
and diffuse" definitions typical of human concepts like *freedom* or *intelligence*, whose clarity
varies with field, purpose, culture, and time [A].

For an *ethical* category, Gallie's **essentially contested concepts** supplies the decisive
twist: for concepts such as *art*, *justice*, and *democracy*, disagreement about the correct
application is not a defect awaiting a better definition — it is *internal to the concept itself*
[S16]. Dharma behaves exactly so (the corpus stages this as a standing dare, "I dare you: define
dharma!" [A]), which means the **over-extension worry** flagged above [A] is not evidence the
analysis failed; it is a property of the kind of thing dharma is.

This reframes the task. A definition is not one true statement of an essence but a **tool**, and
there are several kinds for different jobs — *lexical* (reporting use), *stipulative* (assigning a
meaning), *precising* (sharpening a vague term for a purpose), and *ostensive* (pointing) [S17].
"What *is* a dharma?" therefore has no context-free answer; the productive question is a
*precising* one — for *this* comparative purpose, where shall we draw the line, and what do we
lose by drawing it there? The feature-checklist above is offered in exactly that spirit: a
stipulation fit for cross-cultural comparison, not a discovery of dharma's hidden essence — which
is why its edges are meant to be graded and its cut-off admittedly chosen rather than found.

### The power objection: Asad's genealogy, and what the disclosed tool concedes

The strongest standing objection to this note's whole apparatus is not semantic but
genealogical, and it must be met by name. Talal Asad, against Geertz's universal definition of
religion, argued "that there cannot be a universal definition of religion, not only because its
constituent elements and relationships are historically specific, but because that definition is
itself the historical product of discursive processes" [S18]. The point is not that "religion"
is fuzzy; it is that the very *project* of lifting a transhistorical, functional category out of
the world's ways of living is an act with a specific Christian-then-secular European history,
entangled with administration and empire — an ideological act wearing the costume of an analytic
one [S18]. Every word of this applies to "dharma" as this project universalises it: a Western,
secular, English-language corpus stabilising a Sanskrit word into a cross-cultural functional
category is exactly the move Asad anatomised, and the project's own note on the "world
religions" containers ([[7.5-orientalism]], and Part 6 of the essays) makes the same charge
against the nineteenth century without, until now, turning it on the project's own container [A].

The answer this note can honestly give is the one its own apparatus already implies: concede
the genealogy and remove the concealment. Asad's critique is lethal to definitions that pose as
*discoveries* — neutral descriptions of what religion (or dharma) really is. It has much less
purchase on a definition offered *as* a stipulation: purpose-relative, historically situated,
authored by a nameable someone, judged by its work, and disposable where it fails [S17][A]. The
disclosed tool does not escape Asad's history — nothing does — but it converts the fatal charge
(ideology disguised as analysis) into the tractable one the project must then keep answering in
the open: *for what work, for whom, do we need to stabilise this word at all, and what plurality
do we put at risk by stabilising it?* (the companion essay `defining-definition.md` develops
exactly this move). What cannot be claimed after Asad is innocence; what can be claimed is
authorship, on the record [A]. His parallel argument that "the secular" is itself a positive,
constructed formation rather than religion's residue belongs to the Part 7 frame and is carried
in [[6.5-secularization-nones]].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The corpus admits this exact gap.** Its analytical essay's "Limitations" section calls for
  "clearer criteria distinguishing 'dharma' from adjacent constructs (ideology, worldview,
  moral foundation, habitus)" and warns the framework can become "too broad to be analytically
  discriminating" [A]. The corpus supplies a strong *positive* definition but no *contrastive*
  criteria — the entire boundary analysis here was imported via the external pass (Turiel,
  Sumner, the puruṣārthas). [bias flag]
- **Secular over-extension.** Following the project's non-privileging stance, the corpus
  stretches "dharma" to any "lived ethical-cultural system" [A]. This maximises comparative
  reach but sacrifices precision; this note pushes back by proposing an explicit feature
  checklist and a family-resemblance model.
- **A WEIRD lens smuggled in by the very tools that fix the gap.** Turiel's moral/conventional
  distinction — the cleanest instrument for "dharma vs etiquette" — rests on contested,
  largely Western data [S7][S8]. Using it to draw the dharma/convention boundary partly imports
  a Western framing (developed further in [[5.3-moral-psychology-development]]).
- **"Situational ethics" resists checklists.** Corpus voices insist dharma's essence is
  *situational appropriateness* guided by compassion, not rule-following [A]; this sits in
  tension with any feature list that foregrounds "norms." Resolved by treating a dharma's norms
  as *defeasible guides*, not algorithms.
- **Cited via summaries.** Turiel (1983), Sumner's *Folkways* (1906), the Arthaśāstra/
  Dharmaśāstra, and the puruṣārthas are cited via the SEP and encyclopedic/policy summaries,
  not the primary texts; philosophical treatments that would sharpen "vs etiquette" (Foot 1972)
  and "vs law" (Hart 1961) are noted but not yet consulted. The Gita is the one primary dharmic
  text now available in the corpus, but only for argument-structure claims: its translation is
  anonymous and unreliable (register `verified: unverified`), so it can stress-test the checklist
  (as with svadharma's agent-relativity above [S11]) without supplying any word-level evidence.
- **The agent-relativity point cuts both ways.** That *svadharma* is agent-indexed [S11] is a
  genuine strain on the "general in scope" criterion, but it is drawn from *one* tradition's
  self-theorisation and should be tested against the checklist, not treated as overturning it: the
  checklist is an avowed precising stipulation [S17], and a prototype tradition exhibiting an
  atypical feature is exactly the graded-membership case the family-resemblance model predicts, not
  a refutation of it [A].
- **Asad was absent until July 2026 — a retrieval-loop instance.** The strongest published
  opponent of this note's central move appeared nowhere in the note, the essays, or the corpus:
  the shared library's curated lean carries no genealogical critique of the "religion"/"dharma"
  category, and similarity retrieval never surfaced what the shelf does not hold (the
  mechanism documented in [[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]]). The engagement above was added
  after the project's critical review named the gap; Asad is cited from verified excerpts
  [S18], not a full-text read — a fuller engagement with *Genealogies of Religion* ch. 1
  remains open work.
