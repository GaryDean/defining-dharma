# 3.5.2 Sumarah: Practice and Transmission

**Question:** What is Sumarah practice — sujud, the pamong's spontaneous tuntunan, the inner 'guru sejati', the deliberate absence of creed, canon, technique, hierarchy and fees, and tapa rame (practice amid ordinary life) — and how is it transmitted, including its westward transmission since the 1970s?

## Synthesis

Sumarah's sole practice is **sujud** — a guided group meditation of total surrender in which the
practitioner relaxes body, feeling and mind rather than concentrating, so that "the self functions
as a vehicle" for what the movement calls God's will [S1][S6]. It is deliberately *empty of
apparatus*: no canon, no fixed technique, no ritual conditions, no clergy, no fees — authority is
relocated to an inner "true teacher" (*guru sejati*), and the human guide (**pamong**) is defined
as a momentary *function*, a "clean mirror" whose spontaneous guidance (*tuntunan*) is checked by
collective witnessing and "cannot be faked" [S1][S2][S5][S6]. Transmission is therefore purely
**experiential contact** — person-to-person attunement in weekly sessions, historically in
Javanese, spreading by word of mouth — which made the practice deep but structurally non-scalable:
where Subud's uniform latihan globalised to ~83 countries [S17], Sumarah's pamong-dependent sujud stayed
a Javanese association of a few thousand, with only "several hundred international practitioners
since 1971" and, by policy, "no international organization" [S6][S10]. Westward transmission ran
through exactly two informal waves — Stange's Solo circle from 1971 (exported under the negotiated
label "relaxed meditation") and Laura Romano's European workshops and retreats from 1995 — and in
translation the practice's theistic surrender was softened toward a "philosophy of life" for
spiritual-but-not-religious audiences [S1][S3][S8][S11][S12][S13]. The project's habituation lens
([[5.4-ritual-habituation]]) adds the critical point: Sumarah's anti-technique is itself a
discipline — weekly sessions, staged surrender (*tekad → iman → sumarah*) and practice amid
ordinary life (*tapa rame*) install durable dispositions precisely while denying that any form is
being transmitted [A].

## Findings

### What a session actually looks like

Sumarah has no temples, shrines or sacred sites; group meetings are held "regularly, usually in
the homes of people who act as guides," and members wear no distinctive dress and use no special
symbols — the practice is socially invisible [S5][S9]. A Subud-side comparison (one of the few
non-Stange descriptions) notes that Sumarah meetings are long — "usually three hours" — combining
discussion and meditation led by a pamong, against Subud's wordless ~30-minute latihan [S10].
Practice begins somatically: "the release of tension in the physical body and the stilling of the
five senses and the mind," after which attention shifts from thought into *rasa* (intuitive
feeling) [S9]; the two keynotes are "first centering, through attuning intuitively to the whole
body," then surrendering — an opening that "does not require concentration, pressure or stress"
[S1]. Within the session, guidance (*tuntunan*) is "a critical vehicle for practice": at some
point the pamong may speak spontaneously from attunement, articulating "experience which is
collectively witnessed" by the sitting group [S1][S6].

Laura Romano — the movement's main Western teacher — supplies the only verbatim session transcript
in the corpus: her pamong Pak Wondo speaks intermittently into a long shared silence, closing with
the blessing "rahayu"; his summary of the movement's anti-scripturalism is categorical: "In
Sumarah we have no sacred book. Our sacred book is Life" [S3]. She stresses that a session is not
blissful escape: "most of the time Sumarah meditation is a process of cleansing and facing the
hidden parts of ourselves" [S3]. Practice divides into two registers — the "'special' meditation
and 'daily' meditation" (*sujud pamiji* and *sujud harian*), the aim of daily practice being to
close the gap between the two [S3][S2]; since the movement's fourth phase (confirmed at the 1974
congress), practice has been defined as "a continuous rather than occasional exercise," awareness
diffused through the body across ordinary activity [S4]. This is *tapa rame*, "the 'noisy
retreat'" — practising "peace right in the middle of the battlefield" of work and family life,
meditation as "an instrument of life, for life and in life, not an end in itself" [S3]. Recent
independent Indonesian scholarship confirms the sujud session as the movement's "most essential
ritual," attended together by members who remain Muslims, Christians and Buddhists — an
interreligious ritual in one room [S14].

### Relaxation, not concentration

Sumarah defines itself *against* concentration meditation. The Solo pamong Sudarno Ong, asked to
compare Vipassana and Zen, allowed their value but judged that "most of the methods seem to stop
at the level of concentration"; Sumarah's move is instead to "remove attention from the mind into
feeling, into intuition" [S1]. His signature analogy is "taming a wild horse": rather than roping
the mind down, let its energy release itself until it settles; concentration devices are crutches,
and "the crutch becomes a habit after it has served its true purpose" [S1]. Romano codifies the
same stance for Westerners: "there are no fixed roles, like a specific way of breathing, a
technique to help concentration, a specific position," and no promise of salvation or success
[S3]; Sumarah "disapproves of the use and development of cipto" (visualising/projective mind)
"because it feeds the ego and implies concentration," and "concentration is considered to create
tension" [S2]. The boundary is policed theologically as well: members "frequently rejected the
term 'meditasi', saying it was different from 'sujud' because it led to void and not God" [S4] —
a reminder that beneath the relaxed form the practice remains surrender *to* something (see
[[3.5.4-secular-dharma-question]]).

### Stages without a curriculum

What replaces a graded syllabus is a loosely-held map of deepening surrender. Sukinohartono's own
formulation, kept central by later leader Arymurthy, is the triad **tekad → iman → sumarah** —
"drive, conviction, and surrender" [S1]. Romano renders the same triad as "three stage[s] of a
deeper and deeper relaxation, where aku (the ego) lets go," ending in "a completely neutral
position where no effort is needed anymore" in which *Tuntunan* — "the 'guiding energy' that
literally guides us" — is received [S2]. Crucially the endpoint is never a credential: sumarah "is
a state that can never be taken for granted," and practitioners self-mockingly call theirs *ilmu
kira kira*, "the science of more or less so" [S2].

The movement did once build a formal apparatus — and then dismantled it. Wartime practice split
into youth (*kanoman*) and elder (*kasepuhan*) tracks, the kanoman comprising "karaga, meaning
automatic movement; karasa, meaning sensitizing of intuition; and kasuara, meaning spontaneous
speech" [S4]. The 1950s organisation graded members' consciousness A–E with periodic "checking,"
overlaid by Sukino's levels "Jinem, Junun and Suhul" — and promptly generated the pathology of
"competition for consciousness" (*rebutan martabat*) [S4]. By the fourth phase the levels and
initiations were no longer emphasised: the staging survives as orientation, not hierarchy [S4].
The arc — ecstatic exercises formalised, then shed for silent continuous practice — is Sumarah's
own enactment of the de-ritualisation this project tracks at [[5.4-ritual-habituation]] [A].

### The pamong: authority without lineage

The pamong is Sumarah's answer to the guru problem, and the movement's most distinctive
institution [A]. Doctrine is explicit that "the term 'pamong' refers in principle to function rather
than person": validity of guidance "is entirely dependent on what others, sharing meditation with
pamong, experience for themselves" [S4]. Arymurthy's formulations are categorical: "a person is a
pamong only in that instant of duty"; outside it he "has no special privilege or authority
whatsoever"; and the status is unfakeable — "a person could say a thousand times that he is a
pamong and yet not be one. It cannot be faked" [S5]. A pamong "does not announce himself as such"
but is recognised through signs within shared practice [S5]. The working analogies are all
*transparency* metaphors: Arymurthy's mirror ("you become like a mirror"; the meditator may
"temporarily borrow the mirror of another"), Sudarno's two matched gamelan resonating, Joyo
Sampoerno's tuned radio receivers [S1][S4]. The real teacher is inside: "the 'true teacher' (*guru
sejati* or *hakiki* being terms for this)" — so there are, in the movement's insistence, "no
'guru'" at all [S1][S6].

Authority without lineage still needs an error-correction mechanism, and Sumarah's is collective:
the pamong's words are "collectively witnessed" against the sitting group's own attunement, and
misuse is held to be self-punishing — "wrong advice backfires on its source as well as dirtying
the receiver" [S1]. Stange notes the mechanism's live failure mode: "in practice there is a
tendency to suppress criticism for the sake of unanimity" [S9]. The system's severest test was
leader Surono's 1957–66 drift into claiming to be *penyalur dawuh tunggal* — sole channel of
divine instruction — and spirit-medium politics; the 1966 congress "emphatically rejected the
notion of 'penyalur dawuh tunggal'" and transferred leadership, which insiders read as the
collective check working against exactly the guru-capture the pamong doctrine exists to prevent
[S4] (full narrative at [[3.5.1-origins-and-history]]). Stange's 1991 Charles Strong lecture
generalises the pattern: Javanese sects including Sumarah "positively deny lineages," claiming a
new *wahyu* "direct from God" as "a universal, rather than culture-bound, practice"; a
"democratising modernism deemphasises status, patronage and dependence on guru," and the teacher
"can be no more than a catalyst" who conveys no content [S7]. Sumarah is thus a limiting case for
[[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]]: a tradition that transmits while formally denying it
has anything — text, technique, or teacher — to transmit [A].

### Transmission without canon — and what it costs

From the founding pact onward, apparatus was refused on principle. When co-founder Suhardo was
initiated in 1937 he rejected slametan-style ritual: "this initiation can take place at any time
or place / without any conditions whatsoever" — Stange's gloss: "ritual and secrecy were ruled out
from the start" [S4]. There is "no fixed form of teaching, nor even of technique" [S4]; the
encyclopedic summary runs: "no canon of teachings, no sacred texts," no sacred sites, no fees
beyond voluntary support [S6]. Early growth accordingly ran through "the 'grapevine' (*getok
tular*)" — an experiential chain reaction through kin and friendship networks, not preaching [S4].
The corollary deserves stating as a fact in its own right: Sumarah has never proselytised, and has
nothing to convert anyone *to* — no profession of belief is asked of a newcomer, who remains
Muslim, Christian, Buddhist or unlabelled while sitting [S6][S14]; the invitation, in the author's
summary of decades of direct contact, was only ever to *just sit* [A].
The one early exception, the one-to-one corrective guidance called *nyemak*, was generalised away
after 1949; what remains is transmission "based on this sense of experiential contact rather than
on a specific technique or ritual practice," and the attunement capacity itself (*gandeng rasa*)
[S9] is held to be "something anyone can develop; it is not seen as the preserve of unique,
special or gifted people" [S5].

The costs of this purity are visible in the movement's own record. First, **language and
presence**: sujud is verbally guided and three hours long [S10], and was historically conducted in
(often high) Javanese — Sukino's guidance came "through difficult high Javanese (krama inggil)"
[S4] — transmission requires a mature pamong physically present, which is why Sumarah stayed
Javanese while Subud's silent, identical-everywhere latihan globalised to roughly 10,000 members
in about 83 countries [S17] (the structural contrast is developed at [[4.5-subud-and-sumarah]])
[S10]. Second, **centre-periphery drift**: without a canon
to standardise, "farther from the centre... the tendency to translate Sumarah into a 'religious'
exercise became stronger"; Geertz's 1953 Pare snapshot of members chanting the founder's words
reflected, in Stange's judgement, "the immaturity... of the guides he was exposed to" — periphery
branches re-ritualise what the centre refuses to fix [S4]. Third, **non-scalability by design**:
"although there have been several hundred international practitioners since 1971, there is no
international organization" [S6] — smallness is partly a chosen consequence of refusing
reproducible form [A].

### Westward transmission since 1971

The Western line begins precisely: Stange himself. "My entry into Sumarah, and through it the
beginning of a Western following, was through Sudarno" — the Buddhist-leaning Solo pamong — from
October 1971; with Suyono Hamongdarsono interpreting, an informal international network grew from
the Solo branch [S1][S4], ironically the branch then considered old-fashioned by the Jakarta
leadership [S8]. The organisation's definitive policy came from Arymurthy in September 1973, in a
special meeting for foreigners after the Solo conference [S8]: Westerners are guests, not members
— "I stress that you are not members of Paguyuban Sumarah" — and for the supervising Justice
Department their practice is labelled "relaxed meditation," while spiritually "*sujud* or
meditation, it is without question the same" [S1]. The same address renounced expansionism:
Sumarah would spread worldwide but must never "spiritually 'colonize' other nations," and
foreigners were deliberately "given no distinct forms to identify their practice" — no label,
initiation or teaching to transplant [S1][S4]. The label itself stayed contested: in another
setting Arymurthy insisted "relaxed meditation was not Sumarah," policing the difference between
a relaxation exercise and surrender to God [S4] — the export framing was a diplomatic wrapper, not
a doctrinal equation.

Diffusion then proceeded person-by-person: Australian groups formed in Canberra and Perth (1976),
Sydney (1977) and Melbourne (1978) [S8]. The second wave, from the 1990s, ran outside the
organisation entirely — what Stange calls the spread of Sumarah "in essence... without
institutional connection": Laura Romano's Sumarah workshops around Europe, Suprapto Suryodarmo's
movement-meditation workshops descending from Sudarno's line, and Suyono's "relaxed meditation"
circles; among Suprapto's Western followers, a dozen-plus settled in Solo and sat sujud with Pak
Wondo [S8]. Romano — Milan-born, philosophy MA (Siena), decades in Solo as student of Pak Wondo
and Arymurthy — has taught workshops in Europe, the UK and Australia since 1995 [S3][S13]; her
*Sumarah: Spiritual Wisdom from Java* (2013; Italian original 1999) is the movement's principal
Western text [S11]. The present Western network is exactly what the no-form policy predicts:
small, personal and non-institutional — weekly groups in Italy, Germany, Belgium, the UK,
Australia and the USA, heavily Zoom-based since Covid, coordinated through the insider hub
sumarah.net, with Romano still leading an in-person retreat in July 2026 [S12]. A
therapy-adjacent channel has opened through the UK contact Sandra Reeve, a movement
psychotherapist in the Suprapto lineage, where "comparisons with Sumarah are rife among
practitioners" of Amerta movement work [S16].

What changed in translation is consistent across sources: the explicitly theistic surrender of the
Javanese practice (sujud to *Tuhan*) is presented Westward as "a philosophy of life and a form of
meditation," with God-language softened toward "the universal self" [S3][S12]; the state-facing
"not a religion" stance of New Order Indonesia became, in Europe, a spiritual-but-not-religious
selling point [A]. Romano names the distinctively Western obstacle the practice must now work on:
an educated will-power culture — "if you want it strongly enough... then you'll get it" — that
makes surrender itself the foreign move [S2].

### An anti-technique that still installs dispositions

Read through [[5.4-ritual-habituation]], Sumarah's "no technique, no ritual" is best understood as
a *second-order* discipline rather than an absence of one [A]. The movement removes doctrinal and
ritual scaffolding, but it retains — and depends on — repetition structures that habituate: the
weekly three-hour session [S5][S10]; the entry vows (*Sesanggeman*) that frame practice in a
shared ethical commitment [S1]; the staged relaxation gradient (*tekad → iman → sumarah*) whose
"stages" are precisely trained dispositional depths [S2]; the practice of *eling* ("continuous
remembering"), treated as synonymous with awareness itself [S3]; and *tapa rame*, which converts
all of ordinary life into the practice field [S3]. What is being installed is not a belief or a
motor routine but a trained perceptual disposition — the progressive refinement of *rasa* from
coarse to subtle (*kasar → halus → murni*) [S2][S9] (the epistemology is treated at
[[3.5.3-rasa-epistemology]]). Sudarno's own pedagogy concedes the point in its imagery: the
concentration crutch is dropped *after* "it has served its true purpose" — scaffolding is used and
shed, which is a theory of habituation, not a denial of it [S1]. The nearest Western analogue in
this project is the Alexander Technique ([[5.4.1-alexander-technique]]): a hands-on, largely
non-doctrinal transmission in which a teacher's calibrated contact retrains dispositions the pupil
cannot initially perceive — like the pamong, an authority justified only by the pupil's own
eventual verification [A].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

**The Stange monoculture is at its most acute in this note.** Paul Stange is simultaneously
Sumarah's principal scholar and a committed practitioner — he brokered the very westward
transmission this note documents ("My entry into Sumarah, and through it the beginning of a
Western following, was through Sudarno" [S4]) — so for the practice and its Western history the
corpus's main witness is a participant in both. Every KB source here is Stange or the
Western-practitioner pedagogy of Romano (herself an insider whose texts are devotional, not
critical). Balancing external sourcing was therefore essential: the Subud-side description of
meetings [S10], independent Indonesian scholarship on sujud as interreligious ritual [S14], the
live sumarah.net network data and the BuddhaZine interview [S12][S13]. Two genuinely independent
ethnographic witnesses exist but remain unretrieved — David Gordon Howe's parallel 1980 UNC
dissertation *Sumarah: A Study of the Art of Living* [S15] and Hadiwijono's 1967 doctrinal
analysis of Sumarah's guidance system — and until they are read, the description of *how sujud
feels and works* rests on insider testimony.

**Emic claims are reported, not endorsed.** That tuntunan is divine guidance, that pamong status
"cannot be faked," that wrong advice metaphysically "backfires" — these are the movement's
self-understanding, transmitted by insider sources; the sociological reading (charismatic
authority checked by group consensus) is available in the same material, and Stange himself
concedes the consensus ideal shades into suppression of criticism [S9].

**The "relaxed meditation" label is strategically double.** Arymurthy equates sujud with
meditation when reassuring foreigners and the Justice Department [S1], and denies the equation
("relaxed meditation was not Sumarah") when policing doctrine [S4]. Western presentations inherit
the ambiguity: the corpus's secular/Batchelor-adjacent lean makes it tempting to read Sumarah as a
proto-secular relaxation practice, but the sources are saturated with God, surrender and wahyu —
the softening to "universal self" is a translation artefact of the 1990s export, not the Javanese
practice [S3][S12]. This note follows the project's non-privileging stance and keeps the theism
visible.

**Gaps.** No independent observational account of a full contemporary sujud session (Indonesian or
Western) exists in the corpus; membership and group-size figures for the Western network are
insider-reported; the Hetty Draayer (Netherlands) lineage question and Romano's exact arrival date
in Java remain unresolved [S12][S13]; and whether fourth-phase "continuous meditation" is actually
sustained by ordinary members — as opposed to being the official self-description — is untestable
from the available sources [A].
