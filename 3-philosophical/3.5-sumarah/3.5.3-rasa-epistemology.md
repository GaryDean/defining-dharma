# 3.5.3 Sumarah: Rasa Epistemology

**Question:** What is rasa in Javanese culture and in Sumarah practice — and what theory of knowledge does a rasa-grounded path imply? How does Stange's 'logic of rasa' and his praxis-over-text argument challenge text-centred, deconstructionist and orientalist readings of Javanese religion — and what are the limits of an insider epistemology?

## Synthesis

**Rasa** is a Sanskrit-derived Javanese term whose semantic range runs from the taste of food through emotional feeling to intuitive perception and the deepest apprehension of the ultimate — and which Javanese mystical psychology treats not as an emotion but as a **cognitive organ** with "the same status as 'mind'", the instrument through which inner (*batin*) realities are known [S1][S6][S7]. Sumarah practice operationalises this: meditation (*sujud*) relocates attention from thought into rasa, and knowledge-claims are validated not by faith, text or teacher but by direct recognition in the practitioner's own awareness, cross-checked by a collective felt consensus (*cocok*) in group meditation [S1][S5][S9]. On this base Paul Stange builds a general epistemological argument: Javanese religion is constituted in **praxis**, not text, so text-centred scholarship (Woodward's kraton-Islam thesis) and deconstructionist readings (Keeler, Florida, Pemberton and others) systematically misdescribe it — their "methodological atheism" and "systemic blindness to rasa" amounting to a **new orientalism** that re-subordinates the people it claims to emancipate [S2][S3][S4][S12][S14]. The critique has real force, but the insider epistemology it defends has structural limits: Sumarah's verification procedures are internal to the practice community and effectively unfalsifiable from outside; the seemingly naturalistic "organ of knowing" is, in Sumarah's own terms, theistically underwritten — rasa receives *Tuntunan* (guidance) through *hakiki*, the channel "directly from God", and Sumarah policed its boundary by insisting sujud is not "meditation" precisely because meditation "led to void and not God" [S5][S9]. Constructivist philosophy of mysticism supplies the strongest external counter: if there are no unmediated experiences, a trained rasa may be as much culturally produced as culturally discovered — a live dispute, not a settled result, and one this note's near-total dependence on a single adherent-scholar cannot adjudicate [S13][A].

## Findings

### The semantic range of rasa

The word migrated from Sanskrit, where *rasa* is "best known through Abinagupta's theories of aesthetics and drama in the classical text called the Natyasastra" — a term of art for aesthetic savour before it was one for mystical knowing [S7]. In Java its range became famously wide. Stange's summary formulation: rasa "links the physical sense of 'taste' and 'touch' to emotions... and the deepest mystical apprehension of the ultimate" [S1]. At the everyday level it is simply the feeling-register of life: ordinary Javanese say *saya merasa* ("I feel") in contexts where an English speaker would say "I think" [S7]. At the reflective level it is defined as an organ: "the cognitive organ which, as Javanese mystics understand it, we use to 'know' the intuitive aspects of reality" [S1].

The philological layering matters. Citing Gonda, Stange notes that the Javanese fused the original Sanskrit meanings of rasa (taste, flavour, essence) with *rahasya* ("secret, mystery"), and that in Islamic Java rasa "often served to translate the Arabic *sirr*" (the innermost secret of the heart in Sufi psychology) — so the term carries Indic aesthetics and Sufi interiority simultaneously [S1]. In his encyclopedia gloss the same structure appears in compressed form: *lahir* is the outer reality known through senses and intellect; *batin* the "inner spiritual realities known only through the spiritual heart or *'rasa'*, a Sanskrit term referring in this context to 'intuitive feeling'" [S10].

Rasa is emphatically **pan-Javanese, not Sumarah property**: Sapta Darma has its *tali rasa* (cords of feeling, an esoteric anatomy), Pangestu its *Rahsa Djati*, and rasa-vocabulary saturates kebatinan psychology generally [S1][S10]. A final subtlety: rasa is double-faced, "at once the substance, vibration, or quality of what is apprehended and the tool or organ which receives it" — both the signal and the receiver [S1].

### An epistemic instrument, not an emotion

The crucial claim — the one Western category-schemes most reliably miss — is that rasa is not "feelings" in the sense of affect but a **way of knowing**. Javanese "knowledge" in its significant form is *ngelmu*, gnosis: "it is the whole body, and all organs within it, rather than just the mind that 'knows'" [S1]. In this psychology rasa "has the same status as 'mind'": mind processes the outer world (*alam lahiriyah*), "while rasa is the tool through which we apprehend inner realities, that is *alam batiniyah*" [S1][S6]. And like intellect, rasa is **trainable**: "rasa is susceptible to development in the same way that 'intellect' is" — it is a common human faculty, not the preserve of an esoteric elite [S7][S3].

The insider pedagogy makes the epistemic ranking explicit. Laura Romano, the movement's principal Western teacher, writes that in the spiritual approach rational thought (*pikir*) "is considered the simplest and most elementary tool of knowledge", while rasa "is the intelligence of our feelings and has its own 'logic'" — refined through practice from *rasa kasar* (coarse) toward *rasa halus* (subtle) and ultimately *rasa murni* (pure) [S8]. Notably, Sumarah also **rejects** a rival inner faculty: *cipto* (visualising concentration, the engine of clairvoyance and occult power) is disapproved of "because it feeds the ego and implies concentration" — the path is de-escalation of effort, not psychic acquisition [S8].

### How tuning attention to rasa is claimed to yield knowledge

The mechanics, as Sumarah describes them, are a redeployment of attention. Practice begins with relaxing the body and stilling the senses and thoughts; attention then settles into rasa such that there is "not simply increasing awareness of feeling but rather awareness *through* feeling", shading "into *rasa sejati*, the absolute or true feeling" [S1]. The pamong Sudarno Ong's instruction to Western students was the same move stated as method: "remove attention from the mind into feeling, into intuition" [S9].

What makes this an *epistemology* rather than a relaxation technique is the verification apparatus wrapped around it:

- **Anti-authoritarian verification.** In Sumarah "no 'faith' in the authority of an external teaching or teacher is necessary"; the only proof is "the individual's direct recognition of its truth within their own conscious awareness". Pamong status itself is subject to the same test — in Arymurthy's words, "A person could say a thousand times that he is a pamong and yet not be one. It cannot be faked" [S1].
- **Collective certification.** Individual reception is cross-checked in group meditation: organisational decisions "are not judged on the basis of logical consistency, but on the basis of whether they mesh (*cocok*) with collective feeling, with rasa" [S5]. Even revelation is subject to this: at the 1950 congress Dr. Surono insisted "Even if it is Hakiki it also has to be proven... Even Sukino asks us for our agreement", drawing the contrast "We differ from religions, within which people accept God on faith and without knowledge" [S5]. The movement thus presents itself as *empirical* where religions are fideist — mystical experience "is at the same time both self-validating and empirical", though "not accessible to or through the intellect" [S5].
- **Rasa as social epistemology.** The same faculty is held to operate in ordinary transactions. Stange's analysis of a 1988 Solo arts dispute turns on it: the silence that the Javanese director Sija read as "'felt assent', one indeed registered by his Indonesian associates, was for Manuel [his European counterpart] a 'failure to address the issue'" — communication happening, or failing, "in the feeling domain (rasa) of transaction" [S11].
- **Built-in fallibilism.** The tradition itself hedges its instrument. Stange opens *Politik Perhatian* with the trickster-messenger Narada: "even when something comes through the most refined *rasa*, like news arriving from Narada, we must remain careful" [S4]. Romano reports the self-deprecating tag that Sumarah "has often been defined as *ilmu kira kira*, ('the science of more or less so')" [S8]. And Stange's favourite epistemic image is gustatory and gradualist: "By smelling food we are certain it is real, but only after eating it do we know what it is" [S6].

There is even a record of rasa-training being tested against concrete tasks: in the movement's early decades the exercise of intuitive feeling "sometimes took the form of finding articles that had been hidden", and beginners were trained "to distinguish the vibrations received from feelings emerging from within" [S15]. But the same source shows how retrospective the more dramatic knowledge-claims are: of the celebrated 1944 collective visions of Japanese defeat, Stange concedes that "Given the timing of these visions, there is nothing remarkable about their content" [S15].

### Praxis over text: how Javanese religion should be read

Stange generalises the Sumarah case into a methodological thesis: mysticism, and Javanese religion broadly, must be defined "as practice and experience", not by doctrine, class base or textual content [S5]. His most compact formulation closes the 1990 essay: "Social practices are not embodied ideas, they are the life contexts from which we construct abstraction"; meanings "are located only through the ways in which they are experientially reproduced" [S2]. The equation of religion with scripture is itself historicised as "essentially a modern phenomenon facilitated by the print revolution" [S2].

The test case is his review-demolition of Mark Woodward's *Islam in Java* (1989) [S12]. Stange grants the book's virtue (Javanism as a variant of, not aberration from, Islam) but argues it "essentially presents the slant of Yogya kraton orthodoxy on Javanist Islam" — Woodward, by his own account, "put away" his Indology books four days into fieldwork after a Muhammadiyah informant persuaded him that kejawen beliefs were essentially Islamic [S2]. Against Woodward's claim that "The goal of Javanese mysticism is always union with an omnipotent and omnipresent God", Stange produces a counter-example from inside Sumarah: his own teacher **Sudarno Ong, a Buddhist pamong** who professed "a Krishnamurti styled distaste for both theism and philosophical dualism, remaining rigorously monistic" — adding the political kicker that "in Indonesian public discourses today no purely monistic positions are allowable, due to the dominance of dualistic versions of Islam within the bureaucracy". Public theology, in other words, is a poor guide to practised religion [S2].

Geertz gets subtler treatment: Stange endorses his insight that rasa is "The connecting link" making etiquette, art and mystical practice "different modes of the same reality", while faulting him because "the term 'intuition' is unfortunately absent from his vocabulary" — Geertz saw the pattern but lacked the epistemic category [S1].

### Deconstruction as disempowerment: the new-orientalism charge

Stange's 1991 polemic extends the praxis argument into an indictment of the then-new wave of Java scholarship. The core diagnosis is epistemological: scholarly convention enforces "a methodological atheism" through which "only the voiding of spirituality can come through"; professions of atheism "are read as indications of neutrality" while conviction reads as bias; "References to 'spiritual' are reread as 'political'" — producing "a systemic blindness to 'rasa'" that warps every representation built on it [S3]. His athletics analogy makes the participatory point: a sports writer who declared a runner's time impossible "because the person making the evaluation cannot" run that fast would be laughed off; scholars, he argues, routinely make the equivalent move about contemplative capacities [S3].

Ward Keeler's *Javanese Shadow Plays, Javanese Selves* (1987) [S14] is the emblem: a book on Javanese selfhood in which, on Stange's reading, indigenous rasa-theory is first dismissed (as "theosophical prejudices") and then tacitly restated — "indigenous theory is both dismissed and then appropriated without acknowledgement" [S3]. The endpoint of the argument turns Said's weapon on Said's heirs: deconstruction applied to Javanese spirituality "constitutes a new wave of 'orientalism'", whose deepest form "may lie... in the imperialism of the brain within the body"; "If ever there were a snake devouring its own tail, deconstruction is it" [S4]. Against the "void void" of postmodern negation he sets the "full void" of mystical emptiness — "Mysticism begins precisely where postmodernism ends", with Nagarjuna's dialectic "long pre-empt[ing] Derrida's deconstruction of structural oppositions" [S3]. The positive programme is a participatory epistemology: "We are the instruments of our knowledge as people, not just as intellects"; yet under prevailing rules "learning from rather than about those we study is still heresy" [S3]. He also names the double standard applied to committed researchers: in kebatinan studies, apparently "only those who declare they 'do not know', or who reject it outright, have the right to speak" [S4].

This critique is the strongest single challenge in the DD corpus to the assumption — examined at [[7.5-orientalism]] — that a secular-analytic frame is epistemically neutral [A]. Stange's warning that we risk becoming "agents of a new inquisition if we say that what we do not see cannot be there" deserves to be taken at face value [S3].

### The limits of an insider epistemology

The project's own critical apparatus must now be turned back on the insider claim.

**The advocate is the evidence.** Stange is simultaneously Sumarah's principal scholar and an initiated practitioner, and says so with unusual candour: "my perspective converges with that of my subject and in my presentation I will not distinguish between them" [S5]. In academic registers he brackets ontology — "my argument does not require a particular ontological position" [S1]; "I am not aiming to 'prove' Sumarah experience has been 'vibrationally' linked to national events... I report that such thoughts are held" [S5] — but the bracketing is asymmetric: the ethnographic texture, the counter-examples (Sudarno's monism), and the polemical energy all issue from commitment. Virtually everything this note reports about rasa passes through one insider's pen [A].

**The verification loop is internal.** Every check Sumarah offers — direct recognition, unfakeable pamong status, collective *cocok* — is administered *within* the practice community by people trained into the same categories. Failed guidance has an in-house explanation (practitioners fall; "most Sumarah members concluded that it was Surono himself who had been led astray by the spirits") [S4], and Stange himself records the consensus mechanism's known pathology: "a tendency to repress criticism due to 'belief' that there ought to be unanimity" [S1]. A verification procedure whose negative results are absorbable and whose competent judges are all initiates cannot discriminate, from outside, between a real epistemic faculty and a well-drilled interpretive community [A].

**The athletics analogy cuts both ways.** As a defence it is fair: untrained observers should hesitate to declare trained capacities impossible. As an epistemology it is unfalsifiable: any critic can be diagnosed as insufficiently trained, so no possible observer is positioned to disconfirm. What *would* falsify a rasa-knowledge claim? The tradition's own history suggests candidates — the hidden-object *karasa* exercises were, in effect, blind tests [S15], and claims like a pamong's ability to "know" another's inner state are in principle testable against self-report under controlled conditions. But the movement's mature phases moved *away* from such demonstrations (occult performance was renounced in 1950), and the surviving knowledge-claims — guidance, felt consensus, retrospective vision — are precisely the ones that resist prospective testing; the 1944 visions impress only in retrospect [S15][A]. This is the epistemic profile examined at [[7.4-measurement-falsifiability]]: not fraudulent, but structurally insulated.

**The ground is theistic after all.** Rasa initially looks like a candidate for a *non-metaphysical* ground of knowing — a trainable faculty, no scripture, no priesthood, verification in experience. But in Sumarah's own terms the faculty is a **receiver**, and what it receives is divine: *hakiki* is "the source of spiritual authority and authenticity, the channel through which spiritual guidance comes directly from God", and "By definition hakiki cannot be controlled by individuals" [S5]; *Tuntunan* (guidance) within group sessions "is a critical vehicle for practice" [S9]. The boundary was policed theologically: Sumarah members "frequently rejected the term 'meditasi', saying it was different from 'sujud' because it led to void and not God", and Arymurthy ruled that Sudarno's "relaxed meditation was not Sumarah" [S5]. So the rasa path does not escape the regress examined at [[3.3-grounding-problem]]; it relocates the ground from text to body, and then underwrites the body's deliverances with God. A secular appropriation of rasa-epistemology (the move implicitly at issue in [[3.5.4-secular-dharma-question]]) must either supply a different underwriter or accept that "refined feeling" alone cannot certify what it delivers [A].

**The constructivist counter.** The strongest external objection comes from the philosophy of mysticism: Steven Katz's argument that "there are NO pure (i.e., unmediated) experiences" — mystical experience is shaped through and through by the concepts, expectations and training the mystic brings to it [S13]. On this view, decades of kejawen-Islamic formation (nafsu psychology, wahyu narratives, Sufi heart-vocabulary) do not merely *describe* what rasa finds; they help *constitute* it. Stange's rejoinder — that constructivism is itself the "mentalist epistemology" begging the question against whole-body knowing [S3] — keeps the dispute alive rather than settling it. Honest bookkeeping: this is a standoff between an insider claim that cannot be externally verified and an outsider claim that cannot be experientially refuted [A].

**What survives scrutiny.** Two things, and they are not small. First, as *descriptive ethnography of a knowledge-culture*, the logic of rasa is powerful: it explains consensus practice, silence-reading, etiquette, pamong authority and political charisma as one system, where rival accounts (Anderson's "power", Weber's "charisma") leave the mechanism blank [S1]. Second, as *phenomenology of trained attention*, the claim that systematic attention-relocation changes what a person can notice — in interoception, in social signal-reading — is empirically respectable and connects directly to the metacognitive-skill reading of contemplative practice at [[3.2-metacognitive-function]] and the attention-training findings at [[5.1-neurological-basis]]. The contested step is the last one: from *noticing more* to *knowing the will of God*. Stange's praxis argument earns the first two; nothing in the record compels the third [A].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

**The Stange monoculture is this note's central limitation.** Paul Stange is at once Sumarah's adherent, its translator, and its principal scholar; ten of the twelve KB sources cited here are his, and the two that are not (Romano's practice notes, the encyclopedia entries) are respectively insider pedagogy and Stange again in reference-work dress. The pro-rasa case, its ethnographic evidence, *and* the polemic against its critics all issue from the same committed pen. The note manages this by (a) using Stange's own on-record concessions (consensus represses criticism; retrospective visions unremarkable; ontology bracketed) as the internal counter-weight, and (b) sourcing the opposing positions externally: Woodward [S12] and Keeler [S14] are cited as targets whose books exist and say roughly what Stange engages, but readers should note that *their characterisations in this note come via Stange's polemic* — the Woodward–Stange exchange is a live scholarly dispute (later scholarship, e.g. Ricklefs on Islamisation, weights the Islamic gestalt more heavily than Stange does), and Katz [S13] supplies the standing philosophical objection Stange never answers to constructivists' satisfaction.

**The corpus's secular lean pulls toward a domesticated rasa.** The shared corpus (curated around Boehm, Sapolsky, Graeber, Batchelor, Bregman) is congenial to rasa read as trainable interoception — a naturalisable skill. That reading is available, but it is *not* Sumarah's: the sources are explicit that rasa receives divine guidance and that void-directed meditation was ruled off-limits precisely for its godlessness [S5]. Honouring the project's non-privileging stance, this note reports the theistic underwriting rather than translating it away; the secular-translation question is deliberately deferred to [[3.5.4-secular-dharma-question]].

**Text-condition caveats.** *Politik Perhatian* is a double translation (English essays → Indonesian book → English rendering), so its wording should not be pressed as Stange's original diction [S4]. The freotopia-derived files carry OCR noise; quotes here were verified verbatim against the corpus segments cited. The 1980 thesis file is a later-revised text (it references events to the mid-1980s), so its "current" claims date to roughly the fieldwork-to-1985 window. Romano's texts are devotional-pedagogical, cited only for the movement's self-understanding, not as independent findings.

**Unresolved.** (1) No non-Stange ethnography of rasa-in-Sumarah has been located in the corpus — David Howe's parallel 1980 dissertation (flagged metadata-only in the web-scholarship survey) would be the natural independent check and remains unretrieved. (2) Whether Sumarah's collective-witness procedure ever produced a *documented* reversal of a leadership claim on purely felt grounds (the 1966 Surono deposition is the candidate case, but the record of it is internal). (3) The relation between Javanese rasa and the Indian aesthetic rasa-theory it descends from deserves its own philological treatment beyond Gonda-via-Stange.
