# 3.5.4 Sumarah and the Secular-Dharma Question

**Question:** Is Sumarah a 'secular dharma'? Test it against the project's boundary criteria: its self-description as a philosophy of life rather than a religion, its lack of guru, creed, canon and fee, its theistic core of surrender to God's will, and its regulatory life as an aliran kepercayaan — and assess the claim that Sumarah is a living, non-Western, century-old proof that the 'secular translation' problem has already been solved.

## Synthesis

Tested against the project's boundary criteria ([[1.3-boundary-definition]]), Sumarah is unambiguously a
dharma — named, identity-constituting, practice-transmitted, telos-oriented, reflectively self-aware — and
it is the project's purest case of a dharma stripped of religion's *institutional* apparatus: no canon, no
guru, no clergy, no sacred sites, no demanded creed, with authority relocated into collectively witnessed
experience and faith (*iman*) explicitly redefined as verification-in-experience rather than dogma
[S4][S5][S6][S8]. But it is not a *secular* dharma: its sole practice is surrender to God's will, its
founding charter opens by affirming God and the prophets, its origin is a *wahyu* that members internally
rank with prophetic revelation, and its own leadership policed exactly this boundary — *sujud* leads to
God where meditation leads to a void, and "relaxed meditation was not Sumarah" [S3][S9][S11][S14]. The
famous self-description "not a religion" marks the Indonesian *agama/kepercayaan* boundary — drawn against
the state's Semitic template of scripture, law and prophet, and drawn partly under duress — not the
religion/secular boundary the English phrase suggests [S1][S9][S12]. And the duress ran both ways: after
the 1965 coup and massacres the state declared atheism illegal alongside communism and made "priority
to... surrender to God" the announced price of a movement's legal existence [S12][S23], so the record can
no longer cleanly separate Sumarah's devotion from its survival strategy — a fog that is itself a
finding [A]. On the Samin–Subud spectrum
([[4.1-samin-of-java]], [[4.5-subud-and-sumarah]]) Sumarah is structurally the closest fit to a secular
dharma this project has examined, yet doctrinally it is as theistic as either: theism is not a residue to
be apologised for, as with Samin's *moksha* — surrender to God is the practice's own stated object —
though how much of the surrounding God-language is devotion, how much formulaic Javanese-Indonesian
etiquette, and how much New Order armour is no longer separable [A]. The strong claim —
that Sumarah is living proof the secular-translation problem ([[6.1-secular-translation]]) has already
been solved — accordingly fails as stated but leaves a precise and valuable residue: Sumarah demonstrates
that the *institutional* and *epistemological* halves of the problem are solvable (a rigorous
transformative practice transmitted for ninety years without text, teacher-authority, fee-for-teaching or
doctrinal assent), while the *theological* half was never solved at origin — where a secular Sumarah
exists, the translation happened in transit, in Arymurthy's "relaxed meditation" framing for foreigners
and in the "universal self" English of its Western hub [S3][S4][S18][A].

## Findings

### The case for "secular dharma": everything Sumarah does without

Sumarah lacks, by deliberate design, nearly every institutional feature the secular critique of religion
targets. It "has no canon of teachings, no sacred texts, and no sacred sites or buildings"; there are "no
'guru'" — the only authority is "the 'true teacher' (*guru sejati* or *hakiki*...) inside" [S6]. Group
meetings happen in members' homes; "there are no special places associated with the practice"; members
wear no distinctive dress and display no symbols [S5]. The *pamong* is a momentary function, not an
office: "no 'faith' in the authority of an external teaching or teacher is necessary," verification being
"the individual's direct recognition of its truth" in lived awareness — and pamong status "cannot be
faked" [S5]. Even collective decisions are subject to experiential check rather than charismatic fiat:
from the first congress, Dr Surono insisted "Even if it is Hakiki it also has to be proven... Even Sukino
asks us for our agreement" [S5]. Laura Romano's Western-facing texts open with exactly the phrasing the
question quotes: Sumarah is "a philosophy of life and a form of meditation"; it "does not offer solutions,
does not promise salvation, does not guarantee success"; meditation is "an instrument of life, for life
and in life, not an end in itself"; the ideal is *tapa rame*, practice "right in the middle of the
battlefield" of ordinary work and family — and, in the words of the pamong Pak Wondo, "In Sumarah we have
no sacred book. Our sacred book is Life" [S7].

Two further features push Sumarah toward the secular pole more strongly than any structural absence.
First, *iman* — the middle term of the *tekad → iman → sumarah* path — is explicitly non-dogmatic: a faith
"not a dogmatic one, but... rather based on the proof that reality will give us" [S8]. No doctrinal assent
is demanded at entry or ever; what 6.1 calls the practice-plus-ethics-plus-community bundle is transmitted
whole, while the doctrine layer is left radically thin [A]. Second, membership is multi-religious by
principle: Sumarah "is not identified with any religion," and its members include Muslims, Buddhists and
Christians [S6]; Stange's considered formula is that "Sumarah is neither Indic nor Islamic, but simply
spiritual, with a membership which happens to include Muslims, Christians, Buddhists and even (mainly
among foreign followers) agnostics" [S2]. Independent Indonesian scholarship confirms the picture: the
core ritual, *sujud sumarah*, is attended by practitioners "from multiple religious backgrounds and
identities" who keep those identities — an "interrituality" case study, not a conversion movement [S15].
The organisation understands itself as a means: "Sumarah, both as an organisation and as a practice of
*kebatinan*, is only a means, not an end," and members "acknowledge that there are often non-members who
are more '*sumarah*' in their way of life than many members" [S12] — an anti-institutional reflex few
religions and few secular movements manage.

On the question's "no fee" premise the corpus is thinner: no source documents teaching fees, and the
practice's home-based, buildings-less economy [S5] makes their absence plausible, but the only fee
actually documented in the sources is the *state's* — the Pakem licence regime under which movements paid
to hold meetings at all [S9]. The premise is credible but should be cited as inference, not fact [A].

### The case against: the theistic core is the practice, not its wrapping

Everything above coexists with a theism that is not peripheral but constitutive. The one thing Sumarah
*does* transmit is surrender — and the surrender has an object. The Sesanggeman, the movement's founding
vows, open the practice by declaring that "Sumarah adalah persaudaraan sujud sepenuhnya kepada Allah" — a
brotherhood of total surrender to God — "grounded in the conviction that one Truth underlies all
religions" [S11]; the same charter, in the devotional register of contemporary Indonesian members,
requires belief "that God exists, created the world and the hereafter," and acknowledgement of "His
prophets and their teachings" [S14]. The founding event is a *wahyu*: kebatinan movements including
Sumarah "internally claim the validation of wahyu equal to that of all prophets," deferring publicly to
the softer *ilham* only to avoid offending Muslim orthodoxy [S9]. In 1970 the movement's own sixth
congress went further and fixed Sukinohartono's status as **Warana** — glossed in the organisation's
statutes as the Javanese term for *prophet* — on the record of the 1980 AD/ART [S16]. A movement with a
prophet, a revelation, entry vows affirming God, and a practice defined as making the self "a vehicle for
God's will" [S6] is not secular under any non-trivial reading of the word [A].

Nor is the theism merely notional. Sumarah's leadership actively policed the line between its practice and
a secularisable "meditation": members "frequently rejected the term 'meditasi', saying it was different
from 'sujud' because it led to void and not God," and Arymurthy — the national leader for three decades —
ruled flatly that "relaxed meditation was not Sumarah" [S3]. The practice vocabulary itself is Islamic:
across the kebatinan field, movements "speak of meditation as sujud (surrender) or panembah (prayer)
rather than semadi", whose Indic resonance "renders it suspect to Islam, which sees semadi as implying
entry into a 'Godless void'" [S20]; Sumarah and Sapta Darma name their practice *sujud*, "an Islamic
term", while it is the Pangestu group that *actively* rejects *semadi*, arguing "it implies Buddhist
senses of a void rather than theistic deity" [S9]. Stange's deepest historical thesis cuts the same way:
because *sumarah* "essentially means in Javanese what 'Islam' means in Arabic," the movement is readable
as "a deconfessionalised Islam" and — for all its Indic surface — "a prime vehicle of Islamization" [S1].
The one genuinely non-theist current the record shows, the Buddhist pamong Sudarno Ong — who "explicitly
endorsed the views of Krishnamurti... remaining rigorously monistic" — proves the movement could *tolerate*
an anti-theist teacher; but Stange deploys him against Woodward's "always union with God" claim while
noting in the same breath that "in Indonesian public discourses today no purely monistic positions are
allowable" [S10], and it was exactly Sudarno's "relaxed meditation" lineage that Arymurthy ruled out as
not-Sumarah [S3]. Tolerated at the margin, ruled out at the centre: that is the shape of a theistic
mainstream, not a secular one [A].

### Running the 1.3 boundary test

Against the five markers used across this project ([[1.3-boundary-definition]]):

- **Named** — emphatically: the name *is* the practice ("the condition of total surrender") [S6], a purer
  name-practice identity than any other case in the project [A].
- **Identity-constituting** — yes, but two-layered: members carry *penghayat* identity strongly enough to
  have fought decades of KTP battles, culminating in the 2017 Constitutional Court ruling that let
  Paguyuban Sumarah members record *kepercayaan* on the identity card [S17]; yet most simultaneously
  retain a religious identity — in East Java roughly ninety per cent of members are Muslims [S13], many
  self-described as "KTP Muslims" whose *kesumarahan* is "separated from religious confession" [S11].
  Sumarah constitutes identity the way a discipline does, not the way a confession does [A].
- **Practice-transmitted** — the strongest pass in the whole project: transmission is *only* by practice
  (experiential contact, *nyemak*, pamong attunement), with no text to hand down and explicit doctrine
  that uniformity of style would betray the transmission [S5][S6]; see [[3.5.2-practice-and-transmission]].
- **Telos-oriented** — yes: total surrender, *iman bulat*, the self as vehicle of God's will [S6][S11].
  The telos is what disqualifies the "secular" label, not the "dharma" one [A].
- **Reflectively self-aware** — conspicuously: Sumarah distinguishes itself *by argument* from religion
  ("not a religion"), from occultism (*klenik*), from Sufi orders ("bukan tarekat Islam"), and from
  meditation itself (*sujud* ≠ *meditasi*) [S1][S3][S11]; it periodises its own development in phases and
  reads its history reflexively as a national microcosm [S1].

On 1.3's fuller checklist (normative core, comprehensive scope, cultivation practice, legitimating
narrative, internalisation-and-transmission) Sumarah scores five of five — the Sesanggeman supplies the
norms, the fourth-phase "continuous meditation" extends scope to the whole of daily life, and ninety-one
years of unbroken transmission answer the last criterion [S1][S11][A]. The verdict: a **full dharma**, in
which the only contested word in the question is "secular" [A].

### What "not a religion" actually means: agama, kepercayaan, and compelled speech

The English opposition religious/secular maps badly onto the Indonesian field in which Sumarah lives. What
Sumarah denies being is an *agama* — a category the Indonesian state built on an Islamic template: a
legitimate religion must "emphasise belief in one God, have a clear system of law for their following, a
holy scripture and a prophet" [S9]. Against *that* definition, "not a religion" is accurate: no scripture,
no law, no (publicly avowed) prophet [A]. But the category Sumarah *does* occupy — *kepercayaan terhadap Tuhan
Yang Maha Esa*, "belief in the One Almighty God" — is constitutively theistic: it was the price of legal
existence in a polity where atheism was coded as communism and hence subversive [S9], and it is the exact
wording the 2017 Constitutional Court ruling now allows on the identity card [S17]. Stange is explicit
that in his usage kepercayaan "cannot possibly be set in opposition to 'religion'" — it is the *inner
dimension* of religion, not its negation [S12]. The regulatory history sharpens the point: the state's
1980s formula demoted mystical membership to something "separate from the issue of religion in the same
way that education or sports are" [S9] — the closest any authority ever came to declaring Sumarah
"secular," and it was imposed, resisted, and experienced as a defeat. Meanwhile the "not a religion"
stance itself was partly strategic speech under surveillance: leaders used *wahyu* internally "but soft
peddle it in public to avoid offending orthodox Muslims" [S1], and the whole field was "caught in a double
bind" — winning recognition only by approximating the Islamic model of religious participation [S9].
Reading Sumarah's self-description as a secular manifesto mistakes a hard-won legal-theological position
for a metaphysical one [A].

A third filter must be applied before any pious token in the record is weighed: **convention**. Javanese
and Indonesian public speech is densely formulaic — addresses open with acknowledgements of *Tuhan Yang
Maha Esa* as a matter of etiquette, and the state's own kepercayaan category embeds the formula in its
legal name [S17] — so God-language at the head of a talk or a document is, by itself, weak evidence of
doctrinal weight; the author, from long residence in Indonesia, testifies that such acknowledgements
function as politeness, not profession [A]. The same filter fits what Sumarah conspicuously never did: it
never proselytised, demanded no profession of anyone who sat, and had nothing to convert a sitter *to* —
Muslims, Christians and Buddhists practised side by side and stayed what they were [S15]. Three readings
of Sumarah's God-language therefore stand together — devotion, etiquette, armour — and the record cannot
cleanly apportion the tokens among them [A]. What resists the deflationary reading entirely is a narrower
core: sujud's own definition ("so the self functions as a vehicle for God's will") and *hakiki* as the
channel of guidance are not opening formulae but the practice's self-description across five decades of
texts [S6]; the fairest statement is that the *quantity* of theistic language in the record overstates,
for reasons of etiquette and duress alike, a theistic core that is nonetheless real in the practice's own
terms [A].

### The compulsory God: 1965 and the price of legal existence

The compelled-speech analysis must be pushed one step further, because the compulsion did not shape only
the "not a religion" half of Sumarah's public identity; it shaped the God-half too — and this is the part
that could not be said aloud while the New Order stood [A]. Writing after Suharto's fall, Stange lays out
the machinery plainly: the New Order underlined its religious stance "by declaring atheism, along with
communism, illegal. Accordingly, those who did not profess and maintain belief in God were generally
regarded as subversive" [S12]. Behind that rule stood the events that brought the regime to power — the
1965 coup and the massacres "continuing into 1966, of people suspected of being PKI sympathisers" [S12],
killings whose scholarly estimates centre on half a million dead [S23] — after which "to be Indonesian
requires belief in God" became a founding assertion of the state, and demonstrating that belief required
active participation "in a recognised religious structure" [S12]. For the mystical movements the terms
were explicit: they were "pressured not to engage in politics and to give priority to awareness of, and
surrender to, God. To the extent that the movements could meet these requirements well, the organisations
could become public and open" [S12]. Pakem licensed and recorded every gathering down to private homes
[S9][S12]; movements that failed the test were banned or crushed — Manunggal (1968), Mbah Suro (1967),
Sawito (1976) [S12]; and the whole field was thereby tilted, its politically dangerous Javanist elements
"dampened" while "the tendencies most compatible with Islam" were given space [S12].

Read against that background, Sumarah's post-1965 record is a study in survival conformity [A]. In 1965
itself the movement "made it clear that it had no part in the traditional patterns connected with
ancestral spirits", moved its organisational centre to Jakarta and adopted Indonesian over Javanese
[S12]; its pamong certified members' non-involvement with the communist movement [S4]; it spoke of its
practice in the Islamic idiom of *sujud* rather than the Indic *semadi* that Islam read as "entry into a
'Godless void'" [S9][S20]; its 1970 congress formally fixed the founder as **Warana** — a prophet, the
precise item the state's Semitic template required a legitimate faith to possess [S16]; and the rulings
that godless relaxation "led to void and not God" and that "relaxed meditation was not Sumarah" [S3] were
issued by the same Arymurthy who served as the movement's chief interlocutor with the state and became
the kepercayaan directorate's first Director [S9]. None of this need be read as cynicism, and the
chronology forbids the strong version of the suspicion: the founding record is theistic a full generation
before the coup — the 1935 wahyu, the 1940 Sesanggeman's affirmation of God and the prophets, Surono's
1950 God-and-proof formula [S5][S11][S14]. What 1965 changed was not the presence of Sumarah's God but
his status: from the centre of a devotion to a legal requirement — a question of life and death in
1965–66, and of exposure and suspicion for three decades after [A].

Two consequences follow for this note's question. First, **the God-floor evidence is duress-contaminated**:
every post-1965 act of boundary-policing — the anti-void rulings, the Warana declaration, the *Tuhan Yang
Maha Esa* formatting — was performed under a state that made exactly that boundary the condition of the
movement's existence, so "the theism was the point" cannot be asserted as a purely devotional fact; it
was also, inseparably, the survival strategy [A]. Second, **the silence of the sources is not evidence of
absence**: Stange himself notes that the killings remain "impossible even now to discuss... freely in
Indonesia" [S12], and scholarship written under the New Order — the 1980 thesis, the 1986 article — could
not have named state terror as a driver of Sumarah's theological emphasis without endangering the
movement it described [A]. The project's author, from long residence in Indonesia, testifies to the same
from direct observation: the compulsion was universal, unspoken, and understood by everyone — "the New
Order state was everywhere, listening" — and it is only at this historical distance that the connection
can safely be written down at all [A]. The caution cuts both ways: just as the record cannot prove the
policed theism was pure devotion, it cannot prove it was mere armour. The duress destroyed the very
evidence that would decide the question, which is itself the deepest thing 1965 did to Sumarah's
intellectual history [A].

### The spectrum: Samin — Sumarah — Subud

Placed beside the project's two adjacent Javanese cases, Sumarah's position is distinctive. The **Samin**
([[4.1-samin-of-java]]) are a folk dharma whose this-worldly ethic carries unassimilated metaphysical
residue — an eternal soul, the founder's *moksha* — which the analysis had to treat apologetically as
inherited cosmology rather than practice-core. **Subud** ([[4.5-subud-and-sumarah]]) is overtly theistic:
the latihan is surrender to "the Power of God" [S21], and its name theologises *dharma* itself as
"surrender, trust and sincerity towards Almighty God" [S22]. **Sumarah** inverts the Samin structure: institutionally it is
the most secular-looking of the three — quiet, guided, fee-less in practice, creed-thin, evidence-first,
civically pluralist (the Sesanggeman enjoins members to avoid criticising others' beliefs and to reject
fanaticism [S14]) — but doctrinally its theism is *more* load-bearing than Samin's, because surrender to
God is not an inherited belief sitting beside the practice; it is the practice's definition [S6][S11][A].
So the critique's instinct is half right: Sumarah is genuinely the closer fit to the book's thesis than
Subud or Samin — but only at the level of *form*. Structurally it models what a secular dharma would have
to look like; doctrinally it shows no interest in being one [A].

### Assessing the strong claim: "living proof the secular-translation problem is solved"

For the claim to hold, Sumarah would need to show (a) a transformative practice that works without
metaphysical assent, (b) at origin and not merely in export, (c) sustained at century scale outside the
modern West. Taken as stated, it fails on (b) — but each component is worth separating, because what
survives is substantial [A].

**What supports the claim.** Sumarah demands no doctrinal assent: entry is by practice, verification is
experiential, *iman* is defined as proof-through-experience [S5][S8]. The practice demonstrably survives
transplantation to secular and agnostic Westerners — Stange records agnostics among the foreign following
from the 1970s [S2], and half a century later a live, if small, Western network of weekly groups persists
from Italy to Australia, still framing the practice as a philosophy of life and rendering the theistic
vocabulary as "surrender of the ego to the universal self" [S18]. Against 6.1's central finding — that
translation succeeds when the practice-ethics-community bundle survives and fails when only technique is
extracted — Sumarah is a native instance of the *reconstruction* pole: it kept the bundle and discarded
canon, guru and rite a full generation before Western secular Buddhism attempted the same operation
([[6.1-secular-translation]], [[8.3-post-traditional-dharmas]]) [A]. It is also a standing counter-example
to the claim that transformative depth *requires* scripture, clergy, or creed [A].

**What undercuts it.** First, the theistic self-understanding of Javanese practitioners is not detachable:
the vows affirm God and the prophets [S11][S14], the leadership ruled God-less relaxation out of the
definition [S3], and the movement's own congress canonised its founder as Warana/prophet [S16]. Second —
the decisive point — the secular Sumarah the West received was *made secular in transit*: Arymurthy
devised the "relaxed meditation" label expressly for foreigners and state agencies, told Western
practitioners "I stress that you are not members of Paguyuban Sumarah," and simultaneously held that
"relaxed meditation was not Sumarah" [S3][S4]; Romano's English systematically softens *Tuhan* to "the
universal self" [S18]. The translation problem was therefore not solved at origin; it was performed at the
boundary, by the same mechanisms (strategic relabelling, metaphysical soft-pedalling) that 6.1 documents
in Western secularisations [A]. Third, Sumarah never confronted the grounding problem
([[3.3-grounding-problem]]): it relocated *authority* — from text and guru to collectively witnessed
experience — which is an epistemological democratisation, not a metaphysical secularisation; the
normativity of surrender is still underwritten by God's will, exactly the foundation a secular dharma must
do without [A]. Fourth, the "philosophy of life" self-description is partly compelled speech within a
regulatory regime that made both "religion" and "atheism" dangerous labels [S1][S9][A]. Fifth — cutting
against the first point rather than for the claim — the *theistic* self-description is compelled in equal
measure: the post-1965 state made God-priority the condition on which a movement could remain "public and
open" [S12], so the boundary-policing that anchors the first point is itself duress-evidence, and the
weight it can bear is limited accordingly (see "The compulsory God" above) [A].

**Calibrated conclusion for Stage 2.** Sumarah is living, non-Western, ninety-year proof that the
*institutional* half of the secular-translation problem is solvable — that a named dharma can transmit
deep contemplative practice and a working ethic without canon, guru, creed-assent, clergy or (apparently)
fee — and that the *epistemological* half is solvable too: authority can rest on collectively verified
experience rather than revelation-as-text, even inside a movement founded on revelation. What it does not
prove, and by its own insistence cannot prove, is that the *theological* core is dispensable: Sumarah
kept God, defended God against the void, and exported a God-softened product it declined to call by its
own name. The honest formulation is not "the problem has been solved" but "the problem has been reduced":
Sumarah shows exactly which parts of a dharma survive the removal of religion's machinery — and its
boundary-policing shows where its practitioners drew the line of the removable, though after 1965 that
line was also the one the state enforced on pain of extinction, so belief and compulsion cannot be fully
separated in it [A]. The fairest closing formulation: Sumarah was never free to answer the question this
note asks of it [A].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The Stange monopoly, again.** Paul Stange is both Sumarah's principal scholar and a declared adherent
  ("spiritually... 'born' through Sumarah"); nearly every KB source here is Stange as author, translator,
  or editor, and even his critics' data routes through him. For *this* note the bias cuts in a specific
  direction: the reading of Sumarah as "simply spiritual," neither Indic nor Islamic [S2], and the
  form/essence defence of its revelation against derivation claims, are Stange's argued positions, not
  neutral findings. Balancing here came from non-Stange Indonesian scholarship — the Walisongo
  interrituality study [S15], the 2025 UIN Sunan Kalijaga fieldwork thesis with its primary AD/ART
  citations [S16] — plus the MK ruling [S17] and insider devotional transcripts [S13][S14] that show a
  more frankly theistic register than Stange's academic English.
- **The corpus's secular lean pulls toward the wrong verdict.** The shared corpus (Batchelor, Sapolsky,
  Boehm, Graeber, Bregman) predisposes a reading of Sumarah as proto-secular Buddhism-adjacent
  practice-first wisdom. Following the project's non-privileging stance (as in [[4.5-subud-and-sumarah]]),
  this note deliberately weights Sumarah's own theistic self-descriptions — the Sesanggeman, the
  sujud/meditasi boundary, the Warana declaration — over the secularised register of its Western-facing
  texts, and treats the latter as data about *translation*, not about origin.
- **Strategic versus sincere is undecidable from insider texts — in both directions.** The "not a
  religion" stance is simultaneously doctrinal (one Truth beneath all religions), legal (avoiding the
  agama template), and prudential (Pakem surveillance, anti-communist certification); and the *theistic*
  stance is layered the same way — devotional (sujud's own definition), conventional (the formulaic
  *Tuhan Yang Maha Esa* etiquette of Indonesian public speech), and prudential, since after 1965 atheism
  was illegal and God-priority was the price of remaining open [S12][S23]. No available source separates devotion from duress cleanly — and none written
  under the New Order could have tried, since the massacres themselves were unspeakable there [S12]. The
  note flags the compulsion on both labels but cannot quantify either; the undecidability is permanent,
  because the terror consumed the counterfactual evidence [A].
- **The question's "no fee" premise is unverified.** No corpus source states that Sumarah charges nothing;
  the inference from its home-based, buildings-less economy is reasonable but the only documented fee in
  the record is the Pakem state licence [S9]. External confirmation (e.g. Howe's parallel ethnography, or
  current Paguyuban statutes) is still wanted.
- **The Sudarno Ong counter-current is known almost entirely through Stange** — who was his student. The
  one documented non-theist inside Sumarah is attested by the one scholar with the strongest motive to
  present Sumarah as doctrinally spacious. Howe (1980), Hadiwijono (1967) and Geertz's ch. 21 remain the
  outstanding retrieval targets for a Stange-independent view (see [[3.5-sumarah]]).
- **Numbers remain soft.** Membership figures used here span ~5,000–10,000 across decades, with a
  government count of ~7,200 (c. 2010) [S19]; none supports strong claims about vitality, and the "living proof"
  framing should not be inflated into "thriving proof" — the movement is small, ageing, and its Western
  network is a handful of weekly groups.
- **Unresolved.** Whether phase-four "continuous meditation" functions for ordinary members as the sources
  describe (insider testimony only); whether the internal minority who hold the Wewarah to be Sukino's own
  construction rather than revelation [S16] represents a nascent *internal* secularisation — potentially
  the most important single follow-up for this question — and what Howe's independent ethnography says
  about doctrine-free transmission in practice.
