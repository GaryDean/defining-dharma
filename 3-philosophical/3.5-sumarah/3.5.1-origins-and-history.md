# 3.5.1 Sumarah: Origins and History

**Question:** How did Sumarah arise and evolve — Sukinohartono's 1935 wahyu, the wartime and revolutionary-period growth, the Paguyuban's organizational history and leadership, its place in national kebatinan politics and state regulation of aliran kepercayaan, and its condition today?

## Synthesis

Sumarah began as the revelatory experience (*wahyu*) of a low-ranking Yogyakarta court clerk,
Sukinohartono, in August–September 1935, was carried by a founding troika (Sukino, Suhardo, Sutadi)
into a pre-war network of a few hundred, grew six-fold through the 1945–49 revolution, and was
formalised as **Paguyuban Sumarah** on 27 March 1950 [S1][S13]. Its subsequent history is a
repeating cycle of charismatic centralisation and collective correction: the Surono era (1950–66)
ended in a congress deposing a leader who claimed to be the sole channel of divine instruction, and
the Arymurthy era (1966–97) carried Sumarah to the centre of national *kebatinan* umbrella politics
— Arymurthy became the first Director of the government's kepercayaan directorate in 1979 — before
Suharto's 1980s re-Islamising turn removed him [S1][S2][S6]. Uniquely among the movements this
project examines, Sumarah reads its own institutional history as a spiritual process (the staged
"diffusion of *hakiki*" from founder to base) that mirrors the nation's political history phase for
phase [S1][S4]. Today the Paguyuban survives as a small, ageing, legally recognised *penghayat
kepercayaan* organisation (roughly 5,000–7,000 members against a 1975 peak of ~10,000), a named
beneficiary of the 2017 Constitutional Court ruling on identity cards, while the *practice* diffuses
informally through a Western workshop network that the organisation deliberately never
institutionalised [S5][S7][S15][S18][S19]. Nearly everything known about this history passes through
one insider-scholar, Paul Stange; the independent sourcing assembled here confirms the skeleton
while contesting the founding chronology and the revelation's authenticity [S13][S16][S17].

## Findings

### The kebatinan substrate

Sumarah emerged from *kejawen* Java, where Islam overlaid but never displaced older strata: Stange's
formula is that Islamic idiom "amplified pre-existent Javanism" [S1:0] — and, ironically, that
Sumarah, outwardly an Indic-flavoured mysticism, functions as "a prime vehicle of Islamization"
because its whole content is surrender to the one God [S1:6]. The movement belongs to the modern *kebatinan*
wave: hundreds of organised mystical associations that crystallised in the early twentieth century
in reaction to modernist Islam, several dozen of them (Pangestu, Subud, Sapta Darma, Sumarah,
Hardopusoro) attaining Java-wide reach [S5][S2]. What later became Sumarah's defining move within
this field was its rejection of the *paguron* (guru-cult) model — no guru, no secrecy, no graded
initiations for sale — a deliberate contrast with the occultist sects of the late colonial era
([[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]]) [S1:126][S8:5].

### Sukinohartono and the wahyu of 1935–37

R. Ng. Sukinohartono ("Pak Kino") was born 27 December 1897 in Semanu, Gunungkidul, and worked from
1919 as a tax clerk (*mantri pamicis*) in the Yogyakarta kraton — the lowest rung of the priyayi
world [S1:27][S1:28]. His formation ran through a decade in **Hardopusoro**, a secretive,
initiation-graded, Theosophy-linked sect whose membership included Sukarno's father and uncle; he
left it burdened by a sense of sin and doubting God's existence [S1:30]. By 1934 he had also
received the "contact" circulating in the Yogyakarta circle around Muhammad Subuh (later founder of
Subud) — the entanglement that fuels a still-live priority dispute (see [[4.5-subud-and-sumarah]])
[S1:38][S16].

The founding sequence, as Sukino related it to a Sumarah congress in the early 1950s, ran from
August 1935 into 1937. In August 1935 he "began to experience clear inner contact with Hakiki" — the
channel of guidance direct from God [S1:42]. On 8 September 1935, while praying for Indonesian
independence, he was approached by the spirit of Senopati, founder of Mataram, offering armies of
spirits; **he refused**, on the conviction that spirits and humans should each relate directly to
God — the anti-occult, anti-ancestor-cult turn that became Sumarah's signature [S1:46]. Indonesian
scholarship independently anchors this date and place: 8 September 1935, Wirobrajan, Yogyakarta, is
the founding datum in the Paguyuban's own AD/ART and in recent fieldwork at the Wirobrajan pendopo
[S13][S18]. There followed a judgement-and-dismemberment purification vision (29 June 1936) and a
journey through nine spiritual realms guided by Hakiki and the angel Gabriel, with visions of
Muhammad and Jesus; his commission was to guide others toward total faith (*iman bulat*), himself
functioning only as a "screen" (*warana*) [S2:24]. A second wahyu — a crown radiating sky-blue
light, 4 July 1937 — sealed the teaching authority that operated until 1949 [S1:49]. Sumarah insists
Sukino was never superhuman: guidance came *through*, not *from*, him — an anti-divinisation clause
written into the founding narrative itself [S1:50].

**The contested origin.** Subud-side sources flatly invert this account: Pak Subuh's 1953 letter
claims Sukino was "opened" within the Subud lineage (by Wignosupartono or Pravirodisastra, c.
1932–34) and that Sumarah's "methods remain those I use in Subud", denying it an independent
revelation [S1:38][S16]. Rogge's kebatinan dossier, drawing on Subud testimony plus the second
doctoral ethnography (Howe), dates first experiences to 1932 [S17][S21]. And inside Sumarah's own
Wirobrajan pendopo, 2024 fieldwork records a minority faction holding that the Wewarah and Sujud
Sumarah are Sukino's *own construction*, not wahyu [S13]. Stange resolves the derivation problem
philosophically — the *forms* are derivative, the *essence* is not [S1:38] — but that is a
believer's resolution, not a historian's.

### Founding circle and pre-war growth, 1937–1945

Suhardo, the first follower, verified the revelation in the mode that became Sumarah's epistemology:
not by trusting Sukino but by hearing his own inner voice confirm "Sukino's way of worshipping God
is correct" [S8:7]. Initiation was stripped of ritual from the outset ("at any time or place,
without any conditions whatsoever") [S1:52], and growth ran through the grapevine (*getok tular*) as
an "experiential chain reaction" [S1:56]: about two dozen members by end-1937, one hundred by 1939,
perhaps five hundred by 1945, spreading from the priyayi network across the Javanese social
spectrum, with a rural mass base around Madiun won through Kyai Abdulhamid [S1:57]. The three founders operated as a
*pinisepuh* troika — Sukino for spiritual guidance, Suhardo for education, Sutadi (a former
Volksraad member and newspaper editor) for organisation [S1:60][S1:65]. Sutadi drafted the
**Sesanggeman**, the nine-point Javanese-language moral charter (theist, prophet-affirming,
anti-fanatical, law-abiding), adopted at the movement's first formal conference at his Solo
residence on 22 April 1940 [S1:68][S11:2]. Colonial authorities watched the meetings; growth under
occupation was in depth rather than numbers [S1:57][S1:75].

### Revolution: fighting units, invulnerability, and the pivot, 1945–1949

Sumarah's history and the nation's run in deliberate parallel: "Like the 'nation', Sumarah surfaced
publicly in 1945 but only became established as a formal organisation in 1950" [S3:7]. Sukino wrote
to Sukarno in December 1944; Sukarno's April 1945 postcard urged Sumarah to organise itself
efficiently [S3:7]. During the revolution membership jumped from ~500 to over 3,000 — the largest
single influx in its history, and the cohort from which its later leadership came [S1:75][S4:13].
Two lasykar formed: the **Barisan Berani Mati** ("corps of those unafraid of death") in Central
Java, part of Bung Tomo's Battalion 13, and the **pemuda Sumarah** under Kyai Abdulhamid, who led
pesantren-trained youth into the fighting at Surabaya crying "Allahu Akbar"; both dissolved in 1948
[S3:8].

The famous invulnerability (*kekebalan*) of these units was reinterpreted from inside: "it has never
been suggested that invulnerability meant capacity to stand with impunity in front of a machine
gun... Invulnerability then, was the product not of personal power, but of complete willingness to
die" [S3:29]. Testimony includes a fighter returning from Surabaya with clothes shredded by bullets
but untouched [S3:28]. When first-chakra battle-focus made pemuda foolhardy and deaths mounted,
Sukino received a *dawuh* in early 1946 shifting practice "from the base of the spine to the navel"
— a mid-war doctrinal correction driven by casualty rates [S1:99]. The period's capstone was **sujud
perjuangan** ("surrender-struggle"), articulated not by the founders but by a young Magelang tailor,
Joyosukarto, and confirmed by Sukino in 1947: the inner and outer revolutions are one, "only
appearing, through the trick called ego, to be separate" [S1:103][S4:17]. Politically the movement
stayed unaligned: a February 1948 Madiun membership list shows members across Masyumi, PNI and left
parties, and the Madiun uprising did not split it [S3:20].

### Formalisation and the Surono era, 1949–1966

The night before the Dutch left Yogyakarta (1 July 1949), Sukino received the **Wahyu Iman Suci**,
the clear white light opening the second phase; the ecstatic *kanoman* practices began to drop away
[S1:117]. On 8 March 1950 Dr. Surono, a physician, received the *dawuh* to organise; on **27 March
1950** the Paguyuban Sumarah with its Pengurus Besar (PB) was established [S1:120]. Insider accounts
frame this as state recognition under Sukarno of "an organization of faith" [S11:2]. Crucially,
Sutadi and Suhardo **refused to join** — Suhardo warned that under Surono Sumarah "would experience
deviation" — and the Solo group remained outside the PB until 1966 [S1:120]. The first congress
(Yogyakarta, 25–26 December 1950) counted 3,262 members, 147 of them pamong; there Surono himself
articulated the anti-fideist charter — even Hakiki "has to be proven", and even Sukino asks the
collective for assent [S1:125][S8:7].

The 1950s brought growth (5,115 members by 1955), formalised consciousness-grading (levels A–E), and
outside attestation: Geertz observed the Pare branch in 1953 (the "most tightly organised" and
"sharply anti-intellectual" of the local sects), and Nina Epton visited Sukino's Yogyakarta circle
in 1955 [S1:134][S1:137][S1:143][S22]. Phase three was announced in 1956 through a peripheral
member, Martosuwignio, not Sukino — read internally as proof that Hakiki reception had devolved
beyond the founder [S1:145]. Then came the deviation Suhardo had predicted. From 1957 Surono
progressively sidelined Sukino, replaced congresses with informal board meetings, and in December
1964 claimed to be the **penyalur dawuh tunggal** — the sole channel of God's instruction [S1:160].
He then entered regular contact, through a medium, with the spirit kingdoms of Nyai Loro Kidul,
believing himself commissioned to convert the spirit realm to Sumarah — precisely the
ancestor-and-spirit entanglement Sukino had refused in 1935 [S1:162]. The membership concluded
Surono had been led astray; the fifth congress (Yogyakarta, 24–25 September 1966, 40 of 42 branches
attending) rejected the *penyalur dawuh tunggal* claim, transferred leadership to Arymurthy's new
Jakarta DPP, reinstated Sukino as *warana*, and re-admitted the Solo group, "healing the split which
had occurred in 1950" [S1:168]. Surono returned as an ordinary member in 1979 [S1:169]. Stange's
periodisation makes the political mirroring explicit: Surono's centralisation tracked Sukarno's
Guided Democracy, and Sumarah's 1966 transition was as sharp as the nation's [S1:227][S4:20].

Sukino died in March 1971 in Wirobrajan [S11:2]; the sixth congress (Jakarta, 27 September 1970) had
already fixed his posthumous status, declaring him **Warana** — glossed in the Paguyuban's own 1980
AD/ART as the Javanese term for prophet — and pioneer of the ilmu Sumarah [S13].

### The Arymurthy era and the politics of kepercayaan, 1966–1998

Under Arymurthy (1966–97) Sumarah de-Javanised: headquarters to Jakarta, business in Indonesian
rather than Javanese, ecstatic and occult residues explicitly disowned — "proclaiming itself as an
Indonesian rather than a particularly Javanese group" [S4:24]. From 1971, through Stange himself and
Suyono Hamongdarsono, a small international following formed, which Arymurthy deliberately refused
to institutionalise: foreigners were guests, not members, since international branches would risk
"spiritual colonizing" [S5:1][S9:34,35].

Sumarah's national weight came through the umbrella politics of *aliran kepercayaan*. The legal
anchor was Wongsonegoro's insertion of the word *kepercayaan* into Article 29 of the 1945
Constitution [S2:33]. The state's counter-apparatus was older: the colonial Guru Ordinance of 1926,
continued after independence as **Pakem** (in the Justice Department from 1954), which licensed even
home meditation meetings [S2:7] — Sumarah pamong had to certify non-involvement in the communist
movement after 1965 [S9:16]. Within the recognised field, religions were legitimate only on the
Islamic template (one God, law, scripture, prophet; Sukarno's 1965 list of six), and mysticism was
pressured to "return to its parent religion" [S2:14][S2:24]. The umbrella bodies ran from
Wongsonegoro's **BKKI** (founded Semarang, 21 August 1955; its 1957 petition asked Sukarno for
status equal to the religions) to the Golkar-sponsored **SKK** (1970), in which Sumarah's leaders
were so prominent that its representation "often seemed too strong" [S2:34][S1:177][S1:186]. The
1973 GBHN explicitly recognised kepercayaan as "an independent but legitimate option within the
terms of the Pancasila", technically legalising mystical affiliation on identity cards [S2:37]. The
high-water mark came in 1978–79: a **Direktorat Pembinaan Penghayat Kepercayaan** in the Ministry of
Education and Culture (legislation 1978, office 1979) with **Arymurthy — then Sumarah's leader — as
its first director**, and the SKK renamed HPK at Tawangmangu 1979 [S2:38]. A Sumarah general, Zahid
Hussein, meanwhile worked in Suharto's own Bina Graha offices [S1:177].

The pendulum then swung back. At the 1980 census the expected kepercayaan column "was eliminated at
the last minute", forcing every citizen into a recognised religion [S2:37]. By the mid-1980s
official doctrine held mystical membership to be "separate from the issue of religion in the same
way that education or sports are" [S2:38]. Arymurthy, who insisted that penghayat affiliation
satisfied the constitutional belief-in-God requirement on its own, was removed from the directorate
"on Suharto's instructions" [S1:219]; within Sumarah he was gradually sidelined too, succeeded as
Ketua Umum by Zahid Hussein, then General Soemarsono, then (from the mid-1990s) **Sukosudarso**,
still chairman when Stange's Indonesian edition went to press [S6:485]. Arymurthy's late
guidance (the "tuntunan paket" sessions at Srondol, 1989–97, with talk of phases V and VI that the
DPP never ratified) shows the charisma-versus-organisation tension persisting to his death [S6:486].

### After the New Order: recognition, shrinkage, diffusion

The membership curve is a slow arc: ~10,000 at the 1975 peak; "perhaps half that number" by the
mid-1990s; ~6,000 in Stange's 2002 encyclopedia snapshot; ~7,200 in the government's 2010
kepercayaan encyclopedia; ~5,000 on the movement's own international site
[S7:26][S10:10][S5:0][S18][S19]. On 7 November 2017 the Constitutional Court (Decision
97/PUU-XIV/2016) struck down the Population Administration Law provisions excluding penghayat
kepercayaan, allowing belief to be recorded on the KTP — Paguyuban Sumarah is explicitly among the
registered beneficiary organisations, and by 2025 hundreds in its Gunungkidul–Sleman heartland had
converted their KTP status, though local journalism records persistent stigma [S15][S20]. On the
ground the Paguyuban is now a network of small, ageing branches (Kudus: 78 members in 2023) whose
weekly *sujud* sessions remain, as 2021 fieldwork documents, genuinely inter-religious — Muslims,
Christians and others practising together while keeping their religions [S20][S14]. Contemporary
insider presentations still frame the core practice exactly as the founders did: sujud not as ritual
but as a "training of the heart", performable in any posture, at any time, facing any direction
[S12:1]. Meanwhile the *practice* travels without the organisation: Laura Romano (a student of
Arymurthy and Pak Suwondo) has run Sumarah workshops in Europe, the UK and Australia since 1995,
with weekly groups in Italy, Germany, Belgium, the UK, Australia and the USA and retreats still
scheduled for 2026 — a diffusion the Paguyuban's guest-not-member doctrine predicted and permitted
but never governed [S19][S6:486].

### A life-cycle reading

Against the template of [[2.5-lifecycle-of-a-dharma]], Sumarah is a rare fully-documented
single-lifetime case, and it does not fit a simple ossify-or-fade curve [A]. Twice the movement met
the classic ossification threat — a leader converting collective charisma into personal authority —
and twice the corrective ran through the movement's own founding doctrine: Surono's *penyalur dawuh
tunggal* claim was rejected by congress because guidance is constitutionally collective, and the
graded consciousness levels of the 1950s were later de-emphasised — practice, the movement
concluded, is not a "spiritual olympics" [S1:168][S6:492]. What Sumarah could not correct was
demography and the state: the New Order first instrumentalised kebatinan (SKK under Golkar,
Arymurthy's directorate) and then squeezed it (census, "education or sport"), and the organisation
has shrunk within a religious field that increasingly demands scriptural definition [S2:37][S2:38].
Yet the practice's deliberate formlessness — no canon, no guru, no required culture — is exactly
what lets it outlive its own institution, seeding an informal Western network while the Javanese
Paguyuban ages [S5:1][S19]. In life-cycle terms: the organisation fades; the dharma, so far, seasons
([[7.3-dharma-and-power]] takes up the power mechanics) [A].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

**One insider is the archive.** Paul Stange is both a Sumarah adherent (initiated 1971, trained by
the pamong Sudarno Ong, self-described "Perintis Barat pertama") and the author of effectively every
substantial source in the shared corpus on Sumarah — thesis, articles, encyclopedia entries,
translated teachings [S1][S5][S6]. His history is built on internal documents (the DPP's own 1974
history, Suhardo's 1973 Sejarah, Suwondo's 1,900-page archive) and 1970s interviews with the winning
(Arymurthy) faction; the Surono chapter in particular is victors' history, as Stange partly concedes
[S1:160]. Even ostensibly independent literature routes through him: Howell's bibliography defers to
his thesis, and the 2025 UIN fieldwork thesis cites his LKiS translation [S13]. The genuinely
Stange-free depth — Hadiwijono's 1967 doctrinal analysis, Geertz's 1953–54 Pare chapter, and above
all David Gordon Howe's parallel 1980 UNC ethnography — remains metadata-only and unretrieved; this
note's external sources confirm the skeleton (founder, date, place, sujud, multireligious
membership, decline) but cannot yet independently re-derive the organisational narrative
[S21][S22][S23].

**The founding is contested three ways.** Sumarah's account (independent wahyu, 8 September 1935)
stands against the Subud lineage claim (Sukino "opened" 1932–34, methods derivative) and against an
internal minority at Wirobrajan that treats the teachings as Sukino's own construction
[S1:38][S16][S13]. Stange's form/essence resolution protects the emic claim rather than
adjudicating. This note reports all three and adjudicates none.

**A sibling-note date discrepancy.** The comparative note [[4.5-subud-and-sumarah]] gives
Sukinohartono's dates as "c. 1900–1970", whereas the thesis biography (corpus-verified at seg 27)
gives 27 December 1897 – 27 March 1971 [S1:27]; this note follows the thesis.

**Numbers are soft everywhere.** Membership figures oscillate within single Stange texts (6,000 vs
8,000 vs 10,000, different reference years), the YouTube transcripts corrupt them absurdly (a
1,115,000 artifact), and no post-2010 national figure exists; every figure above is time-stamped and
should be treated as order-of-magnitude [S1][S7:26][S11:2][S18].

**Corpus lean.** The shared corpus's secular/evolutionary curation makes it comfortable with Sumarah
as an object of anthropology and as proto-secular praxis, but the sources themselves are saturated
with God, Gabriel, wahyu and prophecy; the revolution-era material (visions of Japanese defeat,
protective spheres, rajah for victory) is retrospective devotional testimony collected 25+ years
after the events [S1][S3]. The corrective applied here was to draw the contemporary picture from
Indonesian scholarship, court records and local journalism rather than from Stange or the movement's
own site alone [S13][S14][S15][S20]. Honouring the project's non-privileging stance, this note
treats Sumarah as it describes itself — theistic surrender within a brotherhood that insists it is
not a religion — and leaves the secular-translation question to [[3.5.4-secular-dharma-question]].
