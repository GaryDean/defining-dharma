# 3.5 Sumarah as a Philosophy of Life (umbrella)

**Question:** What is Sumarah — the Javanese practice of total surrender — as a philosophy of life, and why does it matter to this project's search for secular dharma? (Umbrella note framing 3.5.1–3.5.4.)

## Synthesis

**Sumarah** is a Javanese *kebatinan* movement born of the revelatory experience (*wahyu*) of the
Yogyakarta court clerk Sukinohartono in 1935, whose name simply *is* its practice: the Javanese word
for total surrender [S1][S14]. Its sole discipline, **sujud**, aims to "surrender every aspect of
personal being so the self functions as a vehicle for God's will," transmitted with almost none of
religion's apparatus — no canon, no sacred texts or sites, no guru, no creed, no fee — through weekly
group sessions in which a guide (*pamong*) speaks spontaneously from attunement and the group's own
*rasa* (intuitive felt-knowing) verifies what is said [S1][S2][S5]. It matters to this project for a
reason the project's own front matter states and then drops: the book's preface names Sumarah the
author's "first recognisable 'secular dharma'", met two decades before any Bodhi College retreat, yet
the essays never return to it — this cluster (3.5.1–3.5.4) is the evidence base for repairing that
omission [A] (see `0-preface.md`). The keystone tension the cluster must hold, and this note states
without resolving, is that Sumarah's self-description — "a philosophy of life and a form of
meditation," emphatically "not a religion" [S3][S2] — coexists with a core as theistic as any creed:
the founding charter opens by affirming God and the prophets, the origin is internally ranked as
*wahyu* "equal to that of all prophets," and the leadership itself policed the boundary, ruling that
meditation without God leads "to void and not God" and that "relaxed meditation was not Sumarah"
[S8][S7][S6]. Nearly everything known about Sumarah passes through one insider-scholar, Paul Stange —
an adherent who connected with the practice in 1971 [S2] as well as the movement's principal historian — and the external sourcing
assembled across this cluster confirms his skeleton while contesting the founding chronology, the
revelation's authenticity, and every membership figure [S14][S15][S17].

## Findings

### Why Sumarah is in this project

The preface of the Stage-2 book locates the oldest root of the whole inquiry in Java: the author's
Asian Studies lecturer at Murdoch, Paul Stange, introduced him to Sumarah — "a quiet Javanese
movement that calls itself not a religion but a philosophy of life, a practice of surrender with no
fixed guru, no creed, and no demand that you believe anything at all" — and the author later built
the international movement's English-language website (sumarah.net) in 1998 [A] (see
`0-preface.md`). That is a strong claim to hang on a movement the essays then never revisit, and it
carries a double insider chain this project must own: the corpus's Sumarah shelf is very largely
Stange's self-curated body of work, and the project author is Stange's former student and the
movement's sometime webmaster [A]. The purpose of the 3.5 cluster is therefore twofold: to give any
future essay on Sumarah a properly sourced foundation, and to test — rather than assume — the
preface's identification of Sumarah as a secular dharma. The test is run at
[[3.5.4-secular-dharma-question]]; this note only frames it.

### The portrait, in one paragraph

Sumarah ("total surrender") crystallised from Sukinohartono's revelations of 1935–37 in Yogyakarta —
dated by the Paguyuban's own statutes to 8 September 1935 in Wirobrajan — grew through the Japanese
occupation and the 1945–49 revolution, and was formalised as **Paguyuban Sumarah** around 1950, led
by Dr. Surono in Yogyakarta (1950–66) and then Arymurthy in Jakarta (1966–97) [S1][S14]. Its practice
is *sujud*: a guided, mostly silent group meditation of progressive relaxation — body, feeling, then
mind — in which attention settles out of thought into *rasa* and the practitioner surrenders to what
the movement understands as God's will [S1][S2]. There is no clergy and no hierarchy of realisation:
the only teacher is the "true teacher" (*guru sejati*) within; the pamong is a momentary function
whose status "cannot be faked"; and even revelation is subject to collective verification — "Even if
it is Hakiki it also has to be proven... Even Sukino asks us for our agreement," as Surono told the
1950 congress, adding "We differ from religions, within which people accept God on faith and without
knowledge" [S1][S5]. Membership — mostly Muslim, with Buddhists and Christians practising alongside,
an "interrituality" documented by independent Indonesian scholarship [S13] — peaked near 10,000 in
the mid-1970s and has roughly halved since [S9]; the movement holds that "Sumarah, both as an
organisation and as a practice of *kebatinan*, is only a means, not an end," conceding that
non-members are often more *sumarah* than members [S9]. Since 1971 a few hundred Westerners have
practised without any international organisation ever being created [S1], and a small workshop
network (Laura Romano's, since 1995) carries the practice in Europe and Australia today [S16].

### The keystone tension: philosophy of life, or surrender to God?

Both halves of Sumarah's identity are real, and this cluster refuses to sand either one down.

**The philosophy-of-life half.** The movement's principal Western teacher opens her introduction
with exactly the phrase: Sumarah is "a philosophy of life and a form of meditation"; it "does not
offer solutions, does not promise salvation, does not guarantee success" [S3]. The middle term of
its path, *iman* (faith), is explicitly non-dogmatic — "not a dogmatic one, but... rather based on
the proof that reality will give us" [S4]. It has "no canon of teachings, no sacred texts, and no
sacred sites or buildings"; "There are no 'guru'" [S1]; in the pamong Pak Wondo's words, "In Sumarah
we have no sacred book. Our sacred book is Life" [S3]. No faith in any external teaching or teacher
is required; the only verification is "the individual's direct recognition of its truth" in lived
awareness [S5]. Structurally, this is the most complete removal of religion's institutional
apparatus this project has found in a living movement [A].

**The theistic half.** The practice those absences serve is surrender *to God*. Sujud's stated aim
is that "the self functions as a vehicle for God's will" [S1][S2]. The Sesanggeman — the founding
vows of 1940 — open with the members' affirmation that they "believe in the existence of God and
acknowledge the prophets" (*"yakin terhadap keberadaan Tuhan dan mengakui para nabi"*), grounded in
one Truth beneath all religions [S8]. Internally, Sumarah — like Subud, Sapta Darma and Pangestu —
claims "the validation of wahyu equal to that of all prophets," deferring externally to the milder
*ilham* only out of tact toward Islamic sensibility [S7]. And when Solonese practitioners drifted
toward a religiously neutral "relaxed meditation" for foreigners, the leadership drew the line from
inside: meditation without God "led to void and not God"; in Arymurthy's ruling, "relaxed meditation
was not Sumarah" [S6]. Stange's own summary formula holds the paradox rather than resolving it:
"Sumarah is neither Indic nor Islamic, but simply spiritual," its membership happening to include
Muslims, Christians, Buddhists "and even (mainly among foreign followers) agnostics" [S6] — yet the
same corpus shows him arguing that Sumarah, outwardly an Indic-flavoured mysticism, functions as "a
prime vehicle of Islamization" precisely because its whole content is surrender to the one God [S6].

Two cautions stop this tension being read too quickly. First, the famous "not a religion" marks an
*Indonesian* boundary — *agama* versus *kepercayaan*, drawn against the state's Semitic template of
scripture, law and prophet, and drawn partly under regulatory duress (by the mid-1980s the state
line was that mystical membership is "separate from the issue of religion in the same way that
education or sports are") [S6][S7]. It is not the religion/secular boundary the English phrase
suggests. Second, the softening is partly an export artefact: the Western hub renders surrender to
God as surrender "to the universal self," and the "relaxed meditation" label was itself a formula
negotiated for state officials and foreigners [S16][S2]. Adjudicating what remains for a *secular*
dharma once both cautions are priced in is the work of [[3.5.4-secular-dharma-question]], not of
this note [A].

### The four children

**[[3.5.1-origins-and-history]]** — *How did Sumarah arise and evolve?* Headline: from
Sukinohartono's 1935–37 wahyu through a founding troika, six-fold revolutionary growth, and the
Paguyuban's 1950 formalisation, Sumarah's institutional history runs as a repeating cycle of
charismatic centralisation and collective correction (Surono deposed in 1966 for claiming to be the
sole channel of divine instruction; Arymurthy carried the movement into national *kebatinan*
umbrella politics, becoming the state kepercayaan directorate's first Director in 1979 before
Suharto's re-Islamising turn removed him) [S6][S7]. Uniquely among this project's cases, Sumarah
reads its own organisational history as a spiritual process — the staged "diffusion of *hakiki*" —
mirroring the nation's political history phase for phase [S12]. Today it survives as a small,
ageing [S21], legally recognised [S20] *penghayat kepercayaan* organisation.

**[[3.5.2-practice-and-transmission]]** — *What is the practice, and how does it travel?* Headline:
sujud is deliberately empty of apparatus — no technique, canon, clergy or fee — with authority
relocated to the *guru sejati* within and the pamong defined as a momentary "clean mirror" checked
by collective witnessing [S1][S5]. Transmission is purely experiential contact, which made the
practice deep but structurally non-scalable: where Subud's uniform latihan globalised, Sumarah
stayed a Javanese association of a few thousand, its westward movement running through exactly two
informal waves (Stange's Solo circle from 1971; Romano's workshops from 1995) — and in transit the
theistic surrender was softened toward "philosophy of life" for spiritual-but-not-religious
audiences [S16]. The note's critical addition: the anti-technique is itself a discipline that
installs durable dispositions while denying any form is being transmitted.

**[[3.5.3-rasa-epistemology]]** — *What theory of knowledge does a rasa-grounded path imply?*
Headline: Javanese mystical psychology treats rasa not as emotion but as a **cognitive organ** [S5] with
"the same status as 'mind'" [S10], and Sumarah operationalises it — knowledge-claims are validated by
direct recognition cross-checked by collective felt consensus, not by faith, text or teacher. On
this base Stange builds his praxis-over-text argument and the charge that deconstructionist Java
scholarship's "methodological atheism" amounts to a new orientalism [S11]. The note takes the critique
seriously and then names the structural limits of an insider epistemology: unfalsifiable-from-outside
verification, and a seemingly naturalistic "organ of knowing" that is, in Sumarah's own terms,
theistically underwritten.

**[[3.5.4-secular-dharma-question]]** — *Is Sumarah a secular dharma?* Headline verdict: tested
against [[1.3-boundary-definition]], Sumarah is unambiguously a dharma — and the project's purest
case of a dharma stripped of religion's *institutional* apparatus — but it is not a *secular* one:
theism is not a residue to be apologised for, it is the practice's entire object. The strong claim
that Sumarah proves the secular-translation problem ([[6.1-secular-translation]]) already solved
fails as stated, but leaves a precise residue: the institutional and epistemological halves of the
problem are demonstrably solvable (ninety years of rigorous transformative practice without text,
teacher-authority, fee or doctrinal assent); the theological half was never solved at origin — where
a secular Sumarah exists, the translation happened in transit [A].

**Relation to [[4.5-subud-and-sumarah]].** The cultural note remains the comparative treatment —
Subud's history, the latihan, the shared 1930s Yogyakarta circle and the priority dispute (the
Subud-side claim that Sukinohartono was "opened" within Subud's milieu before his own wahyu
[S15]), and the *aliran kepercayaan* regulatory frame. This cluster deliberately does not repeat
that ground: 3.5.1 deepens the history 4.5 sketches, 3.5.2 the practice, and 3.5.4 revisits 4.5's
"non-secular boundary case" verdict with a finer instrument [A].

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

**The Stange monoculture, compounded.** The corpus is unusually *rich* on Sumarah — some twenty
digested documents — but nearly all of it is one man's work: Paul Stange is simultaneously Sumarah's
principal scholar and a practising adherent who connected with the practice in 1971 [S2], and his corpus reaches the KB self-curated
(via freotopia.org). The usual corpus lean (secular / evolutionary / Western-academic, curated
around Boehm, Sapolsky, Graeber, Batchelor, Bregman) is here overlaid by a sharper, more personal
one: the project author is Stange's former student, credits him in the preface, and built the
movement's international website [A] (see `0-preface.md`). Every note in this cluster therefore
names the dependence and works to break it. What external sourcing achieved: independent Indonesian
scholarship confirms the founding skeleton from the Paguyuban's own 1980 statutes and documents the
multireligious sujud [S13][S14]; the Subud-side tradition *contests* the founding chronology,
claiming Sumarah derivative of a Subud "opening" [S15]; a minority faction inside Sumarah's own
pendopo holds the teachings to be Sukino's construction rather than revelation [S14]; and three
genuinely Stange-free scholarly witnesses exist but remain metadata-only, flagged for retrieval —
Howe's parallel 1980 ethnography, Hadiwijono's 1967 doctrinal analysis, and Geertz's 1953–54
attestation [S17][S18][S19].

**The secular pull, named.** The corpus lean — and the project's own motive, given the preface —
pulls toward reading Sumarah as secular ethics with vestigial God-language. The sources will not
support that: theism saturates every layer of the movement's own testimony, and the movement itself
policed attempts to de-theise the practice [S6][S8]. Honouring the project's non-privileging stance,
this cluster treats Sumarah as it describes itself — theistic surrender within a philosophy-of-life
container — and locates the secular question in the *translation*, not the original [A].

**Numbers and dates are soft.** Membership figures range by source and era (~500 in 1945; ~10,000
peak c. 1975; ~6,000 in Stange's 2002 entry; ~7,200 in the government's c. 2010 count; ~5,000 on the
movement's own site) and should always be time-stamped [S1][S9]; the founding is 1935 by the
Paguyuban's statutes but 1932–34 in Subud-side accounts [S14][S15]; and Stange's major texts were
revised across decades (the "1980" thesis text reaches the 1980s; the 2009 Indonesian edition is
declared by him the true original), so quotations must be dated to the correct layer — a caution
carried in every child note [A].
