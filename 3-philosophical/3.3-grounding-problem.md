# 3.3 The Grounding Problem

**Question:** Can a secular dharma be normatively binding without metaphysical foundations?
How do dharmic frameworks confront the is/ought gap, and what grounds their authority once
divine command is removed?

## Synthesis

Yes — but only in the same qualified sense that *any* ethic can be, because the question
smuggles in a false premise: that divine command once *did* ground morality, so its removal
opens a deficit. The **Euthyphro dilemma** shows it never did — either acts are good merely
because commanded (morality is then arbitrary) or they are commanded because already good
(the standard is independent of God) [S5] — and the **Münchhausen trilemma** shows that *all*
ultimate justification terminates in regress, circularity, or a dogmatic stopping point [S7].
So no framework, theistic or secular, has the bedrock the question imagines. The **is/ought
gap** (Hume) is likewise real and bites everyone, including the science-of-morality program:
evolution explains why we *do* value (cooperation), not why we *should* — there is "no single
goal for moral behavior as a natural phenomenon" [S4]. What secular dharmas actually do is
ground bindingness in several **non-ultimate but sufficient** ways: a fallibly-held posit that
the highest good is to reduce suffering and promote flourishing [S1][A]; neo-Aristotelian
flourishing for a being of our kind (Foot) [S6]; normativity from the practical standpoint of
agency (Korsgaard) [S7]; coherentist reflective equilibrium [S8]; and the brute fact that we
are constituted to *care* (moral sentiments). The resulting bindingness is **internalised,
practical, and conditional** rather than categorical — and Euthyphro suggests the "categorical,
divine" kind was always an illusion.

## Findings

### Reframing: divine command never grounded morality (Euthyphro)
The advocate of Divine Command Theory faces a dilemma. *First horn*: if God forbids an action
*because* it is wrong, there is a standard of right and wrong independent of God's will — so God
is not the source. *Second horn*: if an action is wrong *merely because* God forbids it,
morality is arbitrary — God could command cruelty and it would become "good" [S5]. The popular
escape — grounding goodness in God's intrinsic loving nature — only "kicks the can": one must
still ask why *that* nature is good [S5]. The upshot: theism faces the grounding regress too, so
removing divine command does not create a *uniquely secular* problem.

### The is/ought gap is real — and bites the science of morality
Hume's gap (no "ought" validly follows from "is" alone) [S9] and Moore's "naturalistic fallacy" [S10] are
genuine constraints, at least on their standard readings (whether Hume intended a strict logical barrier is itself contested). Crucially, they constrain the *naturalist* programme as much as any other.
Mark Sloan's contribution to Sam Harris's *Moral Landscape* challenge makes the point from
inside evolutionary science: it identifies morality's universal *function* — increasing the
benefits of cooperation — but shows "there is no single goal for moral behavior as a natural
phenomenon," so it is "impossible that all questions of morality… must have right and wrong
answers that fall within the purview of science" [S4]. Evolution and cooperation supply a
powerful *description* (an "is"); the binding *ought* is a further, separate commitment.

### No system has ultimate foundations (the Münchhausen trilemma)
Any attempt at *ultimate* justification ends in one of three unsatisfactory outcomes: infinite
regress, circularity, or a dogmatic foundational stop [S7]. This is universal — theist, secular,
and scientific alike. So the demand that a secular dharma supply a metaphysical bedrock that
divine command supposedly provided sets a standard *nothing* meets. The honest question is not
"is it ultimately grounded?" but "is it grounded *well enough* to live by, and how?"

### How secular dharmas actually ground bindingness (five converging routes)
1. **The suffering/flourishing posit, held fallibly.** The characteristic dharmic move is to take
   *dukkha* (suffering) as a brute given and posit its reduction as the highest good: "The highest
   good of human life is to reduce suffering and promote human flourishing for all" [S1]; "a
   morality of harm" centres preventing/minimising harm [A]. This is a posit, but a *non-arbitrary*
   one — it answers to something we already cannot not care about — and it is offered **fallibly**,
   as a "small-t" proposition tested in living, with explicit awareness of the proposer's partial
   standpoint [S1][S2].
2. **Neo-Aristotelian naturalism (Foot).** The good is what conduces to *flourishing for a being of
   our kind*; moral defect is a "natural defect," and practical rationality is species-relative —
   "our reasoning cannot ignore what we need as human beings" [S6]. This grounds ethics in human
   nature and function with no God and no spooky metaphysics — a natural home for "dharma" read as
   *that which sustains a well-lived human life* (cf. [[1.1-etymology]]).
3. **Constructivism (Korsgaard).** Normativity "springs from our own autonomy": we cannot occupy
   the practical standpoint — cannot act at all — without valuing, so obligation derives from the
   inescapable structure of agency rather than from an external fact [S7].
4. **Coherentism / reflective equilibrium (Rawls).** Justification need not rest on an axiom; it can
   come from *mutual support* among considered judgments, principles, and background theories,
   revised toward coherence [S8]. This fits secular dharma's fallibilism exactly — authority as
   earned coherence, not decree.
5. **Moral sentiments (Hume, Smith, the evolved substrate).** We are *bound* because we are built to
   care — empathy and the moral emotions make others' suffering matter to us non-optionally (the
   cooperative substrate of [[1.2-universal-patterns]]; Stoelhorst's "moral sentiments" in
   [[3.1-tension-resolution]]). The binding is psychological-affective, not merely logical.

### What kind of bindingness results — and the dharmic specificity
The bindingness secured is **internalised, practical, and conditional** — closer to a *hypothetical*
imperative ("if you would reduce suffering / flourish / live well with others, then…") than to a
categorical, coercive "moral law" (cf. the etiquette/morality discussion in
[[1.3-boundary-definition]]). A dharma adds something distinctive: it grounds authority less in
propositional argument than in **demonstrated practice** — its claim is *ehipassiko*, "come and
see," tested experientially (does living this way actually reduce suffering and conduce to
flourishing?) [S2]. This relocates the ground from metaphysics to lived consequence — robust for
practice, though it still depends on the posited criterion (flourishing/suffering) doing the work.

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The strongest objection: no law without a lawgiver.** The hardest challenge (Anscombe's
  "Modern Moral Philosophy," and divine-command theorists) is that the very concept of a binding
  moral *"ought"/"obligation"* is a *law* notion that becomes incoherent once the divine lawgiver
  is removed — leaving only preferences dressed as duties [S5]. The secular-dharma reply is
  two-fold: Euthyphro shows the lawgiver never coherently grounded the law anyway; and for beings
  like us, affective + practical + social bindingness is *enough* to live by, and is more honest for
  being revisable. Whether "enough" counts as "binding" is itself a value judgment — the objection
  does not fully dissolve.
- **Whose flourishing? Which suffering?** The suffering/flourishing posit re-imports the
  individual-vs-collective problem of [[3.1-tension-resolution]] (Mindful Solidarity itself expands
  the criterion to include harm from "oppressive social systems" [S1]); and "flourishing" can
  smuggle in substantive values — the very slide Sloan flags against Harris [S4].
- **Relativism worry.** If grounding is posited-and-coherentist, are all dharmas equally valid? That
  is the next note's problem — [[3.4-relativism-universalism]].
- **Corpus bias.** The corpus is firmly committed to a naturalist, fallibilist, suffering-reduction
  grounding (Batchelor, *Mindful Solidarity*, the prosocial science) and largely treats it as
  adequate; it under-engages the serious metaethical objection (Anscombe) and can drift toward
  assuming the evolved cooperative "is" supports its ethical "ought" — a drift its own Mark Sloan
  source actually checks [S4]. This note imports the strongest opposing view to balance.
- **Cited via summaries.** Euthyphro/Divine Command Theory, Foot, Korsgaard, the Münchhausen
  trilemma, and reflective equilibrium are cited via encyclopedic/secondary sources, not primary
  texts; Hume's is/ought and Moore's naturalistic fallacy are now anchored to primary texts (the
  *Treatise* and *Principia Ethica*) alongside SEP.
