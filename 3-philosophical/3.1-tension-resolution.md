# 3.1 Tension Resolution

**Question:** How do dharmic systems navigate the fundamental tension between individual
autonomy and collective welfare? Provide specific examples of how different traditions
resolve this.

## Synthesis

The "fundamental tension" is real but partly **presupposed by a particular metaphysics of
the self** — the bounded, autonomous individual whose interests are prior to, and potentially
opposed to, the collective's. Once that is seen, the striking fact is that most dharmic
traditions resolve the tension less by adjudicating a fixed contest than by **revising what a
self is**. Where the self is taken as prior and atomic (modern liberalism — Rawls's
"unencumbered self"), the tension is sharp and is managed by *adjudication*: rights, limits,
contract [S8]. Where the self is taken as **constituted by its relations** (Confucian *ren*,
Ubuntu's "I am because we are," Buddhist *anattā*/interdependence, Sandel's "situated self"),
the tension largely **dissolves** — my flourishing is not separable from the community's
[S7][S8]. Between these sit two further strategies: **integration via telos** — the individual
flourishes *through* a role (the Gita's *svadharma* performed as *niṣkāma karma*; Stoic
*oikeiōsis*) [S6][S8] — and **permanent management** — the evolutionary/institutional view that
the tension is ineliminable (within- vs between-group selection) and is *tilted*, not abolished,
by good institutions (Ostrom's design principles) [S3][S4]. The corpus's own "mydharma /
ourdharma" framing is a both/and: individual dharmas contribute to and shape collective ones
[S1]. The honest verdict: **each resolution has a signature failure mode**, so a durable dharma
needs both a relational account of the self *and* a protected standpoint for individual
conscience.

## Findings

### The root of the tension
Evolutionarily, the tension is not a philosopher's puzzle but a structural fact: under
**multilevel selection**, traits that raise an individual's fitness *within* a group (free-riding,
resource-grabbing) tend to lower the group's fitness, and vice versa; whether prosociality
evolves depends on whether between-group or within-group selection dominates [S3]. So
self-interest and collective welfare really can pull apart — the dharmas are devices for managing
a genuine, permanent conflict, not an illusory one ([[1.2-universal-patterns]]).

### The decisive move: revising the metaphysics of the self
Whether the tension is sharp or soft depends on the assumed self:
- **Prior/atomic self → sharp tension.** Liberalism imagines "a self that exists prior to its
  ends and freely chooses them"; Rawls's "unencumbered self" is independent of social and communal
  ties [S8]. Here autonomy and welfare are distinct goods to be *balanced*.
- **Constituted/relational self → dissolved tension.** Communitarians (Sandel, MacIntyre, Taylor)
  reply that the self is "not prior to its ends, but constituted by" them [S8] — a view the dharmic
  traditions hold strongly. If the person *is* a node in relationships, then "my good vs the group's
  good" is a false binary.

### A typology of resolution strategies (with examples)

**1. Adjudicate (liberal/rights).** Treat self and collective as distinct interests and balance
them through rights, limits, and contract. Its evolutionary blind spot is the *Homo economicus*
error — designing institutions "for Homo economicus rather than Homo sapiens," forgetting that
self-interest is "bounded by moral sentiments" (Adam Smith) [S5].

**2. Dissolve the dichotomy (relational self).**
- *Ubuntu* — "I am a person through other persons": personhood is *emergent* from webs of
  relationship and mutual care, so human dignity is the capacity to relate communally; this can
  resolve dilemmas "where autonomy conflicts with beneficence" [S7] (developed in [[4.2-ubuntu]]).
- *Confucianism* — *ren* (仁) intertwines personal growth with communal harmony; the self is a
  "social self-consciousness of roles and relations," full personhood achieved *through*
  relationships (parent/child, etc.) ordered by *li* [S7].
- *Buddhism* — *anattā* (no fixed self) and interdependence dissolve the very boundary that makes
  autonomy-vs-collective look like a contest [S7].

**3. Integrate via telos (flourish through role).**
- *Bhagavad Gita* — Arjuna's dilemma pits *svadharma* (his warrior duty) against *sādhāraṇa
  dharma* (universal non-harm). Krishna's resolution is *niṣkāma karma*: perform your role-duty
  without attachment to its fruits — on the premise that there is "no antithesis between the
  individual and society… inseparable and only distinguishable in thought" [S6].
- *Stoicism* — *oikeiōsis* and Hierocles' "circles of concern": we begin caring for ourselves and
  progressively *draw the outer circles inward*, treating strangers as kin, until self-interest and
  others' interest converge through reason [S8].

**4. Manage the permanent tension institutionally.** Accept the tension as ineliminable and build
structures that tilt the balance toward cooperation. Ostrom's **core design principles** —
clear membership, monitoring, proportionality of costs and benefits, graduated sanctions, and
conflict-resolution mechanisms — are precisely "a set of conditions that favor pro-social traits"
and map onto multilevel-selection theory [S3]. At the limit, a **major evolutionary transition**
occurs when between-group selection so dominates that lower-level units cooperate as a new
higher-level individual (cells → organism; persons → society) — the tension "resolved" by
reorganisation to a higher level [S4].

### Each strategy's failure mode (the honest core)
- **Adjudication → atomism/anomie**: an under-weighted collective fragments into isolated
  self-maximisers (the *Homo economicus* society) [S5].
- **Dissolution → collective tyranny**: if the self is wholly constituted by the group, there is no
  protected standpoint from which to dissent. This is "**dharmic tribalism**" — in-group belonging
  curdling into out-group hostility and insularity [S2] — and it shows in the very examples: the
  Gita's *svadharma* resolution licenses Arjuna to kill his kin *because* caste-duty demands it
  (see [[7.3-dharma-and-power]]); Confucian role-hierarchy can subordinate the inferior party;
  Ubuntu can pressure conformity.
- **Telos-integration → imposed roles**: "flourish through your role" is liberating only if the
  role is not dictated by an oppressive order (caste, gender, class).
- **Institutional management → exclusion**: Ostrom's "clear membership" boundary that enables
  in-group trust is the same boundary that defines an out-group.

The upshot: because dissolution-type dharmas can erase the dissenting individual, a durable dharma
also needs the Axial achievement of a *protected critical standpoint* — the "intellectual elite
able to criticise power from outside" ([[2.1-axial-age]]) and the reform cycle ([[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]]) — so that "collective welfare" cannot silently become "the powerful's welfare."

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **The corpus leans to a harmonious "both/and."** Its mydharma/ourdharma framing presents
  individual and collective as mutually shaping and complementary [S1], and the secular-dharma stance
  favours the relational/dissolution pole. This under-weights **tragic** cases where the conflict is
  real and unresolvable without loss — Arjuna genuinely must choose; the whistle-blower genuinely is
  crushed. This note restores those.
- **Light on the liberal-rights resolution.** Individual autonomy is not only a metaphysical error to
  be dissolved; it is also a hard-won *protection* against collective coercion. The corpus says little
  about rights as a dharmic achievement; the note flags it (and defers the deeper normative question
  to [[3.4-relativism-universalism]]).
- **"No antithesis between individual and society" can be ideology.** The Gita's seamless reconciliation
  is also a justification for a hereditary caste order; harmony-talk can mask domination — the recurring
  warning of [[7.3-dharma-and-power]].
- **Evolutionary realism vs aspiration.** Multilevel selection says the tension is permanent [S3][S4];
  no dharma abolishes it. Framings that promise full reconciliation overclaim.
- **Cited via summaries.** The Gita, Confucian *ren*/*li*, Ubuntu, the liberal–communitarian debate, and
  Stoic *oikeiōsis* are cited via encyclopedic/secondary sources, not primary texts.
