# 3.4 Relativism vs. Universalism

**Question:** If dharmas are genuinely plural, are some better than others — and by what
standard? How do dharmic frameworks avoid collapsing into relativism while resisting a single
imposed universalism?

## Synthesis

Plurality does **not** entail relativism. The corpus's "no single *The* Dharma" is *pluralism
with standards*, not "anything goes": it already judges dharmas as adaptive or maladaptive and
tests them by whether they reduce suffering and enable wise cooperation [A]. The middle path
between "anything goes" relativism and "one dharma to rule them all" monism is a **thin universal
floor + thick plural variation**: a few near-universal constraints (limits on intra-group
violence, the wrongness of recreational cruelty, basic capabilities/rights) above which genuine,
valuable diversity flourishes [S2][S3]. Isaiah Berlin gives this its rigorous form — *value
pluralism* is explicitly "an alternative to **both** moral relativism **and** moral absolutism":
plural values are *objective* (grounded in a shared human nature) yet incommensurable [S5]. The
Nussbaum–Sen **capabilities approach** supplies the floor with teeth: a universal threshold of
human capabilities, plurally realised across cultures [S6]. So **yes, some dharmas are better** —
those that meet the floor, enable flourishing under their actual conditions, stay self-correcting,
and respect other dharmas' autonomy. The standard is neither arbitrary (it is anchored in human
nature, suffering, and cooperation — [[3.3-grounding-problem]]) nor a single imposed thick
universal (it underdetermines the plural forms). The hard, unfinished part is *where exactly the
floor sits* [S6][S7].

## Findings

### Pluralism is not relativism (the core distinction)
The Stanford taxonomy separates three things often blurred as "relativism": **descriptive**
(cultures do in fact disagree — empirically true but trivial), **metaethical** (no
culture-independent moral truth; disagreements can't be rationally resolved), and **normative**
(therefore we ought not judge other cultures) [S7]. The dharmic-pluralist stance is *none* of
these. It is **value pluralism**: Berlin insists this is "in no way a gratuitous concession to
relativism," because the multiple values are "objective, part of the essence of humanity rather
than arbitrary creations," founded on "an underlying, shared human nature"; they are merely many,
often incompatible, and incommensurable — not unreal [S5]. The corpus matches this: dharmas
"evolve, hybridize, and sometimes go extinct," subject to cultural selection in which some
increase group viability and others are maladaptive [A] — i.e. they can be *appraised*.

### Descriptive vs normative universality (defusing the argument from disagreement)
The relativist's strongest move is the argument from disagreement: cultures differ, so there is
no universal. But this conflates the descriptive with the normative. The real question "is not
whether everyone *accepts* X, but whether X is *binding* on us all." On that reading, "recreational
cruelty is wrong" is "an incontestable" moral universal: the dissenter "is obligated to avoid
recreational cruelty whether he knows it or not" [S2]. Disagreement about a norm does not refute
its bindingness — so descriptive plurality leaves normative universality intact. (Compare the two
senses of "moral universal": a universally *binding* ought vs an empirically *common* cooperation
strategy — only the first is what universalism asserts [S4].) The relativist's argument tacitly
treats *culture* as the natural unit of moral variation; but the descriptive picture is messier than
that. Moral intuitions vary systematically *within* a single population by age and sex (Bretl &
Goering, on American early adolescents) [S10], and even where norm *content* converges, the
*enforcement* machinery varies culturally — who is licensed to punish, and whether outgroup
wrongdoing is punished harder than the same act by insiders (Espín et al., on Gitano and non-Gitano
Spaniards) [S11]. ▲ Both are descriptive psychology and sit strictly on the *is* side of the wall:
they sharpen the descriptive premise (variation is not neatly culture-bounded) but license *no*
conclusion that any intuition or enforcement style is better — that would be the naturalistic-fallacy
slide the note guards against below [A].

### The structure: a thin floor, thick variation
Blackford states the architecture precisely: moral systems "vary considerably," yet some virtues
(courage, honesty) are valued in any society, and "no human society can tolerate unlimited
ruthlessness… each society insists on limits to intra-group violence." A full account includes
"both the possibilities for variation in moral systems and the boundaries within which variants
proliferate" [S3]. This is exactly the Tinbergen frame of [[1.2-universal-patterns]] redeployed for
*evaluation*: a universal **function** (securing cooperation / reducing suffering) sets the floor;
plural **mechanisms** (the many dharmas) populate the space above it. Curry's seven cross-cultural
moral rules and the cooperative substrate give the floor empirical content ([[1.2-universal-patterns]]).

### A primary-text exhibit: thin floor and thick particularism inside one tradition
The note argues this architecture abstractly, through Berlin and Nussbaum; the Bhagavad Gita
supplies the non-Western, non-secular *primary* exhibit it otherwise lacks. A single text holds
both poles at once. Its **thin universal** is soteriological and metaphysical: the wise regard all
beings equally, "a learned and cultured Brahman, a cow, an elephant, or a dog and an outcaste"
alike (5.18); the divine regards "all beings with equality" (9.29); and liberation is *universally
accessible* — "a sinful person, a woman, a businessman, and even an untouchable person" attain the
supreme state just as the virtuous Brahman (9.32). Yet the *same* text prescribes a **thick,
birth-indexed particularism** of duty: the fourfold caste is divinely authored "according to the
differentiation of *guṇa* and *karma*" (4.13), each *varṇa*'s work "determined according to the
three modes of nature" (18.41–48), and one's own caste-duty, however imperfect, is better than
another's (3.35, 18.47) [S9]. This is neither relativism (there *is* a universal — equal ultimate
worth) nor monism (duty is plural and station-indexed), but it is also not the same architecture as
Berlin's incommensurable-values or Nussbaum's capabilities: the floor here is a floor of ultimate
regard and access, not of this-worldly entitlement, and it coexists with a social order the
capabilities approach would judge a *floor-breach*. ▲ Cited for what the text argues, by
chapter.verse only; the anonymous corpus translation (register `verified: unverified`) supports no
wording-level claim, and per the non-privileging stance the Gita enters as one tradition's
self-theorisation to be tested against the floor, not as an authority over it [A].

### The capabilities approach: the worked-out middle path
Nussbaum and Sen turn the floor into a usable standard. Nussbaum lists **ten central human
capabilities** — life; bodily health; bodily integrity; senses, imagination & thought; emotions;
practical reason; affiliation; other species; play; control over one's environment — as a universal
threshold that can be *realised plurally* in different cultures [S6]. Sen, wary of imposing a fixed
list, treats human rights as "goals towards which every society could progress," prioritising agency
and democratic deliberation; Nussbaum treats the capabilities as "non-negotiable fundamental
entitlements" [S6]. Either way this answers "by what standard?": **a dharma is better insofar as it
secures the central capabilities / enables flourishing for more people, more robustly** — without
dictating the thick cultural form that does so. Human rights function similarly as an *overlapping
consensus*: a floor reached by convergence across traditions rather than by imposition [A].

### So: are some dharmas better? Yes — on four criteria
A dharma is better insofar as it (1) **meets the floor** (limits violence and cruelty, secures basic
capabilities/rights); (2) **enables flourishing/cooperation under its actual conditions** (the
functional/adaptive test [A]); (3) **stays self-correcting and non-oppressive** (the reform capacity
of [[2.4-transmission-institutionalization]] and the protected dissent of [[3.1-tension-resolution]]);
and (4) **respects the autonomy of other dharmas** where reasonable. Worse dharmas breach the floor
(license cruelty, crush capabilities), ossify against correction, or impose themselves on others.

### Avoiding both failure modes
- **Against relativism.** It is self-refuting (the claim "there is no universal moral truth" is
  asserted as a universal truth); morally intolerable (it cannot condemn atrocity); and incoherent for
  *reform* — an internal reformer (abolitionist, suffragist) needs a standard *above* current practice
  to call that practice wrong, which normative relativism denies [S7].
- **Against imposed monism.** Berlin's incommensurability and the capabilities' plural realisation both
  block a single thick universal; the floor *underdetermines* the higher forms [S5][S6]. Moreover,
  imposing one thick dharma is itself a *floor-violation* (it denies others practical reason and
  affiliation). The characteristic trap is mistaking one's own local thick dharma for the universal
  floor — the WEIRD-as-universal error (see [[7.2-appropriation]]).

### The reflexive bite for this project
DD's non-privileging stance is **pluralism-with-a-floor, not relativism**. That is what lets it
coherently condemn failure modes ([[7.1-failure-modes]]) and pursue a defensible thin floor for
grounding machine ethics ([[8.4-encoding-dharma-machines]]). Blackford's "modern moral predicament" —
plural traditions inside one society, plus global problems (climate, pandemics, weapons) demanding
cross-tradition cooperation — is precisely why a thin universal floor is now *practically* necessary,
not merely theoretically tidy [S3] (→ [[8.1-planetary-dharma]]).

## Tensions, gaps & corpus bias

- **Where exactly is the floor?** The thin/thick line is contested and itself culturally inflected:
  one tradition's "floor" (gender equality, individual rights) is another's "thick imposition." This is
  the live Sen-vs-Nussbaum disagreement — Sen refuses a fixed list precisely to avoid smuggling in a
  parochial universalism; Nussbaum insists a list is needed for the standard to have teeth [S6]. Hard
  cases (FGM, caste, apostasy, the moral status of animals) sit right on the line. **Caste, worked as a
  concrete instance rather than a label:** the Gita's own scheme confines two *varṇas* to service
  (18.44) and grounds the order in divine ordination (4.13) [S9], which on criteria (1) (basic
  capabilities) and (3) (self-correcting, non-oppressive) reads as a floor-breach — while the *same*
  text supplies the *internal* resource for reform, its universal-access verses (9.32, 5.18) [S9], which
  is exactly the SEP "reformer objection" mechanism the note invokes at [S7]: a standard *above* current
  practice, found *inside* the tradition, by which the practice can be called wrong. ▲ A full treatment
  needs the secondary scholarship (Ambedkar's critique, Gandhi's allegorical reading) that the corpus
  does **not** contain, so this stays a worked illustration, not a settled adjudication [A].
- **Incommensurability cuts both ways.** If values are genuinely incommensurable (Berlin), then ranking
  dharmas by an aggregate "betterness" is sometimes *impossible* — expect ties and tragic trade-offs,
  not a clean total ordering [S5].
- **"Adaptive = good" is a naturalistic-fallacy risk.** The corpus's evolutionary criterion (viable vs
  maladaptive [A]) can slide from *is* to *ought*: an adaptive-but-cruel dharma, or a just-but-fragile
  one, shows that survival-value and moral worth can come apart (the [[3.3-grounding-problem]] caution).
  This guard now has explicit peer-reviewed backing rather than resting on the note's own voice: the
  evolutionary "functional fitness gained from such mechanisms refers to incremental gains throughout
  evolutionary history and does not necessarily have anything to say about how beneficial these evolved
  psychological and cultural mechanisms are in the contexts of modern-day societies" [S10].
- **Corpus bias.** The corpus *wants* both non-privileging pluralism and the right to judge, but does not
  fully fix where the floor sits, sometimes asserting suffering-reduction as an uncontested standard. The
  philosophical machinery for holding pluralism and universalism together (Berlin, Nussbaum/Sen, the
  relativism taxonomy) is imported here from external sources.
- **Contestation may be built into the concept, not only the values.** Part of why dharmas resist a
  single ranking is that *dharma* — like *justice*, *art*, and *democracy* — is plausibly an
  **essentially contested concept** (Gallie): disagreement over its correct application is internal
  to the concept itself, so some dispute persists even once the floor is fixed. The semantic side of
  this plurality (family resemblance, open texture, definition-as-tool) is treated in
  [[1.3-boundary-definition]] [S8].
- **Cited via summaries — with one primary-text exception.** Berlin, Nussbaum, Sen, and the relativism
  taxonomy are cited via SEP/IEP and secondary sources, not primary texts. The Gita is now the note's
  one *primary* dharmic exhibit [S9], but only for argument structure: its translation is anonymous and
  unreliable (register `verified: unverified`), and the corpus holds **no secondary Gita scholarship**
  (Ambedkar, Gandhi, Matilal), so the caste hard-case stays an illustration pending external sources.
