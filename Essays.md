# In Search of Dharma — Series Blueprint

**In Search of Dharma** is an 8-part essay series that takes the word *dharma*
away from the yoga studio and shows it as something far larger and stranger: the
universal human craft of building an ethical world to live inside. The series is
written as a genuine search — a first-person journey from *"what even is a
dharma?"* through real dharmas in the wild to *"can we build new ones on
purpose?"*

This file is the **master blueprint and status dashboard** for the essays
(Stage 2 of the project). It plays the same role for the essays that
[`Questions.md`](Questions.md) plays for the research notes. The essays are
written *from* the completed research notes — every essay is downstream of cited
evidence (see [Provenance](#provenance--traceability)).

---

## The series at a glance

| | |
|---|---|
| **Premise** | *Dharma* is not an exotic Eastern religion-word. It is what every human group does: assemble a way of living — values, stories, practices — that *holds the group together*. There are many; there is no single *The* Dharma. |
| **Thesis (the destination)** | Dharmas are **made, plural, and unprivileged**. Because they are made rather than found, we can — and now must — build new ones consciously. |
| **Audience** | A smart newcomer with no background, reading at roughly a year-12 level. The series assumes only a vague, orientalist sense of the word and respects the reader's intelligence throughout — clear, never simplistic. |
| **Voice & POV** | First-person guide. A curious narrator on a real search: thinking aloud, following the evidence, willing to doubt and to be surprised. |
| **Length** | ~5,000 words per essay (±1,000). |
| **Sourcing** | Sources named naturally in the prose ("the anthropologist Christopher Boehm spent years watching…"); **no inline citation marks**. Each essay closes with a **Sources & further reading** list tying back to the DD research notes and the original works. |
| **Format** | Standalone but sequential. Each essay stands alone; read in order they form one argument. |

## The arc

The eight Parts are not eight topics — they are one movement in five beats:

> **1 · Dissolve** → **2–5 · Establish** *(universal → keystone case → plural →
> mechanism)* → **6 · Stress-test** → **7 · Pivot** → **8 · Build**

- **Dissolve (1).** Remove the reader's inherited assumption that "dharma" is a
  vague Buddhist/Hindu spiritual thing. Replace it with a working definition.
- **Establish (2–5).** Build the three load-bearing claims — dharmas are
  *universal*, they are *made*, they are *plural* — and show *how* they work on a
  person. The order matters: the abstract claim (2) is made concrete in a single
  nameable case (3) *before* the breadth tour (4), so the parade of examples lands
  as confirmation rather than assertion.
- **Stress-test (6).** The honest reckoning with harm. Placed *before* the
  constructive finale on purpose: a series that only sells dharma reads as
  advocacy. Earning the skeptic's trust here is what licenses Part 8.
- **Pivot (7).** The contemporary condition — old foundations gone, new dharmas
  still thin — turns the question from *"which dharma is true?"* to *"can we build
  a better one?"*
- **Build (8).** The generative turn. The thesis lands in full.

**Threads planted early and paid off late** (a journey needs setups that close):
- *Can a dharma bind you without a god behind it?* — planted in **Part 1**, made
  urgent in **Part 7**.
- *If dharmas are plural, are some better than others?* — planted in **Part 1**,
  resolved into a stance in **Part 8**.
- *The yoga-studio image* the series opens by dissolving (Part 1) returns as a
  named failure mode — appropriation — in **Part 6**.

## The eight Parts

### Part 1 — Defining Dharma
*Dissolve. Take the word back from the yoga studio and replace a vague feeling
with a working definition.*

- **Reader's question:** What *is* a dharma, really?
- **Opening image:** The word the reader thinks they know — Kerouac's *Dharma
  Bums*, "good karma," studio décor — set against its actual root.
- **What it covers:** The etymology as the spine of the essay — Sanskrit *√dhṛ*
  ("to hold, support, sustain"), from Proto-Indo-European *\*dʰer-*, literally
  *"that which holds"*, a cousin of the English word *firm*. From there: a dharma
  is whatever **holds a way of life together**. The plural move — there is no
  single *The* Dharma; secular and religious dharmas sit on the same shelf. The
  boundary — what separates a *dharma* from mere custom, law, etiquette or
  ideology (it is comprehensive, identity-constituting, oriented to a good life,
  and *reflectively self-aware*).
- **Threads planted:** Can "that which holds" still bind without a god behind it?
  (→ 7). If dharmas are plural, are some better than others? (→ 8).
- **Draws on:** [1.1 Etymology](1-foundational/1.1-etymology.md),
  [1.2 Universal Patterns](1-foundational/1.2-universal-patterns.md),
  [1.3 Boundary & Definition](1-foundational/1.3-boundary-definition.md),
  [1.4 Cross-cultural Cognates](1-foundational/1.4-cross-cultural-cognates.md);
  threads of [3.3 Grounding Problem](3-philosophical/3.3-grounding-problem.md) and
  [3.4 Relativism vs. Universalism](3-philosophical/3.4-relativism-universalism.md);
  companion essay `defining-definition.md` (on how meanings themselves drift).
- **Hands off:** If every society grows one of these, where do they come from?

### Part 2 — The Genesis of Dharmas
*Establish: universal. Dharmas aren't handed down from mountaintops — they grow,
predictably, wherever humans must trust strangers.*

- **Reader's question:** Why does this keep happening, in every society, all over
  the world?
- **Opening image:** The small band where everyone knows everyone and no written
  code is needed — set against the first city full of strangers you will never
  see again.
- **What it covers:** Morality as, in the corpus's phrase, "always and everywhere
  a cooperative phenomenon" — Oliver Scott Curry's seven moral rules found across
  60 societies with no counter-examples; the evolutionary substrate of
  reciprocity, reputation and Christopher Boehm's egalitarian bands. The jump from
  kinship-scale to anonymous urban scale forcing explicit, portable codes. The
  Axial Age (c. 800–200 BCE) and why dharmic thinking erupted across disconnected
  civilizations at roughly the same time. The pre-Axial moral baseline the
  codifications built *on* — what was already there, and what writing it down
  added.
- **Draws on:** [2.1 Axial Age](2-historical/2.1-axial-age.md),
  [2.2 Urban Complexity](2-historical/2.2-urban-complexity.md),
  [2.3 Pre-Axial Ethics](2-historical/2.3-pre-axial-ethics.md),
  [5.2 Evolutionary Psychology](5-psychological/5.2-evolutionary-psychology.md),
  [1.2 Universal Patterns](1-foundational/1.2-universal-patterns.md).
- **Hands off:** Enough theory — let's watch one dharma actually get built, by
  people we can name, within living memory.

### Part 3 — The Dharmas of the Samin of Java
*Establish: the keystone case. One dharma in close-up — proof that a dharma can be
made, recently, from the bottom up, by people who left their names.*

- **Reader's question:** What does a living, hand-made dharma actually look like
  up close?
- **Opening image:** The Saminist deadpan to colonial officials — *"Where are you
  from?" "From the forest." "Where are you going?" "To the forest."* — or the
  Kartini Kendeng women sitting outside Indonesia's Presidential Palace in 2016
  and 2017 with their feet set in blocks of cement.
- **What it covers:** Samin Surosentiko in the teak villages of Blora around 1890;
  *Agama Adam*, "the religion of Adam"; the compact, this-worldly code — honesty
  and plain speech, simplicity, self-sufficiency, non-violence, and the conviction
  that land, water and forest are common property. Transmission *orally*, by an
  illiterate founder. Blunt low Javanese (*ngoko*) spoken to officials as a
  refusal of the entire hierarchy of deference — an "internal heresy" of Javanese
  culture, not a foreign import. The dharma's survival into the present as the
  Sedulur Sikep's ecological resistance in the Kendeng karst.
- **▲ Honest framing (do not skip):** Saminism is *not* secular — it has a soul
  and a notion of *moksha*. That is exactly its value here: a **boundary case**
  proving a dharma need be neither a world religion nor a secular philosophy.
  Lightly model the source-caution too — the doctrine is reconstructed from
  outsiders' accounts because the Samin wrote nothing down — so the reader watches
  us being careful.
- **Draws on:** [4.1 Samin of Java](4-cultural/4.1-samin-of-java.md) (in full);
  links onward to [7.3 Dharma & Power](7-critical/7.3-dharma-and-power.md),
  [6.3 Economics, Work & Inequality](6-contemporary/6.3-economics-work-inequality.md),
  [8.1 Planetary Dharma](8-future/8.1-planetary-dharma.md).
- **Hands off:** The Samin are one. Pull back and the world is full of them.

### Part 4 — A World of Dharmas
*Establish: plural. Once you can see dharmas, you can't stop seeing them — and
they are gloriously, instructively different.*

- **Reader's question:** How different, and how alike, do dharmas get?
- **Opening image:** Four people from four worlds answering the same question —
  *how should I live?* — and giving four answers that rhyme.
- **What it covers:** Ubuntu ("I am because we are") and relational personhood
  against Western individualism; the Stoics as a secular-friendly Greco-Roman
  dharma of what is and isn't in your control — and a familiar on-ramp for Western
  readers; indigenous oral dharmas (the Aboriginal Australian Dreaming) and what
  survives without writing — essential versus merely cultural elements; and the
  hardest test of the non-privileging stance, the **Abrahamic frameworks**
  (covenant, halakha, sharia, Christian *caritas*) seen as dharmas among dharmas.
  The throughline: a shared cooperative core, wrapped in very different metaphysics
  and very different answers to *whom must I cooperate with?*
- **▲ Corpus-bias note:** The shared corpus leans secular / evolutionary /
  Western-academic and under-weights the Abrahamic traditions. This essay
  consciously counterweights that lean and should say, lightly, that it is doing
  so. The Stoics are not a dedicated research note — source them freshly (corpus +
  external) at draft time.
- **Draws on:** [4.2 Ubuntu](4-cultural/4.2-ubuntu.md),
  [4.3 Indigenous Oral Dharmas](4-cultural/4.3-indigenous-oral-dharmas.md),
  [4.4 Abrahamic Frameworks](4-cultural/4.4-abrahamic-frameworks.md),
  [1.4 Cross-cultural Cognates](1-foundational/1.4-cross-cultural-cognates.md).
- **Hands off:** These systems aren't just *believed* — they're *installed*. How
  does a dharma get from a page or a chant into your bones?

### Part 5 — How a Dharma Gets Under Your Skin
*Establish: mechanism. A dharma you merely agree with does nothing. The ones that
work rewire you.*

- **Reader's question:** Why does a dharma come to feel like plain truth from the
  inside?
- **Opening image:** The ritual you don't intellectually "believe" that moves you
  anyway; the convert who changed not their argument but their habits.
- **What it covers:** Jonathan Haidt's moral foundations and the WEIRD-sample
  critique — your moral intuitions vary across populations and you did not choose
  them. Ritual, repeated practice and habituation as the *install mechanism* that
  turns a stated ideal into a lived disposition. The neuroscience of contemplative
  practice — handled with calibration: the effects are real but routinely
  oversold. The deep point: a working dharma runs *below* conscious argument, and
  that is precisely where its power — and its danger — comes from.
- **Draws on:** [5.1 Neurological Basis](5-psychological/5.1-neurological-basis.md),
  [5.3 Moral Psychology & Development](5-psychological/5.3-moral-psychology-development.md),
  [5.4 Ritual, Practice & Habituation](5-psychological/5.4-ritual-habituation.md),
  [5.2 Evolutionary Psychology](5-psychological/5.2-evolutionary-psychology.md).
- **Hands off:** Anything this powerful and this far below awareness can drive us
  off a cliff as easily as toward the good.

### Part 6 — When Dharmas Go Wrong
*Stress-test. Everything that makes a dharma work also makes it dangerous.*

- **Reader's question:** If dharmas are so good for us, why has so much harm been
  done in their name?
- **Opening image:** A dharma curdling — the in-group bond that becomes a weapon;
  the liberating practice that becomes a cage.
- **What it covers:** The common failure modes — corruption, capture by power,
  exclusion, ossified dogma. Dharma and power: the same machinery that let the
  Samin *refuse* authority let caste *sanctify* it. Gender — where dharmas have
  subordinated and where they have liberated. Economics and inequality — dharmas
  justifying, constraining, or critiquing material arrangements. And cultural
  appropriation and orientalism — the Western yoga-studio "dharma" as a genuine
  failure mode of cross-cultural borrowing, closing the loop on the image Part 1
  opened with.
- **Draws on:** [7.1 Failure Modes](7-critical/7.1-failure-modes.md),
  [7.2 Appropriation](7-critical/7.2-appropriation.md),
  [7.3 Dharma & Power](7-critical/7.3-dharma-and-power.md),
  [6.3 Economics, Work & Inequality](6-contemporary/6.3-economics-work-inequality.md),
  [6.4 Gender & Dharma](6-contemporary/6.4-gender-and-dharma.md).
- **Hands off:** And most of the old dharmas were built for a world that no longer
  exists. What happens to dharma now?

### Part 7 — Dharmas in a Secular Age
*Pivot. Hundreds of millions now say they're "spiritual but not religious" — not
the absence of a dharma, but the search for one without the old foundations.*

- **Reader's question:** Can a dharma survive once its old foundations are gone?
- **Opening image:** The "spiritual but not religious" seeker; the difficulty of
  holding still under a firehose of information.
- **What it covers:** The secular translation problem — can you keep a dharma's
  transformative power without its metaphysics? Successes and failures, from
  Stephen Batchelor's secular Buddhism to mindfulness stripped down to a wellness
  product. **The grounding problem, now urgent:** can a godless dharma actually
  *bind* — pay off the thread planted in Part 1. Digital-age terrain — attention,
  information overload, social media and algorithmic life as new ethical ground.
  The risk of dharma-as-consumer-product (linking back to Part 6).
- **Draws on:** [6.1 Secular Translation](6-contemporary/6.1-secular-translation.md),
  [6.2 Digital-Age Dharma](6-contemporary/6.2-digital-age-dharma.md),
  [3.3 Grounding Problem](3-philosophical/3.3-grounding-problem.md),
  [3.1 Tension Resolution](3-philosophical/3.1-tension-resolution.md);
  carryover from [7.2 Appropriation](7-critical/7.2-appropriation.md).
- **Hands off:** So the question stops being *"which dharma is true?"* and becomes
  *"can we build a better one, on purpose?"*

### Part 8 — Creating Dharmas
*Build — the destination. We have always made our dharmas; we just told ourselves
we found them. Can we now do it consciously, in time, and for a planet?*

- **Reader's question:** Can we make dharmas on purpose — well, and in time?
- **Opening image:** The deliberate design of a new ethic — and the warning
  standing right beside it.
- **What it covers:** A *planetary dharma* — the Kendeng farmers' common-property
  cosmology scaled to a whole planet under climate pressure. AI and dharma —
  human–machine coexistence, economic disempowerment, Universal Basic Income, and
  what ethical frameworks machines would actually need. A *post-traditional
  dharma* that honours the wisdom traditions while embracing science and
  pluralism. And the reflexive end-point — encoding dharma into machines, this
  very project's own goal placed under scrutiny, with its central risk named: the
  flattening of plural dharmas into a single machine-readable ethic. Here the
  relativism-versus-universalism thread resolves into a *stance*: plural, but not
  anything-goes — a dharma judged by how well it **holds without crushing**. Close
  by returning to the root from Part 1: *that which holds*. We are choosing what
  will hold us next.
- **Draws on:** [8.1 Planetary Dharma](8-future/8.1-planetary-dharma.md),
  [8.2 AI & Dharma](8-future/8.2-ai-and-dharma.md),
  [8.3 Post-Traditional Dharmas](8-future/8.3-post-traditional-dharmas.md),
  [8.4 Encoding Dharma into Machines](8-future/8.4-encoding-dharma-machines.md),
  [3.4 Relativism vs. Universalism](3-philosophical/3.4-relativism-universalism.md),
  [7.4 Measurement & Falsifiability](7-critical/7.4-measurement-falsifiability.md).
- **Hands off:** The close. You already practice a dharma — the invitation is to
  practice it awake.

## Writing conventions

These apply to every essay when drafting begins (Stage 2):

- **Voice.** First-person guide. The narrator is on a real search — curious,
  evidence-led, willing to say "I was wrong about this." Not a lecturer.
- **Reading level.** Roughly year-12. Define nothing the reader can infer;
  patronise nothing. Plain words for hard ideas.
- **Length.** ~5,000 words per essay (±1,000).
- **Structure within an essay.** Open on the concrete image; pose the reader's
  question; follow the search; turn; land the hand-off to the next Part. Standalone
  but sequential.
- **Sourcing.** Name people and works in the prose where it adds authority or
  colour; never use inline citation marks or `[S#]` tags in the body. Each essay
  ends with **Sources & further reading**, grouped, pointing back to the DD
  research notes and to the original works those notes cite.
- **Honesty.** Where the corpus leans (secular / evolutionary / Western-academic),
  the essay says so in passing rather than hiding it — especially Parts 3, 4 and 6.

  `1-defining-dharma.md`, `3-samin-of-java.md` — paralleling the research notes'

## Provenance & traceability

This is **Stage 2** of the project's two-stage pipeline (gather, then write; see
[`METHODOLOGY.md`](METHODOLOGY.md)). The essays are
written *from* the completed, cited research notes — not from scratch. Every
substantive claim in an essay must trace either to a research note (which itself
carries `[S#]` citations) or to fresh external sourcing flagged at draft time. The
prose hides the apparatus; the **Sources & further reading** list preserves the
chain. The point of the whole project — traceable evidence behind every claim —
survives the move from note to essay.
